## Teaching Goal

Help students read trickster and repeated-test tales without labeling cleverness as always good or always bad.

## Student Outcome

The student can explain how a trick or repeated test reveals character and shapes the lesson.

## Misconception Checks

Treating all tricksters as heroes; treating deception as always the lesson; missing what changes across repeated tests.

## Questions

```question
key: u02_l03_q01_trickster_role
type: multiple-choice
prompt: A spider tells a boastful king three riddles to reveal that the king never listens. What is the trickster's main role?
choices:
  - To expose the king's flaw through a test.
  - To prove every ruler is wise.
  - To stop all riddles from being used.
  - To describe the palace walls.
correctAnswer: To expose the king's flaw through a test.
explanation: The trickster uses riddles as a test that reveals the king's poor listening.
hint: Ask what the trick makes visible.
```

```question
key: u02_l03_q02_test_sequence
type: order-items
prompt: Order the repeated tests in the tale.
items:
  - The youngest child shares the water.
  - The eldest child refuses to share bread.
  - The middle child refuses to carry wood.
  - The old woman reveals the safe road.
correctOrder:
  - The eldest child refuses to share bread.
  - The middle child refuses to carry wood.
  - The youngest child shares the water.
  - The old woman reveals the safe road.
explanation: The repeated tests show who refuses help and who chooses generosity.
hint: The first two tests fail; the third test succeeds.
```

```question
key: u02_l03_q03_clever_good_or_bad
type: passage-question
prompt: Read the tale moment and answer.
passageTitle: The Jackal's Bowl
passage: |
  A jackal hid the village bowl so everyone would ask him for help.
  When the children could not eat, an old woman found the bowl under his mat.
question: What does the jackal's cleverness show in this passage?
choices:
  - Cleverness can be selfish when it harms others.
  - Cleverness is always generous.
  - The children hid the bowl.
  - The old woman needed a new mat.
correctAnswer: Cleverness can be selfish when it harms others.
explanation: The jackal uses cleverness to control others, and the children are harmed.
hint: Ask who benefits and who is hurt by the trick.
```


```question
key: u02_l03_q05_trick_motive
type: multiple-choice
prompt: A hare tricks a greedy lion into roaring at his own reflection until he falls into a well. What is the hare's likely motive?
choices:
  - To save the other animals from the lion.
  - To help the lion practice singing.
  - To collect water from the well.
  - To make the reflection become real.
correctAnswer: To save the other animals from the lion.
explanation: The trick protects the animals by using the lion's pride against him.
hint: Ask what problem the trick solves.
```

```question
key: u02_l03_q06_trick_lesson
type: passage-question
prompt: Read the tale and choose the lesson.
passageTitle: The Three Shells
passage: |
  A clever child placed three shells before a greedy trader.
  Each time the trader grabbed the largest shell, it was empty.
  The smallest shell, which he ignored, held the pearl.
question: Which lesson best fits the repeated test?
choices:
  - Greed can make a person miss what matters.
  - Large shells always hold pearls.
  - Children should never use shells.
  - Traders cannot count to three.
correctAnswer: Greed can make a person miss what matters.
explanation: The trader repeats the greedy choice and misses the pearl.
hint: Notice what the trader keeps choosing.
```
