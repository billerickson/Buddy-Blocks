## Teaching Goal

Read a fable through why a character acts and what follows from the choice.

## Student Outcome

The student can identify a character's motive and explain how the consequence supports the moral.

## Misconception Checks

Treating motive as the same as action; assuming the consequence is random; naming greed, pride, or fear without evidence.

## Questions

```question
key: u02_l02_q01_motive_choice_consequence
type: multi-blank-cloze
prompt: Complete the fable chain.
parts:
  - "The hare's motive was "
  - "; his choice was "
  - "; the consequence was "
  - .
blanks:
  - correctAnswer: pride
    acceptedAnswers:
      - pride
      - showing off
  - correctAnswer: to nap during the race
    acceptedAnswers:
      - to nap during the race
      - napping during the race
  - correctAnswer: he lost
    acceptedAnswers:
      - he lost
      - he lost the race
explanation: Pride explains the choice to nap, and losing is the consequence.
hint: Motive is why, choice is what he did, consequence is what happened.
```

```question
key: u02_l02_q02_motive_supported
type: passage-question
prompt: Read the fable moment and choose the motive.
passageTitle: The Fox And The Shadow
passage: |
  A fox saw his shadow stretch long across the road.
  "I must be larger than the lion," he said.
  He marched toward the lion's den and demanded the first share of food.
question: What motive best explains the fox's choice?
choices:
  - Pride makes him think he deserves more.
  - Kindness makes him share with the lion.
  - Fear makes him hide from the road.
  - Hunger makes him plant grain.
correctAnswer: Pride makes him think he deserves more.
explanation: The fox brags about being larger than the lion and demands the first share.
hint: Look for what the fox believes about himself before he acts.
```

```question
key: u02_l02_q03_consequence_best
type: multiple-choice
prompt: In a fable, a mouse gnaws a net to free the lion who spared him earlier. Which consequence best fits the lion's earlier mercy?
choices:
  - The mouse is able to help the lion later.
  - The lion forgets every animal's name.
  - The net becomes a golden crown.
  - The forest turns into stone.
correctAnswer: The mouse is able to help the lion later.
explanation: The earlier mercy leads to later help, which supports the fable's lesson.
hint: Choose the result connected to the earlier choice.
```

```question
key: u02_l02_q04_moral_by_chain
type: passage-question
prompt: Read the fable and choose the moral.
passageTitle: The Proud Drum
passage: |
  A drum boomed loudly in the temple and laughed at a quiet flute.
  During the evening song, the drum tore because it had been struck too hard.
  The flute played softly and carried the melody to the end.
question: Which moral is best supported by the motive and consequence?
choices:
  - Loud pride is not the same as lasting worth.
  - Drums are never useful in music.
  - Quiet things should never be heard.
  - Evening songs are always sad.
correctAnswer: Loud pride is not the same as lasting worth.
explanation: The drum's pride and loudness do not last, while the quiet flute completes the song.
hint: Connect the proud behavior to what happens later.
```
