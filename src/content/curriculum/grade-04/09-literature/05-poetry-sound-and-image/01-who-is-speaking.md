## Teaching Goal

Identify the speaker of a poem or oral passage using clues in the text.

## Student Outcome

The student can distinguish speaker from poet or narrator in supported examples.

## Misconception Checks

Assuming the poet is always the speaker; ignoring "I" or "you" clues; treating every voice as a narrator.

## Questions

```question
key: u05_l01_q01_speaker_clue
type: passage-question
prompt: Read the lines and identify the speaker.
passageTitle: At The Harbor
passage: |
  I lift my oar when dawn is gray.
  I guide the boat across the bay.
question: Who is most likely speaking?
choices:
  - A boat rower or ferryman
  - A mountain
  - A sleeping child
  - A city wall
correctAnswer: A boat rower or ferryman
explanation: The speaker says 'I lift my oar' and guides a boat.
hint: Use the action words as clues.
```

```question
key: u05_l01_q02_speaker_not_poet
type: multiple-choice
prompt: Why should you not always assume the speaker is the poet?
choices:
  - A poem can use an invented voice, like a traveler, river, or hero.
  - Poems never have speakers.
  - The poet must be every character in a drama scene.
  - A speaker is the same as a stanza.
correctAnswer: A poem can use an invented voice, like a traveler, river, or hero.
explanation: The speaker is the voice in the poem, which may be different from the poet.
hint: Speaker means the voice we hear.
```

```question
key: u05_l01_q03_clue_match
type: match-pairs
prompt: Match each speaker clue to what it tells the reader.
pairs:
  - left: I guard the gate
    right: The speaker has a guarding role.
  - left: O traveler, listen
    right: The speaker addresses a traveler.
  - left: My leaves shake
    right: The speaker may be a tree.
  - left: I miss my lost companion
    right: The speaker feels grief or longing.
explanation: Pronouns, address, actions, and feelings help identify speaker.
hint: Look at what the voice says about itself.
```
