## Teaching Goal

Rebuild the idea that a complete sentence has a subject and a predicate that work together to express a complete thought.

## Student Outcome

The student can identify whether a short group of words is complete and name the subject or predicate that is missing.

## Key Ideas

A subject tells who or what the sentence is about; a predicate tells something about the subject.

## Misconception Checks

A capital letter and end mark make any word group complete; the subject must be the first word; a long fragment is a sentence.

## Questions

```question
key: u01_l01_q01_complete_sentence
type: multiple-choice
prompt: Which group of words is a complete sentence?
choices:
  - The bright kite.
  - Flew above the park.
  - The bright kite flew above the park.
  - Above the park on a windy day.
correctAnswer: The bright kite flew above the park.
explanation: A complete sentence has a subject and a predicate. The bright kite is the subject, and flew above the park is the predicate.
hint: Look for both who or what the sentence is about and what happened.
```

```question
key: u01_l01_q02_subject_predicate_jobs
type: match-pairs
prompt: Match each sentence part to its job.
pairs:
  - left: subject
    right: names who or what the sentence is about
  - left: predicate
    right: tells something about the subject
  - left: fragment
    right: is missing a complete sentence part
  - left: complete sentence
    right: has a subject and predicate
explanation: Subjects and predicates work together to make complete sentences.
hint: Ask what each part does for the reader.
```

```question
key: u01_l01_q03_find_subject
type: fill-blank
prompt: "In the sentence, choose the subject: The careful painter cleaned the brushes."
sentenceBefore: "The subject is "
sentenceAfter: .
choices:
  - The careful painter
  - cleaned
  - the brushes
  - careful
correctAnswer: The careful painter
explanation: The sentence is about the careful painter. The predicate tells what the painter did.
hint: Ask who or what cleaned the brushes.
```

```question
key: u01_l01_q04_missing_predicate
type: multiple-choice
prompt: What is missing from this fragment? The blue bicycle near the fence.
choices:
  - a subject
  - a predicate
  - a capital letter
  - a noun
correctAnswer: a predicate
explanation: The fragment names a bicycle, but it does not tell what the bicycle did or is.
hint: The words name something, but do they tell anything about it?
```

```question
key: u01_l01_q05_repair_fragment
type: error-correction
prompt: Fix the fragment by adding a predicate.
sentence: The tired runner.
acceptedAnswers:
  - The tired runner rested.
  - The tired runner stopped.
  - The tired runner won.
explanation: Each correct answer keeps the subject and adds a predicate that tells something about the runner.
hint: Add words that tell what the runner did or was.
```

```question
key: u01_l01_q06_choose_repair
type: multiple-choice
prompt: "Which choice best fixes the fragment: Ran through the puddles."
choices:
  - The children ran through the puddles.
  - Quickly ran through the puddles.
  - Through the puddles.
  - Ran through the puddles after lunch.
correctAnswer: The children ran through the puddles.
explanation: The fragment already has a predicate. Adding The children gives it a subject.
hint: The words tell an action. Ask who did it.
```
