## Teaching Goal

Read confession as shaped inquiry into self, memory, desire, and will.

## Student Outcome

The student can explain how a confessional passage moves from personal memory to a larger question.

## Misconception Checks

Confession is just autobiography; memory is neutral record; inward conflict is only emotion.

## Questions

```question
key: u03_l03_q01_confession_form
type: multiple-choice
prompt: What makes confession more than simple autobiography in this lesson?
choices:
  - A remembered event becomes reflection on self, desire, memory, or will.
  - It lists dates without interpretation.
  - It avoids all inward questions.
  - It never addresses a reader or God.
correctAnswer: A remembered event becomes reflection on self, desire, memory, or will.
explanation: Confession shapes memory into inquiry about the self and its loves.
hint: Look for movement from event to reflection.
```

```question
key: u03_l03_q02_memory_to_reflection
type: passage-question
prompt: Read the confessional passage and answer.
passageTitle: The Stolen Fruit
passage: |
  I remember taking what I did not need. The fruit was not sweet enough to explain me. I loved the theft because it let me love my own crooked freedom.
question: What movement does the passage make?
choices:
  - From remembered event to reflection on desire and will.
  - From weather report to travel plan.
  - From public law to economic theory only.
  - From confession to pure biography without interpretation.
correctAnswer: From remembered event to reflection on desire and will.
explanation: The passage begins with memory, then asks what the act revealed about desire.
hint: Notice the shift after 'The fruit was not sweet enough.'
```
