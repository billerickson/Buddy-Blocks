## Teaching Goal

Build responsible comparisons across form, period, and recurring question.

## Student Outcome

The student can compare two texts by naming shared question, difference in form, and evidence from each.

## Misconception Checks

Synthesis means vague similarity; difference weakens comparison; one text's evidence is enough.

## Questions

```question
key: u06_l03_q01_comparison_need
type: multiple-choice
prompt: What does a strong cross-era comparison need?
choices:
  - A shared question, evidence from both texts, and a real difference in form or context.
  - Only a vague similarity.
  - Evidence from one text only.
  - A claim that ignores genre.
correctAnswer: A shared question, evidence from both texts, and a real difference in form or context.
explanation: Responsible synthesis connects texts while preserving difference.
hint: Look for connection plus evidence plus difference.
```

```question
key: u06_l03_q02_shared_questions
type: match-pairs
prompt: Match each pair of texts or forms to a shared question.
pairs:
  - left: Stoic meditation and Ibsen drama
    right: How should a person act under pressure?
  - left: Socratic dialogue and founding argument
    right: What makes justice or law legitimate?
  - left: Augustine and Montaigne
    right: How does the self examine itself?
  - left: Smith and Marx
    right: How should we understand labor and social order?
explanation: Shared questions allow comparison across very different forms.
hint: Find the recurring question that both sides could address.
```

```question
key: u06_l03_q04_best_comparison_claim
type: multiple-choice
prompt: Which comparison claim is strongest?
choices:
  - Both Augustine and Montaigne examine the self, but confession seeks ordered love while the essay tests judgment through examples.
  - Augustine and Montaigne are exactly the same because both use the word I.
  - Only Augustine has a form; Montaigne just has opinions.
  - They cannot be compared because they lived in different periods.
correctAnswer: Both Augustine and Montaigne examine the self, but confession seeks ordered love while the essay tests judgment through examples.
explanation: The claim names a shared question and a difference in form and purpose.
hint: Choose the claim with both connection and distinction.
```

```question
key: u06_l03_q05_comparison_order
type: order-items
prompt: Put the comparison-building steps in order.
items:
  - Explain a difference in form or context.
  - Name the shared question.
  - Choose evidence from Text A.
  - Choose evidence from Text B.
correctOrder:
  - Name the shared question.
  - Choose evidence from Text A.
  - Choose evidence from Text B.
  - Explain a difference in form or context.
explanation: A comparison begins with a shared question, then uses evidence from both texts and explains difference.
hint: Start with what makes the comparison possible.
```

```question
key: u06_l03_q06_difference_matters
type: multiple-choice
prompt: Why does difference matter in synthesis?
choices:
  - Difference keeps the comparison honest about form, period, audience, and purpose.
  - Difference means comparison is impossible.
  - Difference lets the reader ignore one text.
  - Difference proves the older text is useless.
correctAnswer: Difference keeps the comparison honest about form, period, audience, and purpose.
explanation: Synthesis should connect texts without erasing what makes each one distinct.
hint: The best answer should preserve comparison and honesty.
```
