## Teaching Goal

Help students read comic exaggeration as a way of raising civic and educational questions.

## Student Outcome

The student can distinguish literal surface from satiric target in an ancient comedy-style passage.

## Misconception Checks

Comedy is not serious; satire means the text literally endorses the ridiculous claim; laughter cancels interpretation.

## Questions

```question
key: u02_l02_q01_satire_target
type: passage-question
prompt: Read the comic scene and answer.
passageTitle: The School Of Clouds
passage: |
  A comic teacher announces, "For a fee, I can teach anyone to make the weaker argument defeat the stronger one. No truth required, only clever words."
question: What is the most likely satiric target?
choices:
  - Education that values clever victory over truth.
  - All teachers who charge any fee.
  - Clouds as weather.
  - Every form of public speaking.
correctAnswer: Education that values clever victory over truth.
explanation: The exaggerated boast mocks a kind of education that treats rhetoric as winning without truth.
hint: Ask what serious problem the comic exaggeration exposes.
```

```question
key: u02_l02_q02_surface_target
type: match-pairs
prompt: Match each comic surface detail to the deeper question it raises.
pairs:
  - left: a teacher sells clever arguments
    right: What is education for?
  - left: a fool repeats grand words badly
    right: Does vocabulary equal wisdom?
  - left: citizens laugh at a ridiculous school
    right: How does the city judge learning?
  - left: a weak case is made to sound strong
    right: Can rhetoric distort justice?
explanation: Comedy can use a silly surface to point toward serious civic questions.
hint: Look for the broader issue behind each comic action.
```
