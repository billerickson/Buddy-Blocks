## Teaching Goal

Teach students to track a dialogue as a sequence of questions, answers, definitions, and revisions.

## Student Outcome

The student can identify a Socratic question, an interlocutor's claim, and the turn that changes the discussion.

## Misconception Checks

Dialogue is a simple answer key; Socrates' question is the same as a final doctrine; every speaker has equal authority.

## Questions

```question
key: u02_l01_q01_dialogue_feature
type: multiple-choice
prompt: What should you track first in a Socratic dialogue?
choices:
  - Who asks, who answers, and how the definition changes.
  - Only the last sentence.
  - The author's biography before any passage evidence.
  - Whether the first answer sounds confident.
correctAnswer: Who asks, who answers, and how the definition changes.
explanation: Dialogue meaning often appears through questions, answers, and revisions.
hint: A dialogue moves through speakers, not just through one conclusion.
```

```question
key: u02_l01_q02_dialogue_order
type: order-items
prompt: Put the dialogue movement in order.
items:
  - The first speaker revises the definition.
  - Socrates asks whether the definition fits a hard case.
  - The first speaker offers a definition.
  - The definition appears weaker than before.
correctOrder:
  - The first speaker offers a definition.
  - Socrates asks whether the definition fits a hard case.
  - The first speaker revises the definition.
  - The definition appears weaker than before.
explanation: The dialogue tests a definition by applying pressure, leading to revision or uncertainty.
hint: Start with the offered definition, then the question that tests it.
```

```question
key: u02_l01_q03_next_question
type: dialogue-builder
prompt: Choose the best next Socratic question.
turns:
  - speaker: Interlocutor
    line: Courage is never feeling fear.
  - speaker: Socrates
    line: So courage depends on having no fear at all?
choices:
  - Could someone feel fear and still choose the noble action?
  - What color is courage?
  - Then all fearful people are always wrong, so we are finished.
  - Let us stop asking questions.
correctAnswer: Could someone feel fear and still choose the noble action?
explanation: The question tests the definition with a possible hard case.
hint: A Socratic question often probes whether a definition is too narrow.
```

```question
key: u02_l01_q04_passage_turn
type: passage-question
prompt: Read the exchange and answer.
passageTitle: Testing A Definition
passage: |
  Student: "Justice is helping friends and harming enemies."
  Questioner: "What if a friend is acting unjustly, and an enemy tells the truth?"
question: What does the questioner do?
choices:
  - Tests the definition with a hard case.
  - Changes the subject to friendship only.
  - Proves that justice is impossible.
  - Agrees that enemies should always be harmed.
correctAnswer: Tests the definition with a hard case.
explanation: The question introduces examples that pressure the first definition.
hint: Look at how the second line challenges the first line.
```
