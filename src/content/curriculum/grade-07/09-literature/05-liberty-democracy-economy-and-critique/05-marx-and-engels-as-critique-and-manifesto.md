## Teaching Goal

Read critique, class language, and manifesto rhetoric with genre awareness.

## Student Outcome

The student can distinguish diagnosis, persuasion, prediction, and call to action in a manifesto-style passage.

## Misconception Checks

Critique is mere complaint; manifesto rhetoric is neutral analysis; agreement or disagreement replaces reading.

## Questions

```question
key: u05_l05_q01_manifesto_map
type: multiple-choice
prompt: Which set of features best fits manifesto rhetoric?
choices:
  - Grievance, explanation, audience, obstacle, prediction, and call to action.
  - Meter, rhyme, and chorus.
  - Private memory with no public claim.
  - A neutral list of laboratory results.
correctAnswer: Grievance, explanation, audience, obstacle, prediction, and call to action.
explanation: A manifesto is written to diagnose, persuade, and mobilize.
hint: Think of a text that names a problem and urges action.
```

```question

prompt: Read the manifesto-style passage and answer.
passageTitle: A Call To Workers
passage: |
  You who build the machines are told the machine owns your time. Name the relation honestly, see who profits from it, and join with those whose labor is divided from its fruit.
question: What rhetorical move does the final clause make?
choices:
  - A call to collective action.
  - A neutral lab observation.
  - A private diary memory.
  - A joke with no audience.
correctAnswer: A call to collective action.
explanation: The phrase 'join with those' urges the addressed audience to act together.
hint: Look for the verb that urges action.
```
