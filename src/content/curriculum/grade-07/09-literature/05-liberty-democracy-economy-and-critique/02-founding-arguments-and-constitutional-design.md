## Teaching Goal

Track audience, faction, rights, and institutional reasoning in founding-era argument.

## Student Outcome

The student can identify a constitutional problem and the design feature proposed to answer it.

## Misconception Checks

A founding document is self-explanatory; institutional design is trivia; slogans replace reasons.

## Questions

```question
key: u05_l02_q01_design_argument
type: multiple-choice
prompt: What does a constitutional design argument usually connect?
choices:
  - A public problem, an institutional structure, and a reason the structure helps.
  - A private feeling and a diary entry.
  - A joke and its punchline only.
  - A list of dates with no claim.
correctAnswer: A public problem, an institutional structure, and a reason the structure helps.
explanation: Design arguments explain why a structure answers a civic danger or need.
hint: Look for problem, structure, and reason.
```

```question
key: u05_l02_q02_faction_passage
type: passage-question
prompt: Read the founding-style argument and answer.
passageTitle: A Problem Of Faction
passage: |
  If every passion gathers all power into one place, the public good will bend to the loudest faction. Spread power through offices that must check one another, and ambition may be made to restrain ambition.
question: What design response does the passage propose?
choices:
  - Divide power among offices that check one another.
  - Give all power to the loudest faction.
  - Remove every office from government.
  - Ignore ambition because it never matters.
correctAnswer: Divide power among offices that check one another.
explanation: The passage proposes checked and divided power as a response to faction and ambition.
hint: Look for the structure proposed after the danger is named.
```
