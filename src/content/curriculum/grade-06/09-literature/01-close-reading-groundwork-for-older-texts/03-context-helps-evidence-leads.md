## Teaching Goal

Teach students to use background context as a helper that must return to the passage's words, structure, or situation.

## Student Outcome

The student can choose context that illuminates a passage and reject context-only interpretations.

## Misconception Checks

Context proves meaning without textual evidence; old texts are mainly history lessons; background notes should be memorized instead of applied.

## Questions

```question
key: cl4_u01_l03_q01_context_roles
type: match-pairs
prompt: Match each context note to the way it can help a reader.
pairs:
  - left: A herald was an official messenger.
    right: clarifies a social role
  - left: A laurel wreath could mark honor.
    right: clarifies a symbol or custom
  - left: A chorus may comment on action in drama.
    right: clarifies a genre feature
  - left: A city gate could mark entry and protection.
    right: clarifies setting significance
explanation: Useful context helps explain roles, customs, genre features, or setting details.
hint: Ask what kind of confusion each note would reduce.
```

```question
key: cl4_u01_l03_q02_context_plus_text
type: passage-question
prompt: Use the context note and passage to answer.
passageTitle: The Laurel
passage: |
  Context note: In some ancient settings, a laurel wreath could mark public honor.

  The runner touched the laurel on his brow, then placed it at his mother's feet.
  "The city shouted my name," he said, "but she taught me to endure."
question: How does the context note help interpret the runner's action?
choices:
  - It shows that he gives a sign of public honor to his mother.
  - It proves that his mother won the race.
  - It shows that laurel wreaths were used as money.
  - It proves that the city disliked him.
correctAnswer: It shows that he gives a sign of public honor to his mother.
explanation: The context note explains the laurel as public honor, and the passage shows him placing it at his mother's feet.
hint: Connect the context note to the exact action in the passage.
```

```question
key: cl4_u01_l03_q03_context_overreach
type: multiple-choice
prompt: Which statement overuses context instead of reading the passage?
choices:
  - Because laurel can mark honor, every person wearing laurel must be morally perfect.
  - The laurel helps show that the runner gives public honor to his mother.
  - The mother's teaching matters because the runner says she taught him to endure.
  - The city shouted the runner's name, but he redirects honor toward his mother.
correctAnswer: Because laurel can mark honor, every person wearing laurel must be morally perfect.
explanation: The context note explains a custom, but it does not prove moral perfection.
hint: Look for the claim that goes far beyond both the note and the passage.
```

```question
key: cl4_u01_l03_q06_best_context_note
type: passage-question
prompt: Choose the context note that would help most.
passageTitle: The Chorus Speaks
passage: |
  The two brothers left by opposite doors. Then the chorus turned toward the audience:
  "A house divided by pride eats its own bread with ashes."
question: Which context note most directly helps a reader understand the form of this passage?
choices:
  - In some drama, a chorus comments on the action for the audience.
  - Ancient bread was often baked in round loaves.
  - Many old cities had stone walls.
  - Some heroes carried shields into battle.
correctAnswer: In some drama, a chorus comments on the action for the audience.
explanation: The passage names a chorus speaking to the audience, so the genre note is directly useful.
hint: Choose the note that explains a feature actually present in the passage.
```
