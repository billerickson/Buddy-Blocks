## Teaching Goal

Teach students to assemble a concise claim, evidence, and explanation chain for literary analysis.

## Student Outcome

The student can select a text-grounded claim, match relevant evidence, and complete an explanation of how the evidence supports the claim.

## Misconception Checks

Evidence is any line from the passage; explanation repeats the claim; longer claims are stronger; a claim should be vague enough to fit anything.

## Questions

```question
key: cl4_u01_l04_q01_claim_evidence_match
type: match-pairs
prompt: Match each claim to the evidence that best supports it.
pairs:
  - left: The speaker feels responsible for others.
    right: '"I will answer for the hungry at the gate."'
  - left: The city is afraid.
    right: '"No bell rang, and every shutter stayed closed."'
  - left: The elder values memory.
    right: '"Write the names before the dust takes them."'
  - left: The messenger doubts his welcome.
    right: '"He paused before knocking, hand lifted but uncertain."'
explanation: Strong evidence directly supports the claim's main idea.
hint: Match the claim's key idea to a phrase that shows it.
```

```question
key: cl4_u01_l04_q02_reasoning_chain
type: order-items
prompt: Put the claim/evidence/explanation chain in order.
items:
  - This shows that leadership is measured by service, not praise.
  - The leader refuses the crown and asks for grain for the poor.
  - The passage suggests that true honor comes from serving others.
correctOrder:
  - The passage suggests that true honor comes from serving others.
  - The leader refuses the crown and asks for grain for the poor.
  - This shows that leadership is measured by service, not praise.
explanation: A claim comes first, evidence supports it, and explanation shows how the evidence proves the claim.
hint: Find the broad interpretive sentence, then the textual detail, then the bridge.
```

```question
key: cl4_u01_l04_q03_best_supported_claim
type: passage-question
prompt: Read the passage and choose the best supported claim.
passageTitle: The Exile
passage: |
  The exile stood where the road forked. One path led toward the city that had banished him;
  the other led toward the hills and safety. He turned toward the city and said,
  "A wound is still mine to bind."
question: Which claim is best supported?
choices:
  - The exile chooses duty to the city even though it once harmed him.
  - The exile wants revenge against the hills.
  - The city welcomes him with public celebration.
  - The road proves that all choices are meaningless.
correctAnswer: The exile chooses duty to the city even though it once harmed him.
explanation: The city banished him, but he turns toward it and speaks of binding a wound.
hint: Look for both the past harm and the present choice.
```

```question
key: cl4_u01_l04_q05_best_evidence
type: passage-question
prompt: Read the passage and choose the best evidence for the claim.
passageTitle: The Exile
passage: |
  The exile stood where the road forked. One path led toward the city that had banished him;
  the other led toward the hills and safety. He turned toward the city and said,
  "A wound is still mine to bind."
question: Which evidence best supports the claim that the exile accepts responsibility?
choices:
  - '"A wound is still mine to bind."'
  - '"The road forked."'
  - '"the hills and safety"'
  - '"One path led"'
correctAnswer: '"A wound is still mine to bind."'
explanation: The line shows the exile treating the city's wound as his responsibility.
hint: Choose the evidence that shows responsibility, not just setting.
```

```question
key: cl4_u01_l04_q06_repair_claim
type: error-correction
prompt: Correct the claim so it is supported by the passage.
sentence: The exile proves that safety is always selfish.
acceptedAnswers:
  - The exile chooses duty to the city over his own safety.
  - The passage suggests that the exile values duty more than safety.
  - The exile turns away from safety because he feels responsible for the city.
explanation: The passage supports this exile's choice, but it does not prove a universal rule about safety.
hint: Make the claim about this passage, not every possible situation.
```
