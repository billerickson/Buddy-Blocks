## Teaching Goal

Teach students to track repeated motifs and explain how repetition develops meaning.

## Student Outcome

The student can identify a motif such as honor or homecoming and explain how repeated details build a theme.

## Misconception Checks

A motif is any important word once; repetition is accidental; naming "honor" is the same as explaining a theme.

## Questions

```question
key: cl4_u02_l02_q01_motif_match
type: match-pairs
prompt: Match each repeated detail to the motif it most likely develops.
pairs:
  - left: a worn door-stone touched by each traveler
    right: homecoming
  - left: a cup passed first to the stranger
    right: hospitality
  - left: a name carved on a shield
    right: honor
  - left: a road described as salt-white and lonely
    right: exile
explanation: Motifs are repeated details or ideas that gather meaning across a passage or work.
hint: Look for the repeated idea suggested by each object or action.
```

```question
key: cl4_u02_l02_q02_homecoming_motif
type: passage-question
prompt: Read the passage and identify the motif.
passageTitle: The Threshold
passage: |
  Three times the traveler reached for the threshold stone and drew back his hand.
  The house smoke rose as it had in childhood, but the dogs barked at him as at a stranger.
question: Which motif is most developed by these details?
choices:
  - homecoming complicated by exile
  - victory in public games
  - comic disguise for a festival
  - the making of a law code
correctAnswer: homecoming complicated by exile
explanation: The threshold, childhood smoke, and being treated as a stranger all point to a troubled return home.
hint: Notice both the signs of home and the signs of not belonging.
```

```question
key: cl4_u02_l02_q03_trace_motif
type: order-items
prompt: Put the motif details in the order they appear.
passageTitle: The Threshold
passage: |
  Three times the traveler reached for the threshold stone and drew back his hand.
  The house smoke rose as it had in childhood, but the dogs barked at him as at a stranger.
items:
  - The dogs bark at him as at a stranger
  - The traveler reaches for the threshold stone
  - The house smoke rises as in childhood
correctOrder:
  - The traveler reaches for the threshold stone
  - The house smoke rises as in childhood
  - The dogs bark at him as at a stranger
explanation: Tracing the order helps show how the passage moves from return toward alienation.
hint: Follow the passage sentence by sentence.
```

```question
key: cl4_u02_l02_q04_theme_from_motif

passageTitle: The Threshold

  Three times the traveler reached for the threshold stone and drew back his hand.
  The house smoke rose as it had in childhood, but the dogs barked at him as at a stranger.
question: Which theme statement is best supported?
choices:
  - Returning home can be painful when time and exile have changed belonging.
  - Dogs are always wiser than human beings.
  - Childhood memories prove that nothing ever changes.
  - A threshold stone is more important than a person.
correctAnswer: Returning home can be painful when time and exile have changed belonging.
explanation: The passage combines familiar home images with signs that the traveler no longer fully belongs.
hint: A theme statement should explain the pattern, not just name an object.
```
