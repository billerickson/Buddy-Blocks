## Teaching Goal

Teach students to recognize comic inversion and exaggeration as deliberate forms that can reveal folly.

## Student Outcome

The student can identify what has been reversed or exaggerated and infer why the contrast matters.

## Misconception Checks

Exaggeration means nonsense; comedy has no serious meaning; any reversal is equally important.

## Questions

```question
key: cl4_u04_l01_q01_comic_devices

pairs:
  - left: inversion
    right: expected order is turned upside down
  - left: exaggeration
    right: a trait or action is enlarged beyond normal size
  - left: folly
    right: foolish behavior exposed by the scene
  - left: comic reversal
    right: a turn that creates surprise or correction
explanation: Comedy often uses reversal and overstatement to reveal folly.
hint: Think about expectation, size, foolishness, and surprise.
```

```question
key: cl4_u04_l01_q02_inversion_passage
type: passage-question
prompt: Read the comic passage and identify the inversion.
passageTitle: The Inspection
passage: |
  The inspector arrived to judge the students' handwriting. He unrolled his decree,
  but the letters wandered crookedly across the page. The youngest student raised a hand:
  "Master, shall we correct yours first?"
question: What expectation is inverted?
choices:
  - The inspector judging handwriting needs his own writing corrected.
  - The youngest student refuses to speak.
  - The decree is written perfectly.
  - The students inspect a bridge instead of writing.
correctAnswer: The inspector judging handwriting needs his own writing corrected.
explanation: The authority figure who comes to judge handwriting has poor handwriting himself.
hint: Ask who was expected to correct whom.
```
