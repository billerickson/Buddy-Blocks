## Teaching Goal

Teach students to notice how translation or wording choices shift tone, image, or interpretive emphasis.

## Student Outcome

The student can compare two wording choices and explain which tone or emphasis each creates.

## Misconception Checks

Translations are interchangeable; only plot matters; a single word choice can be interpreted without context.

## Questions

```question
key: cl4_u06_l03_q01_word_tone_match
type: match-pairs
prompt: Match each word choice to its likely tone.
pairs:
  - left: commanded
    right: firm or authoritative
  - left: pleaded
    right: urgent and vulnerable
  - left: murmured
    right: quiet or restrained
  - left: boasted
    right: proud or self-important
explanation: Translation or wording choices can shift tone even when the basic action is similar.
hint: Ask what feeling or attitude each verb carries.
```

```question
key: cl4_u06_l03_q02_translation_pair
type: passage-question
prompt: Compare the two wording choices.
passageTitle: Two Renderings
passage: |
  Version A: The queen commanded the doors be opened.
  Version B: The queen pleaded that the doors be opened.
question: How does Version B change the tone?
choices:
  - It makes the queen sound more vulnerable and urgent.
  - It makes the queen sound more powerful and certain.
  - It removes the queen from the sentence.
  - It proves the doors are already open.
correctAnswer: It makes the queen sound more vulnerable and urgent.
explanation: "'Pleaded' suggests need and vulnerability, while 'commanded' suggests authority."
hint: Compare the feeling carried by the verbs.
```

```question
key: cl4_u06_l03_q04_best_emphasis
type: passage-question
prompt: Compare the two wording choices and choose the best explanation.
passageTitle: Two Renderings
passage: |
  Version A: The queen commanded the doors be opened.
  Version B: The queen pleaded that the doors be opened.
question: Which explanation is most careful?
choices:
  - The verb changes the queen's tone from authority toward vulnerability, though the requested action stays similar.
  - The two versions must describe completely different plots.
  - The verbs have no effect because both sentences mention doors.
  - Version B proves the queen is not a queen.
correctAnswer: The verb changes the queen's tone from authority toward vulnerability, though the requested action stays similar.
explanation: The action is similar, but the connotation of the verb changes how readers hear the queen.
hint: Keep both sameness and difference in view.
```

```question
key: cl4_u06_l03_q05_word_effect_pairs
type: match-pairs
prompt: Match each version to the emphasis it creates.
pairs:
  - left: The queen commanded the doors be opened.
    right: authority and control
  - left: The queen pleaded that the doors be opened.
    right: need and vulnerability
  - left: The queen whispered for the doors to open.
    right: secrecy or restraint
  - left: The queen thundered for the doors to open.
    right: force and anger
explanation: Different verbs create different tones around the same basic request.
hint: Focus on the verb in each sentence.
```

```question
key: cl4_u06_l03_q06_repair_translation_overclaim
type: error-correction
prompt: Correct the overclaim.
sentence: Because one version says 'pleaded,' the queen has no power at all.
acceptedAnswers:
  - Because one version says 'pleaded,' the queen sounds more vulnerable in that moment.
  - The word 'pleaded' makes the queen's request sound more urgent and vulnerable.
  - The wording shifts the queen's tone toward vulnerability, but it does not prove she has no power.
explanation: A word choice can shift tone without proving everything about the character's power.
hint: Keep the tonal effect but remove the unsupported absolute claim.
```
