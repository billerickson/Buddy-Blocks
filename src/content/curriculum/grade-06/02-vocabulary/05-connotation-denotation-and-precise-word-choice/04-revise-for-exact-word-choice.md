## Teaching Goal

Apply denotation, connotation, intensity, formality, and precision to sentence revision.

## Student Outcome

The student can replace a vague or mismatched word with one that better fits audience, purpose, and tone.

## Misconception Checks

Revision only fixes errors; fancy words are automatically better; tone does not matter in word choice.

## Teaching Note

Show two revised versions of one sentence and ask which better fits a school announcement, a story, or a lab note.

## Questions

```question
key: u05_l04_q01_best_revision
type: multiple-choice
prompt: "Which revision best improves the word good in this sentence: 'The speaker gave a good explanation of the data'?"
choices:
  - The speaker gave a clear explanation of the data.
  - The speaker gave a long explanation of the data.
  - The speaker gave a fancy explanation of the data.
  - The speaker gave a dramatic explanation of the data.
correctAnswer: The speaker gave a clear explanation of the data.
explanation: Clear tells what kind of good explanation it was.
hint: Choose the word that fits an explanation.
```

```question
key: u05_l04_q02_audience_revision
type: multiple-choice
prompt: Which sentence best fits a formal email to a principal?
choices:
  - I would like to request permission to start a chess club.
  - Can we make a chess club because chess is fun?
  - I want a chess club, so please approve it.
  - Let us start a chess club now.
correctAnswer: I would like to request permission to start a chess club.
explanation: The sentence uses formal, respectful wording for the audience.
hint: Think about audience and purpose.
```

```question
key: u05_l04_q03_error_correction_precision
type: error-correction
prompt: Revise the sentence by replacing thing with a more exact word.
sentence: The microscope helped us see tiny things in the pond water.
acceptedAnswers:
  - The microscope helped us see tiny organisms in the pond water.
  - The microscope helped us see tiny cells in the pond water.
  - The microscope helped us see tiny particles in the pond water.
explanation: A more precise noun tells what was seen instead of using the vague word things.
hint: Choose a noun that could be seen in pond water through a microscope.
```

```question
key: u05_l04_q04_revision_reason
type: match-pairs
prompt: Match each revision choice to the reason it works.
pairs:
  - left: said -> whispered
    right: adds quiet voice detail
  - left: bad storm -> destructive storm
    right: adds specific damage meaning
  - left: stuff -> supplies
    right: replaces a vague noun
  - left: mad -> frustrated
    right: gives a more precise emotion
explanation: Strong revisions make meaning more exact for the context.
hint: Ask what new information the revised word gives.
```

```question
key: u05_l04_q05_tone_revision
type: fill-blank
prompt: Choose the word that best fits a calm, respectful tone.
sentenceBefore: I
sentenceAfter: with your idea because the evidence points to a different conclusion.
choices:
  - disagree
  - hate
  - destroy
  - mock
correctAnswer: disagree
explanation: Disagree names the difference in ideas without attacking the person.
hint: Choose the word that keeps the tone academic.
```

```question
key: u05_l04_q06_revise_explain
type: multiple-choice
prompt: "Which revision makes the word choice more exact and explains why it fits? Original sentence: 'The book was interesting.'"
choices:
  - The book was suspenseful because each chapter ended with an unanswered question.
  - The book was interesting because it was interesting.
  - The book was good because good is a positive word.
  - The book was gigantic because some books have many pages.
correctAnswer: The book was suspenseful because each chapter ended with an unanswered question.
explanation: A precise revision names what kind of interesting effect the book had.
hint: Choose the answer that replaces interesting and gives context for the choice.
```
