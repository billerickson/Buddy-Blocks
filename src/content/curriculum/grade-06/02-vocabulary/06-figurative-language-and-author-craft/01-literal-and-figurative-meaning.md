## Teaching Goal

Help students decide when language should be read literally and when it points beyond the literal meaning.

## Student Outcome

The student can identify literal and figurative meanings and explain the contextual clue.

## Misconception Checks

Figurative language is nonsense if read literally; every descriptive phrase is figurative; literal meaning is always less important.

## Teaching Note

Use one phrase literally in one sentence and figuratively in another.

## Questions

```question
key: u06_l01_q01_literal_figurative_match
type: match-pairs
prompt: Match each sentence to how the phrase is used.
pairs:
  - left: The ice cracked under the skates.
    right: literal cracking
  - left: His voice cracked during the speech.
    right: figurative or nonliteral change in sound
  - left: The runner flew down the track.
    right: figurative speed
  - left: The bird flew over the track.
    right: literal flying
explanation: The same or similar words can be literal in one context and figurative in another.
hint: Ask whether the words describe something actually happening.
```

```question
key: u06_l01_q02_figurative_meaning
type: passage-question
prompt: Interpret the figurative phrase.
passageTitle: Before The Concert
passage: |
  When the curtain opened, butterflies filled Nia's stomach. She took a slow breath and stepped onto the stage.
question: What does butterflies filled Nia's stomach mean?
choices:
  - Nia felt nervous
  - Nia ate too quickly
  - Real insects were inside Nia
  - The stage had butterflies painted on it
correctAnswer: Nia felt nervous
explanation: The phrase is figurative; her slow breath and stepping on stage show nervousness.
hint: Use the performance context and her reaction.
```

```question
key: u06_l01_q03_literal_or_figurative
type: multiple-choice
prompt: Which sentence uses the phrase 'under the weather' figuratively?
choices:
  - I stayed home because I felt under the weather.
  - The kite flew under the weather balloon.
  - The map was under the weather chart.
  - The bench sat under the rainy sky.
correctAnswer: I stayed home because I felt under the weather.
explanation: Under the weather is an idiom meaning sick or unwell in the first sentence.
hint: Look for the sentence where the phrase does not mean physical location.
```
