## Teaching Goal

Use context to understand idioms and fixed nonliteral phrases.

## Student Outcome

The student can infer an idiom's meaning and choose a plain-language paraphrase.

## Misconception Checks

Idioms should be read word by word; idioms mean the same thing in every possible context; all unusual phrases are idioms.

## Teaching Note

Contrast "spill the beans" as an accident with beans and as revealing a secret.

## Questions

```question
key: u06_l03_q01_idiom_match
type: match-pairs
prompt: Match each idiom to its plain meaning.
pairs:
  - left: break the ice
    right: make people feel more comfortable
  - left: hit the books
    right: study
  - left: spill the beans
    right: reveal a secret
  - left: on the same page
    right: sharing the same understanding
explanation: Idioms have meanings that usually cannot be built word by word.
hint: Choose the meaning people usually intend, not the literal picture.
```

```question
key: u06_l03_q02_idiom_context
type: passage-question
prompt: Use context to interpret the idiom.
passageTitle: Group Project
passage: |
  Before starting the project, the team reviewed the directions together. Once everyone was on the same page, they divided the work.
question: What does on the same page mean?
choices:
  - everyone had the same understanding
  - everyone read from one paper page
  - the directions were missing
  - the project was finished
correctAnswer: everyone had the same understanding
explanation: Reviewing directions together helps the team share the same understanding.
hint: Look at what happened before they divided the work.
```

```question
key: u06_l03_q03_literal_trap
type: multiple-choice
prompt: Which interpretation is a literal-trap mistake for 'Jada hit the books after dinner'?
choices:
  - Jada struck books with her hand
  - Jada studied after dinner
  - Jada worked on schoolwork
  - Jada prepared for class
correctAnswer: Jada struck books with her hand
explanation: Hit the books is an idiom meaning study.
hint: Ask whether the phrase makes sense as a common expression.
```

```question
key: u06_l03_q04_idiom_plain_language
type: fill-blank
prompt: Choose the plain-language meaning.
sentenceBefore: In 'The coach told us to call it a day,' call it a day means
sentenceAfter: .
choices:
  - stop working for now
  - name the calendar date
  - shout at the sun
  - start the hardest part
correctAnswer: stop working for now
explanation: Call it a day means stop an activity for the present time.
hint: Think about what a coach might say at the end of practice.
```

```question
key: u06_l03_q05_idiom_or_not
type: multiple-choice
prompt: Which sentence most clearly contains an idiom?
choices:
  - The surprise quiz caught me off guard.
  - The guard stood by the door.
  - The quiz had ten questions.
  - I sharpened my pencil.
correctAnswer: The surprise quiz caught me off guard.
explanation: Caught me off guard means surprised or unprepared, not physically caught by a guard.
hint: Look for a phrase whose meaning is not word by word.
```

```question
key: u06_l03_q06_explain_idiom_context
type: multiple-choice
prompt: In the sentence 'I almost told my sister about the party, but Mom said not to spill the beans,' which explanation best fits the idiom?
choices:
  - Spill the beans means reveal the party secret before it is time.
  - Spill the beans means drop food on the floor during the party.
  - Spill the beans means cook dinner for the speaker's sister.
  - Spill the beans means write the party date on a page.
correctAnswer: Spill the beans means reveal the party secret before it is time.
explanation: The context shows that the party information should stay secret.
hint: Ask what Mom does not want the speaker to tell.
```
