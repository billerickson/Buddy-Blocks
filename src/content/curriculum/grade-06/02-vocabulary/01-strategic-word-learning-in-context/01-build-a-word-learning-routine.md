## Teaching Goal

Introduce a repeatable process for figuring out unfamiliar words in middle-school texts.

## Student Outcome

The student can name and apply the routine: context, word parts, known words, reference, and meaning-fit check.

## Misconception Checks

Guessing from one clue is enough; reference tools replace context; word parts always settle the meaning.

## Teaching Note

Model one unfamiliar word aloud and show how the first guess improves after checking context, word parts, and fit.

## Questions

```question
key: u01_l01_q01_routine_order
type: order-items
prompt: Put the word-learning routine in a useful order.
items:
  - Verify with a reference if needed
  - Look at the sentence and paragraph context
  - Test the meaning back in the sentence
  - Check meaningful word parts
  - Connect to words you already know
correctOrder:
  - Look at the sentence and paragraph context
  - Check meaningful word parts
  - Connect to words you already know
  - Verify with a reference if needed
  - Test the meaning back in the sentence
explanation: The routine starts with the text, gathers word evidence, verifies when needed, and ends by checking that the meaning fits.
hint: Start with evidence from the sentence before using outside tools.
```

```question
key: u01_l01_q02_strategy_choice
type: multiple-choice
prompt: "A student sees the word dormant in this sentence: 'During the cold months, the plant stayed dormant and did not grow.' What is the best first strategy?"
choices:
  - Use the sentence clue 'did not grow'
  - Pick the first dictionary definition without reading it
  - Assume dormant means colorful because plants can be colorful
  - Skip the word because it is not important
correctAnswer: Use the sentence clue 'did not grow'
explanation: The sentence gives a strong clue that dormant means inactive or not growing.
hint: Look for words nearby that explain what the plant did or did not do.
```

```question
key: u01_l01_q03_strategy_purpose_match
type: match-pairs
prompt: Match each strategy to the question it helps answer.
pairs:
  - left: Context
    right: What nearby words point to the meaning?
  - left: Word parts
    right: Do prefixes, suffixes, or roots give a clue?
  - left: Reference
    right: Which definition can verify the meaning?
  - left: Meaning-fit check
    right: Does the meaning make sense in the sentence?
explanation: Each strategy gathers or checks a different kind of evidence.
hint: Think about what kind of evidence each strategy uses.
```

```question
key: u01_l01_q04_next_step
type: passage-question
prompt: Read the short context and choose the best next step.
passageTitle: One Word, Two Clues
passage: |
  The hikers reached an overlook with a panoramic view. They could see the river, the town, and the mountains all at once.
question: You think panoramic might mean 'wide.' What should you do next?
choices:
  - Test whether 'wide' fits the sentence about seeing many things at once
  - Change the guess to 'dangerous' because hikers can be in danger
  - Ignore the word because the sentence has other nouns
  - Use only the ending -ic and stop
correctAnswer: Test whether 'wide' fits the sentence about seeing many things at once
explanation: A good hypothesis still needs a meaning-fit check in the original context.
hint: After making a reasonable guess, check it in the sentence.
```

```question
key: u01_l01_q05_explain_strategy
type: multiple-choice
prompt: A classmate says, 'I just guess if I do not know a word.' Which response best explains how to make the guess stronger?
choices:
  - Use context clues, then test whether the meaning fits the sentence.
  - Choose a meaning based only on the topic of the passage.
  - Pick the longest dictionary definition and stop there.
  - Skip the word unless it appears in the title.
correctAnswer: Use context clues, then test whether the meaning fits the sentence.
explanation: A stronger guess is based on evidence and a meaning-fit check, not a single unsupported guess.
hint: Look for the response that uses evidence and checks the guess.
```
