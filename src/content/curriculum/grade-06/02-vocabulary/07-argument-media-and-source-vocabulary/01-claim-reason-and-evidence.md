## Teaching Goal

Teach students to separate the main point, support, and proof in a simple argument.

## Student Outcome

The student can identify claim, reason, evidence, and explanation in a short argument.

## Misconception Checks

A reason is the same as evidence; the claim is always first; evidence means any detail.

## Teaching Note

Color-code a three-sentence argument into claim, reason, evidence, and explanation.

## Questions

```question
key: u07_l01_q01_argument_role_match
type: match-pairs
prompt: Match each argument term to its role.
pairs:
  - left: claim
    right: the main point someone wants to prove
  - left: reason
    right: why the claim makes sense
  - left: evidence
    right: facts, examples, or details that support the claim
  - left: explanation
    right: how the evidence connects to the claim
explanation: A strong argument uses these parts together, but each part has a different job.
hint: Separate the point, the why, the proof, and the connection.
```

```question
key: u07_l01_q02_find_claim
type: passage-question
prompt: Identify the claim.
passageTitle: School Garden
passage: |
  Our school should add a small vegetable garden. Gardening would help students learn science outdoors. Last year, a nearby school reported that its garden improved science participation.
question: Which sentence is the claim?
choices:
  - Our school should add a small vegetable garden.
  - Gardening would help students learn science outdoors.
  - Last year, a nearby school reported that its garden improved science participation.
  - nearby school
correctAnswer: Our school should add a small vegetable garden.
explanation: The claim is the main point the rest of the argument supports.
hint: Find the sentence the writer wants readers to agree with.
```

```question
key: u07_l01_q03_evidence_choice
type: multiple-choice
prompt: "Claim: 'The library should stay open later on Tuesdays.' Which choice is evidence, not just a reason?"
choices:
  - A sign-in sheet shows 42 students tried to use the library after closing last month.
  - It would be helpful.
  - Students like libraries.
  - Tuesday is a good day.
correctAnswer: A sign-in sheet shows 42 students tried to use the library after closing last month.
explanation: The sign-in sheet gives a specific fact that can support the claim.
hint: Evidence should be a detail, fact, example, or data point.
```

```question
key: u07_l01_q05_explanation_role
type: multiple-choice
prompt: What does an explanation do in an argument?
choices:
  - It tells how the evidence supports the claim
  - It replaces evidence
  - It lists unrelated facts
  - It makes the claim harder to find
correctAnswer: It tells how the evidence supports the claim
explanation: Evidence does not always speak for itself; explanation connects it to the claim.
hint: Think about the bridge between proof and point.
```

```question
key: u07_l01_q06_write_evidence_link
type: multiple-choice
prompt: "Claim: 'The class should use a shared calendar.' Evidence: 'Three groups forgot project deadlines last month.' Which explanation best links the evidence to the claim?"
choices:
  - The missed deadlines show that a shared calendar could help groups remember important dates.
  - The evidence only repeats the claim without showing why a calendar would help.
  - The missed deadlines show that groups should stop doing projects.
  - The evidence is unrelated because deadlines and calendars are different words.
correctAnswer: The missed deadlines show that a shared calendar could help groups remember important dates.
explanation: The explanation shows why the evidence matters for the claim.
hint: Use a phrase like 'This shows' or 'This matters because.'
```
