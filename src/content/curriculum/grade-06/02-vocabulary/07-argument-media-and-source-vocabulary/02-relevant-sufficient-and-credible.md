## Teaching Goal

Evaluate evidence by fit, amount, and source trustworthiness at a Grade 6 level.

## Student Outcome

The student can decide whether evidence is relevant, sufficient, and credible for a simple claim.

## Misconception Checks

Interesting details are relevant; one example is always sufficient; credible means personally liked.

## Teaching Note

Use a claim about school gardens and compare an expert data point, a personal story, and an unrelated fact.

## Questions

  - left: relevant
    right: connected to the claim

  - left: credible
    right: trustworthy enough to use
  - left: irrelevant
    right: not connected to the claim
explanation: Evidence quality depends on fit, amount, and trustworthiness.
hint: Relevant is about connection; sufficient is about enough.

```question
key: u07_l02_q02_relevant_evidence
type: multiple-choice
prompt: "Claim: 'The soccer field needs better drainage.' Which evidence is most relevant?"
choices:
  - After rain, puddles remain on the field for two days.
  - The soccer team has blue uniforms.
  - The field is next to the gym.
  - The school has a soccer team.
correctAnswer: After rain, puddles remain on the field for two days.
explanation: Puddles after rain connect directly to drainage.
hint: Choose the detail that proves something about drainage.
```

```question
key: u07_l02_q05_fix_irrelevant
type: multiple-choice
prompt: Which evidence is relevant to the claim that the cafeteria line is too slow?
choices:
  - Students wait an average of 18 minutes to buy lunch.
  - The cafeteria walls are yellow.
  - The cafeteria tables are round.
  - The cafeteria serves apples on Tuesday.

explanation: Wall color does not connect to line speed. Waiting time is relevant evidence.
hint: Use evidence about time or waiting.
```
