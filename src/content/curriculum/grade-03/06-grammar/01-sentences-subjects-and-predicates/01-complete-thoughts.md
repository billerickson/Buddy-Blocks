## Teaching Goal

Teach that a sentence must express a complete thought, not merely contain several words.

## Student Outcome

The student can decide whether a word group is a complete sentence and explain what is missing when it is not.

## Misconception Checks

Treating a long phrase as a sentence; assuming end punctuation alone makes a sentence complete; rejecting short commands because the subject is not stated.

## Teaching Note

Contrast `The bright red kite` with `The bright red kite flew over the fence.` Ask which one tells a whole idea.

## Questions

```question
key: u01_l01_q01_complete_sentence
type: multiple-choice
prompt: Which word group is a complete sentence?
choices:
  - The lantern on the porch.
  - After the rain stopped.
  - The lantern glowed on the porch.
  - Bright yellow and warm.
correctAnswer: The lantern glowed on the porch.
explanation: "This word group tells a complete thought: what the lantern did and where it glowed."
hint: Ask whether the words tell a whole idea, not just name or describe something.
```

```question
key: u01_l01_q02_missing_part
type: multiple-choice
prompt: "What is missing from this word group: The small turtle under the log."
choices:
  - A naming part
  - A complete predicate
  - A capital letter
  - A describing word
correctAnswer: A complete predicate
explanation: The words name and describe the turtle, but they do not tell what happens or what is true about it.
hint: Find what the turtle does or what is true about it.
```

```question
key: u01_l01_q03_sort_word_groups
type: match-pairs
prompt: Match each word group to what it is.
pairs:
  - left: The clock ticked softly.
    right: complete sentence
  - left: Beside the garden gate.
    right: missing a sentence core
  - left: A noisy blue truck.
    right: names something but does not tell a whole idea
  - left: Please bring your notebook.
    right: complete command
explanation: A complete sentence tells a whole idea. A command can be complete even when the subject is understood.
hint: "Commands often have an understood subject: you."
```

```question
key: u01_l01_q04_fix_fragment
type: multiple-choice
prompt: Which choice turns the fragment into a complete sentence?
choices:
  - Reagan danced under the old bridge.
  - Under the old bridge.
  - The old bridge.
  - Because under the old bridge.
correctAnswer: Reagan danced under the old bridge.
explanation: The correction adds a subject and predicate so the word group tells a complete thought.
hint: Add who or what, then tell what happened under the old bridge.
```

```question
key: u01_l01_q05_complete_thought_definition
type: fill-blank
prompt: Complete the idea.
sentenceBefore: A sentence must tell a
sentenceAfter: .
choices:
  - complete thought
  - long thought
  - fancy word
  - list of nouns
correctAnswer: complete thought
explanation: A sentence can be short or long, but it must express a complete thought.
hint: Length is not the rule. Meaning is the rule.
```

```question
key: u01_l01_q06_explain_fragment
type: multiple-choice
prompt: "Why is this not a complete sentence: The red kite in the tree?"
choices:
  - It names the kite but does not tell what happened or what is true about it.
  - It is not complete because it has too many describing words.
  - It is not complete because kite is a noun.
  - It would be complete if a period were added.
correctAnswer: It names the kite but does not tell what happened or what is true about it.
explanation: A fragment may have many words, but it still needs a complete thought.
hint: Ask what happened to the kite or what is true about it.
```
