## Teaching Goal

Show that most sentences have a subject and predicate working together.

## Student Outcome

The student can identify the simple subject and simple predicate in straightforward sentences.

## Misconception Checks

Choosing the first word as the subject even when it is an article; selecting the whole sentence as the predicate; missing being verbs.

## Teaching Note

Use two colors or brackets to split sentences such as `The small boat / drifted across the pond.`

## Questions

```question
key: u01_l02_q01_find_subject
type: multiple-choice
prompt: What is the simple subject in this sentence? The silver train stopped at the station.
choices:
  - The
  - silver
  - train
  - station
correctAnswer: train
explanation: The sentence is about the train. The words The and silver help describe which train.
hint: Ask who or what the sentence is about.
```

```question
key: u01_l02_q02_find_predicate
type: multiple-choice
prompt: What is the complete predicate in this sentence? The silver train stopped at the station.
choices:
  - The silver train
  - stopped at the station
  - train stopped
  - at the station
correctAnswer: stopped at the station
explanation: "The predicate tells what the subject did: stopped at the station."
hint: After you find the subject, ask what is said about it.
```

```question
key: u01_l02_q03_match_subject_predicate
type: match-pairs
prompt: Match each subject to a predicate that makes a complete sentence.
pairs:
  - left: The baby bird
    right: chirped in the nest
  - left: My older cousin
    right: built a wooden shelf
  - left: The library lights
    right: glowed after sunset
  - left: A cold wind
    right: blew across the field
explanation: A subject and predicate work together to tell a complete thought.
hint: Each predicate should tell something that can make sense with its subject.
```

```question
key: u01_l02_q04_subject_predicate_frame
type: multi-blank-cloze
prompt: "Complete the subject/predicate frame for this sentence: The careful artist painted a tiny flower."
parts:
  - "The simple subject is "
  - ", and the complete predicate is "
  - .
blanks:
  - correctAnswer: artist
    acceptedAnswers:
      - artist
  - correctAnswer: painted a tiny flower
    acceptedAnswers:
      - painted a tiny flower
explanation: The sentence is about the artist, and the predicate tells what the artist did.
hint: Do not include describing words in the simple subject.
```

```question
key: u01_l02_q05_complete_missing_predicate
type: error-correction
prompt: Add a predicate to make a complete sentence.
sentence: The tiny frog.
acceptedAnswers:
  - The tiny frog jumped.
  - The tiny frog sat on a leaf.
  - The tiny frog is green.
explanation: The correction tells what the frog did or what is true about the frog.
hint: Keep the subject, then add what is said about it.
```

```question
key: u01_l02_q06_explain_parts
type: multiple-choice
prompt: In The candle flickered in the window, what do the subject and predicate do?
choices:
  - The subject candle names what the sentence is about, and the predicate tells what it did.
  - The subject flickered names the action, and the predicate is The candle.
  - The subject is the whole sentence, and the predicate is only the period.
  - The subject and predicate are both describing words.
correctAnswer: The subject candle names what the sentence is about, and the predicate tells what it did.
explanation: The subject and predicate work together to make a complete sentence.
hint: Use the questions who or what and what happened.
```
