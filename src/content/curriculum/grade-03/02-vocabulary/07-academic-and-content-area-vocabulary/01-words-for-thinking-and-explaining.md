## Teaching Goal

Teach high-utility academic task words students see in directions and questions.

## Student Outcome

The student can choose what a direction asks them to do when it uses words such as identify, describe, explain, compare, contrast, summarize, and conclude.

## Misconception Checks

Treating all direction words as "answer"; confusing compare with contrast; giving a long explanation when asked to identify.

## Teaching Note

Display a few question stems and ask what each one is really asking the student to do.

## Questions

```question
key: u07_l01_q01_academic_match
type: match-pairs
prompt: Match each academic word to what it asks you to do.
pairs:
  - left: identify
    right: name or point out
  - left: describe
    right: tell details about
  - left: compare
    right: tell how things are alike
  - left: contrast
    right: tell how things are different
explanation: Academic words in directions tell what kind of thinking to do.
hint: Think about what a teacher expects when using each word.
```

```question
key: u07_l01_q02_explain_direction
type: multiple-choice
prompt: A question says, "Explain how you know." What should your answer include?
choices:
  - A reason or evidence
  - Only one word
  - A drawing with no words
  - A new question
correctAnswer: A reason or evidence
explanation: Explain asks you to tell why or how, often with a reason.
hint: Explain means more than name.
```

```question
key: u07_l01_q03_summarize
type: fill-blank
prompt: Choose the meaning of summarize.
sentenceBefore: To summarize a paragraph, you tell
sentenceAfter: .
choices:
  - the most important ideas
  - every single word
  - only the title
  - a random fact
correctAnswer: the most important ideas
explanation: Summarize means tell the main or most important ideas briefly.
hint: A summary is shorter than the whole text.
```

```question
key: u07_l01_q04_direction_context
type: passage-question
prompt: Read the direction and choose the action.
passageTitle: Science Direction
passage: "Direction: Observe the bean plant each day and record what changes."
question: What does observe ask you to do?
choices:
  - look carefully
  - guess without looking
  - throw away the plant
  - write a story about space
correctAnswer: look carefully
explanation: Observe means look carefully, often to notice details.
hint: Think about what a scientist does before recording changes.
```

```question
key: u07_l01_q05_best_response
type: multiple-choice
prompt: Which response best fits the direction "contrast cats and dogs"?
choices:
  - Cats often purr, but dogs often bark.
  - Cats and dogs are both pets.
  - I like pets.
  - Animals can be furry.
correctAnswer: Cats often purr, but dogs often bark.
explanation: Contrast asks how things are different.
hint: Look for a response that tells a difference.
```

```question
key: u07_l01_q06_academic_sentence
type: fill-blank
prompt: Complete the sentence so compare is used correctly.
sentenceBefore: I can compare apples and oranges by telling how they are
sentenceAfter: .
choices:
  - alike or different
  - spelled
  - in a map
  - before lunch
correctAnswer: alike or different
explanation: A clear sentence shows what the academic word asks someone to do.
hint: Compare two foods by size, taste, color, or shape.
```
