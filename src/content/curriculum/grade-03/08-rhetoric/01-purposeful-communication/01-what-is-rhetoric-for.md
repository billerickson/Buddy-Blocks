## Teaching Goal

Introduce rhetoric as purposeful communication that helps a speaker or writer do something for an audience.

## Student Outcome

The student can recognize rhetoric as clear communication for a purpose, not fancy language or tricking people.

## Misconception Checks

Rhetoric means fancy words; persuasion means getting your way; communication quality is about sounding impressive.

## Teaching Note

Start with familiar examples: a sign, an invitation, a story, and a polite request. Ask what each one is trying to do.

## Questions

```question
key: u01_l01_q01_purposeful_communication
type: multiple-choice
prompt: Which example best shows rhetoric as purposeful communication?
choices:
  - A student writes, 'Please close the gate so the dog stays safe.'
  - A student lists five long words with no sentence.
  - A student shouts louder each time someone disagrees.
  - A student copies a page without knowing what it says.
correctAnswer: A student writes, 'Please close the gate so the dog stays safe.'
explanation: The sentence has an audience, a clear request, and a reason. That is purposeful communication.
hint: Look for words that are trying to do something for a listener or reader.
```

```question
key: u01_l01_q05_persuasion_respect
type: multiple-choice
prompt: Which sentence shows respectful persuasion?
choices:
  - Please vote for extra reading time because it helps us finish our books.
  - Vote my way or you are wrong.
  - Everyone has to want what I want.
  - I will say this louder until you agree.
correctAnswer: Please vote for extra reading time because it helps us finish our books.
explanation: Respectful persuasion gives a reason and does not attack the audience.
hint: Look for a request plus a reason, without threats or insults.
```
