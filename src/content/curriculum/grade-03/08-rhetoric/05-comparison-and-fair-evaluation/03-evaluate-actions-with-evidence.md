## Teaching Goal

Teach students to evaluate actions or choices using evidence rather than personal feeling alone.

## Student Outcome

The student can judge an action or choice and support the judgment with a reason and example.

## Misconception Checks

Feelings alone are enough; evidence can be invented; evaluating an action means judging a person's worth.

## Teaching Note

Use a character choice from a fable and ask, "Was the action careful, fair, or useful? What shows that?"

## Questions

```question
key: u05_l03_q01_best_evaluation
type: passage-question
prompt: Read the scenario and answer.
passageTitle: The Shared List
passage: |
  Four students needed one supply list.
  Rosa read each item aloud so everyone could gather materials.
question: Which evaluation is best supported?
choices:
  - Rosa's choice was helpful because everyone could hear what to gather.
  - Rosa is the best person in the world.
  - The supply list was made of paper.
  - Rosa's choice was bad because lists have words.
correctAnswer: Rosa's choice was helpful because everyone could hear what to gather.
explanation: The evaluation focuses on the action and uses evidence from the scenario.
hint: Choose the judgment that names the action and support.
```

```question
key: u05_l03_q02_evidence_for_evaluation
type: multiple-choice
prompt: "Evaluation: Malik's choice was careful. Which evidence best supports it?"
choices:
  - He checked the directions twice before cutting.
  - He likes the color green.
  - His desk is near the window.
  - He has a folder.
correctAnswer: He checked the directions twice before cutting.
explanation: Checking directions before cutting is evidence of careful action.
hint: Look for an action that shows carefulness.
```

```question
key: u05_l03_q03_action_to_evaluation
type: match-pairs
prompt: Match each action to a fair evaluation.
pairs:
  - left: She waited for both partners to speak.
    right: Her choice was respectful.
  - left: He checked the answer with the group.
    right: His choice was careful.
  - left: They split the markers evenly.
    right: Their choice was fair.
  - left: He explained the first step again.
    right: His choice was helpful.
explanation: Fair evaluations focus on what the action shows.
hint: Match the action to the quality it demonstrates.
```

```question
key: u05_l03_q04_write_evaluation
type: multiple-choice
prompt: "Scenario: Ana noticed a classmate was confused and repeated the direction slowly. Which evaluation uses evidence?"
choices:
  - Ana's action was helpful because repeating the direction slowly made it easier to understand.
  - Ana's action was nice because Ana is nice.
  - The direction was written on paper.
  - Ana's action was bad because directions have words.
correctAnswer: Ana's action was helpful because repeating the direction slowly made it easier to understand.
explanation: A fair evaluation names the action and explains what evidence supports the judgment.
hint: Choose the evaluation that names the action and why it helped.
```

```question
key: u05_l03_q05_feeling_only
type: multiple-choice
prompt: Which evaluation relies only on feeling instead of evidence?
choices:
  - I just do not like his idea.
  - His idea is clearer because it lists the steps.
  - Her choice was fair because each person got a turn.
  - Their plan was careful because they checked the supplies.
correctAnswer: I just do not like his idea.
explanation: A personal feeling alone does not give evidence about the action or idea.
hint: Evidence tells what happened or why the judgment fits.
```
