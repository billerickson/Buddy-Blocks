## Teaching Goal

Teach students to reorder events, reasons, or sentences so a response is easier to follow.

## Student Outcome

The student can identify and fix confusing order in a short response.

## Misconception Checks

All included ideas matter more than their order; sequence words can be added randomly; conclusions can come before support.

## Teaching Note

Compare two responses with the same sentences but different order and ask which one is easier to follow.

## Questions

```question
key: u06_l02_q01_order_response
type: order-items
prompt: Put the response in the clearest order.
items:
  - For example, the list showed who would collect papers.
  - The group needed a plan for cleanup.
  - A job list helped everyone know what to do.
  - That made cleanup faster.
correctOrder:
  - The group needed a plan for cleanup.
  - A job list helped everyone know what to do.
  - For example, the list showed who would collect papers.
  - That made cleanup faster.
explanation: The clear order sets up the problem, gives the idea, adds an example, and ends with the result.
hint: Start with the situation before the solution.
```

```question
key: u06_l02_q02_best_first_sentence
type: multiple-choice
prompt: Which sentence should come first in a retelling?
choices:
  - The group lost the directions before starting.
  - Finally, they finished the model.
  - That solved the problem.
  - This showed careful teamwork.
correctAnswer: The group lost the directions before starting.
explanation: This sentence sets up the problem that the rest of the retelling can explain.
hint: The first sentence should help the audience understand the situation.
```

```question
key: u06_l02_q03_fix_order
type: multiple-choice
prompt: Which revision puts the ideas in a clear time order?
choices:
  - First, we practiced. Then, we fixed one confusing sentence. Finally, we presented.
  - Finally, we presented. First, we practiced. Then, we fixed one confusing sentence.
  - Then, we fixed one confusing sentence. Finally, we presented. First, we practiced.
  - First, we presented. Then, we practiced. Finally, we fixed one confusing sentence.
correctAnswer: First, we practiced. Then, we fixed one confusing sentence. Finally, we presented.
explanation: "The revised order follows the time words: first, then, finally."
hint: Use the sequence words as clues.
```

```question
key: u06_l02_q04_confusing_order
type: passage-question
prompt: Read the draft and answer.
passageTitle: Mixed-Up Draft
passage: |
  This helped us finish the poster.
  We made a list of jobs.
  Our group was confused at first.
question: What is the main order problem?
choices:
  - The result comes before the problem and action.
  - The draft has too many examples.
  - The words are too polite.
  - The claim has no capital letter.
correctAnswer: The result comes before the problem and action.
explanation: The audience needs the problem and action before the result.
hint: Ask what the reader needs to know first.
```

```question
key: u06_l02_q05_sequence_word
type: fill-blank
prompt: Choose the best sequence word.
sentenceBefore: First, our group chose a topic.
sentenceAfter: ", we found two examples."
choices:
  - Next
  - Before that
  - At the same time
  - Because of that
correctAnswer: Next
explanation: "'Next' helps the audience follow the order of actions."
hint: Choose the word that shows what happened after the first step.
```

```question
key: u06_l02_q06_explain_order_revision
type: multiple-choice
prompt: Why is clear order important when retelling a story or explaining a plan?
choices:
  - It helps the audience understand what happened first, next, and last.
  - It makes every sentence longer.
  - It hides the ending from the audience.
  - It replaces reasons and examples.
correctAnswer: It helps the audience understand what happened first, next, and last.
explanation: Order is a revision target because it affects whether the audience can follow the message.
hint: Think about what happens when the ending comes first.
```
