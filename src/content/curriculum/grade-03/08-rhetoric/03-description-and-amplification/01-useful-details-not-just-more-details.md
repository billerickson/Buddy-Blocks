## Teaching Goal

Teach students to judge whether a detail helps the audience understand the point.

## Student Outcome

The student can choose useful details and reject distracting details in a short sentence or paragraph.

## Misconception Checks

More detail is always better; favorite details are always useful; a detail is useful if it is true.

## Teaching Note

Compare two details for the same sentence and ask which one helps the reader know more about the main idea.

## Questions

```question
key: u03_l01_q01_best_detail
type: multiple-choice
prompt: "Main idea: The class garden needs water. Which detail is most useful?"
choices:
  - The soil is dry and cracked.
  - The watering can is blue.
  - The fence has four posts.
  - The garden is beside a sidewalk.
correctAnswer: The soil is dry and cracked.
explanation: Dry, cracked soil helps the audience understand why the garden needs water.
hint: Choose the detail that supports the main idea.
```

```question
key: u03_l01_q02_detail_jobs
type: match-pairs
prompt: Match each detail to how it works in the sentence 'The hallway was ready for visitors.'
pairs:
  - left: The floor was clean.
    right: supports readiness
  - left: The welcome sign was on the door.
    right: shows visitor preparation
  - left: The lockers were closed.
    right: adds order and neatness
  - left: The pencil on the desk was yellow.
    right: distracts from the hallway
explanation: Useful details help the reader see the main idea; distracting details pull away from it.
hint: Ask whether each detail helps prove the hallway was ready.
```

```question
key: u03_l01_q03_distracting_detail
type: passage-question
prompt: Read the paragraph and answer.
passageTitle: A Careful Cleanup
passage: |
  Our table was clean after art.
  We put brushes in the cup and wiped the paint spots.
  The window was square.
  We stacked the paper on the shelf.
question: Which sentence is a distracting detail?
choices:
  - The window was square.
  - We put brushes in the cup and wiped the paint spots.
  - Our table was clean after art.
  - We stacked the paper on the shelf.
correctAnswer: The window was square.
explanation: The window detail does not help explain how the table was clean.
hint: Look for the sentence that changes the topic.
```

```question
key: u03_l01_q05_explain_detail_choice
type: multiple-choice
prompt: "Main idea: The backpack was too heavy. Which choice names a useful detail and explains why?"
choices:
  - "'It held three textbooks' because it shows why the backpack was heavy."
  - "'It had a tiny star sticker' because stickers are interesting."
  - "'It was beside a chair' because every backpack is somewhere."
  - "'It had a zipper' because backpacks often have zippers."
correctAnswer: "'It held three textbooks' because it shows why the backpack was heavy."
explanation: The textbook detail explains the weight. The sticker, location, and zipper details may be true, but they do not support the main idea.
hint: Ask which detail explains 'too heavy.'
```

```question
key: u03_l01_q06_true_but_not_useful
type: multiple-choice

choices:
  - The paper was folded in half.
  - Step two came before step one.
  - Three words were missing.
  - The picture showed the wrong piece.
correctAnswer: The paper was folded in half.
explanation: A folded paper does not explain why the directions were confusing. The other details do.
hint: Look for the detail that does not explain the confusion.
```
