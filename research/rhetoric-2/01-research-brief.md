# Rhetoric 2 Research Brief

Track: Rhetoric  
Level: Level 2  
Intended student profile: students who can retell, describe, state an opinion with reasons, and revise short writing. This level begins formal progymnasmata-style practice through fable, narrative, anecdote, proverb, imitation, and amplification.

This brief defines outcomes and instructional priorities only. It does not define units, lessons, or questions.

## 1. End-Of-Level Outcomes

By the end of Rhetoric 2, students should be able to retell and reshape fables, narratives, anecdotes, chreia-like sayings, and proverbs; identify moral or point; paraphrase, imitate, amplify, and reduce short models; organize beginning/middle/end; add relevant detail; deliver short oral narrations; and write short retellings with a clear point and audience awareness.

## 2. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should know audience, purpose, message, claim, reason, example, description, comparison, basic revision, story sequence, and short oral delivery. They should be able to write several connected sentences with support.

## 3. Major Concept Clusters

- **Fable And Moral:** retelling compact stories with a point.
- **Narrative And Anecdote:** event sequence, character action, turning point, and outcome.
- **Chreia And Proverb Readiness:** sayings attached to a person, action, or situation; explaining wisdom in context.
- **Imitation And Paraphrase:** preserving meaning while changing words, structure, or style.
- **Amplification And Reduction:** expanding with useful detail or compressing to essentials.
- **Oral Narration:** clear sequence, voice, pacing, and listener awareness.

## 4. Recommended Progression

Begin with fable retelling and moral identification, then add paraphrase and imitation. Move to narrative and anecdote with beginning/middle/end. Add proverbs and chreia-like sayings only when students can explain context and point. Teach amplification and reduction as paired skills. End with short oral and written retellings that adapt a model for a clear audience and purpose.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Retelling means copying every sentence.
- A moral can be any nice sentence.
- Imitation means plagiarism.
- Amplification means adding unrelated details.
- Reduction means removing the point.
- A proverb is always literally true.
- Oral narration requires memorizing every word.

## 6. Developmental Constraints And Reading/Load Concerns

Use short models. Keep writing products modest. Separate paraphrase, imitation, amplification, and reduction before combining them. Choose proverbs and anecdotes with accessible meaning. Avoid obscure classical references unless context is taught.

## 7. Assessment Implications

Use order-items for narrative sequence, multiple-choice for moral/point, passage questions for relevant detail, and constructed response for one-sentence paraphrase or expansion. Distractors should include wrong moral, irrelevant detail, copied wording, lost sequence, and audience mismatch. Feedback should name the rhetorical move.

## 8. Spiral Review Priorities

Audience, purpose, message, beginning/middle/end, moral, point, paraphrase, imitation, amplification, reduction, detail, sequence, oral clarity, and revision.

## 9. Concepts That Should Not Be Taught Yet

Avoid formal debate, full refutation/confirmation, ethos/pathos/logos taxonomy, long essays, advanced style figures, and adversarial praise/blame.
