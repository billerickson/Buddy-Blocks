# Grammar 3 Course Map

Track: Grammar
Level: Level 3
Sources: Accepted research brief at `research/grammar-3/01-research-brief.md` and level blueprint at `research/grammar-3/02-level-blueprint.md`

## Course Design Notes

Grammar 3 is organized around sentence control. The course begins with clause foundations, then separates coordination and subordination so students can compare equal ideas with dependent ideas. Modifier and appositive work comes after students can find the main clause. Agreement, voice, and modifier clarity follow because they require students to track reference and action across the sentence. The final unit uses punctuation as a structural signal and asks students to make cumulative revision decisions.

## Unit 1: Clause Foundations And Sentence Boundaries

### Unit Purpose

Build the foundation for all later sentence variety by helping students test whether a word group is a complete sentence, an independent clause, a dependent clause, or a fragment.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify the subject and verb in short clauses.
- Distinguish a complete thought from an incomplete thought.
- Classify independent clauses, dependent clauses, and fragments in clear cases.
- Repair fragments by adding a main clause or completing the thought.
- Use simple clause maps to show sentence boundaries.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Clause, independent clause, dependent clause, fragment, subject, predicate, verb, complete thought, main clause, sentence boundary, because/when/if as dependency signals.

### Prerequisites

Students should know basic subjects, predicates, verb phrases, direct objects, compound subjects and predicates, prepositional phrases, and simple sentence punctuation from Grammar 2.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit extends earlier sentence-part work into clause control. It prepares Unit 2 compound sentences, Unit 3 complex sentences, later modifier placement, and final punctuation decisions.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A word group is complete because it is long.
- A dependent clause is complete because it has a subject and verb.
- Every fragment is missing a subject.
- A capital letter and period automatically make a complete sentence.
- Any sentence with more than one verb must have more than one clause.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Keep subject/verb and complete-thought tests active in every later unit. Revisit dependent clauses when teaching subordinating conjunctions, introductory clauses, and punctuation.

## Unit 2: Coordination And Compound Sentences

### Unit Purpose

Teach students to join two related independent clauses with appropriate coordination and punctuation while avoiding comma splices, run-ons, and overuse of `and`.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify two independent clauses that can stand alone.
- Choose coordinating conjunctions that match meaning relationships.
- Punctuate compound sentences with comma plus coordinating conjunction.
- Use semicolons between closely related independent clauses in clear cases.
- Repair comma splices and run-ons in controlled sentences.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Compound sentence, coordinating conjunction, FANBOYS, comma splice, run-on, independent clause, equal importance, related ideas, semicolon.

### Prerequisites

Students need Unit 1 clause tests, sentence boundary knowledge, basic comma familiarity, and the ability to recognize complete thoughts.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit depends on independent clause identification and prepares students to compare coordination with subordination in Unit 3. It also supports semicolon work and cumulative editing in Unit 6.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Every sentence with `and` is compound.
- A comma alone can join two independent clauses.
- Coordinating conjunctions are interchangeable.
- A semicolon can join any two word groups.
- Longer joined sentences are automatically better.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Review independent/dependent clauses while introducing compound sentences. Continue short sentence-boundary edits in later units so comma splices do not disappear after one unit.

## Unit 3: Subordination And Complex Sentences

### Unit Purpose

Help students use dependent clauses to show time, cause, condition, contrast, and concession, then punctuate complex sentences based on clause order.

### Student Outcomes

- Recognize subordinating conjunctions and the dependent clauses they create.
- Match subordinating conjunctions to meaning relationships.
- Build complex sentences with dependent clauses before or after the main clause.
- Use commas after introductory dependent clauses.
- Choose subordination when one idea frames or depends on another.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Complex sentence, subordinating conjunction, dependent clause, main clause, time, cause, condition, contrast, concession, introductory clause, comma after introductory clause.

### Prerequisites

Students need independent/dependent clause tests, sentence-boundary control, and experience joining equal independent clauses from Unit 2.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit contrasts with Unit 2 coordination and prepares students for introductory modifiers, appositive placement, and cumulative choices among sentence structures.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Subordinating conjunctions can be swapped without changing meaning.
- A dependent clause can stand alone if it is long.
- Every sentence beginning with `because` is wrong.
- Introductory dependent clauses never need commas.
- Complex sentences are always better than simple or compound sentences.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Interleave coordination and subordination choices after the first complex sentence lessons. Later cumulative review should ask which idea is more important before selecting the structure.

## Unit 4: Modifiers, Appositives, And Sentence Detail

### Unit Purpose

Teach students to add detail through adjective phrases, adverb phrases, introductory elements, and appositives while keeping the sentence core clear.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify the sentence core before studying added detail.
- Recognize adjective and adverb phrases in clear cases.
- Use introductory phrases and clauses without losing the main clause.
- Identify appositives that rename nouns.
- Punctuate nonessential appositives in controlled sentences.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Sentence core, modifier, adjective phrase, adverb phrase, introductory phrase, introductory clause, appositive, rename, essential detail, nonessential detail, comma pair.

### Prerequisites

Students need sentence core knowledge, prepositional phrase familiarity, independent and dependent clause control, and basic comma awareness.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit applies clause control to richer sentences. It prepares Unit 5 misplaced modifier corrections and Unit 6 punctuation choices with introductory elements and appositives.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Extra detail is always a separate clause.
- Appositives are any descriptive words after a noun.
- Nonessential details never need punctuation.
- Introductory elements can be separated from the main clause without any signal.
- Added detail is useful even when it hides the main idea.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Continue retrieving clause tests while teaching phrases so students do not confuse phrases with clauses. Review modifiers and appositives in later agreement and cumulative editing lessons.

## Unit 5: Agreement, Voice, And Modifier Clarity

### Unit Purpose

Focus students on grammar choices that affect reader clarity: pronoun-antecedent agreement, clear pronoun reference, active/passive voice recognition, and modifier placement.

### Student Outcomes

- Match pronouns to antecedents in number.
- Revise unclear pronoun references in short sentences.
- Recognize active and passive voice in clear cases.
- Choose active or passive voice when it best supports clarity or emphasis.
- Correct clear misplaced modifiers by moving the modifier near the word it describes.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Pronoun, antecedent, singular, plural, agreement, reference, unclear pronoun, active voice, passive voice, actor, receiver, modifier, misplaced modifier, clarity.

### Prerequisites

Students need pronoun knowledge, noun number, sentence core recognition, verb phrases, modifier/appositive work, and basic sentence editing experience.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit draws on all previous sentence-structure work and prepares the final unit's cumulative editing tasks. Voice and modifier choices also reinforce that grammar supports meaning, not just labeling.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A pronoun is correct if it sounds familiar, even when the antecedent is plural.
- `They` or `it` is clear without a nearby antecedent.
- Passive voice is always wrong.
- The actor and receiver do not matter when identifying voice.
- Modifiers can sit anywhere without changing meaning.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use short delayed review of appositives, introductory elements, and dependent clauses while editing for clarity. Keep passive voice examples clear and avoid advanced style arguments.

## Unit 6: Punctuation And Cumulative Sentence Craft

### Unit Purpose

Bring the level together by using punctuation and sentence-combining choices to show relationships among ideas in short, controlled writing.

### Student Outcomes

- Use commas for introductory dependent clauses, introductory phrases, and nonessential appositives.
- Use semicolons between closely related independent clauses.
- Use colons to introduce a list or explanation in developmentally appropriate sentences.
- Choose among separate sentences, compound sentences, complex sentences, and appositive structures.
- Edit a short passage for targeted Grammar 3 conventions.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Comma, semicolon, colon, introductory element, appositive, closely related independent clauses, list, explanation, sentence combining, revision, clarity, cumulative editing.

### Prerequisites

Students need clause foundations, coordination, subordination, modifier and appositive knowledge, pronoun agreement, voice recognition, and modifier placement.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit reviews and applies Units 1-5. It prepares students for later grammar levels where sentence variety, style, parallelism, and longer editing passages will demand stronger independence.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Semicolons are fancy commas.
- Colons can appear after any phrase before a list.
- Every long pause needs punctuation.
- Revision means making every sentence longer.
- A sentence can be correct but still unclear for the reader.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use mixed but focused review. Each lesson should name the targeted convention and avoid asking students to copyedit for every possible error at once.
