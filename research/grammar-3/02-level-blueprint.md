# Grammar 3 Level Blueprint

Track: Grammar
Level: Level 3
Source: Accepted research brief at `research/grammar-3/01-research-brief.md`

## 1. Level Mission

Grammar 3 helps students move from naming sentence parts to controlling sentence structure for clarity, variety, and meaning. Students learn to distinguish independent and dependent clauses, combine ideas with coordination and subordination, place modifiers and appositives carefully, maintain pronoun-antecedent agreement, recognize active and passive voice in clear cases, and use punctuation as a visible signal of sentence relationships. By the end of the level, students should be able to edit and build sentences intentionally instead of treating grammar as isolated labels.

## 2. Durable Student Outcomes

By the end of Grammar 3, students should be able to:

1. Distinguish complete independent clauses from dependent clauses and fragments.
2. Use coordinating conjunctions to join related independent clauses without comma splices or run-ons.
3. Use subordinating conjunctions to show time, cause, condition, contrast, or concession.
4. Form compound and complex sentences while keeping the main idea clear.
5. Identify and use adjective phrases, adverb phrases, appositives, and introductory clauses to add precise detail.
6. Correct clear misplaced modifiers by moving descriptive words or phrases next to what they modify.
7. Maintain pronoun-antecedent agreement in number and clarity in controlled contexts.
8. Recognize active and passive voice in clear cases and choose the voice that best supports clarity or emphasis.
9. Use commas, semicolons, and colons in developmentally appropriate sentence structures.
10. Revise short sentence sets by choosing coordination, subordination, apposition, or punctuation that matches the intended meaning.

## 3. Concept And Skill Progression

The level begins with a careful return to sentence boundaries. Students review subjects, predicates, verb phrases, and complete thoughts, then learn that an independent clause can stand alone while a dependent clause cannot. Early examples should be short and unambiguous so students can focus on the clause test rather than vocabulary load.

Students then learn coordination. They identify two related independent clauses, choose coordinating conjunctions that fit the meaning relationship, and punctuate compound sentences. This stage prevents the common habit of joining every idea with `and` or using commas alone between independent clauses.

The next stage introduces subordination. Students learn that subordinating conjunctions make a clause dependent and signal a relationship such as time, cause, condition, contrast, or concession. They practice building complex sentences with dependent clauses before and after the main clause, with punctuation attached to sentence structure rather than memorized as a surface rule.

After students can find the main clause, the level adds modifiers and appositives. Students use adjective and adverb phrases, introductory elements, and appositive noun phrases to add detail without losing the core sentence. They learn that modifiers must sit close to the words they describe.

Usage and clarity then become the focus. Students maintain pronoun-antecedent agreement, repair unclear pronoun references, recognize active and passive voice in clear cases, and correct misplaced modifiers. This work asks students to connect grammar choices to reader understanding.

The level ends with punctuation and cumulative sentence craft. Students use commas, semicolons, and colons to show sentence relationships, then revise short passages by choosing whether ideas should be separate, coordinated, subordinated, or set off as appositive detail.

## 4. Vocabulary, Representation, Model, Text, And Form Progression

Vocabulary begins with familiar sentence language: sentence, fragment, subject, predicate, verb, complete thought, clause, independent clause, dependent clause, main clause, and sentence boundary. Students use a simple two-part test: Does it have a subject and verb? Does it express a complete thought?

Coordination vocabulary adds compound sentence, coordinating conjunction, FANBOYS, comma splice, run-on, semicolon, related ideas, and equal importance. Representations include color-coded independent clauses, equation-like sentence frames, and simple clause maps that show two complete ideas joined side by side.

Subordination vocabulary adds complex sentence, subordinating conjunction, dependent clause, time, cause, condition, contrast, concession, introductory clause, and main idea. Representations shift from side-by-side maps to main-clause/dependent-clause maps, arrows showing which idea depends on the other, and sentence frames with movable clause order.

Modifier and appositive vocabulary includes adjective phrase, adverb phrase, introductory phrase, appositive, rename, describe, modify, placement, essential, nonessential, and set off. Models include bracketed sentence cores, modifier arrows, and appositive frames.

Usage and voice vocabulary includes antecedent, pronoun agreement, singular, plural, clear reference, active voice, passive voice, actor, receiver, and emphasis. Students compare paired sentences and label who is doing the action and who receives it.

Punctuation vocabulary includes comma, semicolon, colon, introductory element, list, explanation, closely related independent clauses, and signal. Texts progress from single clean sentences to short two- to four-sentence editing contexts. Student responses progress from recognition to finite corrections, constrained sentence building, and short revision choices.

## 5. Fluency Expectations

Grammar 3 fluency means students can quickly apply a small set of reliable tests and routines: identify the sentence core, decide whether a clause is independent or dependent, name the relationship between two ideas, choose a fitting conjunction, check pronoun agreement, and place punctuation from structure. Speed is not the primary goal, but students should become fluent enough with these routines that later writing and editing tasks do not require relearning every term.

## 6. Conceptual Understanding Expectations

Students should understand that grammar choices shape meaning. A clause is not complete merely because it contains a subject and verb; it must express a complete thought. A compound sentence joins two complete ideas of roughly equal importance, while a complex sentence shows that one idea depends on or frames another. Modifiers and appositives add useful detail only when their placement makes the connection clear. Punctuation should be understood as a signal of structure and relationship, not as decoration.

Students should also understand that correctness and style overlap but are not identical. Passive voice is not always wrong, semicolons are not fancy commas, and longer sentences are not automatically better. Good grammar helps the reader see which ideas matter, which details explain or rename something, and how parts of a sentence fit together.

## 7. Application And Transfer Expectations

Students should apply Grammar 3 skills to sentence combining, focused editing, and short revision decisions. Transfer is visible when a student can read two short ideas and decide whether to keep them separate, join them as a compound sentence, subordinate one idea, or add one idea as an appositive or modifier. Students should also transfer the same structure tests to unfamiliar sentences, not only examples that use familiar words.

Applications should remain developmentally controlled. Students can revise one or two targeted features at a time, choose the best correction from plausible options, order sentence parts, or type finite corrections. Long copyediting passages with many simultaneous error types should wait until students have stronger automaticity.

## 8. Review And Spacing Plan

Review should begin every unit by retrieving the sentence core: subject, verb, predicate, and complete thought. Clause identification should be revisited during coordination, subordination, punctuation, and cumulative editing so students do not treat it as a one-unit skill.

Coordination and subordination should be spaced together after both are introduced. Later lessons should ask students to decide whether two ideas are equal, dependent, explanatory, or descriptive before choosing a structure. Punctuation review should be tied to those decisions rather than isolated symbol drills.

Modifier placement, pronoun agreement, and active/passive voice should reappear in cumulative editing contexts after initial instruction. Review items should be short, diagnostic, and misconception-based: fragments that contain a subject and verb, sentences with `and` that are not compound, semicolons used between dependent clauses, appositives with unclear punctuation, and pronouns with mismatched or unclear antecedents.

## 9. Mastery Evidence For The End Of The Level

End-of-level mastery should be evidenced by a balanced set of clause identification, sentence combining, targeted editing, and punctuation choices. A student who has mastered Grammar 3 can identify independent and dependent clauses, repair fragments and run-ons, choose fitting coordinating or subordinating conjunctions, punctuate compound and complex sentences, add or identify appositives and modifiers, correct clear agreement and modifier errors, and recognize active/passive voice in clear cases.

The strongest evidence is transfer. The student can explain why a sentence structure fits the meaning relationship, choose among multiple correct-looking punctuation options, and revise short sentence groups so the grammar improves clarity rather than merely making the sentence longer.
