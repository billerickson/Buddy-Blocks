# Unit 1: Clause Foundations And Sentence Boundaries

Track: Grammar
Level: Level 3
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/grammar-3/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn to test word groups for sentence completeness. By the end of the unit, they should identify subjects and verbs, distinguish independent clauses from dependent clauses, recognize fragments, and repair fragments in controlled contexts.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

Clause control is the foundation for compound sentences, complex sentences, punctuation, appositives, modifiers, and cumulative editing. If students cannot tell whether a word group can stand alone, later punctuation rules become disconnected tricks.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: sentence completeness, subject-verb structure, complete thought, independence and dependence, sentence boundaries.
- Vocabulary: clause, independent clause, dependent clause, fragment, subject, predicate, verb, complete thought, main clause.
- Representations and models: two-question clause test, bracketed subject/verb markers, stand-alone test, color-coded sentence core, simple clause maps.
- Texts and forms: short single-clause sentences, dependent-clause starters, fragment repairs, sentence/not-sentence comparisons.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should know subjects, predicates, verbs, verb phrases, direct objects, compound subjects and predicates, prepositional phrases, and basic end punctuation.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- A word group is a sentence if it is long.
- A word group with a subject and verb is always complete.
- Fragments are always missing a subject or always missing a verb.
- Dependent clause starters such as `because`, `when`, and `if` do not change sentence completeness.
- A period can turn an incomplete thought into a sentence.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

The unit begins by refreshing the sentence core because students need a reliable way to find the subject and verb. It then adds the complete-thought test and names independent clauses. Next, dependent clauses are introduced as word groups that have a subject and verb but still cannot stand alone. The unit ends with fragment repair so students apply the distinction to editing.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

This opening unit reviews Grammar 2 sentence parts, verb phrases, prepositional phrases, compound subjects and predicates, and simple end punctuation.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

The unit prepares students to build compound sentences from two independent clauses, use subordinating conjunctions to form complex sentences, punctuate introductory clauses, and diagnose comma splices and run-ons.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify the subject and verb in a short clause.
- Decide whether a word group expresses a complete thought.
- Classify independent clauses, dependent clauses, and fragments.
- Explain why a dependent clause cannot stand alone.
- Repair a fragment with a main clause or missing sentence core.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Find The Sentence Core**: Refresh subject, verb, and predicate as the base for clause work.
2. **Independent Clauses Stand Alone**: Use the subject-verb and complete-thought tests to identify independent clauses.
3. **Dependent Clauses Need A Main Clause**: Show how starters such as `because`, `when`, and `if` create dependency.
4. **Repair Fragments With Complete Thoughts**: Edit fragments by adding the missing core or connecting them to a main clause.
