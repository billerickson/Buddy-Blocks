# Grammar - Level 3 Lesson Briefs

Sources: accepted level blueprint, course map, and unit design briefs in `research/grammar-3/`.

## Unit 1: Clause Foundations And Sentence Boundaries

### Lesson 1: Find The Sentence Core

- **Teaching goal:** Refresh the sentence core as the subject plus verb or predicate that every clause depends on.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify the subject and main verb in a short word group.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** The sentence core answers "Who or what is this about?" and "What happens or is true?"
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic nouns, pronouns, verbs, predicates, and simple complete sentences.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Marking words inside prepositional phrases as the subject; mistaking an object for the subject; choosing every verb-like word instead of the main verb.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a short sentence, the student can select or type the subject and main verb and reject distractors.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for identifying the subject or verb; `match-pairs` for subjects to predicates; `multi-blank-cloze` for labeling core parts.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** The core of a sentence tells who or what the sentence is about and what happens or is true. First find the subject. Then find the main verb or predicate that says something about the subject.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short sentences with familiar vocabulary; avoid long prepositional strings until after the core routine is secure.

### Lesson 2: Independent Clauses Stand Alone

- **Teaching goal:** Teach the two-part test for independent clauses: subject plus verb and complete thought.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether a word group is an independent clause.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** An independent clause has a subject, has a verb, and can stand alone as a complete thought.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Sentence core, subject, verb, complete sentence, and end punctuation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating any subject-verb group as complete; judging by length; relying on capital letters and periods instead of meaning.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can classify clear examples and explain which part of the independence test is met.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for classification; `fill-blank` for test language; `match-pairs` for clauses and reasons.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** An independent clause can stand alone as a sentence. Test it with two questions: Does it have a subject and verb? Does it express a complete thought?
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep examples to one clear clause; avoid compound or complex sentences.

### Lesson 3: Dependent Clauses Need A Main Clause

- **Teaching goal:** Show that subordinating starters can make a subject-verb group dependent.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify dependent clauses in clear cases and explain why they need a main clause.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Words such as `because`, `when`, `if`, and `although` make a clause depend on another idea.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Independent clause test, subject and verb, complete thought.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Believing a dependent clause is complete because it has a subject and verb; deleting the dependency word without considering meaning.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can sort independent and dependent clauses and choose a main clause that completes a dependent clause.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for dependent-clause recognition; `match-pairs` for dependent clauses to fitting main clauses; `order-items` for placing dependent and main clauses.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** Some clauses have a subject and verb but still cannot stand alone. If a clause begins with a word like because, when, if, or although, it usually needs a main clause to finish the thought.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use a small set of common subordinators; avoid subtle stylistic fragments.

### Lesson 4: Repair Fragments With Complete Thoughts

- **Teaching goal:** Help students repair sentence fragments by adding a missing core or connecting the fragment to a main clause.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose a complete repair for a fragment in controlled sentences.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A fragment becomes a sentence when it has a complete core and expresses a complete thought.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Sentence core, independent clause, dependent clause, fragment.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Fixing a fragment only by adding punctuation; adding random words that do not complete the idea; creating a run-on while repairing.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can identify the reason a fragment is incomplete and select or type a finite repair.
- **Recommended question types:** `error-correction` for finite fragment repairs; `multiple-choice` for best repair; `match-pairs` for fragment type to repair strategy.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** A fragment is an incomplete sentence. To repair it, ask what is missing: a subject, a verb, or a main clause that completes the thought.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use fragments with one obvious repair path; avoid creative open-ended revisions.

## Unit 2: Coordination And Compound Sentences

### Lesson 1: Two Independent Clauses, One Compound Sentence

- **Teaching goal:** Define compound sentences as two independent clauses joined in a correct way.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify and build a compound sentence from two complete related ideas.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A compound sentence joins two independent clauses that could each stand alone.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Independent clause test and sentence boundary punctuation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Calling any long sentence compound; joining a dependent clause as if it were independent; using a comma alone.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can identify both independent clauses and choose a correct compound sentence.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for compound recognition; `order-items` for arranging two clauses and joiner; `fill-blank` for the comma-plus-conjunction frame.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** A compound sentence has two independent clauses. Each side could be its own sentence, but the writer joins them because the ideas are closely related.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use two short independent clauses; avoid semicolons until later in the unit.

### Lesson 2: Choose The Best Coordinator

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to choose coordinating conjunctions by meaning relationship.
- **Student outcome:** The student can select a coordinator such as `and`, `but`, `or`, `so`, or `yet` that fits the ideas.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Coordinators show relationships: addition, contrast, choice, result, or surprising contrast.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Compound sentence structure and independent clauses.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Using `and` for every relationship; choosing by sound instead of logic; confusing `so` with `but`.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can match relationships to coordinators and complete compound sentences logically.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for conjunction choices; `match-pairs` for relationship to coordinator; `multiple-choice` for best explanation.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** Coordinating conjunctions are small words with important jobs. Choose the word that shows how the two complete ideas are related.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep relationships explicit; avoid rare FANBOYS uses such as formal `for`.

### Lesson 3: Comma Plus Conjunction Or Semicolon

- **Teaching goal:** Compare two correct ways to join closely related independent clauses.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose comma plus conjunction or a semicolon when both sides are independent.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A semicolon can join two closely related independent clauses without a coordinating conjunction.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Compound sentences, coordinating conjunctions, independent clauses.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating semicolons as fancy commas; using a semicolon before a dependent clause; adding both semicolon and conjunction automatically.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can identify when a semicolon is allowed and when comma plus conjunction is a better fit.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for correct punctuation; `error-correction` for finite repairs; `match-pairs` for join pattern to example.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** Two independent clauses can be joined with comma plus a coordinating conjunction. If the ideas are very closely related, a semicolon can join them without the conjunction.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use only clear independent clauses; do not introduce conjunctive adverbs yet.

### Lesson 4: Fix Comma Splices And Run-Ons

- **Teaching goal:** Repair incorrect joins between independent clauses.
- **Student outcome:** The student can fix comma splices and run-ons using a period, comma plus conjunction, or semicolon.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Two independent clauses need a real boundary or a correct joining pattern.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Independent clauses, compound sentences, semicolon use.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Believing a comma alone is enough; joining with `and` without punctuation; fixing every error with a semicolon.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can identify the error and choose a correct repair that preserves meaning.
- **Recommended question types:** `error-correction` for finite repairs; `multiple-choice` for best correction; `passage-question` for targeted editing in a two-sentence context.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** A comma splice happens when two complete sentences are joined with only a comma. A run-on happens when complete ideas run together without a clear boundary.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep each item to two independent clauses; avoid paragraphs with multiple simultaneous errors.

## Unit 3: Subordination And Complex Sentences

### Lesson 1: Subordinating Words Make Clauses Dependent

- **Teaching goal:** Show that subordinating conjunctions turn clauses into dependent clauses.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify the subordinating word and the dependent clause it creates.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** The starter word changes a complete idea into one that needs a main clause.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Dependent clauses and independent clauses.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Ignoring the starter word; treating a dependent clause as complete; thinking every sentence starter is a subordinator.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can mark the subordinating word and classify the clause.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for subordinator recognition; `fill-blank` for dependency starters; `match-pairs` for starter word to clause.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** A subordinating word, such as because, when, if, or although, makes a clause depend on another clause. The dependent clause gives a condition, reason, time, or contrast.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Begin with common subordinators and short clauses.

### Lesson 2: Show Time, Cause, Condition, And Contrast

- **Teaching goal:** Help students choose subordinators based on the relationship between ideas.
- **Student outcome:** The student can match `when`, `because`, `if`, and `although` to time, cause, condition, and contrast.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Subordinating conjunctions are not interchangeable because they signal different relationships.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Subordinating conjunctions and dependent clauses.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Using `because` for every relationship; confusing condition with time; choosing a word that makes the sentence illogical.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can complete complex sentences with logical subordinators and name the relationship.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for conjunction choice; `match-pairs` for relationship labels; `multiple-choice` for best reason.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** Choose the subordinating word that matches the meaning. `When` shows time, `because` shows cause, `if` shows condition, and `although` shows contrast.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Limit the starter set at first; use transparent relationships.

### Lesson 3: Build Complex Sentences Around A Main Clause

- **Teaching goal:** Combine ideas by placing a dependent clause before or after a main clause.
- **Student outcome:** The student can build a logical complex sentence and identify its main clause.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A complex sentence has one main clause and at least one dependent clause.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Main clause, dependent clause, subordinating conjunctions.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Losing the main clause; joining two dependent clauses; creating comma splices when moving clauses.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can order clause parts into a complete complex sentence and identify the main clause.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for clause order; `multiple-choice` for main-clause identification; `multi-blank-cloze` for completing sentence frames.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** In a complex sentence, the main clause can stand alone. The dependent clause adds a reason, time, condition, or contrast.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one dependent clause and one main clause; avoid embedded clauses.

### Lesson 4: Punctuate Introductory Dependent Clauses

- **Teaching goal:** Teach comma use when a dependent clause comes before the main clause.
- **Student outcome:** The student can punctuate complex sentences based on clause order.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** When a dependent clause comes first, place a comma before the main clause.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Dependent clauses, main clauses, complex sentence order.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Placing a comma before every subordinating conjunction; omitting commas after long introductory clauses; adding commas between subject and verb.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can select correctly punctuated complex sentences and repair missing or extra commas.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for punctuation choice; `error-correction` for finite comma edits; `order-items` for dependent clause plus comma plus main clause.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** If the dependent clause comes first, use a comma before the main clause. If the main clause comes first, many common dependent clauses do not need a comma before them.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use clear clause order; avoid exceptions or advanced style choices.

## Unit 4: Modifiers, Appositives, And Sentence Detail

### Lesson 1: Keep The Core, Add The Detail

- **Teaching goal:** Help students separate the sentence core from added descriptive detail.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify the core sentence inside a longer sentence.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Added detail can expand a sentence, but the core still names the main subject and action or state.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Subject, verb, predicate, independent clause.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating introductory or descriptive phrases as the main sentence; deleting essential core words; assuming longer means more complete.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can choose the core sentence that remains after optional detail is removed.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for core identification; `match-pairs` for detail type; `multi-blank-cloze` for subject and verb labels.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** Sentences can include extra detail, but they still have a core. Find the main subject and main verb first; then ask which words add detail.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one added phrase or clause at a time.

### Lesson 2: Adjective And Adverb Phrases Modify Meaning

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to identify what adjective and adverb phrases describe.
- **Student outcome:** The student can match a modifier phrase to the noun, verb, or idea it modifies.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A modifier answers questions such as which one, what kind, where, when, how, or why.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Adjectives, adverbs, prepositional phrases, sentence core.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Labeling every prepositional phrase as one type; attaching a modifier to the nearest noun without checking meaning; ignoring how placement affects meaning.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can identify what a modifier describes and choose a sentence where the modifier is clear.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for modifier to described word; `multiple-choice` for modifier meaning; `fill-blank` for choosing a clear modifier.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** A modifier adds detail. Ask what word or idea the modifier describes. Good placement helps the reader connect the modifier to the right word.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep modifiers concrete; avoid advanced phrase taxonomy.

### Lesson 3: Introductory Elements Lead Into The Main Clause

- **Teaching goal:** Connect introductory phrases and clauses to the main clause they set up.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify introductory elements and choose comma placement in clear cases.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** An introductory element comes before the main clause and prepares the reader for it.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Phrases, dependent clauses, main clauses, comma after introductory dependent clauses.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating an introductory element as a complete sentence; omitting the main clause; placing the comma after the subject instead of after the introduction.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can identify the introductory element and select the correctly punctuated sentence.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for introductory element identification; `error-correction` for comma placement; `order-items` for introduction plus main clause.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** An introductory element comes before the main clause. It might be a phrase or a dependent clause. A comma often signals where the introduction ends and the main clause begins.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid borderline short introductory adverbs; use visible phrase or clause introductions.

### Lesson 4: Appositives Rename Nouns

- **Teaching goal:** Teach appositives as noun phrases that rename another noun.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify an appositive and punctuate a nonessential appositive with comma pairs.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** An appositive sits beside a noun and renames or identifies it.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Nouns, noun phrases, sentence core, commas for added detail.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Calling any description an appositive; using only one comma for an appositive in the middle of a sentence; adding commas around essential names.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can match nouns to appositives and choose correct punctuation for nonessential appositives.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for noun to appositive; `multiple-choice` for punctuation; `error-correction` for finite comma repairs.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** An appositive is a noun or noun phrase that renames another noun. Nonessential appositives are often set off with commas.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use clear appositives with familiar nouns; avoid legalistic essential/nonessential edge cases.

## Unit 5: Agreement, Voice, And Modifier Clarity

### Lesson 1: Pronouns Must Match Their Antecedents

- **Teaching goal:** Teach pronoun-antecedent agreement in number.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose a singular or plural pronoun that matches its antecedent.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A pronoun should agree with the noun it replaces or refers to.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Pronouns, nouns, singular and plural number.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Choosing pronouns by sound alone; using plural pronouns for singular nouns; ignoring compound antecedents.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can identify the antecedent and choose a pronoun that agrees.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for pronoun choice; `match-pairs` for antecedent to pronoun; `multiple-choice` for agreement explanation.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** A pronoun points back to an antecedent, the noun it replaces or refers to. Singular antecedents need singular pronouns, and plural antecedents need plural pronouns.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use common pronouns and clear antecedents; avoid gender-identity edge cases and advanced style debates in this level.

### Lesson 2: Make Pronoun References Clear

- **Teaching goal:** Help students revise unclear pronouns by naming or moving the antecedent.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify an unclear pronoun reference and choose a clearer revision.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A pronoun is clear when the reader can easily tell which noun it refers to.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Pronouns, antecedents, sentence clarity.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Thinking a pronoun is clear if the writer knows what it means; replacing every pronoun with a noun; ignoring nearby competing nouns.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can choose the revision that removes ambiguity without changing meaning.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for best revision; `error-correction` for finite replacements; `passage-question` for interpreting short context.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** A pronoun should point clearly to one noun. If two nouns could fit, revise by naming the noun or moving the pronoun closer to its antecedent.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use sentences with one clear ambiguity; avoid long passages.

### Lesson 3: Active And Passive Voice Show Action Differently

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to recognize active and passive voice in clear cases.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify whether the subject does the action or receives the action.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** In active voice, the subject does the action. In passive voice, the subject receives the action.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Subject, verb, direct object or receiver, basic verb phrases.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating passive voice as always wrong; identifying any form of `be` as passive; ignoring the actor.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can label clear active/passive examples and choose the voice that fits a stated purpose.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for active/passive recognition; `match-pairs` for sentence to voice; `order-items` for actor-action-receiver order.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** Voice shows how the subject connects to the action. Active voice puts the doer as the subject. Passive voice puts the receiver as the subject.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use transitive verbs and clear actors/receivers; avoid perfect/progressive tense distractions.

### Lesson 4: Place Modifiers Where They Belong

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to correct clear misplaced modifiers.
- **Student outcome:** The student can move a modifier next to the word or phrase it describes.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Modifier placement changes meaning; the modifier should be close to what it modifies.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Modifier phrases, sentence core, noun and verb identification.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Thinking the reader will infer the intended meaning; moving the noun instead of the modifier; creating a new ambiguity while repairing.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can identify the misplaced modifier and choose or type a clear correction.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for best correction; `error-correction` for finite sentence repairs; `match-pairs` for modifier to target.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** A modifier should sit close to the word it describes. If a sentence sounds as if the wrong thing is being described, move the modifier to make the meaning clear.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use amusing but clear examples; avoid dangling participles that require advanced rewriting.

## Unit 6: Punctuation And Cumulative Sentence Craft

### Lesson 1: Commas Mark Introductions And Appositives

- **Teaching goal:** Review comma use for introductory elements and nonessential appositives.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose comma placement that marks setup or renaming detail.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Commas can show where an introduction ends or where a nonessential appositive begins and ends.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Introductory elements, appositives, main clause, comma pairs.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Adding commas wherever the reader pauses; using only one comma for a middle appositive; separating subject and verb with a comma.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can identify the structure and select the correctly punctuated sentence.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for punctuation choice; `error-correction` for finite comma edits; `match-pairs` for comma job to example.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** Commas can help the reader see sentence structure. Use a comma after an introductory element. Use comma pairs around a nonessential appositive in the middle of a sentence.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one comma job per item before mixing both.

### Lesson 2: Semicolons And Colons Have Specific Jobs

- **Teaching goal:** Distinguish semicolon and colon uses appropriate for this level.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose a semicolon for related independent clauses and a colon after a complete introduction to a list or explanation.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A semicolon links closely related complete clauses; a colon points forward to a list or explanation after a complete setup.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Independent clauses, compound sentences, lists, explanations.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Using semicolons as fancy commas; placing a colon after an incomplete phrase; using a colon where no list or explanation follows.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can classify the punctuation job and choose the correct mark in controlled sentences.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for punctuation selection; `fill-blank` for choosing the mark; `error-correction` for finite punctuation repairs.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** Semicolons and colons have different jobs. A semicolon joins two closely related independent clauses. A colon comes after a complete setup and points to a list or explanation.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid semicolon-plus-conjunctive-adverb rules and advanced colon style.

### Lesson 3: Choose The Best Sentence Combination

- **Teaching goal:** Help students choose sentence structures based on meaning rather than length.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether to keep ideas separate, coordinate them, subordinate one, or use an appositive.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Sentence combining should show the relationship among ideas clearly.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Independent clauses, compound sentences, complex sentences, appositives, punctuation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Combining every sentence; using `and` for all relationships; subordinating the main idea; adding appositives that do not rename nouns.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can choose the revision that best expresses the intended relationship.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for best revision; `order-items` for arranging sentence parts; `passage-question` for short revision context.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** Good sentence combining is a meaning choice. Equal ideas can be coordinated. One idea can become dependent. A noun can be renamed with an appositive.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Provide the intended relationship in the prompt; avoid open-ended production.

### Lesson 4: Edit A Short Passage With Grammar 3 Tools

- **Teaching goal:** Apply Grammar 3 skills in short targeted editing contexts.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify and correct one named grammar issue in a short passage or sentence set.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Cumulative editing uses the same structure tests in new combinations.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** All Grammar 3 unit concepts.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Trying to fix every possible issue at once; choosing edits that change meaning; ignoring the target skill named in the prompt.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can make targeted edits for clauses, punctuation, agreement, voice, and modifier clarity in short passages.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for context-based edits; `multiple-choice` for best correction; `error-correction` for finite targeted repairs.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** In cumulative editing, name the target first. Ask: Am I checking clauses, punctuation, pronouns, voice, or modifier placement? Then make the edit that improves clarity.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use two- to four-sentence passages; ask for one targeted edit per scored item.
