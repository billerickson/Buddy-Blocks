# History And Civics 1 Course Map

Track: History And Civics
Level: Level 1
Sources: Accepted research brief at `research/history-and-civics-1/01-research-brief.md` and level blueprint at `research/history-and-civics-1/02-level-blueprint.md`

## Course Design Notes

This course map orders History And Civics 1 from core tools into applied inquiry. Students first learn how to think with time and place, then how to use sources, then how to reason about community, economics, and culture. The final unit asks students to combine timeline, map, source, civic, and economic reasoning in a compact inquiry. Lesson counts are intentionally uneven in emphasis but compact in structure: each unit has three focused lessons, and later lessons spiral earlier tools rather than restarting them.

## Unit 1: Time, Sequence, And Change

### Unit Purpose

Build the time language and timeline habits students need before they encounter historical and civic stories. Students learn to order events, use broad time words, and explain simple cause/effect, change, and continuity.

### Student Outcomes

- Use past, present, future, long ago, before, after, decade, century, and sequence accurately.
- Read and build simple timelines from short event descriptions.
- Explain simple cause/effect, change, and continuity in age-appropriate contexts.
- Recognize that history is more than dates; it is a story of people, places, choices, and evidence.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Past, present, future, long ago, before, after, first, next, last, sequence, timeline, event, decade, century, cause, effect, change, continuity, history.

### Prerequisites

Students should be able to answer who/what/where/when questions, sort pictures or facts, and understand family or community past/present at an informal level.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This opening unit supplies the time language used in every later unit. Unit 2 adds place; Unit 3 adds sources; later units repeatedly ask students to locate events in time before explaining civic, economic, or cultural meaning.

### Misconceptions To Address

- History is just memorizing dates.
- A timeline is the same as a number line.
- Any older event must be "first" even when dates or clues show otherwise.
- Change means everything changed; continuity means nothing changed.
- Cause and effect are just two unrelated events near each other.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

3-4 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Review time words in every later unit. Use short timeline retrieval after delays, and vary examples so students apply sequence to family, community, historical, civic, and economic contexts.

## Unit 2: Maps, Globes, And Places

### Unit Purpose

Teach students to use maps and globes as tools for thinking about where people live, move, trade, and make decisions. Students learn directions, symbols, keys, major place categories, and the idea that maps select information.

### Student Outcomes

- Use cardinal and intermediate directions with a compass rose.
- Interpret common map symbols and map keys.
- Distinguish continents, oceans, countries, states, cities, regions, routes, and environments at an introductory level.
- Explain that maps show selected information, not everything about a place.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Map, globe, place, location, direction, north, south, east, west, northeast, northwest, southeast, southwest, symbol, map key, compass rose, continent, ocean, country, state, city, region, environment, route, movement, scale.

### Prerequisites

Students need basic spatial words, classroom map or diagram exposure, and Unit 1 sequence language for following routes or ordered movement.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit connects time to place. Later source lessons include maps as sources; civic and economic units use community maps; culture and inquiry units connect people and choices to environment, movement, and region.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Maps show everything exactly.
- A map key is optional decoration.
- North always means "up" in real life.
- A symbol is the place itself rather than a representation.
- A country, state, and city are the same kind of place.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

3-4 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Spiral map keys, directions, and place vocabulary in source, civics, economics, culture, and inquiry lessons. Use quick map checks with varied symbols and simple routes.

## Unit 3: Sources, Clues, And Evidence

### Unit Purpose

Introduce historical inquiry through concrete sources. Students learn to distinguish primary and secondary sources, notice details, make careful inferences, and support a simple claim with evidence.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify photographs, artifacts, maps, documents, and short texts as sources.
- Distinguish clear primary and secondary source examples.
- Separate what a source shows from what a student infers.
- Support a simple claim with one relevant detail from a source.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Source, primary source, secondary source, photograph, artifact, document, map, timeline, clue, evidence, claim, infer, support, detail, historian.

### Prerequisites

Students need short informational reading or read-aloud comprehension, Unit 1 time language, and Unit 2 map/source awareness.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit gives students the evidence habit required for all later history and civics work. Later units ask students to use source details when explaining rules, economic choices, cultures, symbols, and final inquiry claims.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A primary source is automatically true or complete.
- A secondary source is always less useful.
- Evidence is the same as a guess.
- Any interesting detail supports any claim.
- A picture "says" only what the viewer already thinks.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

3-4 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Return to source type, detail, evidence, claim, and inference in each later unit. Use small source sets rather than long readings.

## Unit 4: Communities, Rules, And Civic Life

### Unit Purpose

Build concrete civic understanding through communities, rules, laws, leaders, rights, responsibilities, and participation. Students learn that civic life is about shared problems and respectful action, not just memorizing symbols.

### Student Outcomes

- Explain why communities use rules and laws.
- Distinguish rules from laws in simple cases.
- Describe leader responsibilities and citizen responsibilities.
- Connect rights with responsibilities and respectful participation.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Community, rule, law, leader, citizen, right, responsibility, vote, participate, public, private, common good, respectful, decision.

### Prerequisites

Students need familiar community experiences, basic civic words, and source/timeline/map habits from Units 1-3.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit applies evidence and map thinking to community life. It prepares Unit 7 work with symbols and documents and supports the final inquiry unit, where students explain civic choices with evidence.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Rules and laws are the same in every community.
- Rights mean doing anything one wants.
- Responsibilities are only chores.
- A leader is just a famous person.
- Voting or participation matters only for adults.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

3-4 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Review source evidence through civic scenarios. Revisit rights and responsibilities in symbol, document, and capstone lessons.

## Unit 5: Needs, Wants, Work, And Trade

### Unit Purpose

Teach basic economics as a way to understand daily life, historical choices, and civic decisions. Students learn needs, wants, goods, services, producers, consumers, trade, money, scarcity, and choice.

### Student Outcomes

- Distinguish needs and wants in context.
- Identify goods, services, producers, and consumers.
- Explain trade, money, scarcity, and choice in simple scenarios.
- Connect economic ideas to historical and community life.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Need, want, good, service, producer, consumer, work, trade, money, scarcity, resource, choice, cost, benefit.

### Prerequisites

Students need basic community vocabulary, sorting skills, and the ability to read short scenarios.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit builds on communities and prepares cultural and historical daily-life comparisons. The inquiry unit asks students to identify economic choices in a short source set.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A want is unimportant just because it is not a need.
- Money and trade are the same thing.
- A producer must make an object, never provide a service.
- Scarcity means there is none at all.
- Historical people made choices without limits or needs.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

3-4 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Interleave needs/wants and goods/services in culture and civic scenarios. Use diagnostic comparisons rather than isolated vocabulary drills.

## Unit 6: Culture, Daily Life, And Historical Stories

### Unit Purpose

Help students use time, place, sources, civics, and economics to understand people and cultures respectfully. Students learn that culture includes many parts of life and that historical stories should be coherent, contextual, and evidence-based.

### Student Outcomes

- Describe culture as more than food and holidays.
- Compare daily life across time or place using respectful language.
- Use evidence to describe a person, group, place, or event.
- Recognize change and continuity in cultural and historical examples.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Culture, tradition, language, belief, art, work, family life, technology, invention, contribution, context, perspective, compare, contrast, respectful, change, continuity.

### Prerequisites

Students need Units 1-5 tools: timeline language, map/place vocabulary, source evidence, civic ideas, and economic vocabulary.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit synthesizes earlier tools in short historical and cultural stories. It prepares Unit 7 by connecting symbols and documents to civic meaning, and it prepares Unit 8 by giving students practice with respectful evidence-backed explanations.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Culture means food and holidays only.
- Old means less advanced in every way.
- A single source tells the whole story of a culture.
- Comparing cultures means ranking them.
- Contributions are only made by famous leaders.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

3-4 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Review source detail, map location, time language, and economics inside cultural examples. Require concise evidence statements rather than long essays.

## Unit 7: Symbols, Documents, And Shared Ideals

### Unit Purpose

Teach civic symbols, documents, speeches, and shared ideals as evidence of what communities value. Students learn to explain meaning and purpose without reducing civics to symbol memorization.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify national, state, and local symbols as civic sources.
- Explain that symbols, documents, and speeches can communicate shared ideals.
- Connect rights, responsibilities, leaders, and participation to civic meaning.
- Use respectful evidence from a symbol, short document excerpt, or speech line.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Symbol, document, speech, flag, seal, monument, pledge, motto, ideal, liberty, justice, responsibility, citizen, leader, evidence, meaning.

### Prerequisites

Students need source evidence, community/civic vocabulary, and respectful discussion habits.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit extends Unit 4 civics and Unit 3 source work. It prepares the final inquiry unit by asking students to interpret symbols and civic texts as evidence rather than isolated facts.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A symbol's meaning is only its picture.
- Memorizing a civic word is the same as understanding it.
- Rights do not connect to responsibilities.
- A document or speech cannot be a source.
- Civic ideals are only national, never local or community-based.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

3-4 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use short excerpts and image-based sources. Revisit community, rights, responsibilities, and evidence sentence frames.

## Unit 8: Inquiry Capstone: Claims With Evidence

### Unit Purpose

Bring the level together through compact inquiry tasks. Students use timelines, maps, sources, civic/economic vocabulary, and respectful explanation to answer historical or civic questions with evidence.

### Student Outcomes

- Ask or answer a focused historical or civic question.
- Use a timeline, map, and short source together.
- Choose relevant evidence for a simple claim.
- Write or select a concise claim-and-evidence explanation.
- Check whether an answer respects time, place, source details, and context.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Inquiry, question, claim, evidence, source, timeline, map, cause, effect, change, continuity, place, community, choice, responsibility, conclusion.

### Prerequisites

Students need all prior unit tools and should be comfortable with short texts, images, maps, timelines, and sentence frames.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This final unit reviews every level strand and prepares later History And Civics levels, where students will study broader historical periods, denser sources, and more formal civic reasoning.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A claim can be supported by any detail.
- Evidence can be invented from memory when a source is present.
- A map or timeline is extra decoration.
- A longer answer is always a better answer.
- One source answers every possible question.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

3-4 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use mixed retrieval across all prior vocabulary and representations. Keep source sets short and structured so students practice integration without overload.
