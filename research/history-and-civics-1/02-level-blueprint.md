# History And Civics 1 Level Blueprint

Track: History And Civics
Level: Level 1
Source: Accepted research brief at `research/history-and-civics-1/01-research-brief.md`

## 1. Level Mission

History And Civics 1 gives beginning history and civics students a durable orientation to time, place, evidence, community life, and civic responsibility. Students learn that history is not a list of disconnected dates; it is an evidence-based account of people, places, choices, needs, rules, change, and continuity. By the end of the level, students should be able to read simple timelines and maps, use short sources carefully, explain basic civic and economic ideas, describe cultures and historical situations respectfully, and support a simple claim with evidence from a text, image, map, artifact, or timeline.

## 2. Durable Student Outcomes

By the end of History And Civics 1, students should be able to:

1. Use basic historical time language to sequence events and explain change, continuity, cause, and effect in simple cases.
2. Read and use timelines to order events, notice before/after relationships, and connect events to broad period language.
3. Use maps and globes to identify places, directions, symbols, map keys, regions, and simple relationships among location, environment, and movement.
4. Distinguish primary and secondary sources in accessible cases and identify what evidence a source can provide.
5. Make a simple historical or civic claim and support it with one relevant piece of evidence from a source, map, timeline, or short text.
6. Explain basic economic ideas: needs, wants, goods, services, producers, consumers, trade, money, and scarcity.
7. Explain why communities use rules, laws, leaders, rights, responsibilities, symbols, and participation to solve shared problems.
8. Describe people, cultures, places, events, and daily life with accurate, respectful language.
9. Combine time, place, source, civic, and economic reasoning in short inquiry tasks.

## 3. Concept And Skill Progression

The level begins with time because students need sequence and broad period language before they can make sense of historical stories. Students first distinguish past, present, and future; then they read and build simple timelines; then they use before, after, long ago, decade, century, cause, effect, change, and continuity in compact contexts.

The second progression strand is place. Students learn map and globe basics early so later historical and civic examples have a spatial frame. They move from cardinal directions and map keys into continents, oceans, countries, states, cities, regions, environment, and movement. The goal is not memorizing every place, but using geography as a tool for understanding where people live, travel, trade, and make community choices.

Students then learn how historians know things. They begin with concrete sources such as photographs, artifacts, maps, simple documents, and short texts. They distinguish primary and secondary sources, notice source details, separate what a source shows from what they infer, and begin using sentence frames such as "I know this because..." and "The evidence is..."

Civic and economic reasoning comes next through concrete community situations. Students examine why groups make rules, how laws differ from classroom or family rules, what leaders do, how rights and responsibilities fit together, and how people participate respectfully. They connect community life to needs, wants, scarcity, work, goods, services, producers, consumers, trade, and money.

The later level uses coherent historical and cultural stories to combine the strands. Students compare daily life across time and place, describe culture beyond food and holidays, recognize contributions and choices of varied people, and avoid simple "old means worse" thinking. The level ends with mixed inquiry: students use a timeline, map, and short source set to answer a question with a claim and evidence.

## 4. Vocabulary, Representation, Model, Text, And Form Progression

Early vocabulary should stabilize time and sequence language: past, present, future, long ago, before, after, first, next, last, sequence, event, decade, century, cause, effect, change, and continuity. Representations should begin with picture sequences and short timelines before introducing timelines with uneven intervals or broad period labels.

Geography vocabulary should include map, globe, place, location, direction, north, south, east, west, northeast, northwest, southeast, southwest, symbol, map key, continent, ocean, country, state, city, region, environment, route, movement, near, far, and scale as exposure. Representations should include classroom maps, simple community maps, world maps, globes, keys, compass roses, routes, and region labels.

Source and inquiry language should include source, primary source, secondary source, photograph, artifact, document, map, timeline, clue, evidence, claim, infer, support, reliable for, and question. Text forms should progress from captions and one-sentence descriptions to short paragraphs, short source excerpts, image sets, and small source packets with guided prompts.

Civics and economics vocabulary should include community, rule, law, leader, citizen, right, responsibility, vote, symbol, pledge, document, need, want, good, service, producer, consumer, trade, money, scarcity, choice, and resource. Models should include scenario cards, role labels, simple decision charts, goods/services sorts, and right/responsibility pairs.

Culture and historical-story vocabulary should include culture, tradition, belief, language, art, work, family life, technology, invention, contribution, compare, contrast, respectful, perspective, and context. Response forms should begin with selected evidence and sentence frames, then move toward one- or two-sentence explanations and short constructed responses.

## 5. Fluency Expectations

History And Civics 1 does not require computation fluency. It does require quick, accurate use of core tools and vocabulary. Students should become fluent enough to order events on a simple timeline, identify basic map directions and key symbols, classify clear primary and secondary sources, sort civic/economic examples into taught categories, and use evidence sentence frames without restarting the whole lesson. Retrieval practice should be brief, spaced, and mixed so students retain tool vocabulary while applying it in new historical and civic contexts.

## 6. Conceptual Understanding Expectations

Students should understand that history is built from sources and evidence, not guesses or memorized date lists. They should know that timelines show order over time, maps select and symbolize information about places, and sources give clues that still need careful reading.

Students should understand that communities make rules and laws for purposes, that leaders have responsibilities rather than just fame, and that rights are connected to responsibilities and other people's rights. They should recognize that economic choices happen because people have needs and wants but limited resources.

Students should also understand that cultures include language, beliefs, arts, work, family life, institutions, traditions, and technologies. They should describe past people and cultures respectfully, noticing context and avoiding the idea that older ways of life were simply "less advanced" in every way.

## 7. Application And Transfer Expectations

Students should apply tools across new examples: use a timeline to explain sequence in a family, community, or historical story; use a map key to answer a question about place or movement; use a photograph, artifact, map, or text as evidence for a simple claim; classify a community choice as civic, economic, geographic, or historical when appropriate; and explain a right/responsibility or need/want relationship in a new scenario.

Transfer is visible when students meet an unfamiliar but accessible source or map and still ask, "What does this show?", "Where is this?", "What happened before or after?", and "What evidence supports the answer?" The level should move students away from isolated recall toward small, evidence-backed explanations.

## 8. Review And Spacing Plan

Review should be cumulative from the start. Time words and sequence should reappear in map, source, civics, economics, and culture lessons. Map symbols, directions, place words, and region language should recur whenever a historical or civic story has a location. Source type, evidence, claim, and inference should be retrieved in every later unit after the source unit introduces them.

Civics and economics should be interleaved with historical stories rather than treated as separate vocabulary banks. Rights and responsibilities can be reviewed in community scenarios, symbol lessons, and inquiry tasks. Needs, wants, scarcity, goods, services, and trade should recur in daily-life and culture lessons. Review questions should be diagnostic: wrong answers should reveal a timeline, map, source, civic, economic, or respectful-language misconception.

## 9. Mastery Evidence For The End Of The Level

End-of-level mastery should be evidenced by mixed tasks rather than a single recall quiz. A student who has mastered History And Civics 1 can read a simple timeline and map, identify relevant source evidence, explain basic civic and economic ideas in context, describe people and cultures respectfully, and write or select a claim supported by evidence.

The strongest mastery evidence is a compact inquiry task: given a short historical or civic question, a timeline, a map, and one or two short sources, the student can answer with an accurate claim and one clear evidence sentence. The answer should show time order, place awareness, source use, and age-appropriate civic or historical understanding.
