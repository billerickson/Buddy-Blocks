# Unit 6: Culture, Daily Life, And Historical Stories

Track: History And Civics
Level: Level 1
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/history-and-civics-1/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn to describe cultures and historical daily life with evidence, context, and respect. By the end of the unit, students can identify parts of culture, compare daily life across time or place, and write accurate, respectful descriptions supported by source details.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit comes after students have time, map, source, civic, and economic tools. Culture and historical stories require all of those tools if students are going to do more than memorize isolated trivia.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: culture, daily life, context, perspective, comparison, contribution, change and continuity.
- Vocabulary: culture, tradition, language, belief, art, work, family life, technology, invention, contribution, context, perspective, compare, contrast, respectful, change, continuity.
- Representations and models: culture-web diagrams, daily-life comparison charts, source-detail evidence charts, change/continuity charts.
- Texts and forms: short adapted historical and cultural descriptions, image captions, respectful sentence frames.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need all prior unit tools: time sequence, place/map vocabulary, source evidence, civic roles, and basic economic vocabulary.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Culture means only food, clothing, and holidays.
- Older tools or customs are automatically worse.
- A single artifact or image explains a whole culture.
- Comparing cultures means ranking them.
- Only famous leaders make contributions.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin by broadening the meaning of culture. Then compare daily life across time and place so students apply time, place, civics, and economics. End with respectful historical descriptions so students practice using evidence without stereotyping or presentism.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Review timelines for change/continuity, maps for place and movement, sources for evidence, civic vocabulary for community life, and economics for work, needs, wants, and trade.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares students to interpret symbols and civic documents as cultural/civic sources and prepares capstone inquiry responses that require respectful evidence-backed explanation.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify several parts of culture beyond food and holidays.
- Compare daily life across time or place using evidence.
- Name a change and a continuity in a short example.
- Avoid ranking language when comparing cultures.
- Use a source detail to support a respectful description.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **What Culture Includes**: Identify culture as language, beliefs, arts, work, family life, institutions, traditions, and more.
2. **Daily Life Across Time And Place**: Compare daily life using time, place, source, civic, and economic clues.
3. **Respectful Historical Descriptions**: Describe people and cultures accurately with evidence and context.
