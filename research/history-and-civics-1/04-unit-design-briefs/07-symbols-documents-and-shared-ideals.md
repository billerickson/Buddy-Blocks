# Unit 7: Symbols, Documents, And Shared Ideals

Track: History And Civics
Level: Level 1
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/history-and-civics-1/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn to treat civic symbols, documents, speeches, and shared ideals as meaningful sources. By the end of the unit, students can explain what a symbol or civic text communicates and connect it to rights, responsibilities, leaders, citizens, and participation.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit follows culture and civic life so students interpret symbols and documents as part of community meaning, not as isolated things to memorize. It also directly prepares the final inquiry unit.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: symbol, civic meaning, shared ideal, document as source, speech as source, action connected to ideals.
- Vocabulary: symbol, document, speech, flag, seal, monument, pledge, motto, ideal, liberty, justice, responsibility, citizen, leader, evidence, meaning.
- Representations and models: symbol-detail charts, short document excerpts, speech lines, ideal/action pairs, local/state/national source examples.
- Texts and forms: brief civic excerpts, image captions, sentence frames such as "This symbol can stand for ___ because..."

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need Unit 3 evidence habits, Unit 4 civic vocabulary, Unit 6 respectful interpretation, and reading/listening support for short excerpts.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- A symbol's meaning is only the object pictured.
- Memorizing a civic word is the same as explaining it.
- Documents and speeches are not sources.
- Rights do not connect to responsibilities.
- Shared ideals belong only to national government, not local communities.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with visual symbols because students can observe details and infer meaning with support. Then move to documents and speeches as text-based sources. End with ideals in action so civic meaning connects to participation and responsibility.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Review source evidence, rights/responsibilities, community leadership, culture, respectful language, and time/place context when interpreting symbols or civic texts.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares students for capstone inquiry tasks that combine sources and civic meaning. It also prepares later History And Civics levels that will use denser documents and more formal civic concepts.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify symbols, documents, and speeches as civic sources.
- Explain a symbol's meaning using details.
- Identify an ideal in a short civic line or motto.
- Connect an ideal to a responsibility or action.
- Avoid treating symbol recognition as full understanding.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Symbols Show Shared Meaning**: Use details in symbols to infer what a community may value.
2. **Documents And Speeches As Sources**: Read short civic text excerpts as evidence of ideas and ideals.
3. **Ideals In Community Action**: Connect ideals such as liberty, justice, safety, or fairness to responsible civic choices.
