# Unit 1: Time, Sequence, And Change

Track: History And Civics
Level: Level 1
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/history-and-civics-1/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students build the basic time language and sequencing habits needed for historical thinking. By the end of the unit, students can use past, present, future, before, after, decade, century, timeline, cause, effect, change, and continuity to organize short historical or community examples.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit comes first because students cannot reason well about historical stories until they can place events in time. It also establishes the level's central idea: history is not just a date list; it is a story about people, places, choices, change, and evidence.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: time order, broad period language, event sequence, cause/effect, change, continuity.
- Vocabulary: past, present, future, long ago, before, after, first, next, last, decade, century, timeline, event, cause, effect, change, continuity.
- Representations and models: picture sequences, three-event timelines, simple labeled timelines, cause/effect pairs, change/continuity charts.
- Texts and forms: short family, school, community, and historical examples; sentence frames such as "First ___, then ___" and "One thing changed, but ___ stayed the same."

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should be able to answer who, what, where, and when questions; distinguish yesterday/today/tomorrow informally; sort pictures or events; and talk about family or community past/present with support.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- History is just dates to memorize.
- A timeline works exactly like a number line.
- Any old-looking event must come first.
- Cause and effect are any two events near each other.
- Change means everything is different, and continuity means nothing changed.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with past, present, and future because those words anchor all later time language. Move next to timeline reading so students can use ordered representations instead of only verbal sequence. End with cause/effect, change, and continuity so students begin explaining why time order matters.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

This is the first unit, so review draws from students' informal prior knowledge: family stories, school routines, community change, and familiar first/next/last sequencing.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares map route sequencing, source dating, civic and economic scenario order, cultural change over time, and final inquiry tasks that require timeline evidence.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Classify examples as past, present, or future.
- Order events using before/after language.
- Read a simple timeline and identify first, next, last, earliest, or latest events.
- Distinguish cause/effect from mere sequence in simple examples.
- Explain one change and one continuity from a short scenario.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Past, Present, And Future**: Establish broad time language and distinguish events that already happened, are happening now, or may happen later.
2. **Reading A Simple Timeline**: Use a short timeline to order events and answer before/after questions.
3. **Cause, Effect, Change, And Continuity**: Explain simple why/what-changed relationships without treating every event as a cause.
