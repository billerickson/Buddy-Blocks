# Unit 2: Maps, Globes, And Places

Track: History And Civics
Level: Level 1
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/history-and-civics-1/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn to use maps and globes as tools for understanding place. By the end of the unit, students can use directions, compass roses, symbols, map keys, and basic place categories to answer simple questions about location, region, environment, and movement.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit follows time because every later historical or civic example needs both when and where. Students need map habits early so geography can be interleaved naturally with sources, communities, economics, culture, and inquiry.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: location, direction, symbolization, map selection, region, route, movement, environment.
- Vocabulary: map, globe, place, north, south, east, west, northeast, northwest, southeast, southwest, compass rose, symbol, key, continent, ocean, country, state, city, region, route, scale, environment.
- Representations and models: compass roses, classroom maps, community maps, world maps, map keys, route maps, region labels.
- Texts and forms: brief map captions, "Which direction?" prompts, symbol-key matching, one-sentence route explanations.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need basic left/right/near/far language, simple diagram reading, Unit 1 sequence words, and exposure to maps or globes in everyday settings.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- A map shows every detail about a place.
- The key is not needed if the symbol looks familiar.
- North means "up" wherever a person is standing.
- A symbol is the real place rather than a representation.
- Cities, states, countries, continents, and oceans are interchangeable labels.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Start with directions because students need a stable compass vocabulary for all map tasks. Then teach symbols and keys because map reading depends on interpreting selected information. Finish with place categories and movement so students can connect maps to human and historical activity.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Review Unit 1 sequence language when following routes: first go north, next turn east, then reach the river. Use before/after language when comparing map routes or movements.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares map-as-source work, community maps, trade and movement scenarios, culture-place connections, symbol interpretation, and final inquiry tasks that require timeline and map evidence together.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Use a compass rose to identify directions.
- Match map symbols to their meanings using a key.
- Identify whether a label names a city, state, country, continent, ocean, or region in simple cases.
- Explain that maps show selected information.
- Use a simple route or map clue to answer a place question.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Directions And Compass Roses**: Use cardinal and intermediate directions to describe location and movement.
2. **Map Symbols And Keys**: Interpret symbols by using a map key instead of guessing from the picture alone.
3. **Places, Regions, And Movement**: Distinguish place categories and use maps to describe routes, regions, and environments.
