# Unit 3: Sources, Clues, And Evidence

Track: History And Civics
Level: Level 1
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/history-and-civics-1/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn that historians and citizens use sources as evidence. By the end of the unit, students can identify common source types, distinguish clear primary and secondary examples, notice useful details, and support a simple claim with evidence.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit follows time and maps because students now have two key tools for reading sources: when and where. It also prepares every later unit to ask for evidence instead of unsupported recall.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: source, evidence, primary/secondary source, detail, inference, claim, support.
- Vocabulary: source, primary source, secondary source, photograph, artifact, document, map, timeline, clue, evidence, claim, infer, detail, historian.
- Representations and models: image sources, artifact descriptions, short document excerpts, simple maps, timelines, claim/evidence T-charts.
- Texts and forms: short captions, one-paragraph informational texts, sentence frames such as "The source shows..." and "This supports the claim because..."

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need Unit 1 time language, Unit 2 map vocabulary, short informational reading or read-aloud comprehension, and the ability to identify details in images or text.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- A primary source always tells the full truth.
- A secondary source is useless because it was made later.
- Evidence is the same as an opinion or guess.
- Any detail can support any claim.
- An image has only one possible detail to notice.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin broadly with what counts as a source so students include images, artifacts, maps, documents, and texts. Then classify primary and secondary sources in clear cases. Finish by connecting source details to claims, which is the core habit needed for later inquiry.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Review timelines when a source has a date and maps when a source shows place. Ask students to identify when and where before they decide what evidence a source provides.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares civic evidence, economic scenario evidence, cultural descriptions, symbol/document interpretation, and the capstone claim-with-evidence sequence.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify photographs, artifacts, documents, maps, timelines, and short texts as sources.
- Classify clear primary and secondary source examples.
- Choose an observed detail rather than an unsupported guess.
- Match a claim to relevant evidence.
- Explain what a source can and cannot show.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **What Counts As A Source?**: Recognize varied source forms and the kinds of clues each can provide.
2. **Primary Or Secondary?**: Distinguish sources made during an event from sources made later about the event.
3. **Claims Need Evidence**: Use a source detail to support a simple historical or civic claim.
