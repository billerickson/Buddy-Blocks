# History And Civics 1 Research Brief

Track: History And Civics  
Level: Level 1  
Intended student profile: beginning history and civics students, likely upper-elementary learners, ready to move from personal/community knowledge into maps, timelines, sources, civic ideas, and coherent historical stories.

This brief defines outcomes and instructional priorities only. It does not define units, lessons, or questions.

## 1. End-Of-Level Outcomes

By the end of History And Civics 1, students should be able to:

1. Use basic historical time language: past, present, future, long ago, century, decade, sequence, before, after, cause, effect, change, and continuity.
2. Read and use simple timelines to order events and explain sequence.
3. Use maps, globes, cardinal/intermediate directions, symbols, map keys, scale exposure, continents, oceans, countries, states, cities, and regions at an introductory level.
4. Distinguish primary and secondary sources in simple cases and use source details to answer questions.
5. Identify basic needs, wants, goods, services, producers, consumers, trade, money, and scarcity in age-appropriate contexts.
6. Explain why communities use rules, laws, leaders, rights, responsibilities, and civic participation.
7. Recognize national, state, and local symbols and explain their civic meaning without reducing civics to symbol memorization.
8. Describe selected historical people, places, events, and cultures using accurate, respectful language.
9. Make simple claims about history or civics and support them with evidence from a short text, image, map, artifact, or timeline.
10. Build knowledge through coherent stories rather than disconnected trivia.

## 2. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students entering this level should usually be able to:

- Understand family/community past and present at an informal level.
- Read simple maps or classroom diagrams with support.
- Recognize basic U.S. civic words such as rule, law, leader, vote, flag, and community.
- Read short informational passages or listen to read-alouds.
- Answer who, what, where, and when questions.
- Sort pictures or facts into categories.

## 3. Major Concept Clusters

### Historical Thinking

Students need sequence, change over time, cause/effect, and evidence from sources. Level 1 should build habits before abstract historiography.

### Geography And Spatial Thinking

Maps and places give history a mental frame. Students should repeatedly connect events and cultures to location, environment, movement, and region.

### Civics And Community

Students should learn why people make rules, how communities solve problems, what leaders do, and how rights and responsibilities fit together.

### Economics And Daily Life

Basic economics helps students understand choices in history and civic life: needs, wants, scarcity, work, goods, services, trade, and money.

### Culture And Historical Stories

Students should encounter coherent historical and cultural stories, including everyday life, inventions, traditions, conflict at an age-appropriate level, and contributions of varied people.

### Sources, Claims, And Evidence

Students should learn that historians use sources and that claims need support. Evidence can come from images, artifacts, maps, timelines, and short texts.

## 4. Recommended Progression

1. Begin with time, sequence, timelines, and personal/community history.
2. Teach map and globe basics early so every later historical story can be located.
3. Introduce sources through photographs, artifacts, maps, simple documents, and read-aloud excerpts.
4. Teach community, rules, responsibilities, and leadership through concrete situations.
5. Add basic economics through needs/wants, goods/services, producers/consumers, and trade.
6. Move into coherent historical stories organized around people, places, events, and everyday life.
7. Interleave geography and civics inside history rather than treating them as separate trivia banks.
8. Teach simple claims with evidence: "I know this because the map/photo/text shows..."
9. Spiral key vocabulary and source skills across new places and time periods.
10. End with mixed inquiry tasks using timeline, map, source, and short explanation.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- "History is just dates." History is evidence-based stories about people, places, choices, and change.
- "A timeline is the same as a number line." Timelines show time order and intervals, but scale may vary.
- "Maps show everything exactly." Maps select and symbolize information.
- "Old means less advanced in every way." Students need respect for different technologies, needs, and contexts.
- "A primary source is always true." Sources give evidence but still need context and interpretation.
- "Rules and laws are the same in every community." Communities make rules/laws for different purposes and levels.
- "Rights mean doing anything I want." Rights connect with responsibilities and others' rights.
- "A leader is anyone famous." Leadership involves role, responsibility, and influence.
- "Culture means food and holidays only." Culture includes language, beliefs, practices, arts, work, family life, and institutions.

## 6. Developmental Constraints And Reading/Load Concerns

- Keep source texts short and scaffold unfamiliar vocabulary.
- Use maps, images, timelines, and artifacts to reduce text-only load.
- Avoid excessive dates; anchor time with sequence and broad period language first.
- Handle violence, injustice, war, and oppression accurately but age-appropriately.
- Avoid presentism: students need help understanding past choices in context while still naming harm clearly.
- Do not turn civics into partisan debate. Focus on institutions, responsibilities, evidence, and respectful participation.
- Use coherent narratives; disconnected names and dates are hard to remember and easy to misuse.
- Support claims with concrete evidence rather than long essays.

## 7. Assessment Implications

- Use map tasks for location, direction, symbol/key, and place relationships.
- Use order-items for timelines and sequence.
- Use image/source questions to ask what evidence a source provides.
- Use multiple-choice and short constructed responses for civic concepts and basic economics.
- Ask students to support a simple claim with one piece of evidence.
- Use distractors that reveal real misunderstandings: source type confusion, map symbol errors, timeline sequence errors, needs/wants confusion, rights/responsibilities confusion.
- Feedback should point to evidence: map key, timeline order, source detail, civic role, or economic choice.
- Mastery should require transfer across new sources and maps, not only recall of practiced facts.

## 8. Spiral Review Priorities

- Timeline language and sequence.
- Map/globe vocabulary and directions.
- Primary/secondary source, evidence, claim.
- Community, rule, law, leader, citizen, right, responsibility.
- Need, want, good, service, producer, consumer, trade, scarcity.
- Place, region, environment, movement.
- Cause/effect, change/continuity, compare/contrast.
- Respectful language for people, cultures, and historical contexts.

## 9. Concepts That Should Not Be Taught Yet

- Dense chronological surveys with many dates and rulers.
- Formal historiography, source criticism frameworks, or competing schools of historical interpretation.
- Partisan political platforms, current electoral strategy, or ideological debate as main content.
- Advanced constitutional law or detailed court doctrine.
- Graphic war, atrocity, or oppression content beyond age-appropriate treatment.
- Economic models involving inflation, markets, interest rates, or macroeconomics.
- Long document-based essays as the main assessment.
