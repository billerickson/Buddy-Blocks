# Spanish 4 Research Brief

Track: Spanish  
Level: Level 4  
Intended student profile: learners at the Novice High to Intermediate Low transition who can produce connected sentences and are ready for scaffolded narration, paragraph-length communication, and more flexible familiar-topic interaction.

This brief defines outcomes and instructional priorities only. It does not define units, lessons, or questions.

## 1. End-Of-Level Outcomes

By the end of Spanish 4, students should be able to communicate at an Intermediate Low level in familiar contexts: narrate simple past events with scaffolded preterite and imperfect recognition/use, discuss near future and simple future plans, use direct object pronouns in controlled contexts, make comparisons, describe chores, travel, shopping, health, celebrations, and regional culture, explain preferences with reasons, interpret short connected resources, and write short paragraphs with sentence strings.

## 2. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should control connected present-tense sentences, regular present patterns, high-frequency irregulars, question formation, adjective agreement, articles, daily routines, hobbies, community vocabulary, and short text interpretation.

## 3. Major Concept Clusters

- **Intermediate Low Communication:** sentence strings, familiar-topic conversations, short presentations, and simple paragraph writing.
- **Narration Across Time:** preterite and imperfect in highly scaffolded, meaning-first contexts; sequencing past events and describing background.
- **Plans And Preferences:** near future, simple future expressions, reasons, comparisons, and polite functional phrases.
- **Expanded Domains:** chores, travel, shopping, health, celebrations, regional culture, and community life.
- **Pronoun And Cohesion Tools:** direct object pronouns, connectors, sequence words, and paragraph organization.

## 4. Recommended Progression

Review present-tense communication and connected sentences, then introduce past-time narration through stories, photos, and timelines. Teach preterite/imperfect contrast as meaning in context before accuracy demands. Add near future and plans, then travel/shopping/health/celebration domains. Introduce direct object pronouns through repeated controlled contexts. End with short paragraph and conversation tasks that combine narration, description, and reasons.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Past tense is one Spanish form matching English past.
- Preterite and imperfect can be chosen by a single keyword rule.
- Direct object pronouns go where English puts objects.
- Future meaning requires only a future tense form.
- A paragraph is just separate translated sentences.
- Comparisons use English word order.
- Cultural celebrations are interchangeable across Spanish-speaking regions.

## 6. Developmental Constraints And Reading/Load Concerns

Keep past narration scaffolded with visuals, timelines, and sentence frames. Do not expect full spontaneous control of preterite/imperfect. Limit pronoun types so direct objects are not confused with indirect/reflexive pronouns. Keep authentic resources short and glossed. Paragraph writing should be brief, organized, and modeled.

## 7. Assessment Implications

Assess meaning and communication before full form accuracy. Use passage/listening questions for past-time meaning, multi-blank cloze for controlled forms, dialogue-builder for travel/shopping/health exchanges, constructed response for short paragraphs, and match-pairs for pronoun/reference relationships. Distractors should reveal tense-as-time-only errors, pronoun placement confusion, comparison errors, and unsupported cultural generalization.

## 8. Spiral Review Priorities

Present tense, high-frequency irregulars, ser/estar, gustar, articles/adjectives, questions, connectors, time expressions, routines, places, food, weather, directions, preterite/imperfect meaning, near future, direct objects, comparisons, and cultural geography.

## 9. Concepts That Should Not Be Taught Yet

Avoid full subjunctive system, all object pronoun combinations, por/para mastery, complex relative clauses, compound tenses beyond exposure, extended unscripted debate, and literary analysis in Spanish.
