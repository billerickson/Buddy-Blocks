# Grade 07 Math Research Brief

## Track

- Track slug: `grade-07-math`
- Level: Grade 7 mathematics
- Intended student profile: middle-grade learners extending Grade 6 ratio, rational-number, expression, equation, geometry, and statistics foundations into proportional relationships, rational-number operations, linear expressions/equations/inequalities, scale/circle/angle/solid geometry, sampling, comparative inference, and probability. Lessons should be concise but intellectually serious, with explicit instruction, worked examples, retrieval, interleaving, and misconception checks.

## 1. End-of-Level Outcomes

By the end of Grade 7 math, students should be able to:

- Analyze proportional relationships and solve real-world and mathematical problems involving unit rates, ratios of fractions, constant of proportionality, tables, graphs, equations, scale drawings, and percent problems.
- Distinguish proportional relationships from non-proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, verbal descriptions, and context.
- Apply and extend operations with fractions to add, subtract, multiply, and divide rational numbers, including integers, decimals, and fractions.
- Explain rational-number operation rules through number-line, context, and property-based reasoning, not only memorized sign rules.
- Use properties of operations to add, subtract, factor, and expand linear expressions with rational coefficients.
- Understand how rewriting an expression can reveal a contextual relationship, such as percent increase as multiplication by 1.05.
- Solve multi-step real-life and mathematical problems with positive and negative rational numbers in any form, converting between forms when useful and estimating for reasonableness.
- Write and solve equations of the forms `px + q = r` and `p(x + q) = r`; write, graph, and interpret inequalities of the forms `px + q > r` and `px + q < r`.
- Solve scale drawing problems, construct and describe geometric figures, identify possible triangle conditions, and describe plane sections of right rectangular prisms/pyramids.
- Use formulas for area/circumference of circles, angle relationships, and area/surface area/volume of composed two- and three-dimensional figures.
- Use random samples to draw informal inferences about populations; compare two numerical data distributions using center and variability.
- Develop, use, and evaluate probability models for simple and compound events using lists, tables, tree diagrams, and simulations.

## 2. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need Grade 6 foundations:

- Ratio language, equivalent ratios, unit rates, percent as rate per 100, ratio tables, double number lines, and coordinate graphs.
- Fraction division and fluent computation with nonnegative rational numbers.
- Negative numbers as locations on a number line and as quantities in context; absolute value as distance from zero.
- Writing and evaluating expressions, identifying equivalent expressions, and understanding variables.
- Solving one-step equations/inequalities and using equations to represent relationships.
- Area of triangles/quadrilaterals/polygons, surface area, and volume of right rectangular prisms with fractional side lengths.
- Statistical questions, dot plots/histograms/box plots, measures of center and variability.
- Ability to shift among tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

## 3. Major Concept Clusters

- Proportional relationships: unit rates with fractions, constant of proportionality, tables, graphs through origin, equations `y = kx`, percent, scale drawings.
- Rational-number operations: integer operations, fractions/decimals/percents as rational numbers, operation properties, estimation.
- Expressions and equations: equivalent linear expressions, factoring/expanding, multi-step equations and inequalities, contextual interpretation.
- Geometry: scale drawings, constructability of triangles, circles, angle relationships, area/surface area/volume of composite figures, cross-sections.
- Statistics and probability: random sampling, informal inference, comparing distributions, probability scale, experimental vs theoretical probability, compound events.
- Mathematical practices: proportional reasoning, structure, precision with units, model choice, reasonableness, and critique of claims from data.

## 4. Recommended Progression

1. Start with retrieval of Grade 6 ratios, fraction division, number-line rational numbers, expressions, equations, area/volume, and data displays.
2. Extend ratio reasoning to proportional relationships. Use tables, double number lines, graphs, and equations side by side before formalizing constant of proportionality.
3. Teach percent as proportional reasoning, including discounts, taxes, tips, markups, simple interest, percent increase/decrease, and finding the whole from a part and percent.
4. Teach scale drawings as a geometry application of proportionality. Include length and area scale effects explicitly.
5. Develop rational-number operations through contexts and number lines, then properties. Sequence addition/subtraction before multiplication/division, and repeatedly connect opposite, distance, and zero-pair ideas.
6. Move into expressions after rational coefficients are usable. Use area models and contextual quantities to support expanding, factoring, and combining like terms.
7. Teach equations and inequalities from contexts. Compare arithmetic and algebraic solution paths and require interpretation of the solution or solution set.
8. Teach circles, angle relationships, and composite area/surface area/volume with formula meaning and diagrams. Avoid formula memorization without unit and structure checks.
9. Teach sampling and comparative inference after students can interpret center and variability from Grade 6. Use small simulated samples and visual overlap.
10. Teach probability as a number from 0 to 1, then connect theoretical models, observed frequencies, compound events, and simulation.
11. Interleave proportionality, rational operations, equations, and geometry so students learn to choose models across contexts rather than apply the most recent procedure.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Additive reasoning in proportional contexts: adding a fixed amount instead of multiplying by a scale factor.
- Treating every relationship shown in a table or graph as proportional, including lines not through the origin or tables without a constant ratio.
- Confusing unit rate direction, such as miles per hour vs hours per mile.
- Percent base confusion: using the part as the whole, or treating percent increase/decrease as adding/subtracting percentage points without identifying the original amount.
- Assuming scale factor applies to area without squaring the length scale factor.
- Memorizing integer sign rules without understanding subtraction as adding the opposite or multiplication/division patterns.
- Treating absolute value as "make positive" rather than distance from zero.
- Combining unlike terms, such as `3x + 4 = 7x`, or distributing only to the first term.
- Solving equations by moving symbols without preserving equality.
- Reversing inequality meaning or failing to interpret a solution set in context.
- Thinking a larger circumference formula input always means area or confusing radius and diameter.
- Applying angle facts visually without identifying supplementary, complementary, vertical, or adjacent relationships.
- Believing random samples are automatically representative regardless of selection method or sample size.
- Comparing distributions only by center and ignoring variability/overlap.
- Treating probability near 1 as certain, near 0 as impossible, or expecting experimental results to match theoretical probability exactly in small samples.

## 6. Developmental Constraints and Reading/Load Concerns

- Grade 7 students can handle abstraction, but many topics require coordinating several representations. Lessons should isolate the representation shift being taught.
- Proportional reasoning is conceptually central and fragile; avoid premature shortcuts such as cross-multiplication before students understand ratios, unit rates, and constant of proportionality.
- Rational-number operations require careful language: sign, direction, distance, opposite, increase/decrease, debt/credit, and temperature should not be conflated.
- Algebraic manipulation has high symbolic load. Use worked examples, faded steps, and error analysis before independent multi-step equations.
- Statistics/probability contexts can become reading-heavy. Keep scenarios concise and focus on sample method, center/variability, or model choice.
- Use constrained explanations in the app: choose the valid conclusion, identify the error, match representation to equation, order solution steps, or select the best estimate.
- Feedback should name the violated structure: constant ratio, same whole/base, equality preservation, representative sample, or probability model.

## 7. Assessment Implications

- Assess proportional relationships across tables, graphs, equations, and contexts, including non-examples.
- Include percent problems that vary the unknown: part, whole, percent, percent change, and final amount.
- Assess rational-number operations with number-line/context items and computation items.
- Use error-analysis items for sign rules, distribution, combining like terms, and equation solving.
- Require students to interpret equation/inequality solutions in context, not just solve symbolically.
- Use geometry assessments that distinguish radius/diameter, circumference/area, length scale/area scale, and surface area/volume.
- Use data/probability items that ask what conclusion is justified, whether a sample is representative, and why observed frequencies may differ from theoretical probability.
- App-scorable formats should include multiple-choice, fill-blank, match-pairs, order-items, passage-question for short contexts, and numeric text-input. Constructed-response-like reasoning should be converted to selected explanation or ordered reasoning.

## 8. Spiral Review Priorities

- Fraction division and complex fractions as unit-rate preparation.
- Equivalent ratios, ratio tables, double number lines, and percent as rate per 100.
- Decimal/fraction/percent conversions and benchmark percents.
- Integer location, opposite, distance, and absolute value.
- Operations with rational numbers in mixed forms.
- Distributive property, combining like terms, and equivalent expressions.
- One-step equations/inequalities before multi-step work.
- Area of triangles/quadrilaterals, volume/surface area of prisms, and unit reasoning.
- Measures of center and variability from Grade 6.
- Probability language: impossible, unlikely, equally likely, likely, certain; fractions/decimals from 0 to 1.

## 9. Concepts That Should Not Be Taught Yet

- General linear functions with slope-intercept form `y = mx + b` as a Grade 8-style function unit, beyond proportional equations `y = kx` and informal slope as unit rate.
- Systems of linear equations.
- Formal function notation, nonlinear functions, exponents/radicals, scientific notation, and irrational numbers.
- Pythagorean Theorem, transformations, formal congruence/similarity proofs, and coordinate-plane distance.
- Formal statistical inference, p-values, correlation/regression, or bivariate association beyond Grade 7 informal comparisons.
- Polynomial operations, quadratic expressions, or high-school algebraic manipulation.
- Trigonometry or formal geometric proof.

## 10. Source Notes

- CCSS-M Grade 7 critical areas identify proportional relationships, rational-number operations with expressions/equations, scale/circle/solid geometry, and sampling/inference as the level core. These anchor the outcomes and progression.
- CCSS-M standards emphasize analyzing proportional relationships, using rational numbers in multiple forms, solving contextual equations/inequalities, and informal inference/probability. The assessment implications mirror those emphases.
- Common Core Progressions informed the sequence from Grade 6 ratio reasoning into Grade 7 proportionality, then toward Grade 8 linear relationships, keeping `y = kx` distinct from broader linear functions.
- IES fractions guidance shaped the warning against premature cross-multiplication and supports conceptual understanding of ratios, rates, and proportions before procedures.
- IES study guidance supports worked examples, interleaved practice, spacing, retrieval, concrete/abstract representation links, and deep explanatory questions.
- NCTM Principles to Actions shaped the focus on goals, representation connections, purposeful questions, procedural fluency from understanding, and productive error analysis.
- NCTM Catalyzing Change for middle school reinforced that Grade 7 should develop shared foundations for later algebra through proportional reasoning, expressions/equations, statistics, and modeling rather than narrow acceleration into Grade 8/high-school topics.
