# Memory Works 1 Course Map

Track: Memory Works
Level: Level 1
Sources: Accepted research brief at `research/memory-works-1/01-research-brief.md` and level blueprint at `research/memory-works-1/02-level-blueprint.md`

## Course Design Notes

Memory Works 1 begins by teaching the memory routine directly, then applies it to increasingly varied forms: sayings, poems, facts, rules, maps, timelines, and mixed review. The sequence protects students from treating memory work as raw repetition. Each unit introduces a new kind of memory target while continuing to retrieve earlier targets after short and longer delays.

## Unit 1: Memory Practice Habits

### Unit Purpose

Establish the core routine students will use across the level: understand the target, chunk it, recall it, check it, correct errors, and return to it later. Students learn that active retrieval and spaced practice are different from simply looking again.

### Student Outcomes

- Distinguish study, recognition, cued recall, and independent recall.
- Use a look/listen, chunk, cover, recall, check, correct, and space routine.
- Choose helpful cues such as first words, categories, rhythm, or sequence markers.
- Identify and practice the missed chunk instead of restarting without a plan.
- Explain why spaced review helps memory last.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Study, recognition, recall, retrieval, cue, chunk, model, cover, check, correct, error, spaced review, cumulative review.

### Prerequisites

Students should be able to repeat short phrases, read or listen to short targets, follow a simple routine, tolerate small mistakes, and compare an attempt with a model.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This opening unit provides the routine used by every later unit. Sayings, poems, fact families, rules, map lists, timeline pegs, and recitations all depend on chunking, cueing, retrieval, correction, and spacing.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Studying means only reading or listening again.
- Remembering once means the target is mastered forever.
- A mistake means the whole target must be restarted.
- The fastest recall is always the best recall.
- Cues are cheating rather than temporary supports.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Begin cumulative review immediately. Each lesson should bring back the previous routine step and add a small delay before recall. Later units should keep naming the routine until students can choose steps independently.

## Unit 2: Sayings, Meaning, And Exact Words

### Unit Purpose

Use short sayings and proverbs to show that memory work needs both exact words and meaning. Students memorize brief sayings, paraphrase them, and choose situations where they fit.

### Student Outcomes

- Memorize a short saying with exact wording.
- Explain a saying in student-friendly language.
- Match a saying to a fitting situation.
- Notice and correct near-wording errors.
- Use meaning as a cue for exact recall.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Saying, proverb, exact words, paraphrase, meaning, situation, example, non-example, near wording.

### Prerequisites

Students need the Unit 1 memory routine, basic sentence comprehension, and experience explaining a simple idea in their own words.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit applies the practice routine from Unit 1 to meaningful language. It prepares students for poem meaning in Unit 3 and for grammar, history, and civic lines that require exact wording plus understanding.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Memorized words do not need to mean anything.
- A saying can be changed freely without changing its force.
- A paraphrase should copy the exact words.
- Any situation with a similar word must fit the saying.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

3-4 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Review the Unit 1 routine while practicing sayings. Bring sayings back during later units as short fill-blank, paraphrase, and situation-match items.

## Unit 3: Poems, Rhythm, And Recitation

### Unit Purpose

Introduce poem memory and oral recitation. Students learn to use rhythm, repeated sound, line breaks, and phrase chunks to remember short poem selections and recite them with clear voice and pacing.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify rhythm, rhyme, repeated sound, or repeated words as memory supports.
- Memorize a short couplet or stanza by chunks.
- Recite with clear voice, steady pacing, and attention to meaning.
- Use a first-word or line cue without depending on it forever.
- Review a poem after a delay and repair missed words.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Poem, line, couplet, stanza, rhythm, rhyme, repeated sound, first-word cue, recitation, voice, pacing, expression.

### Prerequisites

Students need the Unit 1 routine, experience with exact wording from Unit 2, and comfort speaking short phrases aloud.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

Poem recitation extends exact wording into expressive oral performance. It prepares the final performance unit and supports later literature, rhetoric, and language learning.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Recitation means racing through memorized words.
- Rhythm is decorative rather than a memory aid.
- A missed word ruins the whole recitation.
- Expression matters only after perfect recall.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Return to sayings from Unit 2 as quick exact-word review. Space poem chunks across multiple lessons so students experience delayed recall rather than same-session echoing only.

## Unit 4: Facts, Forms, And Patterns

### Unit Purpose

Apply memory routines to compact academic targets: math fact families, measurement equivalences, grammar definitions, and language forms. Students learn that facts and rules are easier to retain when they are connected to examples and categories.

### Student Outcomes

- Recall small fact families and explain the relationship among the facts.
- Memorize high-use measure equivalences with units.
- Recall short grammar definitions and identify examples.
- Use flash cards as retrieval prompts, not as passive study cards.
- Choose a fitting cue for a fact, rule, or form.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Fact family, equivalence, measure, unit, definition, example, rule, form, category, flash card, front, back.

### Prerequisites

Students need recall and correction routines, simple arithmetic readiness, basic units such as inches and minutes, and awareness of nouns and verbs from earlier language study.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit shows how memory work supports other tracks without replacing them. It prepares the mixed recall demands in Units 5 and 6 by adding non-literary targets to the cumulative pool.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Facts are isolated one-way answers.
- Units can be dropped once the number is known.
- A definition is memorized even if no example can be identified.
- Flash cards are for flipping quickly rather than retrieving before checking.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

3-4 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Interleave sayings and poem lines with compact fact targets so students practice shifting memory methods. Review units and definitions after delay, especially where distractors can expose wrong-category errors.

## Unit 5: Maps, Timelines, And Ordered Knowledge

### Unit Purpose

Teach students to memorize categories and ordered knowledge without mixing them up. Students practice continents and oceans, compass directions, map cues, and simple history pegs as meaningful sequences.

### Student Outcomes

- Sort geography targets into correct categories.
- Recall a short ordered list with a stable cue.
- Use map direction language to support recall.
- Place simple history pegs in chronological order.
- Notice when a memory error comes from wrong order or wrong category.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Category, list, order, sequence, map, compass, north, east, south, west, continent, ocean, timeline, chronology, peg.

### Prerequisites

Students need category language, order words, the Unit 1 routine, and experience correcting a missed or swapped part.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit broadens memory work into spatial and chronological knowledge. It prepares the final unit by adding list and sequence targets to the cumulative review pool.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A list can be remembered in any order even when order carries meaning.
- Continents and oceans belong to the same category.
- Map directions are just labels rather than location cues.
- A history timeline is a random list of famous words.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

3-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Review earlier sayings, poem chunks, facts, and definitions between map and timeline items. Use mixed prompts that ask students to identify the target type before recalling it.

## Unit 6: Cumulative Recall And Confident Performance

### Unit Purpose

Bring the level together through mixed retrieval, cue fading, error repair, short recitation planning, and an end-of-level challenge. Students show that they can use the memory routine across different target types.

### Student Outcomes

- Interleave different memory targets without confusing the task.
- Move from cued recall toward independent recall.
- Prepare a short recitation with a practice plan.
- Correct errors by returning to the missed chunk or cue.
- Complete a cumulative challenge with recall, explanation, and transfer.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Interleave, mixed review, independent recall, cue fading, rehearsal, recitation plan, performance, mastery, transfer.

### Prerequisites

Students need all earlier memory routines and a cumulative pool of sayings, poem lines, facts, rules, map categories, and timeline pegs.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This capstone unit retrieves all earlier units and prepares Memory Works 2 by asking students to plan practice more independently and perform short selections with confidence.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Mixed review is unfair because it is not grouped by topic.
- A cue should remain forever if it helped once.
- Performance is only about perfection.
- Mastery means never making a mistake.
- One strong same-day recall proves long-term retention.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Every lesson in this unit should be cumulative. Use varied question types and memory targets so students practice selecting the right approach before recalling. End with delayed review evidence rather than only immediate recall.
