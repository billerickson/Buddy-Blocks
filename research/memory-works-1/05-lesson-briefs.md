# Memory Works 1 Lesson Briefs

Sources: accepted level blueprint, course map, and unit design briefs in `research/memory-works-1/`.

## Unit 1: Memory Practice Habits

### Lesson 1: Study, Recognition, And Recall

- **Teaching goal:** Help students tell the difference between looking again, recognizing an answer, and pulling an answer from memory.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify whether a task asks for study, recognition, cued recall, or independent recall.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Use a simple ladder: study means looking or listening; recognition means choosing what looks familiar; cued recall means remembering with a clue; independent recall means remembering without the model.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Students can read or listen to short phrases and choose among simple answer options.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Thinking recognition is the same as recall; thinking rereading proves memory; thinking a cue is cheating.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student classifies short practice tasks and explains why recall is stronger than simply rereading.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for classifying task types; `match-pairs` for terms to meanings; `constructed-response` for a short explanation of why recall strengthens memory.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show the phrase "North, east, south, west." Let students look at it, choose it from options, recall it with "N, E, S, W," and then recall it without looking. Name each step.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep all memory targets under one sentence; use familiar words; avoid technical neuroscience language.

### Lesson 2: Chunk The Target

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to break a memory target into meaningful chunks before practicing.
- **Student outcome:** The student can divide a short target into useful chunks and explain why the chunks help.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Chunking turns one long target into smaller practice parts, such as "Look / chunk / cover / recall / check."
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Students understand part and whole, can repeat short phrases, and know that memory targets can be practiced in pieces.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Splitting a target at random; making chunks too large; thinking chunking changes the target.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a short saying or routine, the student chooses useful chunks and recalls one chunk accurately.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for putting chunks in sequence; `fill-blank` for missing chunk words; `constructed-response` for explaining a chunk choice.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use "Look, chunk, cover, recall, check" and place each action on a separate card. Then combine the cards back into the full routine.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use targets of 5-8 words; avoid asking students to chunk long poems yet.

### Lesson 3: Cover, Recall, Check

- **Teaching goal:** Establish the habit of recalling without looking and then checking against a model.
- **Student outcome:** The student can describe and use the cover-recall-check cycle for a short target.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** The student studies the model, covers it, recalls from memory, uncovers or checks, and marks what matched.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Students can compare a recalled phrase with a model and identify same or different words.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Checking while still looking; changing the model to match the attempt; skipping the check if the attempt felt easy.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student orders the steps of the cycle and identifies whether an attempt matched the model.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for the cycle; `multiple-choice` for correct checking behavior; `text-input` for supported recall of a very short target.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Model an attempt with one missing word and think aloud: "I will not guess that it was right. I will check the model."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep the target short enough that checking can focus on method, not decoding load.

### Lesson 4: Correct The Missed Part

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to treat errors as information and practice the missed chunk directly.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a missed chunk and choose a next practice step that repairs it.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Error correction means locate the missed part, practice that part, reconnect it to the whole, and review later.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Students know the cover-recall-check routine and can compare an attempt with a model.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Starting over every time; ignoring small wording errors; practicing the easy part again while avoiding the weak part.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a model and a flawed recall attempt, the student identifies the missed part and selects a focused correction.
- **Recommended question types:** `error-correction` for fixing a recalled phrase; `multiple-choice` for choosing the best next step; `constructed-response` for a short repair plan.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Normalize errors by saying that a mistake is a map to the next practice step.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one-error examples; avoid long edited passages.

## Unit 2: Sayings, Meaning, And Exact Words

### Lesson 1: Learn A Short Saying

- **Teaching goal:** Help students memorize short sayings with exact wording.
- **Student outcome:** The student can recall and complete short sayings such as "Well begun is half done" and "Measure twice, cut once."
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A saying can be chunked into two parts, practiced from a first-word cue, and checked for exact wording.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Unit 1 chunking, cover-recall-check, and correction routines.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Replacing words with near synonyms; remembering the meaning but not the wording; treating commas and pauses as unimportant for oral memory.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student completes missing words, orders chunks, and identifies exact versus near wording.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for missing words; `order-items` for saying chunks; `text-input` for short exact recall after support.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Start with "Well begun / is half done." Say it aloud in two chunks before asking for recall.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use very short sayings; do not introduce several unfamiliar sayings at once.

### Lesson 2: Put The Meaning In Your Own Words

- **Teaching goal:** Show that meaning supports memory and that paraphrase is different from exact recall.
- **Student outcome:** The student can paraphrase a short saying without losing its main idea.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Exact recall preserves the original words; paraphrase explains the idea in new words.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Students know several short sayings and can explain a familiar sentence.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Copying the saying as a paraphrase; changing the meaning while changing the words; thinking meaning is unnecessary for memorization.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches sayings to accurate paraphrases and writes a short explanation of one saying.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for sayings to meanings; `multiple-choice` for best paraphrase; `constructed-response` for a brief student-friendly explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare exact recall of "A stitch in time saves nine" with the paraphrase "Fix a small problem early before it grows."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use everyday scenarios; avoid abstract moralizing beyond a sentence or two.

### Lesson 3: Match Sayings To Situations

- **Teaching goal:** Help students apply memorized sayings to fitting situations.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose which saying fits a short scenario and explain the clue.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Meaning helps decide when a saying applies; a shared word alone is not enough.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Students can recall and paraphrase several short sayings.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Matching by one surface word; choosing a saying because it sounds familiar; ignoring the situation's main problem.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects a fitting saying for a scenario and identifies why it fits.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for short scenarios; `multiple-choice` for saying choice; `constructed-response` for a brief reason.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Read a two-sentence situation, ask what the problem is, then choose the saying that names the lesson.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep scenarios to two sentences; use familiar school, home, and practice contexts.

## Unit 3: Poems, Rhythm, And Recitation

### Lesson 1: Use Rhythm And Repeated Sound

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to notice rhythm, rhyme, and repeated sound as memory supports.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a sound pattern or repeated word that can help recall a poem line.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Sound patterns give the ear clues about what comes next.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Students have heard rhyming lines and can repeat short phrases aloud.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Sound patterns are decoration only; a poem should be memorized word by word with no attention to sound; rhyme means every line must rhyme.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies repeated sounds or words and uses them to complete a short line.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for identifying sound clues; `fill-blank` for completing a rhyming or repeated line; `speaking-prompt` for saying a line aloud.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use short original practice lines such as "Bright birds build by the bay" to notice repeated beginning sounds.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid complex poetic terminology; focus on what students can hear.

### Lesson 2: Learn A Couplet By Chunks

- **Teaching goal:** Help students memorize a two-line poem target by phrase chunks and first-word cues.
- **Student outcome:** The student can recall the couplet "Small seeds sleep in winter ground; / Spring wakes green where roots are found" with support and then with less support.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A couplet can be learned by line, phrase, and first-word cue before full recall.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Unit 1 chunking and Unit 3 rhythm awareness.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Practicing only from the beginning; ignoring line breaks; using the first-word cue as a permanent crutch.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student orders the two lines, fills missing phrase chunks, and recites or types the couplet with light cues.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for line order; `multi-blank-cloze` for missing phrase chunks; `speaking-prompt` for oral rehearsal.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** First read the couplet aloud for meaning, then mark chunks: "Small seeds sleep / in winter ground" and "Spring wakes green / where roots are found."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one couplet only; support unfamiliar imagery with a quick meaning check.

### Lesson 3: Recite With Voice And Pacing

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to recite learned lines clearly, with pauses and attention to meaning.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose delivery choices that support a clear recitation.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Recitation is communication: voice should be clear, pace should be steady, and pauses should follow meaning.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Students have practiced a short saying or couplet and can speak one or two lines aloud.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Faster is better; expression means acting wildly; quiet mumbling counts if the words are correct.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies strong delivery choices and records or rehearses a short recitation using a checklist.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for delivery decisions; `speaking-prompt` for oral practice; `constructed-response` for a short recitation plan.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Contrast a rushed reading with a clear reading and ask which one helps the listener understand.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep performance low-stakes; assess preparation and clarity, not theatrical polish.

### Lesson 4: Review A Poem After A Delay

- **Teaching goal:** Show students how delayed review strengthens poem memory and reveals weak chunks.
- **Student outcome:** The student can review a learned couplet after a delay, identify a missed chunk, and repair it.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A delayed recall attempt is useful because it shows what still needs practice.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Students know the couplet from Lesson 2 and the correction routine from Unit 1.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Forgetting after delay means failure; reviewing means rereading the whole poem several times; one missing word ruins the full target.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student completes delayed-recall prompts and chooses a repair step for a missed word or line.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for delayed word recall; `error-correction` for fixing a flawed line; `constructed-response` for a repair plan.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Frame delayed review as a checkup: it shows what is strong and what needs one more focused practice.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Reuse the same couplet; do not add a new poem in the delayed-review lesson.

## Unit 4: Facts, Forms, And Patterns

### Lesson 1: Math Facts As Families

- **Teaching goal:** Help students memorize related math facts as connected families rather than isolated facts.
- **Student outcome:** The student can recall related multiplication and division facts for small number sets such as 3, 4, and 12.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A fact family uses the same numbers in related equations, such as 3 x 4 = 12, 4 x 3 = 12, 12 / 3 = 4, and 12 / 4 = 3.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Students know equal groups, multiplication symbols, and simple division language at a readiness level.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Memorizing each fact separately; reversing division incorrectly; treating the product as a factor.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches related facts, completes missing numbers, and explains how one fact can cue another.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for related facts; `fill-blank` for missing factors or quotients; `text-input` for supported recall.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Put the three numbers 3, 4, and 12 in a triangle and build all related equations from the same set.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use small whole-number facts; do not turn the lesson into speed drilling.

### Lesson 2: Measures Worth Knowing

- **Teaching goal:** Teach high-use measurement equivalences as exact memory targets with units.
- **Student outcome:** The student can recall measures such as 12 inches = 1 foot, 3 feet = 1 yard, 60 minutes = 1 hour, and 100 cents = 1 dollar.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** The unit is part of the target; "60 = 1" is not meaningful without minutes and hour.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Students have heard basic length, time, and money units.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Dropping units; mixing measure types; treating all equivalences as base-ten.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches measures, completes missing units or numbers, and catches wrong-unit errors.
- **Recommended question types:** `flash-card` for retrieval; `match-pairs` for equivalences; `error-correction` for wrong-unit statements.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show each equivalence as a two-sided card and require a recall attempt before turning the card over.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Limit to four equivalences; avoid conversion word problems.

### Lesson 3: Grammar Definitions With Examples

- **Teaching goal:** Connect short grammar definitions to examples so students remember both wording and meaning.
- **Student outcome:** The student can recall simple definitions for noun and verb and identify examples of each.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** "A noun names a person, place, thing, or idea" and "A verb shows action or being" become useful when paired with examples.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Students have encountered nouns and verbs in earlier language work.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Reciting a definition without recognizing examples; thinking every important word is a noun; thinking verbs only show visible action.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student completes definitions, matches examples, and explains why a word fits the definition.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for exact definition parts; `match-pairs` for term to example; `constructed-response` for a short explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Teach the definition, then immediately test it with examples such as teacher, playground, run, and is.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep grammar categories to noun and verb; avoid edge cases.

## Unit 5: Maps, Timelines, And Ordered Knowledge

### Lesson 1: Continents And Oceans As Categories

- **Teaching goal:** Help students sort geography memory targets into continent and ocean categories.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify whether a named place belongs in the continent list or ocean list and recall examples from each.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Category memory keeps unlike lists from blending together.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Students know that land and water are different map features and can sort examples by category.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Mixing continents and oceans; thinking any large place name is the same kind of target; memorizing names without a category cue.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student sorts continent and ocean names and uses the category label as a recall cue.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for names to categories; `multiple-choice` for category decisions; `constructed-response` for a recall strategy.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use two headings, "continents" and "oceans," before naming examples. The category is the first cue.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use common English names; do not require full map placement yet.

### Lesson 2: Compass Directions As Cues

- **Teaching goal:** Teach compass directions as ordered map cues.
- **Student outcome:** The student can recall north, east, south, and west in a stable clockwise order and use direction language in simple map prompts.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A compass rose gives the list a shape: north at top, east at right, south at bottom, west at left.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Students know top, right, bottom, and left and have seen simple maps.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Swapping east and west; treating compass words as random labels; using classroom left/right instead of map orientation.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student orders directions and identifies direction cues on simple prompts.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for clockwise order; `fill-blank` for missing direction; `multiple-choice` for map-direction choices.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Draw a simple compass rose and have students point through the order while saying the words.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid complex maps or diagonal directions; use only N, E, S, W.

### Lesson 3: History Pegs In Order

- **Teaching goal:** Introduce a short chronological peg sequence for history memory.
- **Student outcome:** The student can order the sequence Ancient Egypt, Ancient Greece, Ancient Rome, Middle Ages, Exploration, Early America.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A timeline peg is a memory hook that helps later history fit into order.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Students understand before and after and can order familiar events.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating timeline pegs as random facts; putting familiar terms first even if they are later; confusing ancient and early America.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student orders the pegs and identifies which peg comes before or after another.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for full sequence; `multiple-choice` for before/after checks; `fill-blank` for missing peg.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a horizontal line and place each peg from oldest to latest, emphasizing that the list is a scaffold, not a complete history course.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Do not add dates yet; use the six pegs only.

### Lesson 4: Keep Lists From Getting Mixed

- **Teaching goal:** Help students identify the type of memory target before recalling the list.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether a prompt asks for a saying, measure, grammar definition, compass direction list, geography category, or timeline sequence.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** The first step in mixed list recall is naming the kind of target.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Students have practiced the target types from Units 1-5.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Answering with a familiar list even when the category is wrong; using the same cue for every list; ignoring order when order matters.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student classifies target types, chooses a fitting cue, and corrects wrong-category answers.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for target type; `match-pairs` for target to cue; `error-correction` for wrong-category recall.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show two prompts side by side: "Name an ocean" and "Name a continent." Ask why the same answer cannot fit both.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar targets only; keep mixed sets short.

## Unit 6: Cumulative Recall And Confident Performance

### Lesson 1: Interleave Old And New Targets

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to practice mixed memory targets instead of only one kind at a time.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify target type, choose a strategy, and answer mixed recall prompts from earlier units.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Interleaving changes the target type, so students must choose the memory route before recalling.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Students know earlier sayings, poem chunks, facts, measures, definitions, geography categories, compass directions, and timeline pegs.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Mixed review is unfair; the newest target is the only one worth practicing; every prompt should use the same cue.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student answers a mixed set and explains one strategy choice.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for target to strategy; `multiple-choice` for target type; `text-input` for short mixed recall.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Present a mixed mini-deck and ask students to name the target type before answering.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep each prompt short; avoid introducing new content while teaching interleaving.

### Lesson 2: Fade Cues Toward Independent Recall

- **Teaching goal:** Help students move from strong cues to lighter cues and then independent recall.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose the next cue level for a target and attempt recall with reduced support.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Cue fading might move from full model, to first words, to first letters, to no cue.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Students have used first-word cues, categories, and line cues in earlier units.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Keeping the strongest cue forever; dropping all cues before the target is ready; believing a cue means the target is not learned.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student orders cue levels and selects an appropriate next step after a practice result.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for cue ladder; `multiple-choice` for next practice step; `constructed-response` for a cue-fading plan.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Demonstrate with "Well begun is half done": full saying, "Well ___," "W b i h d," and blank recall.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use known targets; keep metacognitive language concrete.

### Lesson 3: Plan And Practice A Short Recitation

- **Teaching goal:** Guide students to prepare a short oral recitation with a realistic practice plan.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose a target, plan chunked rehearsal, and practice with a clear-voice checklist.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A recitation plan includes target, chunks, cue, practice times, check step, and delivery goal.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Students have recited short sayings or poem lines and practiced correction after errors.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Recitation preparation means repeating the whole piece over and over; performance is only for perfect students; delivery does not need practice.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student builds a short plan and completes a speaking practice prompt.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for rehearsal steps; `constructed-response` for a plan; `speaking-prompt` for a short oral practice.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Keep the performance private or low-stakes. The point is prepared recall and clear communication.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Limit the recitation to one saying, one couplet, or another short target.

### Lesson 4: End-Of-Level Mixed Memory Challenge

- **Teaching goal:** Assess cumulative recall, explanation, correction, and transfer across Memory Works 1.
- **Student outcome:** The student can complete a mixed set and explain how to practice a new short target.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Mastery means recalling older targets, using meaning, correcting errors, and choosing a practice method for new content.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** All prior Memory Works 1 lessons.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Mastery means no mistakes; only exact-word targets count as memory work; a new target should be memorized by rereading only.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student recalls items from multiple units, explains one target's meaning, fixes an error, and chooses a practice routine for a new target.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for strategy choice; `text-input` and `fill-blank` for short recall; `constructed-response` for transfer explanation; `speaking-prompt` for optional final recitation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Present the challenge as a celebration of tools learned, not a high-stakes performance.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep the mixed challenge concise; avoid long passages or public-performance pressure.
