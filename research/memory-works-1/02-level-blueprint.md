# Memory Works 1 Level Blueprint

Track: Memory Works
Level: Level 1
Source: Accepted research brief at `research/memory-works-1/01-research-brief.md`

## 1. Level Mission

Memory Works 1 teaches beginning memory-work students how to memorize, retain, and use short meaningful material with confidence. Students learn that memory is strengthened by active retrieval, spacing, meaning, chunking, feedback, and calm repetition over time. Across the level, they practice with short sayings, poems, facts, rules, map sequences, timeline pegs, and recitation tasks, always moving from supported exposure toward accurate recall, meaningful explanation, and low-stakes performance.

## 2. Durable Student Outcomes

By the end of Memory Works 1, students should be able to:

1. Explain the difference between rereading, recognition, cued recall, and independent recall.
2. Break a short memory target into meaningful chunks and practice one chunk at a time.
3. Use cues, first words, categories, rhythm, map positions, or sequence markers to support recall.
4. Check recalled material against a model and correct the missed part instead of restarting blindly.
5. Use spaced review to strengthen a target after short and longer delays.
6. Accurately recall and explain short sayings, poem lines, facts, rules, map categories, and timeline pegs.
7. Recite a short learned selection with clear voice, steady pacing, and attention to meaning.
8. Transfer the same memory routine to new content from math, grammar, geography, history, literature, and language study.
9. Treat mistakes as useful feedback and choose a next practice step based on the error.
10. Complete a mixed cumulative recall task that includes older and newer memory targets.

## 3. Concept And Skill Progression

The level begins with the practice routine itself. Students compare passive study with active recall, learn the cycle of look or listen, chunk, cover, recall, check, correct, and space, and practice on very short targets so early success is likely. The first targets are brief enough that students can focus on method rather than volume.

Students then apply the routine to sayings and short meaningful lines. They learn that exact wording matters, but meaning helps the words stick. Practice moves from recognizing a saying, to filling a missing word, to paraphrasing meaning, to choosing a situation where the saying fits.

Poem and recitation work adds rhythm, repeated sound, pacing, and expressive delivery. Students memorize short couplets and short original practice stanzas by phrase, then recite them with attention to sense rather than speed.

The middle of the level broadens memory work into cross-curricular forms: math fact families, measures, grammar definitions, language patterns, geography categories, map directions, and timeline pegs. Students learn that some targets are lists, some are rules, some are categories, some are ordered sequences, and each form needs a fitting cue.

The level ends with interleaved and cumulative practice. Students mix memory types, fade cues, recover from errors, prepare a short recitation, and complete a final challenge that asks for delayed recall, explanation, and transfer of the memory routine.

## 4. Vocabulary, Representation, Model, Text, And Form Progression

Core memory vocabulary should begin with familiar language: practice, remember, repeat, check, mistake, clue, part, whole, and later. It should then become more precise: study, recognition, recall, retrieval, cue, chunk, model, correction, spaced review, cumulative review, recitation, pacing, expression, sequence, category, and transfer.

Representations should begin with visible chunks, covered words, first-letter cues, simple check marks, and side-by-side model and recall forms. Later lessons should add flash cards, category charts, ordered lists, map direction diagrams, simple timelines, phrase ladders, and recitation checklists.

Texts and forms should progress from one-line sayings and two-line poem targets to short rule statements, fact families, measure equivalences, geography lists, history pegs, and mixed review prompts. Written production should stay short and supported; oral rehearsal and spoken recall should be central.

## 5. Fluency Expectations

Fluency in Memory Works 1 means accurate, calm, retained recall, not speed alone. Students should become fluent with the practice routine, with short oral recitation, and with small sets of high-use facts and language patterns. A fluent student can recall a learned target after a delay, notice a missed chunk, repair it, and try again with improved accuracy.

Timed pressure should not be a primary evidence source. Brief quick-recall items are useful only after students understand the target and have practiced it with support.

## 6. Conceptual Understanding Expectations

Students should understand that memory is strengthened when the mind actively retrieves information. They should know that rereading can help with exposure, but recall practice is what shows and strengthens memory. They should understand why spacing helps: a target practiced again later has to be retrieved, not merely echoed from short-term memory.

Students should also understand that meaning supports memory. A saying, poem line, rule, fact, or sequence is easier to retain when the student can paraphrase it, connect it to a category, locate it on a map or timeline, or use it in a brief example. Memorization and understanding should be treated as partners.

## 7. Application And Transfer Expectations

Students should apply the memory routine to new short targets across subjects. In math, they might use fact families or measure equivalences. In grammar, they might recall a concise definition and identify an example. In geography and history, they might recall categories, locations, or ordered pegs. In literature and rhetoric, they might recite a line with voice and meaning.

Transfer is visible when students choose a helpful practice method without being told exactly what to do: chunking a long line, using a first-word cue, grouping a list by category, placing events in order, checking one missed word, or scheduling a later review.

## 8. Review And Spacing Plan

Review should begin in Unit 1 and continue throughout the level. New lessons should include quick retrieval of an earlier routine, target, or distinction. The first review delay may be minutes, but later lessons should revisit material after several lessons and across unit boundaries.

The review pattern should be cumulative and mixed. Sayings should return during poem work; poem chunks should return during recitation lessons; fact families, measures, grammar definitions, geography lists, map cues, and history pegs should reappear in mixed sets. Question design should make the review diagnostic by using distractors that reflect likely errors: swapped order, near wording, wrong category, weak cue choice, or confusing recognition with recall.

## 9. Mastery Evidence For The End Of The Level

End-of-level mastery should include a balanced body of evidence. A student should accurately recall several short targets after delay, explain the meaning of at least one saying or poem line, use a cue or chunking plan for a new target, correct an error by practicing the missed part, recite a short selection with clear voice and pacing, and complete a mixed cumulative set that includes facts, rules, map or timeline knowledge, and memory-process choices.

The strongest evidence is transfer: the student can say what kind of target is in front of them, choose a practice method, retrieve it with decreasing support, and use feedback to improve without treating a mistake as failure.
