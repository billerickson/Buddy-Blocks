# Classical Literature 2 Course Map

Track: Classical Literature  
Level: Level 2  
Sources: Accepted research brief at `research/classical-literature-2/01-research-brief.md` and level blueprint at `research/classical-literature-2/02-level-blueprint.md`

## Course Design Notes

This course map moves from shared reading tools into increasingly flexible literary interpretation. Students begin by refreshing genre, retelling, character, and evidence habits, then apply those tools to longer fables and folktales, adapted Greek and Roman myth cycles, heroic and journey episodes, short poems, paired versions, and drama scenes. The map is intentionally uneven: some units carry more lessons because students need repeated exposure to story sequence, motive, theme, and comparison before the final transfer work.

## Unit 1: Story Tools For Longer Tales

### Unit Purpose

Stabilize the story-reading habits students need before longer classical and traditional texts. Students refresh genre labels, retelling, event sequence, character, setting, problem, outcome, moral, theme, and short evidence. The unit extends beginning-middle-end retelling into linked episodes, cause and effect, turning points, and meaningful omissions.

### Student Outcomes

- Retell a longer adapted story by selecting key events in order.
- Explain cause and effect between story events.
- Distinguish character trait, feeling, motive, choice, and consequence.
- Tell the difference between an event and a larger lesson, moral, or theme.
- Use a short story detail as evidence in a selected or completed answer.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Retell, sequence, episode, key event, cause, effect, turning point, resolution, character, trait, feeling, motive, choice, consequence, moral, theme, evidence, because, for example.

### Prerequisites

Students should know basic story elements, beginning-middle-end order, simple genre labels, character and setting, and the idea that a story can teach a lesson.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit extends Level 1 story reading and supplies the shared language used in every later unit. Unit 2 uses these tools for fables and folktales. Units 3 and 4 use them for myth cycles and heroic choices. Units 5 and 6 reuse evidence and sequence habits in poetry, drama, comparison, and transfer.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Retelling every detail instead of selecting key events.
- Treating a theme as one event from the story.
- Calling any emotion a character trait.
- Describing what a character did but not why.
- Choosing evidence because it sounds interesting rather than because it supports the answer.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Brief genre and story-element review should continue throughout the level. Later units should repeatedly ask students to sequence events, match choices to consequences, and choose evidence for a theme or motive.

## Unit 2: Fables, Folktales, Motives, And Morals

### Unit Purpose

Use longer fables and folktales to deepen character, motive, consequence, trickster pattern, and moral reasoning. Students learn that a moral should fit the events, not simply repeat a familiar saying, and that cleverness can be admirable, risky, or harmful depending on the story.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify the main choice and consequence in a fable or folktale.
- Distinguish a stated moral from an implied lesson.
- Explain how a trickster or repeated test shapes the story.
- Choose evidence that supports a moral or character claim.
- Compare two tales using one focus, such as choice, trick, consequence, or lesson.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Fable, folktale, moral, implied lesson, trickster, clever, foolish, greedy, proud, humble, repeated test, warning, reward, consequence, evidence, comparison focus.

### Prerequisites

Students need Level 1 familiarity with short fables and folktales, basic moral language, and Unit 1 tools for sequence, motive, consequence, and evidence.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit applies Unit 1 story tools in compact traditional tales. It prepares Unit 3 by making pattern recognition visible, prepares Unit 4 by complicating hero and monster labels, and prepares Unit 6 by establishing focused comparison.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Assuming the moral is any slogan the student already agrees with.
- Thinking trickster means "good character" or "bad character" in every story.
- Confusing a punishment or reward with the story's whole lesson.
- Comparing stories by listing random details instead of using one criterion.
- Missing how repeated tests reveal a character.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

5-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Interleave quick checks on event/theme differences and trait/feeling differences from Unit 1. Keep fable and folktale genre features active later when students compare myths, legends, and adapted versions.

## Unit 3: Myth Cycles, Names, Places, And Patterns

### Unit Purpose

Introduce adapted Greek and Roman myth cycles through story-centered background knowledge. Students learn names, places, relationships, and objects only as they help explain the story. The unit emphasizes transformation, promise, forbidden choice, hospitality, fate, reward, consequence, and the idea that traditional myths often have multiple versions.

### Student Outcomes

- Track important names, places, and objects in an adapted myth.
- Explain how a promise, forbidden choice, transformation, or hospitality test drives events.
- Identify the consequence of a character's choice in a myth.
- Recognize that myths may have multiple versions while still preserving major patterns.
- Use respectful historical-distance language when discussing old stories.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Myth, Greek, Roman, Olympus, underworld, oracle, hero, god or goddess, transformation, promise, forbidden choice, hospitality, fate, reward, consequence, version, adapted retelling, historical distance.

### Prerequisites

Students need Unit 1 sequence and evidence tools, Unit 2 pattern and moral reasoning, and readiness for unfamiliar names supported by concise context.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit builds from fable and folktale pattern reading into classical myth cycles. It prepares Unit 4's heroic and journey episodes by introducing mythic obstacles, helpers, choices, and consequences. It also supplies names and motifs for later poetry, comparison, and transfer tasks.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Treating myths as lists of names to memorize.
- Assuming every myth has one fixed correct version.
- Thinking old stories are automatically models to imitate.
- Confusing a character's power with wisdom or goodness.
- Losing the event sequence because names and places feel unfamiliar.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

5-7 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use small recurring retrieval of names, places, and motif meanings. Review fable and folktale differences so students do not call every old story a myth. Continue evidence checks for motives, choices, and consequences.

## Unit 4: Quests, Tests, And Heroic Choices

### Unit Purpose

Develop students' ability to read heroic and journey episodes as structured stories with tasks, helpers, obstacles, choices, costs, and returns. The unit resists simple hero-good and monster-bad labels by asking students to notice pride, courage, cleverness, loyalty, restraint, and consequences.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify the task, obstacle, helper, choice, cost, and outcome in a quest or journey episode.
- Explain how a repeated test or turning point reveals character.
- Distinguish courage, pride, cleverness, and wisdom in a specific story context.
- Compare two heroic choices using one named criterion.
- Select evidence for a claim about a hero, helper, or opponent.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Quest, journey, task, obstacle, helper, monster, hero, cost, return, test, turning point, courage, pride, cleverness, wisdom, restraint, loyalty, evidence, consequence.

### Prerequisites

Students need myth-cycle orientation from Unit 3, sequence and cause/effect tools from Unit 1, and focused comparison habits from Unit 2.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit extends myth patterns into longer heroic episodes. It prepares Unit 6 transfer work by requiring students to identify a pattern in a new episode and compare a character's choice to a known story. It also gives poetry lessons concrete story images and voices to revisit.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Calling a character heroic because the story names the character as a hero.
- Treating strength as the same thing as wisdom.
- Ignoring the cost of a heroic choice.
- Thinking a monster or opponent has no story function.
- Comparing two heroes by appearance or fame instead of action, choice, or consequence.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Continue spaced review of myth motifs and evidence language. Mix in fable and folktale comparison prompts so students see that trick, test, reward, and consequence can appear across genres.

## Unit 5: Poetry, Sound, And Image

### Unit Purpose

Introduce short classical or traditional poems and oral passages as crafted language. Students listen for speaker, stanza, repetition, rhythm, image, simile, and personification, and they connect those features to meaning rather than treating them as decoration.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify the speaker of a short poem or oral passage.
- Recognize stanza, repetition, rhythm, and sound patterns when they guide meaning or memory.
- Explain what a vivid image helps the reader picture.
- Distinguish literal description from simile and personification in supported examples.
- Connect a poem's language feature to its mood, idea, or story memory.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Poem, speaker, stanza, line, repetition, rhythm, image, mood, simile, personification, literal, figurative, oral tradition, read aloud.

### Prerequisites

Students need basic poem familiarity, Unit 1 evidence habits, and enough story background from Units 2-4 to connect poetic images to tales and characters.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit broadens the level from plot-centered reading into literary language. It prepares Unit 6 drama and comparison work by emphasizing speaker, voice, and language choices. It also reviews motifs from earlier units through compressed poetic images.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Thinking poetry is only about rhyme.
- Treating figurative language as decorative but meaningless.
- Confusing the poet with the speaker in every poem.
- Calling any description a simile.
- Ignoring repetition or rhythm because it is not a plot event.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Revisit earlier stories through images and repeated phrases. Review character, theme, and evidence in very short passages so students practice carrying prose tools into poetry without overloading the reading task.

## Unit 6: Versions, Drama, And Transfer

### Unit Purpose

Bring the level together through paired versions, short drama scenes, and a final transfer routine. Students compare stories with one clear focus, track speaker and dialogue in brief scenes, and apply the full Level 2 toolkit to a new adapted tale or episode.

### Student Outcomes

- Compare two versions or related stories using one named focus.
- Identify narrator, speaker, dialogue, conflict, and choice in a short scene.
- Explain how a change in speaker, ending, helper, obstacle, or lesson changes a story.
- Apply motif language to a new adapted tale without forcing a label.
- Support a theme, character, or comparison claim with specific evidence.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Version, adaptation, compare, contrast, criterion, focus, narrator, speaker, dialogue, scene, conflict, stage direction, choice, transfer, evidence, theme, motif.

### Prerequisites

Students need all earlier unit tools: retelling, motive, consequence, moral/theme, genre and motif recognition, myth background, heroic pattern, poetry voice, and evidence language.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This final unit consolidates the entire level and prepares later classical literature study, where students will compare adaptations, translations, genres, sources, and allusions with more independence.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Comparing by listing every difference instead of naming a focus.
- Assuming a changed ending makes a version wrong.
- Confusing narrator, speaker, and character dialogue.
- Forcing a motif label when the story evidence does not support it.
- Giving a theme or comparison without evidence.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

5-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use mixed review of every major level habit: sequence, motive, consequence, theme, moral, genre, motif, speaker, image, comparison, and evidence. The final transfer lesson should use a new adapted story so students demonstrate flexible application rather than memory of a familiar passage.
