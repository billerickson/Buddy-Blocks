# Classical Literature 2 Lesson Briefs

Sources: accepted level blueprint, course map, and unit design briefs in `research/classical-literature-2/`.

## Unit 1: Story Tools For Longer Tales

### Lesson 1: Build A Strong Retelling

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to retell a longer tale by choosing key events and placing them in order.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify which events belong in a strong retelling and order them accurately.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A strong retelling keeps the events that explain the problem, important choices, turning point, and resolution; an event chain helps students leave out small details.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Beginning-middle-end retelling, character, setting, problem, and outcome.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Including every detail; omitting the turning point; confusing event order after a flashback-like explanation or repeated episode.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a short adapted tale, the student selects key events, rejects minor details, and orders the chosen events.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `order-items` for event sequence; `multiple-choice` for key-event selection; `passage-question` for checking retelling against a short passage.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** "A retelling is shorter than the whole story. Keep the events that explain what changed: the problem, the important choice or turning point, and the ending. Small details can be left out if the main story still makes sense."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one short passage at a time, no more than five events in an ordering task, and clear event wording.

### Lesson 2: Cause, Effect, And Turning Points

- **Teaching goal:** Help students explain how one story event leads to another and how a turning point changes the direction of the story.
- **Student outcome:** The student can match causes to effects and identify the event where the story changes.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A cause-and-effect chain shows "because this happened, that happened"; a turning point is the event that changes what becomes possible or necessary.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic event sequence and retelling from Lesson 1.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating every earlier event as the cause; confusing the most exciting event with the turning point; reading effects as random.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the cause of a consequence and names the turning point in a compact story.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `match-pairs` for cause/effect pairs; `multiple-choice` for turning-point selection; `multi-blank-cloze` for completing a because/so chain.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** "Stories are not just lists of events. One event often causes the next. A turning point is a moment that sends the story in a new direction."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar story situations and keep cause/effect wording concrete.

### Lesson 3: Trait, Feeling, Motive, Choice

- **Teaching goal:** Separate common character-reading terms so students can interpret characters precisely.
- **Student outcome:** The student can distinguish a trait, feeling, motive, choice, and consequence in a short story situation.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Character map with five labels: trait is a steady quality, feeling is a momentary emotion, motive is why the character acts, choice is what the character does, and consequence is what follows.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Character and event basics; cause/effect from Lesson 2.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Calling any emotion a trait; giving an action when asked for motive; calling a consequence a punishment even when it is neutral.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student labels character information correctly and chooses evidence for a trait or motive.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `match-pairs` for term-to-example matching; `multiple-choice` for motive or trait identification; `fill-blank` for choosing precise labels in a sentence.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** "Character words do different jobs. A trait tells what a character is often like. A feeling can change quickly. A motive tells why a character acts. A choice is the action. A consequence is what happens after."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one character at a time and avoid abstract trait labels until examples are provided.

### Lesson 4: Evidence For Themes And Morals

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to support a theme or moral with specific story evidence.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose a theme or moral that fits a story and match it with supporting evidence.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Theme-and-evidence frame: "The story teaches or shows ___ because ___."
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Moral, theme, key events, cause/effect, and character choice.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Choosing a theme from personal opinion; using an unrelated detail as evidence; confusing one event with the theme.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses a supported theme or moral and selects the event that best supports it.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `passage-question` for story-based theme checks; `multiple-choice` for best evidence; `multi-blank-cloze` for completing theme/evidence sentences.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** "A theme or moral is a larger idea from the story. It is not just one event, and it is not just what you already believe. Strong answers use evidence: a detail or event that proves the idea fits."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep themes concrete and age-appropriate; use short evidence phrases rather than long quotations.

## Unit 2: Fables, Folktales, Motives, And Morals

### Lesson 1: What Makes A Fable Work?

- **Teaching goal:** Identify fable features and connect a compact animal or human tale to its moral.
- **Student outcome:** The student can recognize fable signals and choose a moral that fits the whole story.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Fables are short tales, often with animals or simple character types, that teach a moral through a choice and consequence.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Moral, theme, character choice, and evidence from Unit 1.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Calling every animal story a fable; choosing a moral because it sounds wise; ignoring the consequence.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies fable features and selects the moral supported by the key event.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `multiple-choice` for fable signal recognition; `passage-question` for moral selection; `match-pairs` for feature-to-example matching.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** "A fable is usually short and teaches a moral. The moral must match the story's choice and consequence. Some fables use animals, but the animal is not enough by itself."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short fables with one central choice; avoid rare animal vocabulary.

### Lesson 2: Motive And Consequence In Fables

- **Teaching goal:** Read a fable through why a character acts and what follows from the choice.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a character's motive and explain how the consequence supports the moral.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Motive-choice-consequence chain for fable interpretation.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Trait, feeling, motive, choice, consequence; fable features.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating motive as the same as action; assuming the consequence is random; naming greed, pride, or fear without evidence.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches motive to choice and consequence in a fable and chooses a supported moral.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `multi-blank-cloze` for motive-choice-consequence chains; `passage-question` for consequence meaning; `multiple-choice` for diagnostic distractors.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** "In many fables, the lesson becomes clear when you ask three questions: Why did the character act? What choice did the character make? What happened because of that choice?"
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep fables to one main character or two contrasting characters.

### Lesson 3: Tricksters And Tests

- **Teaching goal:** Help students read trickster and repeated-test tales without labeling cleverness as always good or always bad.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain how a trick or repeated test reveals character and shapes the lesson.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A trickster choice map asks who tricks whom, why, what test is repeated, and what the result shows.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Motive, consequence, fable/folktale features, and theme support.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating all tricksters as heroes; treating deception as always the lesson; missing what changes across repeated tests.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the trick or test, the motive behind it, and the lesson it supports.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `match-pairs` for test/result pairs; `passage-question` for trickster motive; `order-items` for repeated-test sequence.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** "A trickster story often asks us to think carefully. Cleverness can help, harm, protect, or expose a problem. Repeated tests show what a character keeps doing and what changes."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use no more than three repeated tests; make the trick's target and result explicit.

### Lesson 4: Folktale Patterns Across Tales

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to recognize shared folktale patterns while preserving story differences.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a pattern such as repeated test, magical help, youngest child, impossible task, or reward/consequence in a tale.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Pattern card with "pattern," "story event that shows it," and "how this tale uses it."
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Folktale, repeated test, consequence, and evidence.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Assuming all tales with the same pattern teach the same lesson; flattening cultural or story differences; naming a pattern without evidence.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches a pattern to evidence and chooses how the pattern functions in that tale.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `match-pairs` for pattern-to-event matching; `multiple-choice` for pattern function; `passage-question` for evidence-based recognition.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** "Folktales often share patterns, such as repeated tests, magical help, or a reward after a hard task. A pattern helps you notice structure, but you still need the details of this tale."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid broad cultural claims; focus on the story passage in front of the student.

### Lesson 5: Compare Morals With One Focus

- **Teaching goal:** Compare two fables or folktales by one focus, especially choice, consequence, or lesson.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose a focused comparison statement and avoid unfocused lists.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** One-focus comparison frame: "Both stories show ___, but they differ in ___."
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Fable and folktale features, moral support, and pattern recognition.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Comparing every detail at once; assuming similar events create identical morals; ignoring evidence from one story.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the comparison focus and chooses a claim supported by both stories.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `passage-question` for paired text comparison; `multiple-choice` for best focused claim; `multi-blank-cloze` for completing comparison language.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** "A strong comparison uses one focus. You might compare choices, consequences, helpers, tricks, or morals. Do not list everything. Ask: what exactly am I comparing?"
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use very short paired passages or concise summaries; keep only one comparison criterion active per item.

## Unit 3: Myth Cycles, Names, Places, And Patterns

### Lesson 1: Myth Is More Than A Name List

- **Teaching goal:** Orient students to myths as stories where names, places, and objects matter because of their story roles.
- **Student outcome:** The student can match a mythic name, place, or object to what it does in the story.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Name-role-place chart: "Who or what is it? Where does it appear? What job does it have in the story?"
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Genre basics, retelling, and evidence selection.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Memorizing names without roles; confusing Greek/Roman context with any old story; losing the main plot in unfamiliar vocabulary.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student uses a brief context note to identify the role of a name, place, or object.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `match-pairs` for name-role matching; `multiple-choice` for story job; `passage-question` for context-supported identification.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** "In myths, names, places, and objects are clues for the story. You do not need a giant list. Ask what each name, place, or object does in this episode."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Limit new proper nouns and provide pronunciation-friendly wording when possible.

### Lesson 2: Promises, Warnings, And Forbidden Choices

- **Teaching goal:** Show how promises, warnings, and forbidden choices shape mythic consequences.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify the warning or promise and explain how a choice leads to the outcome.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Warning-choice-consequence chain.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Cause/effect, motive, consequence, and myth orientation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating warnings as background details; calling the consequence unfair without tracking the choice; missing the promise that sets up the conflict.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches a warning or promise to the later consequence and chooses a supported explanation.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `order-items` for chain sequence; `passage-question` for consequence interpretation; `multi-blank-cloze` for warning/choice/result language.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** "Many myths turn on a promise, warning, or forbidden choice. Track it carefully. The later consequence often makes sense only if you remember what was promised or forbidden."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep the myth excerpt focused on one warning or promise.

### Lesson 3: Transformation Stories

- **Teaching goal:** Read transformation as a meaningful story event, not just a magical surprise.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain what changes, why it changes, and what the transformation shows.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Transformation chart: before, cause, new form, consequence, meaning.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Cause/effect, motive, consequence, and theme support.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating transformation as decoration; focusing only on the new shape; ignoring what the change reveals about choice, warning, or theme.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the cause and significance of a transformation in an adapted myth.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `match-pairs` for before/after roles; `passage-question` for meaning of transformation; `multi-blank-cloze` for before-cause-after statements.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** "In a transformation story, a character or object changes form. Ask three questions: What changed? What caused the change? What does the change show about the story?"
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use age-appropriate transformations and avoid graphic myth content.

### Lesson 4: Hospitality, Helpers, And Consequences

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to recognize welcome, refusal, aid, and consequence patterns in myths.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a hospitality or helper pattern and explain its effect on the story.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Pattern map: host or stranger, welcome or refusal, help or harm, consequence.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Pattern recognition, motive, consequence, and myth context.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Thinking helpers are minor extras; missing that hospitality can be a test; treating reward or consequence as random.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies who gives or refuses help and how that choice changes the outcome.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `match-pairs` for helper-to-effect; `multiple-choice` for pattern recognition; `passage-question` for consequence support.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** "In many old stories, welcoming a stranger or helping a traveler matters. A helper, host, or refused guest can change the whole story."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep role labels clear; avoid assuming students know ancient customs beyond the passage.

### Lesson 5: Versions Of A Myth

- **Teaching goal:** Introduce version awareness by comparing two adapted myth retellings with respect.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify what stays similar and what changes between versions without calling every difference an error.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Version comparison card: shared pattern, changed detail, effect of change.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Myth orientation, one-focus comparison, and evidence.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Thinking one version must be the only correct story; comparing every difference; ignoring the shared pattern.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses a focused comparison of two versions and identifies the effect of a changed detail.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `passage-question` for paired summaries; `multiple-choice` for version statements; `multi-blank-cloze` for "same pattern, different detail" language.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** "Traditional myths are often retold in more than one version. A difference does not always mean one version is wrong. Compare one focus: what stays the same, what changes, and how the change affects the story."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short paired summaries and keep the comparison criterion explicit.

## Unit 4: Quests, Tests, And Heroic Choices

### Lesson 1: Map A Quest

- **Teaching goal:** Teach the quest pattern as a flexible story map.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify task, journey, obstacle, helper, choice, cost, and return in a quest episode.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Quest map with seven possible parts; not every quest uses every part in the same way.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Myth motifs, sequence, cause/effect, and character choice.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating quest as simply "a trip"; ignoring cost or return; forcing every quest into exactly the same parts.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student orders quest elements and matches each element to its story event.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `order-items` for quest sequence; `match-pairs` for element-to-event matching; `passage-question` for identifying the task or cost.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** "A quest is a journey with a task. Watch for the task, obstacle, helper, choice, cost, and return. The map helps you read, but the story details still matter."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one quest episode, not a full epic; limit to four or five elements per question.

### Lesson 2: Obstacles, Helpers, And Costs

- **Teaching goal:** Explain how difficulty, aid, and cost shape a heroic episode.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify the obstacle, helper, and cost and explain their story functions.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Obstacle-helper-cost chart: what blocks the hero, who or what helps, what the success costs.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Quest map and myth-cycle consequence tracking.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Thinking a helper is unimportant; treating cost as the same as failure; noticing the monster but not the challenge it creates.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches obstacle, helper, and cost to story details and explains how each affects the outcome.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `match-pairs` for role-to-detail; `passage-question` for story function; `multiple-choice` for cost versus failure distinctions.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** "A heroic story is shaped by more than winning. Obstacles make the task hard, helpers make success possible, and costs show what the victory required."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep each question focused on one role unless students are matching a small set.

### Lesson 3: Courage, Pride, Cleverness, Wisdom

- **Teaching goal:** Interpret heroic qualities in context rather than as simple labels.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose whether a story detail shows courage, pride, cleverness, wisdom, restraint, or loyalty.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Quality-and-evidence frame: the quality must fit the action and the consequence.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Trait, motive, consequence, evidence, and quest roles.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Calling all brave actions wise; calling all clever actions good; treating pride and confidence as identical.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses a quality supported by a story detail and rejects an unsupported label.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `multiple-choice` for evidence-based quality labels; `passage-question` for character interpretation; `match-pairs` for quality-to-action matching.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** "Heroic stories often mix good qualities and mistakes. Courage, pride, cleverness, wisdom, restraint, and loyalty are not the same. Use the action and consequence as evidence."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Provide the quality words with short examples before asking students to classify.

### Lesson 4: Compare Heroic Choices

- **Teaching goal:** Compare two heroic choices using one criterion such as motive, risk, help, consequence, or restraint.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose a focused comparison and support it with details from both episodes.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Two-choice comparison frame with one criterion.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Quest map, character quality evidence, and one-focus comparison.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Comparing fame or surface details; using evidence from only one story; assuming all heroic choices are equally wise.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the comparison focus and selects a claim that fits both choices.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `passage-question` for paired heroic episodes; `multiple-choice` for best comparison claim; `multi-blank-cloze` for completing a comparison sentence.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** "When you compare heroic choices, choose one focus. You might compare motive, risk, help, cost, consequence, or restraint. A strong comparison uses details from both stories."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use brief paired summaries and make the focus explicit.

## Unit 5: Poetry, Sound, And Image

### Lesson 1: Who Is Speaking?

- **Teaching goal:** Identify the speaker of a poem or oral passage using clues in the text.
- **Student outcome:** The student can distinguish speaker from poet or narrator in supported examples.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Speaker clue chart: pronouns, addressed audience, feelings, and situation.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Character, narrator, and basic poem familiarity.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Assuming the poet is always the speaker; ignoring "I" or "you" clues; treating every voice as a narrator.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses who is speaking and identifies a clue that supports the choice.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `passage-question` for speaker identification; `multiple-choice` for clue selection; `match-pairs` for pronoun or address clues.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** "The speaker is the voice we hear in a poem or passage. The speaker may not be the poet. Look for clues: I, you, what the speaker feels, and whom the speaker addresses."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use very short passages with clear speaker clues.

### Lesson 2: Repetition, Rhythm, And Memory

- **Teaching goal:** Show how repeated language and rhythm help meaning, mood, or memory.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify repeated words or rhythmic patterns and explain their effect.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Repetition-and-effect frame: repeated words can emphasize an idea, create mood, or make a line easier to remember aloud.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Poem, line, speaker, and evidence.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating repetition as accidental; thinking rhythm matters only if a poem rhymes; naming a repeated word without explaining its effect.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies repetition and chooses the best explanation of its purpose.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `multiple-choice` for effect of repetition; `passage-question` for line-based evidence; `fill-blank` for repeated phrase recognition.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** "Poems and oral passages often repeat sounds, words, or patterns. Repetition can emphasize an idea, create a mood, or help people remember the words."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep passages short enough to reread aloud mentally; avoid technical meter terms.

### Lesson 3: Images, Similes, And Personification

- **Teaching goal:** Distinguish vivid image, simile, and personification in short poetic lines.
- **Student outcome:** The student can classify a line and explain what it helps the reader picture or understand.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Three-part sort: image shows a sensory picture, simile compares using like or as, personification gives human action or feeling to a nonhuman thing.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Literal description, speaker, and evidence.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Calling any image a simile; calling any animal action personification; identifying the term but not the meaning.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student classifies a line and chooses the meaning or picture it supports.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `match-pairs` for term-to-line matching; `multiple-choice` for classification; `passage-question` for meaning of figurative language.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** "An image helps you picture or sense something. A simile compares two things using like or as. Personification gives a human action or feeling to something nonhuman."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use concrete images and familiar comparison language; avoid edge cases.

### Lesson 4: Poetry And Story Meaning

- **Teaching goal:** Connect poetic features to theme, mood, or remembered story pattern.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain how speaker, repetition, image, simile, or personification supports meaning.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Feature-to-meaning chain: feature, evidence line, effect on meaning.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Speaker, repetition, rhythm, image, simile, personification, theme, and evidence.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Listing a feature without connecting it to meaning; treating mood as the same as theme; ignoring the story pattern behind an image.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses the best explanation of how a language feature supports a poem's idea or mood.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `passage-question` for feature/effect reading; `multiple-choice` for best explanation; `multi-blank-cloze` for feature-evidence-meaning sentences.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** "A poem's features should help you understand something. Ask: what feature do I notice, what line shows it, and how does it affect the meaning or mood?"
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep poem excerpts compact; do not require long written explanation.

## Unit 6: Versions, Drama, And Transfer

### Lesson 1: Compare Versions With A Focus

- **Teaching goal:** Compare two retellings or versions by one named feature.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a shared pattern, changed detail, and effect of the change.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Version comparison frame: same pattern, changed detail, effect on character, ending, lesson, or mood.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Version awareness, focused comparison, myth and folktale patterns.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating difference as error; listing all differences; comparing without naming a focus.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses a focused comparison claim supported by both versions.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `passage-question` for paired summaries; `multiple-choice` for comparison focus; `multi-blank-cloze` for same/different/effect statements.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** "Versions of a story can share a pattern but change details. Compare one focus: a helper, ending, choice, obstacle, lesson, or mood. Then ask how the change affects the story."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep paired texts short; name the comparison focus in the prompt.

### Lesson 2: Dialogue And Conflict In A Scene

- **Teaching goal:** Read a short drama scene by tracking speakers, conflict, and choice.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify who speaks, what the conflict is, and which choice changes the scene.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Scene chart: speaker, line, conflict, choice, result.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Dialogue, character motive, choice, consequence, and speaker clues.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Reading all dialogue as the narrator; missing conflict because no one explains it directly; choosing a line without noting who said it.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects the correct speaker, conflict, and turning choice in a short scene.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `dialogue-builder` for choosing the best next line; `passage-question` for speaker/conflict questions; `order-items` for scene event sequence.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** "In a drama scene, characters speak in turns. Track who says each line, what each character wants, and which choice changes the scene."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use two or three speakers only; keep stage directions brief and clear.

### Lesson 3: Narrator, Speaker, And Character Voice

- **Teaching goal:** Distinguish narrator, poem speaker, and character dialogue across prose, poetry, and drama.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify who tells, who speaks, and how voice shapes meaning.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Voice sort: narrator tells the story, speaker is the voice in a poem, character voice appears in dialogue.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Speaker from Unit 5 and dialogue from Lesson 2.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Using narrator and speaker interchangeably; assuming every "I" is the author; ignoring how voice reveals attitude.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student classifies a short excerpt by voice and chooses a clue that proves it.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `match-pairs` for voice type to example; `passage-question` for clue-based identification; `multiple-choice` for attitude or voice effect.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** "Different forms use voice differently. A narrator tells a prose story. A speaker is the voice in a poem. A character voice appears in dialogue. Look for clues before you decide."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short excerpts and clear labels; avoid first-person passages with intentionally ambiguous authorship.

### Lesson 4: Apply Motifs To A New Tale

- **Teaching goal:** Apply motif language to a new adapted tale while rejecting unsupported labels.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a supported motif and explain which story event shows it.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Motif-evidence check: name the motif, point to the event, explain fit, reject labels that do not fit.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Quest, test, trickster, transformation, hospitality, promise, fate, reward/consequence, and evidence.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Forcing a familiar motif onto any old story; choosing a motif because of one word; ignoring counterevidence.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies one or more motifs supported by a new tale and rejects a tempting unsupported motif.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `passage-question` for motif recognition; `multiple-choice` for unsupported-label diagnosis; `match-pairs` for motif-to-event matching.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** "A motif is a pattern that appears in many stories, but the story must support the label. Ask: what event shows the motif? If no event shows it, do not force it."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar motif terms but a new story; keep the passage short enough for rereading.

### Lesson 5: Final Transfer: Retell, Theme, Compare

- **Teaching goal:** Consolidate Level 2 skills through a new adapted tale that requires retelling, theme support, and comparison.
- **Student outcome:** The student can retell a new tale, choose a supported theme, identify a pattern, and compare it to a known story focus.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Final transfer checklist: retell key events, identify choice and consequence, choose supported theme, name a motif, compare one focus.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** All Level 2 units.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Relying on memory of familiar stories instead of reading the new text; giving a theme without evidence; comparing without a focus.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student completes a sequence of app-scorable tasks that demonstrate retelling, theme support, motif recognition, and focused comparison.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `order-items` for retelling sequence; `passage-question` for theme and evidence; `multiple-choice` and `multi-blank-cloze` for comparison and transfer language.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** "In this final lesson, use your whole Level 2 toolkit. Read the new tale carefully. Retell the key events, notice the choice and consequence, choose a theme with evidence, name a supported pattern, and compare one focus."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one new adapted tale with clear episodes; break assessment into small questions rather than one long written response.
