# Unit 6: Versions, Drama, And Transfer

Track: Classical Literature  
Level: Level 2  
Stage: Unit Design Brief  
Source: `research/classical-literature-2/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students consolidate Classical Literature 2 by comparing versions, reading short drama scenes, and applying the full level toolkit to a new adapted tale. By the end of the unit, students should use one clear comparison focus, track speaker and dialogue, identify supported motifs, and answer with brief evidence.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This final unit asks for transfer rather than new isolated terms. Students have already practiced story structure, fables and folktales, myths, heroic choices, and poetic voice. Now they use those tools together in paired texts, scenes, and a new story.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: comparison criterion, version, adaptation, dialogue, scene conflict, narrator, speaker, character voice, motif transfer, evidence-supported theme.
- Vocabulary: version, adaptation, compare, contrast, criterion, focus, narrator, speaker, dialogue, scene, conflict, stage direction, choice, transfer, evidence, theme, motif.
- Representations and models: one-focus comparison frame, scene speaker chart, dialogue choice map, final transfer checklist.
- Texts and forms: paired adapted versions, short drama scenes or dialogues, and one new adapted tale or episode.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need all earlier level tools: sequence, cause and effect, motive, choice, consequence, theme, moral, genre, motif, speaker, image, comparison, and evidence.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- A different version is automatically wrong.
- Comparing means naming every detail.
- Narrator, speaker, and character dialogue are interchangeable.
- A motif label can be forced even if the story evidence is weak.
- Evidence can be skipped if the answer feels obvious.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with paired-version comparison using one focus. Then shift to drama scenes, where speaker, conflict, and choice must be tracked through dialogue. Revisit narrator, speaker, and character voice to connect poetry and drama. Then apply motif language to a new tale. End with a cumulative transfer lesson that requires retelling, theme support, and comparison.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Every lesson should retrieve earlier terms in a purposeful way. The unit should mix fable, folktale, myth, heroic, poem, and drama labels only when the prompt asks students to use the labels to make sense of a text.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This final unit prepares future classical literature study by establishing habits for adaptation comparison, version awareness, speaker tracking, evidence-based interpretation, and respectful discussion of culturally distant stories.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Compare two versions with one focus.
- Identify speaker, narrator, dialogue, conflict, and choice in a scene.
- Match character voice to evidence.
- Apply motif language to a new tale without overclaiming.
- Retell, support a theme, and compare a new tale to a known pattern.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Compare Versions With A Focus**: Compare paired retellings by one chosen feature.
2. **Dialogue And Conflict In A Scene**: Track speaker, conflict, and choice in a short drama scene.
3. **Narrator, Speaker, And Character Voice**: Distinguish who tells, who speaks, and how voice shapes meaning.
4. **Apply Motifs To A New Tale**: Identify supported motifs and reject forced labels.
5. **Final Transfer: Retell, Theme, Compare**: Retell a new adapted tale, support a theme, and compare it to a known pattern.
