# Unit 5: Poetry, Sound, And Image

Track: Classical Literature  
Level: Level 2  
Stage: Unit Design Brief  
Source: `research/classical-literature-2/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn to read short poems and oral passages as crafted language. By the end of the unit, students should identify speaker, stanza, repetition, rhythm, image, simile, and personification when these features shape meaning, memory, mood, or story connection.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

After several story-centered units, students are ready to slow down and notice language. Poetry and oral passages should not become isolated term drills; they deepen students' sense that sound, image, and voice can carry meaning just as plot events do.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: speaker, voice, repeated language, rhythm and memory, image, mood, literal and figurative meaning.
- Vocabulary: poem, speaker, stanza, line, repetition, rhythm, image, mood, simile, personification, literal, figurative, oral tradition, read aloud.
- Representations and models: speaker clue chart, repetition-and-meaning frame, image sketch note, figurative-language sort.
- Texts and forms: short adapted poems, oral passages, chants, or poem-like excerpts connected to classical and traditional story worlds.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need basic poem familiarity, Unit 1 evidence habits, and enough story knowledge from Units 2-4 to understand compressed references and images.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Poetry is only about rhyme.
- The poet and speaker are always the same.
- Repetition is accidental or only for sound.
- Any comparison is a simile even without comparison language.
- Personification means any description of an object.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with speaker because voice affects how students interpret every poem. Then study repetition and rhythm as memory and meaning tools. Move into vivid image, simile, and personification. End by connecting poetic features back to story meaning so students do not treat terms as labels detached from comprehension.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Review evidence language from Unit 1 and motifs from Units 2-4 through short lines and images. Students should continue distinguishing theme from event, but in compressed poetic contexts.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares Unit 6 drama by strengthening speaker and voice recognition. It also supports comparison of form: the same story pattern may feel different as prose, poem, or dialogue.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify speaker clues.
- Recognize stanza, line, repetition, and rhythm in short passages.
- Match images to what they help the reader picture.
- Distinguish literal description, simile, and personification.
- Connect a language feature to meaning or mood.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Who Is Speaking?**: Identify speaker and voice clues in a short poem or oral passage.
2. **Repetition, Rhythm, And Memory**: Explain how repeated sound or language supports meaning.
3. **Images, Similes, And Personification**: Distinguish vivid image, comparison, and human-like description.
4. **Poetry And Story Meaning**: Connect a poetic feature to theme, mood, or remembered story pattern.
