# Unit 2: Fables, Folktales, Motives, And Morals

Track: Classical Literature  
Level: Level 2  
Stage: Unit Design Brief  
Source: `research/classical-literature-2/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students use longer fables and folktales to interpret motives, consequences, trickster patterns, repeated tests, and morals. By the end of the unit, students should support a stated or implied lesson with story events and compare two tales with one clear focus.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

Fables and folktales are compact enough for early Level 2 interpretation, but rich enough to require more than basic plot recall. They give students a manageable bridge from Unit 1 tools to the longer myth and hero episodes that follow.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: genre purpose, stated moral, implied lesson, trickster behavior, repeated tests, motive-consequence reasoning, focused comparison.
- Vocabulary: fable, folktale, moral, implied lesson, trickster, clever, foolish, greedy, proud, humble, repeated test, warning, reward, consequence, comparison focus.
- Representations and models: moral support chart, trickster choice map, repeated-test ladder, one-focus comparison frame.
- Texts and forms: adapted fables, folktales, and paired short tales with shared choices or lessons.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need Unit 1 skills for event sequence, motive, consequence, theme, moral, and evidence, plus Level 1 familiarity with fables and folktales.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- The moral is any familiar saying.
- A trickster is always the hero or always the villain.
- A reward or punishment automatically states the whole lesson.
- Repeated events are just extra details.
- Comparing means listing every same and different detail.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Start by distinguishing fable and folktale purposes. Then study motive and consequence in fables, where the lesson is often compact. Move into trickster and repeated-test tales, where moral judgment is more layered. End with comparison so students practice using one focus before the myth and version work becomes more complex.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Each lesson should retrieve Unit 1 terms such as key event, motive, choice, consequence, moral, theme, and evidence. Retelling should be used only as much as needed to support interpretation.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares students to recognize patterns in myths, interpret choices in heroic episodes, compare versions, and avoid unsupported theme claims in the final transfer unit.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify genre signals for fable and folktale.
- Match a character's motive to a consequence.
- Select a supported moral or implied lesson.
- Explain the function of a trickster or repeated test.
- Compare two tales using one named criterion.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **What Makes A Fable Work?**: Identify fable signals and connect a compact story to a moral.
2. **Motive And Consequence In Fables**: Explain why a character acts and what follows from the choice.
3. **Tricksters And Tests**: Read cleverness, deception, and repeated tests with care.
4. **Folktale Patterns Across Tales**: Recognize shared folktale patterns without flattening story differences.
5. **Compare Morals With One Focus**: Compare two tales by choice, consequence, or lesson.
