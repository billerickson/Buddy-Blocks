# Unit 3: Myth Cycles, Names, Places, And Patterns

Track: Classical Literature  
Level: Level 2  
Stage: Unit Design Brief  
Source: `research/classical-literature-2/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students enter adapted Greek and Roman myth cycles with enough background knowledge to understand stories without treating mythology as a memorization list. By the end of the unit, students should track essential names, places, objects, choices, patterns, and consequences in story context.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

Students have already practiced sequence, motive, consequence, moral, and pattern reading in shorter forms. Myth cycles now add unfamiliar names, places, versions, and cultural distance, so the unit deliberately teaches orientation and restraint before heroic episodes become longer.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: story-centered background knowledge, myth cycle, classical setting, transformation, promise, warning, forbidden choice, hospitality, fate, consequence, multiple versions.
- Vocabulary: myth, Greek, Roman, Olympus, underworld, oracle, hero, god or goddess, transformation, promise, warning, forbidden choice, hospitality, fate, version, adaptation, historical distance.
- Representations and models: name-role-place chart, motif map, choice-and-consequence chain, version comparison card.
- Texts and forms: adapted Greek and Roman myths and short paired retellings or summaries.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need Unit 1 story tools, Unit 2 pattern and moral reasoning, and tolerance for unfamiliar names with support.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Myths are mainly lists of names.
- A powerful character must be wise or good.
- A myth has exactly one correct version.
- Old stories should be judged only by modern approval or rejected entirely.
- A forbidden choice is random unless the warning is tracked.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Open with the idea that myth background serves story understanding. Then focus on promises, warnings, and forbidden choices because these drive many mythic consequences. Move to transformation stories, then hospitality and helper patterns. End with versions so students learn to compare variations without calling one automatically wrong.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Review Unit 1 event chains and Unit 2 lesson support as students track myth consequences. Keep fable, folktale, and myth genre differences active in short mixed checks.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares Unit 4 quests and heroic episodes by introducing mythic patterns, obstacles, helpers, choices, and costs. It also supplies names and motifs for poetry, drama, version comparison, and final transfer.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify the role of a name, place, or object in a myth.
- Match a mythic pattern to the story event that shows it.
- Explain how a warning, promise, forbidden choice, or hospitality test affects the plot.
- Recognize multiple-version language.
- Choose respectful context statements about old stories.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Myth Is More Than A Name List**: Use names, places, and objects only as story clues.
2. **Promises, Warnings, And Forbidden Choices**: Track how a warning or promise shapes consequences.
3. **Transformation Stories**: Explain how a change of form affects character, plot, or theme.
4. **Hospitality, Helpers, And Consequences**: Notice welcome, aid, refusal, and reward or consequence patterns.
5. **Versions Of A Myth**: Compare two adapted versions without treating difference as error.
