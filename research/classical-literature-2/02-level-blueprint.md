# Classical Literature 2 Level Blueprint

Track: Classical Literature  
Level: Level 2  
Source: Accepted research brief at `research/classical-literature-2/01-research-brief.md`

## 1. Level Mission

Classical Literature 2 helps students move from short, single-episode stories into longer classical and traditional works that require sequence tracking, motive reading, theme support, and comparison. Students meet adapted myths, legends, fables, folktales, epic episodes, poems, and short dialogue scenes as literary works, not as trivia lists. By the end of the level, students should be able to retell a linked episode accurately, explain a character's choice and consequence with story evidence, identify a theme or moral that actually fits the text, recognize recurring story patterns, and compare two stories or versions using one clear criterion.

## 2. Durable Student Outcomes

By the end of Classical Literature 2, students should be able to:

1. Retell longer adapted classical and traditional stories in accurate sequence, including key events, turning points, and meaningful omissions.
2. Explain character traits, motives, choices, consequences, and changes using short evidence from the story.
3. Distinguish an event from a moral, theme, or central wisdom statement, and support the larger idea with specific details.
4. Recognize recurring story patterns such as quest, journey, test, trickster, transformation, forbidden choice, hospitality, promise, fate, and reward or consequence.
5. Distinguish fable, myth, legend, folktale, epic episode, poem, and drama scene by purpose, structure, and literary signals.
6. Compare two stories, versions, characters, choices, endings, or lessons using one named focus.
7. Identify narrator, speaker, dialogue, stanza, repetition, rhythm, image, simile, and personification when those features affect meaning.
8. Use a growing bank of classical names, places, objects, and story patterns as background knowledge for later literature and allusion study.
9. Discuss old and culturally important stories with accuracy, humility, and awareness that age does not make every action admirable.
10. Answer compact comprehension and interpretation questions with evidence language such as "the story shows," "because," and "for example."

## 3. Concept And Skill Progression

The level begins by stabilizing Level 1 knowledge: fable, myth, folktale, legend, poem, character, setting, moral, and beginning-middle-end retelling. Early lessons ask students to notice story jobs: who wants what, what gets in the way, what choice is made, and what follows. Retelling quickly expands from three-part sequence into linked episodes with repeated tests, cause and effect, turning points, and resolutions.

Students then deepen fable and folktale reading. They learn that a moral or lesson is not just an opinion attached to a story; it must fit the events. Character work becomes more precise as students separate traits from passing feelings, motives from actions, and consequences from punishments or rewards that merely happen later.

The middle of the level moves into adapted Greek and Roman myth cycles. Students track names, relationships, places, and objects only as needed to understand a story. They learn to identify mythic patterns such as transformation, promise, forbidden choice, hospitality, fate, and reward or consequence. This prepares students to read heroic and journey episodes as structured literary events, not merely as adventures.

After story structure and motif knowledge are active, students meet quest and hero episodes. The focus is on task, obstacle, helper, choice, cost, pride, courage, cleverness, and limits. Students should recognize that heroes and monsters are not always simple good-versus-bad labels; classical stories often ask readers to think about wisdom, restraint, loyalty, and consequence.

The level then broadens into poetry, oral language, comparison, and drama. Students listen for repetition, rhythm, stanza, speaker, vivid image, simile, and personification when these features shape meaning. Comparison work is deliberately focused: one shared pattern, one character choice, one ending, one lesson, or one version difference at a time. Short dialogue scenes prepare students to track speaker, conflict, and decision.

The level ends with transfer. Students meet a new adapted tale or episode and show that they can retell it, identify a meaningful pattern, support a theme with evidence, compare it to a known story, and use respectful context language.

## 4. Vocabulary, Representation, Model, Text, And Form Progression

Early vocabulary should refresh story basics: fable, myth, folktale, legend, poem, character, setting, problem, event, lesson, moral, beginning, middle, end, retell, compare, and evidence.

Story-structure vocabulary should then expand to sequence, cause, effect, turning point, resolution, episode, repeated test, motive, trait, choice, consequence, reward, punishment, and theme. Representations should include event chains, cause-and-effect chains, character choice maps, and simple evidence frames.

Genre and pattern vocabulary should grow through story use: epic episode, drama scene, narrator, speaker, dialogue, stanza, repetition, rhythm, image, simile, personification, quest, journey, test, trickster, transformation, forbidden choice, hospitality, promise, fate, helper, obstacle, and return. These terms should appear in service of reading, not as isolated memorization.

Classical background knowledge should be built through compact story context: Greek, Roman, Olympus, underworld, oracle, hero, monster, labyrinth, golden apple, Trojan, Aesop, and selected names, places, and objects from taught stories. Students should not be asked to memorize long pantheons or genealogies.

Text forms should move from short adapted fables and folktales to linked myths, heroic episodes, short poems or oral passages, paired versions, and short dramatic dialogues. Response forms should begin with selected explanations and matching tasks, then move toward compact text-input answers, sequence ordering, comparison choices, and evidence-based selected responses.

## 5. Fluency Expectations

Fluency in this level means literary fluency rather than speed. Students should become increasingly fluent at retelling in order, naming the genre or form, identifying a character's motive and consequence, distinguishing theme from event, recognizing common motifs, and using evidence language in short answers.

Students should also develop retrieval fluency for recurring classical names, places, story objects, and literary terms introduced through stories. This practice should be spaced and mixed, but it should remain attached to meaning. A student should not be considered fluent simply because the student can match names from a list without knowing their role in a story.

## 6. Conceptual Understanding Expectations

Students should understand that stories have structure: events happen in order, later events often depend on earlier choices, repeated tests reveal character, and endings resolve or complicate the central problem. They should understand that retelling is selective: a strong retelling keeps events that explain what changed.

Students should understand that character interpretation requires evidence. A trait is more stable than a feeling, a motive explains why a character acts, and a consequence follows from a choice or event. They should also understand that older stories may invite admiration, warning, wonder, or critique; reading respectfully does not require approving every action.

Students should understand that themes and morals are larger ideas supported by the story. A fable may state its moral, while a myth, legend, folktale, poem, or drama scene may imply a broader lesson. Students should reject themes that are too narrow, unsupported, or merely personal preferences.

Students should understand that genre and motif labels help readers notice patterns, but they do not replace reading the details. A quest, transformation, trickster event, or hospitality test can appear in different cultures, forms, and versions with different meanings.

## 7. Application And Transfer Expectations

Students should apply their learning to unfamiliar but age-appropriate adapted stories, excerpts, poems, and dialogues. Transfer is visible when a student can read or listen to a new story, track the event sequence, identify the important choice, explain a motive or consequence, choose a supported theme, and connect the story to a known pattern without being told which label to use.

Students should also transfer comparison habits. Given two related stories or versions, they should choose one focus, such as helper, obstacle, ending, character choice, lesson, or pattern, and make a precise same/different claim. They should learn to avoid unfocused lists of details.

Application should include compact evidence use. Students can choose the best evidence, complete a short evidence sentence, identify the event that supports a theme, or select the comparison statement that fits both texts. Long essays, formal literary argument, and unsupported personal reactions are not mastery targets for this level.

## 8. Review And Spacing Plan

Review should be cumulative and light, with delayed retrieval of the terms and habits students need for later lessons. Fable, myth, folktale, legend, poem, and drama scene should reappear after their first unit in mixed genre checks. Retelling, event sequence, motive, consequence, theme, moral, and evidence should remain active across every story unit.

Motifs should be spaced across stories rather than taught as one detached list. Quest, test, trickster, transformation, hospitality, promise, fate, and reward or consequence should be revisited in new contexts so students learn to recognize patterns flexibly.

Poetry and form vocabulary should be reviewed after students return to prose, especially speaker, stanza, repetition, image, simile, and personification. Comparison should be practiced in small doses throughout the level, beginning with single-criterion comparisons and ending with paired-version transfer.

Feedback should name the reading move behind the answer: event order, evidence, motive, consequence, genre signal, speaker, image, motif, or comparison focus. Distractors should diagnose real misunderstandings such as confusing theme with event, motive with action, feeling with trait, version with genre, or any old story with a myth.

## 9. Mastery Evidence For The End Of The Level

End-of-level mastery should be evidenced by a balanced body of compact tasks. A student who has mastered Classical Literature 2 can retell an unfamiliar adapted story in order, choose or complete a cause-and-effect chain, explain a character's motive and consequence, identify a supported theme or moral, and point to a detail or event that supports it.

The student can distinguish major forms and genres, recognize recurring classical and traditional motifs, identify the speaker or narrator when it matters, and explain how repetition, image, simile, or personification contributes to meaning in a short poem or passage.

The strongest mastery evidence is transfer: the student reads a new adapted tale or paired excerpt, selects a meaningful comparison focus, avoids unsupported claims, applies known pattern language carefully, and answers with brief, specific evidence.
