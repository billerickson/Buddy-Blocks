# Spanish 1 Level Blueprint

Track: Spanish  
Level: Level 1  
Source: Accepted research brief at `research/spanish-1/01-research-brief.md`

## 1. Level Mission

Spanish 1 gives beginning learners a usable novice foundation: they learn to understand and produce short, familiar Spanish messages about themselves, people they know, classroom life, dates, weather, likes, food, places, and simple routines. The level treats grammar as meaning support rather than as an abstract survey, so students meet forms through greetings, questions, dialogues, short readings, and supported speaking or writing. By the end of the level, students should be able to interpret predictable Spanish, participate in brief rehearsed exchanges, present a few connected sentences about familiar topics, notice core Spanish sound-spelling patterns, and use feedback, cognates, visuals, and context to keep learning.

## 2. Durable Student Outcomes

By the end of Spanish 1, students should be able to:

1. Greet people, say goodbye, use polite expressions, and respond to common classroom directions or survival phrases.
2. Introduce themselves and exchange basic information such as name, age, origin, grade, feelings, and where they are.
3. Ask and answer memorized or recombined questions in familiar contexts using high-frequency question words.
4. Interpret short written or spoken Spanish made from familiar words, cognates, visuals, and predictable sentence patterns.
5. Produce short presentational statements about self, family, school, weather, calendar, likes, foods, places, and simple routines.
6. Recognize and use key Spanish sound-spelling patterns, including clear vowels, silent `h`, `ñ`, `ll/y`, `j`, exposed `r/rr`, accent marks, and inverted punctuation.
7. Use high-frequency nouns with articles, singular/plural forms, basic adjective agreement, common prepositions, numbers, dates, time phrases, and simple connectors.
8. Use a small set of high-frequency present-tense verbs and formulas in limited contexts: `ser`, `estar`, `tener`, `hay`, `me gusta/me gustan`, `ir a`, `querer`, and common regular present-tense chunks.
9. Notice cultural products and practices from Spanish-speaking communities and make simple, respectful comparisons.
10. Build novice language-learning habits: listen or read for gist first, use cognates carefully, retrieve language cumulatively, and revise based on feedback.

## 3. Concept And Skill Progression

The level begins with communication routines that make Spanish immediately useful: greetings, names, farewells, politeness formulas, classroom phrases, and simple exchange patterns. Early work should give students repeated input before production, with short dialogues, response choice, and oral rehearsal. Pronunciation and orthography are introduced through these familiar words so students hear and see Spanish as a sound-spelling system rather than a list of translations.

Students then build high-reuse language for numbers, age, dates, time, weather, and common descriptions. These domains support frequent retrieval and let students answer many everyday questions with controlled language. Form work remains limited and purposeful: `tener` supports age and possession, `hace` supports weather chunks, and calendar language supports dates and birthdays.

The middle of the level develops nouns, articles, plurals, colors, adjective agreement, `hay`, and simple location language through classroom and object contexts. Students learn that Spanish nouns carry grammatical gender and number, that articles are meaningful clues, and that many adjectives follow and agree with nouns. These ideas prepare students to describe people and family members without overloading them with full grammar charts.

Students next use `ser`, `estar`, `tener`, subject pronouns, family vocabulary, origin language, and descriptive adjectives to communicate identity and relationships. They learn limited contrasts such as `soy` for identity/origin and `estoy` for condition/location, but exhaustive `ser`/`estar` rule charts are reserved for later levels.

The level then expands into likes, activities, school life, food, places, and simple routines. `Me gusta/me gustan` is taught as a useful chunk before full indirect-object grammar. Common verbs appear as formulaic or highly regular present-tense patterns only when students need them for familiar messages, such as `estudio`, `hablo`, `voy a`, `quiero`, and routine verbs.

The level ends with integrated novice tasks. Students interpret short profiles, menus, school schedules, maps, and dialogues; build brief exchanges; and produce a compact self-presentation or day plan using learned language. Review tasks should require students to choose a fitting form, question word, article, or response based on meaning rather than translate word by word.

## 4. Vocabulary, Representation, Model, Text, And Form Progression

Vocabulary begins with high-frequency social and classroom language: `hola`, `buenos dias`, `adios`, `por favor`, `gracias`, `¿Como te llamas?`, `Me llamo...`, `escucha`, `lee`, `repite`, and `no entiendo`. Pronunciation work uses familiar words, cognates, names, maps, and brief classroom commands.

Numbers and calendar language progress from 0-31, age, dates, days, months, seasons, birthdays, weather chunks, and basic time expressions. Students should repeatedly connect numbers to age, dates, prices, class times, and quantities rather than treat counting as an isolated list.

Concrete vocabulary then expands through classroom objects, colors, sizes, and locations. Representations should include labeled scenes, simple maps, object descriptions, `hay` statements, and short item inventories. Article and adjective patterns should be taught with clear examples such as `el libro rojo`, `la mesa roja`, `los lapices`, and `las sillas`.

Identity vocabulary includes subject pronouns, `ser` forms for identity, origin, and traits; `estar` for feelings and location; `tener` for age, family, and possession; family nouns; descriptive adjectives; and nationality/origin phrases. Texts should be short profiles and paired comparisons, not long readings.

Later vocabulary covers activities, school subjects, schedules, foods, drinks, places, transportation-light phrases, and daily routine chunks. Forms include `me gusta/me gustan`, `no me gusta`, `quiero`, `voy a`, selected regular present-tense first- and third-person forms, question words, negation with `no`, connectors such as `y`, `pero`, `tambien`, and brief presentational sentence frames.

## 5. Fluency Expectations

Fluency in Spanish 1 means accurate, comprehensible use of familiar language with growing independence, not speed or native-like spontaneity. Students should retrieve common greetings, classroom phrases, numbers 0-31, days, months, weather expressions, articles for taught nouns, key question words, and high-frequency chunks with increasing reliability after spaced practice.

Students should be able to respond to familiar questions without rebuilding every sentence from English: `Me llamo...`, `Tengo... años`, `Soy de...`, `Estoy...`, `Hay...`, `Me gusta...`, `Voy a...`, and `Quiero...`. Typed production should remain brief and supported; oral production should emphasize rehearsal, intelligibility, and confidence with familiar phrases.

## 6. Conceptual Understanding Expectations

Students should understand that Spanish is not English with swapped words. Word order, articles, adjective placement, punctuation, pronunciation, and politeness patterns can differ while still communicating familiar meanings. They should understand that grammatical gender belongs to nouns, that articles and endings signal gender and number, and that adjective agreement helps words fit together.

Students should understand that common verbs and formulas carry meaning in context. `Ser` can identify, describe, and state origin in the limited contexts taught; `estar` can express condition or location; `tener` can express age and possession; `hay` says what exists; `me gusta` expresses liking as a chunk; and `ir a` points to a place or near-future plan in simple contexts. They should also understand that cognates are useful clues but not guarantees.

## 7. Application And Transfer Expectations

Students should transfer learned language into new but familiar contexts: a new class schedule, a short profile, a classroom scene, a weather report, a menu, a family description, or a map of places. They should choose a greeting that fits the time of day, a question word that fits the answer, an article that fits a familiar noun, a verb chunk that matches the message, and a response that continues a dialogue.

Transfer is visible when students can interpret gist from partial knowledge, correct a common beginner error, answer a personalized question with a learned frame, and combine two or three familiar sentences for a brief presentational task. Cultural transfer should be respectful and concrete: students compare practices such as greetings, school schedules, foods, and naming conventions without treating one country or practice as representing all Spanish speakers.

## 8. Review And Spacing Plan

Review should be cumulative from the first unit. Greetings, polite phrases, classroom commands, numbers, dates, weather, question words, articles, adjective agreement, and core verb chunks should recur in later contexts after short and longer delays. New units should begin with quick retrieval of the language needed for the new communicative function, then revisit older language through mixed dialogues, short readings, and production prompts.

Vocabulary should be spaced by meaning domain and by communicative use. For example, numbers return in age, dates, time, prices, quantities, and schedules; colors return in objects, clothing, food, and descriptions; `tener` returns in age, family, possession, and needs; and question words return in every dialogue. Review should be diagnostic, using plausible beginner errors such as wrong article, wrong question word, English word order, false cognate, singular/plural mismatch, or `ser`/`estar` confusion.

## 9. Mastery Evidence For The End Of The Level

End-of-level mastery should be shown through a balanced body of novice tasks. A student can interpret short familiar dialogues, profiles, schedules, menus, classroom scenes, and weather or calendar statements. The student can participate in brief exchanges by choosing or producing fitting replies, ask a small set of memorized questions, and answer with short sentences or chunks.

The student can present a compact description of self, family, school life, likes, food preferences, places, and a simple routine using familiar vocabulary and sentence frames. The student can recognize and correct common beginner errors involving articles, adjective agreement, word order, question words, `tener` for age, `hay`, `me gusta`, and limited `ser`/`estar` contrasts. The strongest evidence includes transfer across modes: reading or hearing a familiar pattern, selecting the meaning clue, and then using a related pattern in a short written or spoken response.
