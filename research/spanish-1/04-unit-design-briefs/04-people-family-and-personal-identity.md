# Unit 4: People, Family, And Personal Identity

Track: Spanish  
Level: Level 1  
Stage: Unit Design Brief  
Source: `research/spanish-1/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students describe themselves and familiar people with limited but useful identity language. They use `ser`, `estar`, `tener`, subject pronouns, family vocabulary, possessive chunks, origin phrases, and descriptive adjectives to understand and produce short personal profiles.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit follows classroom-object description because students can transfer article, plural, and adjective-agreement ideas from concrete objects to people. It also gives students the personal language they need before discussing likes, school life, routines, and capstone presentations.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: identity, origin, traits, feelings, location, family relationships, possession, descriptive agreement.
- Vocabulary: subject pronouns, family nouns, origin phrases, common countries or places, feelings, descriptive adjectives.
- Forms: `yo soy`, `tu eres`, `el/ella es`, `estoy`, `esta`, `tengo`, `tiene`, `mi`, `tu`, `de`, `Soy de...`.
- Representations and texts: family trees, short profiles, ID cards, paired comparison sentences, profile-completion frames.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need greetings, names, age with `tener`, numbers, articles, singular/plural awareness, color and adjective agreement, and simple question-response routines.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- `Ser` and `estar` can be swapped whenever English says "is."
- Subject pronouns are required in every Spanish sentence.
- `Tener` is only for possession and cannot express age.
- Family nouns do not need articles or agreement cues.
- Adjectives for people work exactly like English adjectives.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with `soy`, `eres`, and `es` for identity because students already know names and basic introductions. Then contrast limited `estar` uses for feelings and location. Family and `tener` follow because they reuse age and introduce possession. Descriptive adjectives extend Unit 3 agreement. The final profile lesson combines identity, family, origin, and description in short texts.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

This unit reviews greetings, name questions, age, numbers, articles, adjective agreement, dates, and basic classroom response routines. Students continue to use polite dialogue openings and short question-answer exchanges.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

Identity and family language prepares students to express likes, school-life opinions, food preferences, places, routines, and a final self-presentation. The limited `ser`/`estar` contrast remains a spiral review priority.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Choose `ser` forms for identity, origin, and traits in taught contexts.
- Choose `estar` forms for feelings and location in taught contexts.
- Use `tener` for age, family, and possession.
- Interpret family-tree and profile information.
- Make descriptive adjectives agree with familiar people.
- Produce or complete a short personal profile.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Soy, Eres, Es For Identity**: Use `ser` chunks to identify self and others.
2. **Feelings And Location With Estar**: Use limited `estar` chunks for how someone feels and where someone is.
3. **Family And Tener**: Describe family relationships, age, and possession with `tener`, `mi`, and `tu`.
4. **Describing People With Agreement**: Use familiar adjectives to describe people accurately.
5. **Short Profiles About People**: Interpret and produce compact profiles about self, family, and familiar people.
