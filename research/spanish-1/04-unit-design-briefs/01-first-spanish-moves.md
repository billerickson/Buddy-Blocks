# Unit 1: First Spanish Moves

Track: Spanish  
Level: Level 1  
Stage: Unit Design Brief  
Source: `research/spanish-1/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students begin using Spanish for real novice purposes: greeting, saying goodbye, being polite, asking and answering names, responding to classroom phrases, noticing Spanish sound-spelling patterns, and using cognates with care. By the end of the unit, students can build and interpret a short introductory exchange.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit comes first because new language learners need immediate success, clear routines, and confidence with partial understanding before they can manage vocabulary domains or grammar patterns. It also establishes the product habit of exposure before unsupported recall.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: greeting by context, polite exchange, classroom response, pronunciation as a sound-spelling system, cognate strategy, formulaic chunks.
- Vocabulary: `hola`, `buenos dias`, `buenas tardes`, `buenas noches`, `adios`, `hasta luego`, `por favor`, `gracias`, `de nada`, `perdon`, `¿Como te llamas?`, `Me llamo...`, `¿Como estas?`, `Estoy...`, classroom commands.
- Forms: memorized chunks, question-answer pairs, short dialogue turns, response choices.
- Representations and texts: mini dialogues, classroom direction cards, name tags, cognate lists in context, pronunciation examples.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need no prior Spanish. They should be able to follow simple English prompts, repeat short phrases, compare sounds, and recognize that a new language may organize words and politeness differently.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Spanish words should be pronounced as if they were English.
- Cognates are always exact translations.
- Polite phrases are optional decorations rather than communication tools.
- `Me llamo` should be translated word by word.
- A question mark at the beginning is unnecessary because English does not use one.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

The sequence begins with greetings and politeness because these carry immediate interpersonal purpose. It then adds names and well-being so students can complete a simple introduction. Classroom survival phrases come next because they support all future lessons. Sound-spelling work follows after students have familiar words to pronounce. The unit ends with cognates and mini dialogues so students combine meaning-making and exchange skills.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

This is the first unit, so review comes from everyday communication experience: greetings, names, courtesy, classroom routines, and English-Spanish word similarity. The unit should surface what students already know informally without assuming formal Spanish study.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

The unit prepares every later unit by establishing greetings, question-response routines, classroom commands, pronunciation expectations, cognate strategy, and the idea that Spanish chunks can communicate meaning before students analyze every word.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Choose a greeting or farewell that fits a simple context.
- Match polite and classroom phrases to meanings or actions.
- Ask and answer name and well-being questions with rehearsed chunks.
- Recognize sound-spelling clues in familiar words.
- Use cognates and context to infer meaning without overgeneralizing.
- Build a short introductory dialogue in a logical order.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Greetings And Polite Openings**: Use greetings, farewells, and courtesy phrases in fitting social contexts.
2. **Names And Simple Introductions**: Ask and answer name and well-being questions with memorized chunks.
3. **Classroom Survival Phrases**: Recognize and respond to common classroom commands and help phrases.
4. **Spanish Sounds And Written Clues**: Notice vowels, silent `h`, `ñ`, `j`, `ll/y`, accent marks, and inverted punctuation in familiar words.
5. **Cognates And Mini Dialogues**: Use cognates and context to understand and build short introductory exchanges.
