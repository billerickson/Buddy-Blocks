# Spanish 1 Course Map

Track: Spanish  
Level: Level 1  
Sources: Accepted research brief at `research/spanish-1/01-research-brief.md` and level blueprint at `research/spanish-1/02-level-blueprint.md`

## Course Design Notes

Spanish 1 is organized around novice communication functions rather than a grammar survey. The sequence begins with immediately usable social and classroom language, builds high-reuse domains such as numbers, dates, weather, objects, and descriptions, then expands into identity, family, likes, school, food, places, routines, and integrated novice tasks. Lesson counts are uneven because some units introduce core form systems while others consolidate and apply. Every unit should include interpretive input before production, short cumulative retrieval, and feedback that names the useful clue.

## Unit 1: First Spanish Moves

### Unit Purpose

Give students immediate entry into Spanish through greetings, names, polite expressions, classroom survival phrases, sound-spelling patterns, cognates, and simple dialogue moves. The unit establishes the habit of listening or reading for meaning before producing language.

### Student Outcomes

- Greet someone, say goodbye, use polite phrases, and respond to a few classroom directions.
- Ask and answer name and well-being questions using rehearsed chunks.
- Recognize common Spanish sound-spelling features in familiar words.
- Use cognates and context clues carefully to infer meaning.
- Build a short, polite introductory exchange.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Greetings and farewells, `hola`, `adios`, `buenos dias`, `buenas tardes`, `por favor`, `gracias`, `de nada`, `perdon`, `¿Como te llamas?`, `Me llamo...`, `¿Como estas?`, `Estoy...`, `bien`, `mal`, `mas o menos`, classroom commands, Spanish vowels, `ñ`, silent `h`, `j`, `ll/y`, inverted punctuation, cognates.

### Prerequisites

Students need no prior Spanish. They should be able to follow short English directions, repeat short phrases, tolerate partial understanding, and recognize basic terms such as question, sentence, noun, and verb when taught in context.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This opening unit creates the social routines and pronunciation base for all later lessons. Greetings, polite expressions, classroom phrases, cognates, and response patterns recur throughout every later unit.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Spanish pronunciation works like English pronunciation.
- Cognates always have exactly the same meaning as English words.
- Accent marks and inverted punctuation are decoration.
- `Me llamo` can be translated word by word as if it were English.
- Speaking quickly matters more than being clear and polite.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

5-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Revisit greetings, classroom commands, polite phrases, and pronunciation clues in every later unit. Use short dialogue-builder and speaking-prompt review so the routines stay active without becoming isolated drills.

## Unit 2: Numbers, Calendar, Weather, And Time

### Unit Purpose

Build high-reuse survival language for numbers, age, dates, birthdays, weather, seasons, and basic time. These domains give students useful answers to everyday questions and create repeated opportunities to retrieve numbers and question words.

### Student Outcomes

- Understand and use numbers 0-31 in familiar contexts.
- Ask and answer age using `tener` as a chunk.
- Read and state days, months, dates, and birthdays.
- Interpret and produce common weather and season statements.
- Understand basic time-of-day and class-time expressions.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Numbers 0-31, `¿Cuantos años tienes?`, `Tengo... años`, days, months, `hoy`, `mañana`, `ayer` as exposure, dates, birthdays, seasons, `¿Que tiempo hace?`, `hace sol`, `hace frio`, `hace calor`, `llueve`, `nieva`, `son las...`, `es la una`.

### Prerequisites

Students need Unit 1 greetings, question punctuation, basic pronunciation, and comfort with repeated oral and written phrases. They also need English concepts of number, date, weather, and clock time.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

The unit extends Unit 1 exchanges into personal information and daily context. Numbers return later in family size, class schedules, food prices, quantities, time, and routine statements.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Translating `Tengo... años` as "I have years" without understanding it as the Spanish age formula.
- Treating all date and time patterns like English word order.
- Confusing similar number words such as `dos`, `doce`, and `veinte`.
- Using weather chunks as word-for-word English translations.
- Forgetting that Spanish days and months are normally lowercase.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

5-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Numbers should be spaced through later units rather than completed in one block. Dates, time, and weather should recur in lesson openings, short readings, school schedules, and capstone tasks.

## Unit 3: Classroom Objects And Descriptions

### Unit Purpose

Use concrete classroom language to teach nouns with articles, singular/plural forms, colors, basic adjective agreement, and `hay` or location statements. Students learn that articles and endings carry meaning.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify common classroom objects with `el`, `la`, `los`, and `las`.
- Form simple singular and plural noun phrases for familiar words.
- Describe objects with colors and size words using basic agreement.
- Interpret and produce `hay` statements about what is in a scene.
- Locate objects with simple prepositions and `esta/estan` exposure.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Classroom objects, articles, grammatical gender, singular, plural, color words, size adjectives, `hay`, `no hay`, `esta`, `estan`, `en`, `cerca de`, `al lado de`, `encima de`, `debajo de`, noun-adjective order and agreement.

### Prerequisites

Students need Unit 1 classroom commands and pronunciation clues, Unit 2 numbers for quantities, and readiness to compare Spanish and English word order.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit prepares students to describe people, family, school supplies, food, and places. Article, plural, and adjective-agreement patterns become review priorities for the rest of the level.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Grammatical gender means biological gender.
- Articles can be skipped because English sometimes uses no article.
- Adjectives always come before nouns.
- Plural means only adding `s` without considering article changes.
- `hay` and `esta` are interchangeable in every context.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Interleave object vocabulary with numbers, colors, and location. Later units should recycle articles and adjective agreement with family members, food, places, and school subjects.

## Unit 4: People, Family, And Personal Identity

### Unit Purpose

Help students describe themselves and familiar people using `ser`, limited `estar`, `tener`, subject pronouns, family nouns, origin language, and descriptive adjectives. The unit turns earlier object description patterns into human descriptions and simple profiles.

### Student Outcomes

- Use `soy`, `eres`, and `es` in identity and description statements.
- Use `estoy` and `esta` in limited feeling and location contexts.
- Describe family relationships and possession with `mi`, `tu`, and `tengo`.
- Describe people with familiar adjectives that agree in gender and number.
- Interpret and produce a short profile about a person or family.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Subject pronouns, `ser` for identity/origin/traits, limited `estar` for feelings/location, `tener`, family nouns, `mi`, `tu`, `de`, origin, nationality exposure, descriptive adjectives, `alto/a`, `bajo/a`, `simpatico/a`, `inteligente`, `trabajador/a`, `comico/a`, `serio/a`, agreement review.

### Prerequisites

Students need greetings, name and age chunks, numbers, articles, singular/plural awareness, and basic adjective placement and agreement from Unit 3.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit consolidates personal-information language and prepares for likes, school life, routines, and capstone self-presentations. `Ser`, `estar`, `tener`, family vocabulary, and adjectives continue as cumulative review.

### Misconceptions To Address

- `Ser` and `estar` are interchangeable.
- Subject pronouns must always be stated.
- `Tengo` can be replaced with an English-like `soy` for age.
- Adjective endings do not need to match the noun.
- `de` always translates the same way in every phrase.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

5-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use short profile readings to review numbers, dates, origin, feelings, descriptions, and family. Later units should repeatedly ask who someone is, where they are, what they have, and what they like.

## Unit 5: Likes, Activities, And School Life

### Unit Purpose

Extend personal communication into preferences, activities, school subjects, schedules, and common present-tense school actions. Students use `me gusta/me gustan`, `no me gusta`, `quiero`, and selected regular verb chunks to talk about familiar school and free-time life.

### Student Outcomes

- Express likes and dislikes with `me gusta`, `me gustan`, and `no me gusta`.
- Recognize and use common activity vocabulary in short phrases.
- Interpret school subjects, class schedules, and basic opinions.
- Use a limited set of first-person and third-person present-tense chunks for school actions.
- Ask and answer short preference and school-life questions.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Activities, infinitives, `me gusta`, `me gustan`, `no me gusta`, `tambien`, `pero`, `quiero`, school subjects, schedule words, `primero`, `despues`, `estudio`, `hablo`, `leo`, `escribo`, `practico`, opinions such as `facil`, `dificil`, `interesante`, `divertido/a`, `aburrido/a`.

### Prerequisites

Students need identity language, `tener` for age and possession, basic adjectives, numbers, days, time phrases, and question-response routines.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

The unit builds on self-description and prepares students for food preferences, place choices, daily routines, and integrated conversations. It also introduces simple connectors that support longer but still novice-level messages.

### Misconceptions To Address

- `Me gusta` means "I like" word for word.
- `Me gustan` is optional when talking about plural nouns.
- All verbs should be conjugated in full charts before use.
- Opinions do not need to agree with the noun they describe.
- A schedule is translated directly from English order.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

5-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Spiral question words, days, time, numbers, adjectives, and `ser` descriptions through school schedules and preference dialogues. Keep `me gusta` as a formulaic chunk while helping students notice singular/plural cues.

## Unit 6: Food, Places, Routines, And Novice Capstone

### Unit Purpose

Bring Level 1 language together in everyday contexts: food and drink preferences, polite ordering, places in the community, going somewhere, simple daily routines, question formation, and a capstone novice exchange or presentation.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify common foods and drinks and state simple preferences or wants.
- Use polite ordering or request chunks such as `Quiero...` and `por favor`.
- Describe where someone is or where someone is going with `estar` and `ir a` chunks.
- Sequence a simple day or routine using familiar time and connector language.
- Ask and answer mixed novice questions in short dialogues.
- Produce a short integrated presentation about self, family, school, likes, food, places, and routine.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Foods, drinks, meals, `quiero`, `quisiera` as polite exposure, `tengo hambre`, `tengo sed`, places, `voy a`, `vas a`, `va a`, `en`, `a`, `la escuela`, `la casa`, `el parque`, `la biblioteca`, routine chunks, time phrases, question words, connectors, review of `ser`, `estar`, `tener`, `hay`, and `me gusta`.

### Prerequisites

Students need the full earlier course base: greetings, numbers, calendar, weather, articles, adjective agreement, identity, family, likes, school, time, question words, and confidence with short controlled production.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This final unit is cumulative. It prepares later Spanish levels by giving students durable novice routines for interpreting, exchanging, and presenting familiar information without introducing later grammar systems such as past tenses, commands beyond chunks, object-pronoun systems, or full verb paradigms.

### Misconceptions To Address

- `Quiero` is always equally polite in every setting.
- `Voy a` can be translated word by word without considering the destination phrase.
- `Estoy en` and `voy a` mean the same thing.
- Longer Spanish is better even if accuracy and clarity break down.
- Capstone production should be unscripted paragraph writing rather than a controlled novice message.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

6-7 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use this unit for intentional mixed review. Each lesson should retrieve earlier language in a new context, and the final capstone should sample interpretive reading, dialogue choice, sentence building, short writing, and optional speaking.
