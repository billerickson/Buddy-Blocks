# Unit 2: Ratio Language And Equivalent Ratios

Track: Math
Level: Grade 6
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/grade-06-math/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students understand ratios as multiplicative comparisons between quantities and represent those comparisons with language, tape diagrams, ratio tables, double number lines, and equations. By the end of the unit, students should generate equivalent ratios by scaling and identify additive reasoning as a common but incorrect strategy.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit follows operation readiness because ratio reasoning depends on multiplication, division, fraction magnitude, and clear unit language. It appears before rates and percents so students learn what a ratio compares before converting every situation into a calculation.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: multiplicative comparison, part-to-part comparison, part-to-whole comparison, equivalent ratios, scaling, additive versus multiplicative change.
- Vocabulary: ratio, for every, to, part-to-part, part-to-whole, equivalent, scale, multiplicative comparison, tape diagram, ratio table, double number line.
- Representations and models: labeled pictures, tape diagrams, ratio tables, double number lines, equations such as `3 cups juice for every 2 cups water`, and short explanation statements.
- Texts and forms: short mixture, class, recipe, game, and pattern contexts with quantities kept visible.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need multiplication and division meanings, fraction and whole-number comparison, table reading, number-line intervals, and the readiness habit of labeling units.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- A ratio is just a fraction without needing a context.
- Adding the same number to both quantities preserves a ratio.
- The order of quantities in a ratio does not matter.
- Part-to-part and part-to-whole ratios are interchangeable.
- A table is a ratio table if the totals grow regularly, even when the multiplicative relationship changes.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Start with ratio language and what the comparison means. Then distinguish part-to-part and part-to-whole because this supports later percent and mixture reasoning. Introduce tape diagrams before tables to keep the quantities visual. Move to ratio tables and double number lines for equivalent ratios. End with error analysis focused on additive reasoning, the most important Grade 6 ratio misconception.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Review Unit 1 multiplication, division, fraction benchmarks, units, and estimation. Each ratio lesson should ask whether the answer's size and units make sense.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares rates, unit rates, percents, geometry scaling contexts, data comparisons, and financial unit-price decisions. It also gives algebraic relationship lessons a concrete context for tables and equations.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify the quantities a ratio compares.
- Distinguish part-to-part from part-to-whole ratios.
- Build and interpret tape diagrams, ratio tables, and double number lines.
- Generate equivalent ratios by multiplying or dividing both quantities by the same factor.
- Explain why additive changes do not usually preserve a ratio.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **What A Ratio Compares**: Identify and describe the two quantities in a ratio relationship.
2. **Part-To-Part And Part-To-Whole**: Distinguish ratios that compare parts to each other from ratios that compare a part to the total.
3. **Tape Diagrams For Ratios**: Use equal-size units in tape diagrams to make ratio structure visible.
4. **Ratio Tables And Multiplicative Scaling**: Generate equivalent ratios by scaling both quantities by the same factor.
5. **Double Number Lines For Equivalent Ratios**: Connect equivalent ratios to aligned marks on double number lines.
6. **Spot And Fix Additive Ratio Reasoning**: Diagnose and correct the error of adding the same amount to both quantities.
