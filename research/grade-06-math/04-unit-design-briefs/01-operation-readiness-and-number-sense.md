# Unit 1: Operation Readiness And Number Sense

Track: Math
Level: Grade 6
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/grade-06-math/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students refresh the operation, place-value, fraction, decimal, and reasonableness tools that Grade 6 mathematics depends on. By the end of the unit, students should estimate before calculating, interpret fractions and decimals by magnitude, use division language precisely, and decide whether a task calls for exact computation, estimation, or explanation.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit comes first because Grade 6 immediately asks students to reason multiplicatively, divide fractions, work with rates, and interpret rational numbers. A compact readiness unit makes fragile arithmetic visible without turning the course into a Grade 5 review. It also establishes the course habit that models, estimates, and units matter.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: magnitude, place value, estimation, exact versus approximate answers, division interpretations, fraction and decimal benchmarks, answer reasonableness.
- Vocabulary: benchmark, estimate, reasonable, quotient, divisor, dividend, factor, product, multiple, decimal place, numerator, denominator, unit, inverse operation.
- Representations and models: number lines, place-value charts, area models, fraction strips, open-number-line jumps, equations, estimate-check statements.
- Texts and forms: short computation prompts, one-step applied contexts, "Which is reasonable?" checks, brief explanation stems such as "This answer makes sense because..."

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should have Grade 5 experience with multi-digit whole-number operations, decimals, fraction addition and subtraction with unlike denominators, fraction multiplication, simple division involving unit fractions and whole numbers, volume, and first-quadrant coordinates. Fluency may be uneven, so lessons should make reasoning visible.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Estimation is a less careful version of exact calculation rather than a planning or checking tool.
- A larger-looking fraction or decimal is automatically greater.
- Division always makes smaller.
- The quotient is just a number, not an answer to a question with units.
- Decimal placement can be guessed by lining up digits visually.
- Units are optional once the computation is done.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with estimation and reasonableness so students learn to predict size before procedure. Then refresh decimal place-value operations and fraction benchmark comparisons as two high-leverage Grade 6 supports. Next, revisit multiplication/division meanings with special attention to what a quotient represents. End by asking students to choose the right tool: exact calculation, estimation, or explanation.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

This is the opening unit, so review comes from Grade 5: fraction magnitude, decimal operations, whole-number division, number-line reasoning, volume/measurement units, and simple expressions. Review should be concise and diagnostic rather than exhaustive.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares ratio tables, unit rates, percents, fraction division, decimal algorithms, rational-number ordering, expression evaluation, geometry formulas, statistics summaries, and financial modeling. The recurring language of benchmark, unit, quotient, and reasonable should be reused throughout the level.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Estimate products, quotients, sums, and differences before or after exact calculation.
- Use decimal place value to compute and check.
- Compare fractions and decimals using benchmarks and number lines.
- Interpret division situations and label quotients.
- Decide whether exact computation, estimation, or an explanation best fits a task.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Estimate Before You Calculate**: Use benchmarks and compatible numbers to predict reasonable answer size.
2. **Decimal Place Value And Operations**: Connect decimal operations to place value and units.
3. **Fraction Magnitude And Benchmarks**: Compare fractions with 0, 1/2, and 1 benchmarks before calculating.
4. **Multiplication, Division, And What The Quotient Means**: Revisit division as answering a specific question in context.
5. **Choose Exact, Estimate, Or Explain**: Decide which mathematical response a prompt needs and justify the choice.
