# Math - Grade 6 Lesson Briefs

Sources: accepted level blueprint, course map, and unit design briefs in `research/grade-06-math/`.

## Unit 1: Operation Readiness And Number Sense

### Lesson 1: Estimate Before You Calculate

- **Teaching goal:** Establish estimation as a planning and checking tool for Grade 6 computations.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose benchmark or compatible numbers and predict a reasonable answer range before exact calculation.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Number-line benchmarks and rounded quantities show the likely size of a sum, product, difference, or quotient.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Place value, rounding, multiplication/division meanings, and comparison language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Estimation is guessing; exact answers are always needed; estimates should be made only after calculating.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a computation or short context, the student chooses a reasonable estimate and explains why an unreasonable answer is too large or too small.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for reasonable estimate selection and best reasonableness explanation; `order-items` for estimation steps.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare an exact computation with a quick estimate and ask how the estimate can catch an impossible answer.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep contexts one sentence; use friendly numbers first and avoid exact multi-step arithmetic as the target.

### Lesson 2: Decimal Place Value And Operations

- **Teaching goal:** Refresh decimal computation through place-value units and estimation checks.
- **Student outcome:** The student can add, subtract, multiply, or divide decimals in simple Grade 6 contexts while tracking place value and units.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Decimal places name tenths, hundredths, and thousandths; operations must preserve the size of those units.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Decimal place names, whole-number operations, powers-of-ten patterns, and estimation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Aligning digits instead of place values; placing decimal points by visual pattern; ignoring units after computing.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student computes a decimal result, identifies a valid estimate, and rejects a decimal point error.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for place-value statements; `text-input` for numeric answers; `multiple-choice` for decimal point and estimate checks.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use 4.8 x 3 and 4.8 divided by 3 to show how estimates predict whether the result should be around 15 or around 1.5.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep decimal numbers to tenths/hundredths unless the target is place value; quote numeric answers in question blocks.

### Lesson 3: Fraction Magnitude And Benchmarks

- **Teaching goal:** Strengthen fraction comparison through benchmarks before procedure.
- **Student outcome:** The student can compare fractions to 0, 1/2, and 1 and use those benchmarks to order or compare fractions.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Fraction size depends on the relationship between numerator, denominator, and the whole, not on one number alone.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Numerator, denominator, equivalent fractions, number lines, and common benchmark fractions.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Larger denominator means larger fraction; compare only numerators; ignore whether a fraction is near 0, 1/2, or 1.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student correctly places or compares fractions using benchmark reasoning and explains the comparison.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for benchmark choice and best comparison explanation; `order-items` for least-to-greatest.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Sort fractions into "less than half," "equal to half," and "more than half" before computing common denominators.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep denominators familiar; use number-line language rather than long stories.

### Lesson 4: Multiplication, Division, And What The Quotient Means

- **Teaching goal:** Reconnect multiplication and division to context and quotient interpretation.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether a division quotient represents group size, number of groups, a rate, or a comparison.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A quotient answers a specific question; labels and units reveal its meaning.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Equal groups, sharing division, grouping division, multiplication as comparison, and units.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Division is one generic procedure; the quotient label does not matter; division always makes smaller.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a division context, the student identifies what the quotient represents and selects or writes a correct label.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for quotient meaning and best interpretation; `match-pairs` for contexts to labels.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare 24 cookies shared by 6 people with 24 cookies packed 6 per bag; both divide, but the quotient answers different questions.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short contexts with visible units; avoid fraction division until Unit 4.

### Lesson 5: Choose Exact, Estimate, Or Explain

- **Teaching goal:** Build judgment about the mathematical response a task requires.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether a prompt calls for an exact answer, an estimate, or a written explanation and complete the response.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Different tasks ask for different kinds of mathematical evidence.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Unit 1 estimation, decimal operations, fraction benchmarks, division meanings, and reasonableness language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Every math question expects a single exact number; explanations can ignore computation; estimates are acceptable when exactness is requested.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student classifies a task, solves or explains appropriately, and notes why that response type fits.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for response-type classification and best explanation; `passage-question` for compact contexts.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Present three prompts that use similar numbers but ask "about how many," "exactly how many," and "how do you know?"
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar contexts; keep explanations to one or two sentences.

## Unit 2: Ratio Language And Equivalent Ratios

### Lesson 1: What A Ratio Compares

- **Teaching goal:** Introduce ratios as comparisons between two quantities.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify the two quantities in a ratio and describe the comparison in words.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A ratio names a relationship, such as "3 red tiles for every 2 blue tiles."
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Multiplication/division meanings, comparison language, and units.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A ratio is just one number; the order of quantities does not matter; totals alone define the ratio.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies both quantities, preserves order, and writes or selects a matching ratio statement.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for ratio recognition; `fill-blank` for ratio language; `match-pairs` for situation to ratio statement.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a simple two-color tile set and say the relationship in several equivalent verbal forms.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep quantities small and contexts concrete; avoid rate language at first.

### Lesson 2: Part-To-Part And Part-To-Whole

- **Teaching goal:** Distinguish part-to-part ratios from part-to-whole ratios.
- **Student outcome:** The student can tell whether a ratio compares two parts or one part to the total and write the matching ratio.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** The same situation can support different ratios depending on which quantities are compared.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Ratio language, total as sum of parts, and basic fraction language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Part-to-part and part-to-whole ratios are interchangeable; the total can be ignored; ratio order is flexible.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the comparison type and writes a correct ratio for a given question.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for comparison type and best explanation; `match-pairs` for ratio to description.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** With 4 dogs and 6 cats, contrast dogs to cats, cats to dogs, and dogs to total animals.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep all quantities visible; do not combine with percent yet.

### Lesson 3: Tape Diagrams For Ratios

- **Teaching goal:** Use tape diagrams to represent ratio units visually.
- **Student outcome:** The student can interpret or complete a tape diagram for a ratio and use it to find related quantities.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Equal-size tape units show the repeated parts in a ratio relationship.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Ratio language, multiplication, division, and part/whole reasoning.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Tape segments in one ratio must show actual object size; unequal segments can represent equal ratio units; totals are found by adding only one side.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student uses a tape diagram to identify one ratio unit, a total, or a missing quantity.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for diagram interpretation; `fill-blank` for missing unit values; `text-input` for missing quantities.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Draw 2 equal parts for one quantity and 3 equal parts for another, then scale each part to a known value.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use text-based diagram descriptions in questions; keep scale factors small.

### Lesson 4: Ratio Tables And Multiplicative Scaling

- **Teaching goal:** Generate equivalent ratios using multiplicative scaling in tables.
- **Student outcome:** The student can complete a ratio table by multiplying or dividing both quantities by the same factor.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Equivalent ratios preserve the same multiplicative relationship.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Ratio meaning, multiplication/division facts, tables, and factors.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Add the same number to both quantities; scale only one quantity; match rows by total rather than relationship.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student completes missing table entries and explains the scale factor.
- **Recommended question types:** `multi-blank-cloze` for table entries; `multiple-choice` for valid equivalent ratio and scaling explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show a table row of 2 cups to 5 cups and ask what happens if the recipe doubles and triples.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use small integer scale factors before fractional or decimal scale factors.

### Lesson 5: Double Number Lines For Equivalent Ratios

- **Teaching goal:** Connect equivalent ratios to aligned positions on double number lines.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use a double number line to find or interpret equivalent ratio pairs.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Aligned marks show quantities that grow together by the same scale factor.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Number lines, ratio tables, skip counting, and multiplication.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Marks can be uneven without meaning; aligned quantities can be mixed across different scale factors; only one side needs a pattern.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects or completes aligned pairs on a double number line and explains the scaling.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for missing marks; `order-items` for mark sequence; `multiple-choice` for aligned pair selection.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Build a double number line from a ratio table so students see the table rows become aligned marks.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep line intervals regular; avoid cluttered diagrams in text prompts.

### Lesson 6: Spot And Fix Additive Ratio Reasoning

- **Teaching goal:** Diagnose additive reasoning in ratio problems and replace it with multiplicative scaling.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain why adding the same number to both quantities does not usually preserve a ratio.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Equivalent ratios are made by multiplying or dividing both quantities by the same factor.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Equivalent ratios, ratio tables, scale factors, and multiplication/division.
- **Likely misconceptions:** If both quantities increase by the same amount, the ratio stays the same; close numbers mean close ratios.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies an additive error and corrects the ratio using scaling.
- **Recommended question types:** `error-correction` for worked-example repair; `multiple-choice` for misconception checks and best explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare 2:3 to 4:5 and 4:6 with a tape diagram or table.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one error at a time; keep explanations focused on multiplying both quantities.

## Unit 3: Unit Rates, Percents, And Applied Rates

### Lesson 1: Rate Language And Units

- **Teaching goal:** Interpret rates by attending to both quantities and their units.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain what statements such as "60 miles per hour" or "$4 per pound" mean.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A rate compares quantities with different units, and "per" tells which quantity is tied to one unit.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Ratio language, quotient meaning, and unit labels.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Per language is decorative; unit order does not matter; a rate is the same as a total.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies both units and describes the meaning of a rate in words.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for rate meaning and unit explanation; `match-pairs` for rate to interpretation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Contrast "30 miles in 2 hours" with "15 miles per hour."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar contexts and avoid compound unit conversions.

### Lesson 2: Find The Unit Rate

- **Teaching goal:** Find a quantity per 1 by dividing and label the result.
- **Student outcome:** The student can compute a unit rate and state which quantity is per one unit of the other.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Unit rate answers, "How much for 1?"
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Rate language, division, equivalent ratios, and decimal quotients.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Divide in the wrong order; omit the "per" unit; use total amount instead of amount per 1.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student calculates a unit rate and selects the correct unit label.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for unit labels; `text-input` for numeric unit rates; `multiple-choice` for correct division setup.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a ratio table that shrinks 48 miles in 4 hours to 12 miles in 1 hour.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep arithmetic manageable; use decimals only when the target includes decimal division.

### Lesson 3: Unit Prices And Better Buys

- **Teaching goal:** Apply unit rates to compare prices fairly.
- **Student outcome:** The student can compute cost per item or per ounce and decide which option is the better buy.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Unit price compares costs for the same amount.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Unit rate, decimal division, money notation, and comparison.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Lowest total price is always best; largest package is always best; units can be ignored.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student compares two options using cost per unit and explains the choice.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for compact price contexts; `text-input` for unit price; `multiple-choice` for decision explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare two snack bags with different sizes and prices, then normalize both to one ounce.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use no more than two options per item; avoid taxes or discounts here.

### Lesson 4: Percent Means Per 100

- **Teaching goal:** Introduce percent as a rate per 100 and connect it to fractions and decimals.
- **Student outcome:** The student can interpret and convert common percents using per-100 reasoning.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** 35% means 35 per 100, or 35/100, or 0.35.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Equivalent ratios, fractions with denominator 100, decimal hundredths, and double number lines.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Percent is always a whole-number count; percent values cannot connect to fractions or decimals; the percent sign changes the quantity without context.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches percent, fraction, decimal, and per-100 statement.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for equivalent forms; `fill-blank` for per-100 statements; `multiple-choice` for percent interpretation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a 100-square grid or percent bar to show 35 out of 100.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Start with common percents and denominator 100; avoid percent increase/decrease.

### Lesson 5: Benchmark Percents Of A Quantity

- **Teaching goal:** Use benchmark percents to find amounts mentally or with simple steps.
- **Student outcome:** The student can find 10%, 25%, 50%, 75%, and 100% of familiar quantities and explain the benchmark strategy.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Benchmark percents connect to fractions: 50% is half, 25% is one fourth, and 10% is one tenth.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Percent as per 100, fractions, decimals, division by 2, 4, and 10.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Multiply by the percent number directly; treat 25% as 25 times the quantity; ignore whether the result is reasonable.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student finds benchmark percent amounts and selects a strategy that fits the percent.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for strategy choice; `fill-blank` for amount; `text-input` for numeric answers.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Put 50%, 25%, and 10% on a percent bar and connect each to a fraction of the whole.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use whole-number quantities that divide cleanly at first.

### Lesson 6: Rate And Percent Decision Problems

- **Teaching goal:** Choose a rate or percent model to solve applied Grade 6 decisions.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether a problem involves a unit rate, unit price, benchmark percent, or equivalent ratio and solve it.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Good applied reasoning begins by naming the relationship before calculating.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Unit rates, unit prices, percent per 100, benchmark percents, and estimation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Use the newest procedure for every situation; ignore units; choose an answer that is numerically correct but contextually wrong.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects a model, solves, and explains why the answer fits the decision.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for applied scenarios; `multiple-choice` for model choice and decision explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Offer a "model menu" with unit rate, percent bar, ratio table, and estimate, then choose before solving.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep scenarios compact with no hidden assumptions; avoid multi-discount adult financial contexts.

## Unit 4: Fraction Division And Decimal Fluency

### Lesson 1: Division Situations With Fractions

- **Teaching goal:** Build meaning for fraction division through contexts before procedures.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify the dividend, divisor, and question being answered in a fraction-division story.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Fraction division answers a sharing or grouping question involving fractional quantities.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Fraction magnitude, division meanings, unit labels, and simple fraction multiplication.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Fraction division is only a symbol manipulation; the quotient has no unit; division always makes smaller.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student classifies a story and states what the quotient will represent.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for story interpretation and label explanation; `match-pairs` for context to quotient meaning.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare "3/4 cup shared into 3 servings" with "3/4 cup packed in 1/4-cup scoops."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar measurement contexts; do not ask for complex computation first.

### Lesson 2: How Many Fraction Groups?

- **Teaching goal:** Use measurement division to find how many fractional groups fit in a quantity.
- **Student outcome:** The student can model and solve questions like "How many 1/4-cup scoops are in 3 cups?"
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Measurement division asks how many groups of the divisor fit in the dividend.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Division meanings, fraction number lines, unit fractions, and repeated addition.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating the divisor as the number of groups; stopping after one whole; expecting a smaller quotient.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student uses a number line or repeated fraction groups to find the number of groups.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for group counts; `order-items` for number-line steps; `text-input` for quotient.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Mark fourths from 0 to 3 and count how many 1/4 jumps fit.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Start with unit fractions and whole-number dividends before non-unit fractions.

### Lesson 3: Share Fractions Equally

- **Teaching goal:** Use sharing division to find the size of each equal share when the total is fractional.
- **Student outcome:** The student can model and solve questions like "3/4 cup shared equally among 3 people."
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Sharing division asks how much is in each group.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Equal sharing, fraction partitioning, multiplication by unit fractions, and quotient labels.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Dividing the denominator only; giving each group the divisor; losing the whole that is being shared.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student finds each share and labels it in context.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for correct model and interpretation; `fill-blank` for share amount.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Partition a 3/4 strip into 3 equal shares and name one share.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use visual language; keep denominators small and contexts concrete.

### Lesson 4: Connect Models To Divide Fractions

- **Teaching goal:** Link fraction-division models to equations and the reciprocal procedure.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain how a diagram or equation supports an efficient fraction-division computation.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Multiplying by the reciprocal is an efficient procedure that should match the division situation.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Fraction multiplication, measurement and sharing division, equivalent fractions, and area/tape models.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Flip the wrong fraction; apply the procedure without checking meaning; think the procedure is unrelated to the model.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects the correct reciprocal setup and connects it to the quotient meaning.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for setup and model-to-equation reasoning; `error-correction` for wrong reciprocal use.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show `2/3 divided by 1/6` as "how many sixths fit in two thirds" before writing `2/3 x 6`.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid compound fractions and mixed numbers; use one model per item.

### Lesson 5: Decimal Division With Place Value

- **Teaching goal:** Divide decimals using place-value reasoning and estimation.
- **Student outcome:** The student can compute decimal quotients and explain why the decimal point belongs where it does.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Decimal division can be checked by estimating the quotient's size and tracking decimal place values.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Decimal place value, whole-number division, unit rate, and estimation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Move decimal points by memorized pattern only; produce a quotient far outside the estimate; ignore trailing zeros.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student computes a decimal quotient and chooses a reasonable estimate.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for estimate and decimal placement; `text-input` for quotient; `fill-blank` for place-value reasoning.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Estimate first: `12.6 divided by 3` should be a little more than 4, not 0.42 or 42.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use terminating decimals; keep long division length modest.

### Lesson 6: Multi-Digit And Decimal Operation Checks

- **Teaching goal:** Use estimation, inverse operations, and context to verify multi-digit and decimal calculations.
- **Student outcome:** The student can spot unreasonable results and choose a valid check for whole-number or decimal operations.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A calculation is stronger when it passes a size check, an inverse check, and a context check.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Multi-digit operations, decimal place value, estimation, inverse relationships, and units.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Repeating a procedure is always a check; close-looking answers are acceptable; units do not affect reasonableness.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies an error in a worked computation or chooses a valid check equation.
- **Recommended question types:** `error-correction` for computation or unit errors; `multiple-choice` for valid checks and reasonableness.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show one wrong decimal quotient and ask which estimate would have caught it.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Focus each item on one checking idea; avoid long calculations when the target is error analysis.

## Unit 5: Rational Numbers And The Coordinate Plane

### Lesson 1: Positive And Negative Contexts

- **Teaching goal:** Give signed numbers meaning as positions or changes relative to a reference point.
- **Student outcome:** The student can match positive and negative numbers to contexts such as elevation, temperature, debt, or game score.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Zero is a reference point; signs show direction or side from that reference.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Number lines, comparison, context labels, and integer exposure.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Negative means bad in every context; the sign can be dropped; zero is always "nothing" rather than a reference.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student writes or selects signed numbers for contextual statements and explains the reference point.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for context to signed number; `multiple-choice` for sign interpretation and reference explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare 5 degrees above zero and 5 degrees below zero on a vertical number line.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use concrete contexts; avoid operations with signed numbers.

### Lesson 2: Opposites On Number Lines

- **Teaching goal:** Locate numbers and their opposites on horizontal and vertical number lines.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify opposites and explain their symmetry around zero.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Opposites are the same distance from zero in different directions.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Positive/negative contexts, number lines, and distance language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Opposite means reciprocal; the opposite of a negative number is always larger without context; vertical number lines work differently.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student names opposites and places them correctly on number lines.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for opposites; `multiple-choice` for number-line location; `match-pairs` for number to opposite.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Fold a number line at zero to show matching distances on both sides.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep numbers as integers and simple fractions/decimals; use clear sign spacing.

### Lesson 3: Absolute Value As Distance

- **Teaching goal:** Interpret absolute value as distance from zero.
- **Student outcome:** The student can find and explain absolute value in numerical and contextual situations.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Absolute value is never negative because distance is nonnegative, but context may still need direction.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Opposites, number-line distance, and positive/negative contexts.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Absolute value means change the sign; `| -8 |` and `-8` mean the same thing; distance includes direction.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student finds absolute values and explains when distance alone answers the question.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for meaning and context explanation; `text-input` for numeric absolute values.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Ask how far -6 and +6 are from zero, then compare that distance with their positions.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use simple absolute value notation; avoid nested symbols.

### Lesson 4: Compare And Order Rational Numbers

- **Teaching goal:** Compare positive and negative fractions and decimals using number-line reasoning.
- **Student outcome:** The student can order rational numbers from least to greatest and justify the order.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Numbers farther right on the number line are greater, even when negative magnitudes differ.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Fraction/decimal magnitude, negative numbers, number lines, and comparison symbols.
- **Likely misconceptions:** The negative number with the larger absolute value is greater; fractions and decimals cannot be compared together; ignore zero as a boundary.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student orders mixed rational numbers and explains at least one comparison.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for least-to-greatest; `multiple-choice` for comparison and reasoning.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Place -2, -1/2, 0, 0.75, and 1 on a line before using symbols.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep lists short; use familiar benchmark fractions and terminating decimals.

### Lesson 5: Plot Points In All Four Quadrants

- **Teaching goal:** Extend coordinate plotting to all four quadrants.
- **Student outcome:** The student can plot ordered pairs and identify quadrants based on coordinate signs.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** The x-coordinate moves left or right; the y-coordinate moves up or down.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** First-quadrant plotting, signed numbers, x- and y-axis language, and ordered pairs.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Reverse x and y; ignore negative signs; name quadrants by counting grid squares only.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches ordered pairs to locations or quadrant descriptions.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for quadrant and point interpretation; `fill-blank` for coordinate signs; `match-pairs` for ordered pair to description.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Practice "x first, then y" as a movement routine from the origin.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use small integer coordinates; avoid dense coordinate lists.

### Lesson 6: Interpret Coordinates In Context

- **Teaching goal:** Use coordinate pairs to answer contextual questions about maps, movement, and distance parallel to axes.
- **Student outcome:** The student can interpret what coordinates mean and use them to answer simple context questions.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Coordinates describe location from a reference point; horizontal and vertical distances can be read from coordinate differences.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Four-quadrant plotting, ordered pairs, rational-number comparison, and distance from zero.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Coordinates are labels with no units; distance can be negative; diagonal movement is the same as horizontal/vertical distance.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student interprets signs and distances in a coordinate context.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for map contexts; `multiple-choice` for interpretation; `text-input` for simple distances.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a simple coordinate map with home at the origin and points east/west/north/south.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep maps simple and distances axis-aligned.

## Unit 6: Expressions, Equivalence, And Algebraic Language

### Lesson 1: Variables As Quantities

- **Teaching goal:** Establish variables as numbers that are unknown or changing, not labels.
- **Student outcome:** The student can interpret a variable and a coefficient-like expression in context.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A letter can stand for a quantity; `5m` means five groups of the quantity `m`.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Multiplication as groups, unknowns in equations, and unit labels.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A variable names an object; `5m` means five meters in all contexts; variables are always one fixed mystery number.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student explains what a variable represents and interprets a simple product with a variable.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for variable meaning and context interpretation; `fill-blank` for expression language.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare "5 meters" with `5m` where `m` is the number of minutes or muffins.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid multiple variables at first; keep contexts concrete.

### Lesson 2: Evaluate Expressions With Substitution

- **Teaching goal:** Evaluate expressions by substituting a given value for a variable.
- **Student outcome:** The student can replace a variable with a value and compute the expression accurately.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Substitution turns an algebraic expression into a numerical expression.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Variables as quantities, arithmetic operations, and order of operations readiness.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Replace only some occurrences of the variable; treat the variable as a label; ignore parentheses or operation order.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student evaluates simple expressions for given values and explains the substitution step.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for substitution; `text-input` for values; `multiple-choice` for correct substituted expression.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show `3x + 4` with `x = 5`, first rewriting as `3(5) + 4`.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one variable and small numbers; introduce decimals/fractions only after whole-number examples.

### Lesson 3: Exponents And Order Of Operations

- **Teaching goal:** Use whole-number exponents and order of operations to evaluate numerical expressions.
- **Student outcome:** The student can interpret exponents as repeated multiplication and apply operation order correctly.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** `4^3` means `4 x 4 x 4`; exponents are evaluated before multiplication/addition in standard order.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Multiplication, grouping symbols, and numerical expressions.
- **Likely misconceptions:** `4^3` means `4 x 3`; always work left to right; parentheses are optional.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student expands exponents, orders steps, and evaluates expressions.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for exponent to expanded form; `order-items` for operation steps; `text-input` for expression values.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Contrast `3^2`, `3 x 2`, and `3 + 2` using repeated multiplication language.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep bases and exponents small; avoid negative bases.

### Lesson 4: Write Expressions From Words

- **Teaching goal:** Translate concise verbal descriptions into algebraic expressions.
- **Student outcome:** The student can write or choose expressions for sums, differences, products, quotients, and grouped quantities.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Words describe operations and quantities; the expression records the calculation without solving it.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Variables, operation vocabulary, and expression evaluation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Reverse subtraction or division; write an equation when only an expression is requested; solve instead of represent.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects or writes expressions that match verbal phrases and simple contexts.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for expression choice; `text-input` for typed expressions when simple; `error-correction` for reversed phrases.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare "6 less than n" and "n less than 6" with concrete values.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use concise phrases; avoid overly tricky wording.

### Lesson 5: Properties And Equivalent Expressions

- **Teaching goal:** Use properties of operations to identify equivalent expressions.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether two expressions are equivalent and name the property or reasoning.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Equivalent expressions have the same value for every allowed value of the variable.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Variables, substitution, operation properties, and arithmetic equality.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Same numbers mean same expression; checking one value proves equivalence in all cases; order can change for subtraction or division.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies equivalent and non-equivalent expressions and justifies the choice.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for equivalence and reasoning; `match-pairs` for expression pairs.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Test expressions with a value, then connect to a property such as commutative or associative reasoning.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep expressions short; do not require formal proof.

### Lesson 6: Distribute To Show Equivalent Forms

- **Teaching goal:** Use distribution to connect grouped expressions to expanded equivalent forms.
- **Student outcome:** The student can rewrite expressions like `4(x + 3)` as `4x + 12` and explain the structure.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Multiplying a group means multiplying each part of the group.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Multiplication over addition, area models, variables, and equivalent expressions.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Multiply only the first term; add the outside factor instead of multiplying; distribute over subtraction incorrectly.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses or writes a correct distributed expression and identifies a common error.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for correct expansion and model explanation; `error-correction` for distribution errors.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use an area model with width `x + 3` and height 4 to show two rectangles.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use positive coefficients and simple binomials; avoid combining like terms as a separate goal.

## Unit 7: Equations, Inequalities, And Variable Relationships

### Lesson 1: Equations As Balanced Statements

- **Teaching goal:** Reinforce equality as a relationship between two expressions.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether an equation is true for a value and explain what equality means.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** The equals sign means both sides have the same value.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Expression evaluation, variables, and arithmetic equality.
- **Likely misconceptions:** The equals sign means "compute next"; the expression on the left is always the problem; balance is visual only.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies true equations and explains how both sides compare.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for true/false equality and equality explanation; `fill-blank` for balancing values.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare `8 + 5 = 13` with `13 = 8 + 5` and `8 + 5 = 6 + 7`.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep equations visually clean; use one variable or blank at a time.

### Lesson 2: Solve One-Step Equations

- **Teaching goal:** Solve one-step equations using inverse operations while preserving equality.
- **Student outcome:** The student can solve addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division equations within Grade 6 expectations.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** An inverse operation undoes the operation on the variable.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Equations as balance, operation inverses, and expression evaluation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Move numbers by changing signs without reasoning; apply the inverse to one side only; combine all visible numbers.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student solves a one-step equation and states the inverse operation used.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for missing solution; `text-input` for solution values; `multiple-choice` for inverse-operation choice.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show `x + 7 = 19` as a balance and ask what removes 7 from both sides.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use positive numbers first; avoid multi-step equations.

### Lesson 3: Check A Solution In Context

- **Teaching goal:** Use substitution and context to verify proposed solutions.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether a number solves an equation and whether it answers the original question.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A solution makes the equation true and fits the context.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Substitution, one-step solving, units, and context interpretation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A solved number is correct without checking; any number that makes arithmetic work fits the context; units are not part of checking.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student substitutes a proposed value, evaluates both sides, and accepts or rejects the solution with a reason.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for valid solution and context fit; `error-correction` for wrong check.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a ticket-price equation and ask whether a fractional or negative solution would make sense.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use compact contexts; keep substitution arithmetic simple.

### Lesson 4: Inequalities And Solution Sets

- **Teaching goal:** Interpret inequalities as statements with many possible solutions.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose values that satisfy an inequality and describe the solution set in words.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** An inequality compares quantities and often has more than one solution.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Rational-number order, comparison symbols, variables, and context language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Inequalities have one exact answer; `>` and `<` can be read in either direction; "at least" and "at most" are the same.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student tests values and translates context phrases into inequalities.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for solution values and solution-set description; `fill-blank` for inequality symbols.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Contrast "you need exactly 12 points" with "you need at least 12 points."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar constraints; avoid compound inequalities.

### Lesson 5: Graph Inequalities On A Number Line

- **Teaching goal:** Represent inequality solution sets on a number line.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use open or closed circles and shading direction to graph simple inequalities.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** The boundary value and symbol decide whether the endpoint is included and which direction is shaded.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Inequality symbols, number-line order, and solution sets.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Always use a closed circle; shade toward the larger-looking side; reverse direction for negative boundary values.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches inequalities to number-line graphs and identifies graphing errors.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for graph interpretation; `match-pairs` for inequality to description; `error-correction` for graph errors.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Test one value on each side of the boundary to decide shading direction.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Describe graphs textually with clear endpoint and direction; use simple integer boundaries.

### Lesson 6: Tables, Graphs, And Variable Relationships

- **Teaching goal:** Connect dependent and independent variables across tables, graphs, equations, and verbal rules.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify the independent and dependent variables and complete or interpret a simple relationship.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** One quantity can depend on another; the same relationship can be shown in several representations.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Variables, expressions, coordinate plotting, rate tables, and equations.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Dependent and independent variables can be swapped without meaning change; graph points are just dots; table patterns do not connect to equations.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches a verbal rule to a table, graph description, and equation and identifies which variable depends on which.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for representation matching; `multi-blank-cloze` for table completion; `multiple-choice` for variable explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a simple rule such as `cost = 3 x number of notebooks` and show it in words, table, graph, and equation.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use linear relationships with whole-number inputs; avoid slope terminology.

## Unit 8: Geometry, Measurement, And Coordinate Figures

### Lesson 1: Area Of Parallelograms And Triangles

- **Teaching goal:** Connect area formulas for parallelograms and triangles to visual decomposition.
- **Student outcome:** The student can find area using base and height and explain why the formula fits the shape.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Parallelograms can be rearranged into rectangles, and triangles are half of related parallelograms or rectangles.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Rectangle area, multiplication, fractions as halves, and perpendicular height.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Use any side as height; forget the one-half for triangles; confuse area with perimeter.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies base and height, computes area, and chooses the correct unit.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for base/height choice and formula reasoning; `text-input` for area.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Cut and move a triangle from a parallelogram to form a rectangle, then compare areas.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use labeled diagrams in text; keep dimensions whole numbers first.

### Lesson 2: Decompose Polygons For Area

- **Teaching goal:** Find polygon area by decomposing into known shapes.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose a decomposition, compute part areas, and combine them.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Complex polygons can be split into rectangles, triangles, or parallelograms with known area formulas.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Area formulas, addition, multiplication, and grid interpretation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Count boundary length instead of area; overlap or miss pieces; use a single formula for every polygon.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects a valid decomposition and finds total area with square units.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for decomposition choice; `multi-blank-cloze` for part areas; `text-input` for total area.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show two different valid decompositions of the same polygon and compare totals.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use simple L-shapes and polygons with given dimensions; avoid visual clutter.

### Lesson 3: Volume With Fractional Edge Lengths

- **Teaching goal:** Extend rectangular prism volume to fractional edge lengths.
- **Student outcome:** The student can compute volume as length x width x height and interpret cubic units.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Volume counts how many unit cubes or fractional unit cubes fill a prism.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Grade 5 volume, fraction multiplication, rectangular prisms, and unit labels.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Fractional dimensions cannot produce volume; use surface area instead of volume; omit cubic units.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student substitutes fractional or decimal dimensions into the volume formula and labels the result.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for formula/unit choice and unit interpretation; `text-input` for volume.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a prism with dimensions `1/2`, `4`, and `3` to show half-size layers.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use simple fractions and rectangular prisms only.

### Lesson 4: Nets And Surface Area

- **Teaching goal:** Use nets to find surface area of rectangular prisms.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify all faces in a net, find each face area, and total them.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Surface area is the sum of the areas of every face.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Rectangle area, multiplication, addition, nets, and prism vocabulary.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Count only visible faces; confuse surface area with volume; double the wrong faces.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student computes surface area from dimensions or a net and explains the square-unit label.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for face to dimensions; `multi-blank-cloze` for face areas; `text-input` for total surface area.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Unfold a box into a net and label matching opposite faces.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use rectangular prisms; keep dimensions small and diagrams describable.

### Lesson 5: Coordinate Plane Polygon Problems

- **Teaching goal:** Use coordinates to find side lengths and areas of polygons with sides parallel to axes.
- **Student outcome:** The student can interpret coordinates, find horizontal/vertical distances, and use them in area or perimeter tasks.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Coordinate differences give distances along horizontal or vertical sides.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Four-quadrant plotting, rational-number order, distance from zero, and area/perimeter.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Add coordinate values instead of finding distance; treat distance as negative; reverse x and y.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student finds side lengths from coordinates and solves a polygon measurement task.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for distance reasoning and coordinate explanation; `text-input` for side length or area.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare points with the same y-coordinate and count horizontal units between x-values.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use axis-aligned polygons; avoid diagonal distance formulas.

### Lesson 6: Choose The Right Measure And Unit

- **Teaching goal:** Distinguish perimeter, area, surface area, volume, and distance by meaning and units.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide which measure a situation asks for and use the correct unit.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** The question and unit dimension reveal whether we measure boundary, surface, space, or length.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Area, perimeter, surface area, volume, coordinate distance, and unit labels.
- **Likely misconceptions:** All geometry answers use square units; surface area and volume are interchangeable; formulas can be chosen by shape alone.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects the correct measure, solves or explains, and labels the answer correctly.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for measure choice and reasoning; `error-correction` for unit errors.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Sort prompts into "cover," "wrap," "fill," "edge," and "distance" categories.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep contexts clear and concrete; avoid multi-stage geometry calculations.

## Unit 9: Statistics, Financial Decisions, And Cumulative Modeling

### Lesson 1: Statistical Questions And Variability

- **Teaching goal:** Distinguish statistical questions from nonstatistical questions.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether a question anticipates variability in data and revise a nonstatistical question when appropriate.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A statistical question expects answers that vary across people, objects, or repeated measurements.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Data collection, question wording, and context interpretation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Any question with a number is statistical; one-person questions are always statistical; variability means error.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student classifies questions and explains where variability would come from.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for classification and variability explanation; `error-correction` for revising a question.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare "How tall is Maya?" with "How tall are students in our class?"
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short questions; avoid advanced sampling language.

### Lesson 2: Dot Plots, Histograms, And Shape

- **Teaching goal:** Interpret data displays and describe distribution shape.
- **Student outcome:** The student can read dot plots and histograms and describe clusters, gaps, peaks, and overall shape.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A display shows how data values are distributed, not only individual values.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Number lines, graph reading, frequency, and comparison language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Tallest bar is the mean; gaps do not matter; histograms and bar graphs are the same in meaning.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student reads a display and makes a correct statement about the distribution.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for display descriptions; `multiple-choice` for interpretation and shape description.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a small dot plot and ask what values are common, rare, or missing.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use small datasets and text-described displays; avoid tedious counting.

### Lesson 3: Mean, Median, And Outliers

- **Teaching goal:** Compare mean and median as measures of center and interpret outlier effects.
- **Student outcome:** The student can compute or identify mean and median for small datasets and choose which better represents a typical value.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Mean balances data; median marks the middle; outliers can pull the mean.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Addition, division, ordering numbers, and distribution language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Mean is always the best typical value; median is found without ordering; outliers should always be removed.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student finds mean/median and explains how an outlier affects the choice of center.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for ordering data; `text-input` for mean/median; `multiple-choice` for choice of center.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare two datasets with the same median but one very large outlier.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use small whole-number datasets; keep arithmetic manageable.

### Lesson 4: Range, IQR, And MAD As Spread

- **Teaching goal:** Use spread measures to describe variability in a distribution.
- **Student outcome:** The student can interpret range, interquartile range, and mean absolute deviation at a Grade 6 level.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Spread describes how far data values are from each other or from the center.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Ordering data, median, subtraction, absolute distance, and mean.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Center fully describes a dataset; larger center means larger spread; absolute deviations can be negative.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses or computes a spread measure for a small dataset and interprets it in context.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for interpretation and comparing spread; `fill-blank` for range/IQR.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show two datasets with the same median but different spread and ask which is more consistent.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep datasets small; define IQR and MAD in prompt context when used.

### Lesson 5: Budgets, Unit Prices, And Choices

- **Teaching goal:** Apply rates, decimals, percents, and constraints to simple financial decisions.
- **Student outcome:** The student can compare unit prices, track income and expenses, and decide whether a choice fits a budget or savings goal.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Financial decisions involve quantities, rates, constraints, and tradeoffs.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Unit rates, percent benchmarks, decimal operations, inequalities, and reasonableness.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Total price alone decides value; a budget can be exceeded if the item is important; debit and credit choices have no timing or constraint differences.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student solves a compact financial scenario and explains the decision using numbers and units.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for scenarios; `text-input` for totals or unit prices; `multiple-choice` for choice explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a weekly allowance or club budget with income, expenses, and one decision to make.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep contexts age-appropriate; avoid adult credit-score, loan, or compound-interest details.

### Lesson 6: Mixed Modeling With Data And Finance

- **Teaching goal:** Choose representations and operations for applied data and money scenarios.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether to use a table, graph, unit rate, percent, equation, inequality, or data summary and solve the problem.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Model choice depends on the structure of the question, not the most recent lesson.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Units 1-9 skills through budgets and data displays.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Use one favorite model for every problem; skip units; compute before understanding what is being compared.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects a fitting model, solves accurately, and explains why the model fits.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for model choice and reasoning; `passage-question` for applied prompts.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Present a short scenario and ask students to choose a model before doing any arithmetic.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one or two-step tasks; keep numbers friendly enough that model choice is the focus.

### Lesson 7: Grade 6 Math Mastery Task

- **Teaching goal:** Demonstrate cumulative Grade 6 mastery through mixed-domain modeling.
- **Student outcome:** The student can solve a compact task that combines representation choice, computation, explanation, and reasonableness checks.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** End-of-level mastery means choosing and connecting tools across domains.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Full Grade 6 level: operations, ratios, rates, percents, fraction division, rational numbers, expressions, equations, inequalities, geometry, statistics, and finance.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A correct calculation is enough; the newest topic is always the right tool; explanations can ignore units or context.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student completes mixed tasks, justifies choices, checks reasonableness, and corrects at least one misconception.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for compact capstone contexts; `multiple-choice` for model, misconception checks, explanation, and transfer.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Frame the lesson as a "choose your tool" challenge: identify structure, choose a model, calculate, and check.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use several compact items rather than one long project; keep explanation-selection items short.
