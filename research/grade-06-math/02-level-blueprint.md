# Grade 6 Math Level Blueprint

Track: Math
Level: Grade 6
Source: Accepted research brief at `research/grade-06-math/01-research-brief.md`

## 1. Level Mission

Grade 6 Math helps students make the transition from elementary arithmetic into middle-school mathematical reasoning. Students strengthen whole-number, decimal, and fraction fluency; learn ratios, rates, and percent as multiplicative relationships; extend the number line to negative rational numbers; use variables, expressions, equations, inequalities, and relationships as meaningful representations; and apply geometry, statistics, and financial contexts to real problems. By the end of the level, students should not only calculate accurately; they should choose models, interpret quantities with units, connect representations, critique reasonableness, and explain the structure behind their answers.

## 2. Durable Student Outcomes

By the end of Grade 6 Math, students should be able to:

1. Represent ratio relationships with words, tape diagrams, double number lines, tables, equations, and percent language.
2. Solve rate, unit-rate, and percent-as-rate-per-100 problems while keeping the order and meaning of units clear.
3. Divide fractions by fractions and operate with multi-digit whole numbers and decimals using models, equations, procedures, and reasonableness checks.
4. Interpret positive and negative rational numbers on number lines and coordinate planes, including opposites, order, and absolute value as distance from zero.
5. Write, evaluate, and interpret numerical and algebraic expressions with variables, exponents, and properties of operations.
6. Generate equivalent expressions and solve one-variable equations and inequalities in Grade 6 contexts.
7. Connect dependent and independent variables across verbal descriptions, tables, graphs, and equations.
8. Solve measurement and geometry problems involving polygon area, coordinate-plane polygons, volume, surface area, and nets with correct units.
9. Analyze statistical questions and data distributions using displays, center, variability, shape, clusters, gaps, and context.
10. Apply mathematical process habits: model situations, choose representations, explain reasoning, use feedback, and test whether answers are reasonable.

## 3. Concept And Skill Progression

The level begins by stabilizing number-system readiness: place-value reasoning, estimation, decimal operations, fraction magnitude, fraction multiplication meanings, common denominators, and division interpretations. This opening work is not a full elementary review; it prepares students to use arithmetic with enough confidence that new Grade 6 ideas do not collapse under weak computation or fragile magnitude sense.

Students then develop ratio reasoning as multiplicative comparison. The sequence starts with concrete ratio language and part-to-part, part-to-whole, and rate contexts before moving into tape diagrams, equivalent ratios, tables, and double number lines. Students learn that equivalent ratios are made by multiplying or dividing both quantities by the same factor, not by adding the same amount.

Rates and percents come after ratio foundations. Students learn unit rate as "per 1" and percent as "per 100," using double number lines, tables, equations, and carefully labeled units. Percent stays at the Grade 6 boundary: benchmark percents, percent of a quantity, and percent as a rate per 100, not full Grade 7 percent increase/decrease or chained tax/tip/discount procedures.

Fraction division and decimal fluency follow because they demand strong magnitude reasoning and will support later rate, geometry, and data work. Students interpret fraction division through measurement, sharing, tape diagrams, area models, and equations before connecting to efficient procedures. Decimal and multi-digit whole-number operations are practiced with estimation and reasonableness checks so procedures remain tied to quantity.

The number system then extends to negative rational numbers. Students use horizontal and vertical number lines, real-world contexts, opposites, ordering, absolute value, and all four quadrants of the coordinate plane. The goal is rational-number meaning and interpretation, not full signed-operation fluency.

Algebraic reasoning develops from numerical expressions into variables, exponents with whole-number bases, expression evaluation, properties, equivalent expressions, equations, inequalities, and dependent/independent relationships. Variables should be interpreted as quantities that can vary or be unknown, not as decorative labels.

Geometry and measurement come after algebra and coordinate-plane work so students can use formulas, variables, units, nets, decompositions, and coordinates meaningfully. Statistics follows with real data questions, displays, center, variability, and distribution language. The level ends with applied modeling and financial contexts that interleave ratios, operations, rational numbers, expressions, geometry, data, and reasonableness.

## 4. Vocabulary, Representation, Model, Text, And Form Progression

Early vocabulary should stabilize arithmetic and magnitude language: factor, multiple, quotient, divisor, dividend, decimal place, benchmark, estimate, reciprocal, product, common denominator, numerator, denominator, reasonable, and unit.

Ratio vocabulary should progress from informal comparisons to formal precision: ratio, "to," "for every," part-to-part, part-to-whole, rate, unit rate, "per," equivalent ratio, ratio table, double number line, percent, per 100, tape diagram, and multiplicative comparison. Representations should move from labeled pictures and tape diagrams to ratio tables, double number lines, equations, and verbal interpretations.

Rational-number vocabulary should include positive, negative, opposite, rational number, coordinate plane, x-axis, y-axis, origin, quadrant, ordered pair, absolute value, distance from zero, greater than, less than, elevation, debt, temperature, and balance. Models should move from one-dimensional number lines to vertical number lines, context tables, and four-quadrant coordinate grids.

Algebra language should grow carefully: numerical expression, algebraic expression, variable, coefficient readiness, term, factor, exponent, base, evaluate, equivalent expressions, property, distribute, equation, solution, inequality, solution set, dependent variable, independent variable, table, graph, rule, and relationship. Forms should include expressions, equations, inequalities, tables, graphs, and short written interpretations.

Geometry and statistics vocabulary should include area, base, height, triangle, parallelogram, polygon, coordinate polygon, volume, rectangular prism, fractional edge length, net, surface area, square units, cubic units, statistical question, variability, distribution, dot plot, histogram, box plot, mean, median, range, interquartile range, mean absolute deviation, cluster, gap, peak, and outlier. Text forms should stay compact but increasingly ask students to interpret scenarios, compare models, explain choices, and revise faulty reasoning.

## 5. Fluency Expectations

Grade 6 fluency should balance accuracy, efficiency, flexibility, and meaning. Students should fluently divide multi-digit whole numbers and operate with multi-digit decimals using standard algorithms, while also estimating before or after computation to judge reasonableness. They should be increasingly efficient with fraction arithmetic needed for Grade 6 contexts, especially fraction division, but conceptual interpretation remains part of mastery.

Students should fluently use ratio tables, double number lines, benchmark percents, common unit rates, equivalent ratio scaling, order of operations with exponents, expression evaluation for simple substitutions, and one-step equation or inequality solving. Fluency practice should be short, spaced, and mixed after meaning is secure; speed alone should not serve as the main evidence of mastery.

## 6. Conceptual Understanding Expectations

Students should understand ratios as relationships between quantities, not just fractions written with a colon. They should know why multiplicative scaling preserves a ratio and why additive changes usually do not. They should understand that unit rates depend on which quantity is "per 1," and that percent describes a rate per 100.

Students should understand fraction division as a question about how many groups, what size groups, or how quantities compare, depending on context. They should connect the invert-and-multiply procedure to models and equations instead of treating it as the meaning.

Students should understand rational numbers as positions and distances on a number line. Negative values are less than positive values even when their absolute values are larger. Absolute value is distance from zero; the context determines whether that distance, direction, or signed value is being asked for.

Students should understand algebraic expressions, equations, inequalities, and relationships as tools for representing quantities. The equals sign states equality, an equation's solution makes the statement true, an inequality can have many solutions, and a table, graph, equation, and verbal rule can describe the same relationship.

Students should understand measurement dimensions: area covers a surface in square units, volume fills space in cubic units, and surface area totals the areas of faces. Statistics should be understood as reasoning about variability, not simply computing an average.

## 7. Application And Transfer Expectations

Students should apply Grade 6 mathematics in contexts involving recipes, prices, speed, unit conversion, percent benchmarks, sharing, packing, budgets, income and expenses, coordinate maps, floor plans, nets, data from class or community questions, and short financial decisions. Contexts should be concrete enough for Grade 6 readers but varied enough that students cannot rely on key words or a single diagram type.

Transfer should be visible when students choose among a tape diagram, double number line, table, equation, number line, graph, coordinate plane, net, data display, or written explanation without being told which representation to use. Students should connect answers to units, labels, and reasonableness, and they should be able to explain why a plausible wrong answer reflects a misconception.

## 8. Review And Spacing Plan

Review should be cumulative across the level. Whole-number and decimal operations, fraction magnitude, common denominators, multiplication/division meanings, number-line reasoning, coordinate plotting, and basic equation language should appear in short warmups and applied problems before they become central again in later units.

Ratio reasoning should be spaced after Units 2 and 3 through percent, rate, data, geometry, and financial contexts. Fraction division and decimal operations should return in rate, measurement, and modeling tasks. Rational-number ordering and coordinate-plane reasoning should reappear in geometry and data contexts. Algebra vocabulary should be retrieved inside geometry formulas, statistics summaries, and financial modeling.

Review should be diagnostic, not random. Distractors and feedback should name the relevant structure: multiplicative comparison, unit rate order, percent per 100, fraction division meaning, distance from zero, equality, solution set, dependent variable, square units, cubic units, variability, or reasonableness.

## 9. Mastery Evidence For The End Of The Level

End-of-level mastery should be evidenced by a balanced body of mixed tasks. A student who has mastered Grade 6 Math can solve ratio, rate, percent, fraction division, decimal operation, rational-number, expression, equation, inequality, relationship, geometry, statistics, and financial problems using accurate procedures and meaningful representations.

The student can move among representations: ratio table to double number line, fraction model to equation, expression to verbal meaning, table to graph, net to surface area, data display to summary, and context to mathematical model. The student can check units, explain a strategy, identify an unreasonable answer, and correct common misconceptions in worked examples.

Strongest mastery evidence includes transfer across domains: using a unit rate in a budget decision, interpreting negative coordinates in a geometry problem, evaluating expressions inside formulas, deciding whether mean or median better describes data, and selecting a model for an unfamiliar but Grade 6-appropriate situation.
