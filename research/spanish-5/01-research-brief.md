# Spanish 5 Research Brief

Track: Spanish  
Level: Level 5  
Intended student profile: learners with Intermediate Low foundations who can use connected sentences and short paragraphs in familiar contexts. This level should serve as an Intermediate Mid capstone for compact school-based Spanish.

This brief defines outcomes and instructional priorities only. It does not define units, lessons, or questions.

## 1. End-Of-Level Outcomes

By the end of Spanish 5, students should be able to interpret longer connected reading/listening on familiar topics; sustain simple conversations across familiar domains; present short organized paragraphs; express opinions with reasons and simple evidence; make polite requests using conditional phrases; understand commands and present perfect exposure; use common subjunctive chunks for wishes/needs without mastering the full system; discuss media, community, culture, and everyday problems; compare cultures with nuance; and complete project-based communication tasks.

## 2. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should control present-tense communication, simple past narration, near future, direct object pronouns in controlled contexts, comparisons, familiar-topic vocabulary, short paragraph writing, and interpretive strategies for short connected resources.

## 3. Major Concept Clusters

- **Intermediate Mid Capstone Communication:** short paragraphs, sustained familiar conversations, and presentations with organization.
- **Opinion And Evidence:** preferences, reasons, examples, simple support, and respectful disagreement.
- **Functional Language:** polite requests, commands, advice, needs/wishes chunks, problem-solving, and community interactions.
- **Connected Interpretive Work:** short articles, posts, announcements, interviews, videos, charts, and cultural resources with support.
- **Culture Comparisons:** regional diversity, media, community life, celebrations, school, public services, and social practices.

## 4. Recommended Progression

Start with cumulative review of present, past, future, and paragraph connectors. Build interpretive stamina with short connected resources. Add opinion-with-reason routines, then polite requests, commands, advice, and community/media topics. Treat present perfect and subjunctive as useful chunks/exposure, not full grammar systems. End with project-based presentational/interpersonal tasks requiring interpretation, planning, and revision.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Intermediate communication means native-like accuracy.
- Opinions need no evidence.
- Politeness phrases translate directly from English.
- Commands, subjunctive, and conditional are just interchangeable "advanced" forms.
- Culture comparison means ranking cultures.
- Longer texts must be understood word for word.
- Project work can ignore grammar accuracy because the topic is interesting.

## 6. Developmental Constraints And Reading/Load Concerns

Use authentic or authentic-looking resources with glosses and clear tasks. Keep projects bounded. Avoid overwhelming students with multiple new grammar systems at once. Use correction selectively so communication remains central. Allow drafts and rehearsal for presentations. Culture work must avoid stereotypes and include regional variation.

## 7. Assessment Implications

Assess integrated modes: interpret a resource, respond in conversation, and present a short organized message. Use passage/listening questions, dialogue-builder, constructed response, speaking-prompt, multi-blank cloze for focused forms, and project checkpoints. Distractors should reveal evidence-free opinions, literal politeness translation, overgeneralized culture claims, wrong command/request form, and misunderstanding of main idea versus detail.

## 8. Spiral Review Priorities

Present and past narration, near future, connectors, questions, direct objects, comparisons, ser/estar, gustar-like verbs, opinion frames, reasons/evidence, commands/request chunks, culture comparisons, interpretive strategies, pronunciation, and accent-aware spelling.

## 9. Concepts That Should Not Be Taught Yet

Avoid full subjunctive mastery, complex object-pronoun clusters, literary analysis in Spanish, advanced debate, extended research papers in Spanish, nuanced political controversy, and unscripted discussion of unfamiliar abstract topics as required mastery.
