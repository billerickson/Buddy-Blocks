# French 2 Research Brief

Track: French  
Level: Level 2  
Intended student profile: continuing novice learners who can use memorized words, phrases, and simple modeled sentences from French 1. This level should expand Novice Mid communication across familiar topics.

This brief defines outcomes and instructional priorities only. It does not define units, lessons, or questions.

## 1. End-Of-Level Outcomes

By the end of French 2, students should be able to understand and use expanded novice language about school, classes, supplies, people, descriptions, feelings, food requests, places, directions, dates, time, and routines. They should ask and answer predictable questions, interpret short readings/listening for key words and main ideas, produce simple sentence-level descriptions with support, use common present-tense action chunks, and make simple cultural comparisons across Francophone contexts.

## 2. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should know French 1 greetings, classroom phrases, numbers, calendar words, family, likes/dislikes, food, places, high-frequency chunks, basic articles/gender, simple adjective agreement, être/avoir/il y a/aller/faire/aimer chunks, common question words, negation with ne...pas, tu/vous, and basic sound-spelling patterns.

## 3. Major Concept Clusters

- **Novice Mid Communication:** predictable exchanges, short descriptions, classroom/school routines, requests, preferences, and directions.
- **Expanded Vocabulary Domains:** larger numbers, dates/time, classes, supplies, people, feelings, food, places, directions, and activities.
- **French Sound-Spelling Growth:** accents, final silent consonants, nasal vowels, liaison exposure, elision, and common vowel combinations.
- **Present-Time Chunks:** high-frequency verbs, negation, question frames, articles, adjective agreement, and early partitive chunks for food.
- **Culture In Context:** school, meals, schedules, greetings, public spaces, and Francophone regions.

## 4. Recommended Progression

Start with cumulative French 1 review and listening routines. Expand numbers, dates, time, and school contexts. Add descriptions of people, feelings, and places, then food requests and directions. Continue sound-spelling practice in every domain. Introduce present-tense action chunks through communication before charts. End with short reading/listening and supported sentence production.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Students may pronounce every written final letter.
- Students may omit articles because English sometimes does.
- Students may treat accents as optional decoration.
- Students may overuse English word order for adjectives and questions.
- Students may treat `être`, `avoir`, `faire`, `aller`, and `il y a` as interchangeable.
- Students may ignore tu/vous politeness and number.
- Students may read cognates without checking context.

## 6. Developmental Constraints And Reading/Load Concerns

French listening and spelling load is high for English speakers; use repeated audio and visual supports. Keep input short and contextualized. Do not require full verb charts as the main object. Use accent-tolerant scoring when meaning is the target, but always model correct accents. Keep culture embedded in language tasks, including Francophone communities beyond France.

## 7. Assessment Implications

Assess interpretive listening/reading, predictable interpersonal responses, sound-spelling recognition, and supported presentational sentences. Use flash-card ladders, listening-question, dialogue-builder, passage-question, fill-blank, match-pairs, and short text-input. Distractors should reveal wrong article/gender, false cognate, pronounced silent letter, tu/vous mismatch, wrong question word, or negation-frame error.

## 8. Spiral Review Priorities

Greetings, classroom phrases, tu/vous, numbers/calendar/weather, articles and adjective agreement, être/avoir/il y a/aller/faire/aimer, question words, ne...pas, food/place vocabulary, pronunciation, accents, liaison exposure, cognates, and Francophone culture.

## 9. Concepts That Should Not Be Taught Yet

Avoid full present-tense paradigms for all verb groups, passé composé/imparfait, object pronouns, full partitive system mastery, subjunctive, conditional, extended paragraphs, and unscripted conversation beyond familiar exchanges.
