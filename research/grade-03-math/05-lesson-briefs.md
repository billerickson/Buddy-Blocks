# Math - Grade 3 Lesson Briefs

Sources: accepted level blueprint, course map, and unit design briefs in `research/grade-03-math/`.

## Unit 1: Grade 3 Number Readiness

### Lesson 1: Hundreds, Tens, And Ones

- **Teaching goal:** Rebuild place-value understanding for three-digit numbers as hundreds, tens, and ones.
- **Student outcome:** The student can read, write, build, and decompose three-digit numbers using digit-value language.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Base-ten drawings, expanded form, and place-value charts show that a digit's value depends on its place.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Counting by ones and tens, two-digit place value, and basic comparison language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating each digit as its face value only; reading 406 as "forty-six"; ignoring zero as a placeholder.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a three-digit number, the student can identify each digit's value, write an expanded form, and choose a matching model.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for digit-value checks; `match-pairs` for numbers to expanded forms; `text-input` for numeric or expanded-form production after models.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Start with one number built three ways: base-ten picture, place-value chart, and expanded form. Ask what changes if the same digit moves places.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep numbers to three digits; avoid dense word problems; use clear spacing in expanded notation.

### Lesson 3: Addition Strategies That Still Work

- **Teaching goal:** Refresh flexible addition strategies for sums within 100 and supported sums within 1,000.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose a place-value, make-ten, compensation, or number-line strategy to add accurately.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Addition can be made easier by decomposing addends and preserving the total.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Sums within 20, tens and ones, open number lines, and expanded form.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Counting all from 1 for every sum; adding digits without place value; changing one addend without compensating.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can solve an addition expression, identify a useful strategy, and explain why the total stays the same.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for strategy choice; `fill-blank` for missing decomposed parts; `text-input` for final sums.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare 48 + 25 by jumping 20 then 5 on a number line and by adding tens and ones.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep stories brief; prioritize numerical expressions and diagrams before multi-step contexts.

### Lesson 4: Subtraction As Difference And Take-Away

- **Teaching goal:** Review subtraction as both taking away and finding the difference.
- **Student outcome:** The student can solve subtraction problems using count-back, count-up, place-value, or number-line reasoning.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A number line can show subtraction as moving back or as the distance between two numbers.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Addition facts, comparing quantities, open number lines, and place-value decompositions.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Believing subtraction always means "take away"; subtracting smaller digits from larger digits regardless of place; forgetting subtraction can check addition.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can match a subtraction expression to a take-away or comparison situation and solve with a valid model.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for meaning of a context; `match-pairs` for situations to expressions; `text-input` for differences.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use one pair of numbers, such as 83 and 57, to show both "83 minus 57" and "how far from 57 to 83."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar contexts and avoid unnecessary details; keep number-line jumps visible and labeled.

### Lesson 5: Equations, Boxes, And Balance

- **Teaching goal:** Strengthen the idea that equations show equal relationships, not just answer slots.
- **Student outcome:** The student can solve equations with a box or blank in different positions and explain what makes both sides equal.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** The equals sign means "has the same value as"; a box can stand for an unknown number.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Addition and subtraction facts, basic equation reading, and comparison of equal quantities.
- **Likely misconceptions:** The answer must come after the equals sign; solve by combining every visible number; the box always means the final answer.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can solve equations such as 36 + __ = 52 and __ - 18 = 24 and justify the unknown value.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for missing numbers; `multiple-choice` for true equations; `text-input` for unknown values.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Present 8 + 5 = __ and __ = 8 + 5, then ask what stayed the same.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep equations visually simple; avoid variables or formal algebra language beyond box, blank, and unknown.

### Lesson 6: Skip Counting With Structure

- **Teaching goal:** Connect skip counting to equal jumps and equal groups rather than rote chanting alone.
- **Student outcome:** The student can skip count by 2s, 5s, 10s, and 100s and identify the equal jump or group size.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Skip counting shows repeated equal increases on a number line or in equal groups.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Counting sequences, addition of equal numbers, and number-line direction.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Memorizing a sequence without knowing the jump size; switching jump sizes midstream; counting the starting number as the first jump.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can complete a skip-count sequence, name the jump size, and connect it to equal groups.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for sequence order; `fill-blank` for missing terms; `multiple-choice` for identifying the jump size.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show 0, 5, 10, 15 on a number line and label each jump as "+5."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep sequences short; use consistent start points before including nonzero starts.

### Lesson 8: Choose A Model And Check

- **Teaching goal:** Establish the habit of choosing a useful representation and checking whether the answer makes sense.
- **Student outcome:** The student can select a number line, strip diagram, equation, or array for a simple problem and explain the choice.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Different models reveal different structures; checking connects the answer back to the situation.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Unit 1 models, addition/subtraction strategies, equal groups, and basic equation reading.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Using the most recent model for every problem; drawing without connecting to quantities; checking only means recalculating quickly.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses a fitting model for a one-step problem and identifies a reasonable check.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for best model; `match-pairs` for problem type to representation; `constructed-response` for a short explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show the same problem with two possible models and discuss which one makes the relationship clearer.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one-step contexts with controlled language; require brief explanations, not long written justifications.

## Unit 2: Multiplication As Equal Groups And Arrays

### Lesson 1: Equal Groups Or Not?

- **Teaching goal:** Help students identify when multiplication is appropriate by checking for equal-size groups.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether a situation shows equal groups and explain why multiplication does or does not fit.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Multiplication requires groups of the same size.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Counting, addition, equal groups from Unit 1, and basic comparison language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Multiplying any groups even when the sizes differ; focusing on total objects only; assuming repeated contexts are always multiplication.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a picture or short story, the student can classify it as equal groups or not and name the group size when valid.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for valid equal groups; `match-pairs` for examples and non-examples; `constructed-response` for a one-sentence reason.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Contrast 3 bags with 4 apples each and 3 bags with 4, 5, and 3 apples.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use small group counts and clear pictures; avoid hidden quantities in early prompts.

### Lesson 2: Groups Of A Number

- **Teaching goal:** Represent multiplication language as "number of groups" and "size of each group."
- **Student outcome:** The student can model and describe statements such as 5 groups of 3 using drawings and repeated addition.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** "5 groups of 3" means five equal groups, each with three items.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Equal groups, counting totals, skip counting, and repeated addition readiness.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Reversing groups and group size in the story; drawing unequal groups; counting the number of groups as the total.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can draw or choose a model for a groups-of statement and write the matching repeated addition.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for phrase to model; `fill-blank` for "___ groups of ___"; `text-input` for total after support.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Build one "groups of" statement physically or visually before writing any equation.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep group numbers from 2 to 6 at first; use familiar objects and short phrases.

### Lesson 4: From Repeated Addition To Multiplication

- **Teaching goal:** Introduce multiplication equations as compact notation for repeated equal addends.
- **Student outcome:** The student can translate between repeated addition and multiplication equations for equal groups.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** 4 + 4 + 4 can be written as 3 x 4 because there are 3 equal addends of 4.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Equal groups, repeated addition, factors as group count and group size.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Writing multiplication for unequal addends; using the total as a factor; thinking multiplication and repeated addition are unrelated.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can match a repeated-addition expression to a multiplication equation and explain the factors.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for repeated addition to multiplication; `multiple-choice` for matching equation; `text-input` for product.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show repeated addition first, circle the equal addends, then name how many addends and how large each addend is.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use the `x` symbol consistently; do not introduce properties yet beyond direct translation.

### Lesson 6: Match Stories, Arrays, And Equations

- **Teaching goal:** Build translation among multiplication contexts, visual models, and symbolic equations.
- **Student outcome:** The student can match a story, array, repeated addition expression, and multiplication equation that represent the same situation.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** The same multiplication structure can appear as words, pictures, repeated addition, or an equation.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Equal groups, arrays, repeated addition, multiplication equations, and factor meanings.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Matching by numbers alone rather than relationships; ignoring group size in the story; choosing an array with the right total but wrong structure.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can correctly match all representations and state what the factors mean.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for representation matching; `multiple-choice` for best equation; `constructed-response` for a short factor explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use one story and build its representation chain from context to drawing to equation.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep stories one sentence; avoid two-step contexts in this translation lesson.

### Lesson 7: Explain The Factors

- **Teaching goal:** Make factor interpretation explicit in multiplication contexts.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain what each factor represents in a story, array, or equation.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Factors are not just numbers; in context, one factor can name how many groups and the other the size of each group.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Multiplication equations, equal groups, arrays, and row/column language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Saying both factors simply mean "the answer"; swapping factors without understanding the story; failing to distinguish factor from product.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can label each factor and the product in a multiplication situation.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for factor meaning; `fill-blank` for labeled equations; `constructed-response` for a sentence explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Present 6 x 4 in two different contexts and compare how the factor meanings are described.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep explanation frames available; focus on one equation and one context at a time.

### Lesson 8: Multiplication In New Contexts

- **Teaching goal:** Apply equal-group multiplication reasoning in unfamiliar but controlled contexts.
- **Student outcome:** The student can recognize multiplication structure in new situations and solve using a model or equation.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Multiplication can model any situation with equal groups, not only classroom arrays.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Equal groups, arrays, multiplication equations, factor interpretation, and skip counting.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Relying on surface words instead of equal groups; using addition when equal groups could be structured; overlooking unequal groups.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies equal groups in a new context, writes a matching equation, and finds the product.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for short contexts; `multiple-choice` for equation choice; `text-input` for products after model support.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use contexts such as packs, rows, teams, shelves, and tickets, and ask what the equal groups are before calculating.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep contexts familiar and one-step; avoid large numbers or embedded irrelevant details.

## Unit 3: Division As Sharing, Grouping, And Unknown Factors

### Lesson 1: Sharing Into Equal Groups

- **Teaching goal:** Introduce division as sharing a total into a known number of equal groups.
- **Student outcome:** The student can model a sharing division problem and identify how many are in each group.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** When the number of groups is known, division can find the size of each equal group.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Equal groups, fair sharing, multiplication as groups, and counting totals.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Making unequal shares; treating the number of groups as the quotient; ignoring all objects must be used in basic cases.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can share a total equally, write or select the division equation, and interpret the quotient as group size.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for correct sharing model; `fill-blank` for quotient; `constructed-response` for interpreting the answer.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show 12 counters shared into 3 plates and ask how many are on each plate.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use totals that divide evenly; keep group counts small and diagrams clear.

### Lesson 2: Making Equal Groups

- **Teaching goal:** Introduce division as making equal groups of a known size.
- **Student outcome:** The student can model a grouping division problem and identify how many groups can be made.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** When the size of each group is known, division can find the number of groups.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Equal groups, skip counting, multiplication factors, and sharing division from the prior lesson.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Confusing group size with number of groups; stopping before all objects are grouped; treating grouping and sharing as identical.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can group a total into equal-size groups, select the equation, and explain what the quotient counts.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for grouping model; `text-input` for number of groups; `match-pairs` for context to division meaning.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use 18 objects packed 3 per bag and ask how many bags can be filled.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use visual grouping support; avoid remainders and ambiguous leftover contexts.

### Lesson 3: What Does The Quotient Mean?

- **Teaching goal:** Help students interpret a quotient based on the division situation.
- **Student outcome:** The student can state whether the quotient represents group size or number of groups.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** The same number sentence can answer different kinds of questions depending on the context.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Sharing division, grouping division, equal groups, and division equations.
- **Likely misconceptions:** The quotient always means "how many in each group"; the equation alone tells the full story; labels are unnecessary.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a division story and equation, the student identifies what the quotient represents and supplies an appropriate unit or label.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for quotient interpretation; `match-pairs` for story to answer meaning; `constructed-response` for a short label explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare 20 divided by 4 as 4 teams sharing 20 players and as groups of 4 players.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep both comparison stories parallel; emphasize labels in answer choices.

### Lesson 5: Division As An Unknown Factor

- **Teaching goal:** Make division a question about a missing multiplication factor.
- **Student outcome:** The student can solve division facts by asking which factor makes the related product.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** 32 divided by 4 asks, "4 times what equals 32?"
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Multiplication facts, factors and products, arrays with missing sides, and division notation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Guessing quotient facts separately from multiplication; using addition instead of factor reasoning; reversing dividend and divisor.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student writes or chooses a related multiplication equation to solve a division fact.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for division fact to related multiplication; `fill-blank` for missing factor; `text-input` for quotient.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Put the division fact and related multiplication sentence side by side with the same three numbers.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use facts within current multiplication readiness; avoid mixed symbolic density at first.

### Lesson 6: Fact Families For Multiplication And Division

- **Teaching goal:** Build multiplication/division fact families from one equal-group relationship.
- **Student outcome:** The student can generate or complete related multiplication and division equations using the same three numbers.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A fact family shows how two factors and a product are connected by multiplication and division.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Multiplication equations, division equations, unknown factors, and commutativity for multiplication as exposure.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Including unrelated numbers; writing invalid division turnarounds; assuming every number order makes a true equation.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can complete a fact family such as 6, 7, 42 with valid multiplication and division equations.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for related facts; `multi-blank-cloze` for completing equation sets; `multiple-choice` for invalid fact-family member.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Start from one array and rotate between equations while keeping the same total and factors visible.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep fact families to basic facts; use equation spacing so dividend, divisor, and quotient are not visually crowded.

### Lesson 7: Choose Sharing Or Grouping

- **Teaching goal:** Help students decide which division interpretation fits a problem situation.
- **Student outcome:** The student can classify division contexts as sharing or grouping and solve with an appropriate model.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Division can answer "how many in each group?" or "how many groups?"
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Sharing division, grouping division, quotient interpretation, and unknown-factor reasoning.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating all division as sharing; selecting an equation without interpreting the question; omitting labels from the quotient.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student sorts mixed division problems, solves them, and labels the quotient correctly.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for interpretation choice; `match-pairs` for context to model; `constructed-response` for quotient label.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Ask students to underline the question words that reveal whether group count or group size is unknown.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short, direct contexts; avoid remainders, multi-step contexts, and tricky wording.

## Unit 4: Multiplication And Division Strategies

### Lesson 1: Anchor Facts: Twos, Fives, And Tens

- **Teaching goal:** Establish reliable multiplication and division anchor facts for 2s, 5s, and 10s.
- **Student outcome:** The student can solve and explain facts involving 2, 5, and 10 using patterns, skip counting, or arrays.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Anchor facts are easy-to-see structures that support harder facts later.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Skip counting, equal groups, arrays, and related division facts.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Reciting patterns without connecting them to groups; confusing 5s and 10s; treating division facts as unrelated.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can solve mixed 2, 5, and 10 multiplication/division facts and identify the supporting pattern.
- **Recommended question types:** `flash-card` for spaced retrieval after exposure; `multiple-choice` for pattern checks; `text-input` for products and quotients.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show arrays and skip-count sequences for 2s, 5s, and 10s side by side, then name why they are useful anchors.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep focus on one anchor family at a time before mixing; avoid timed pressure in first exposure.

### Lesson 2: Doubles And Near Doubles

- **Teaching goal:** Use doubles to derive related multiplication facts.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use known doubles, such as 4 x 2 or 6 x 2, to reason about nearby facts.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Doubles and near doubles can be represented by arrays split into equal or nearly equal parts.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Multiplication as equal groups, 2s facts, addition doubles, and array splitting.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Doubling the wrong factor; treating near doubles as exact doubles; losing one group when adjusting.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can explain a fact such as 3 x 6 as 2 x 6 plus one more 6.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for best derived fact; `fill-blank` for decompositions; `constructed-response` for a short strategy explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Split a 3 by 6 array into a 2 by 6 part and a 1 by 6 part.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep arrays visible; use one-step strategy explanations with sentence frames.

### Lesson 3: Turnaround Facts In Multiplication

- **Teaching goal:** Teach commutativity for multiplication while preserving contextual meaning.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use turnaround facts to solve products and explain that the product stays the same.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** 3 x 7 and 7 x 3 have the same product, even if the story may describe groups differently.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Factors, products, arrays, row and column language, and multiplication equations.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Applying commutativity to division; saying the two multiplication stories must be identical; confusing factors with product.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can match turnaround facts, use one to solve the other, and identify a division non-example.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for turnaround facts; `multiple-choice` for valid commutative statements; `constructed-response` for context distinction.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Rotate an array and ask what changed and what did not change.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Contrast multiplication and division with small numbers; avoid formal property language until after concrete examples.

### Lesson 4: Break Apart A Factor

- **Teaching goal:** Use distributive reasoning to solve harder multiplication facts.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decompose one factor, find partial products, and combine them to get the total product.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A larger array can be split into two smaller arrays whose products add to the original product.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Arrays, addition within 100, known multiplication facts, and factor meaning.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Splitting both factors without tracking parts; adding factors instead of products; losing rows or columns in the split.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can solve a fact such as 7 x 6 by breaking 7 into 5 + 2 or 6 into 3 + 3.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for valid decompositions; `multi-blank-cloze` for partial products; `constructed-response` for strategy reasoning.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Draw a 7 by 6 array and physically mark a split into a 5 by 6 and 2 by 6 array.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use diagrams for every new decomposition; keep partial products within known fact families.

### Lesson 5: Nines And Neighbor Facts

- **Teaching goal:** Derive nines and other harder facts from tens or nearby known facts.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use 10 times a number minus one group, or another neighbor fact, to find a product.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A difficult fact can be close to an easier fact, and the difference can be adjusted.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Tens facts, subtraction within 100, arrays, and factor decomposition.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Subtracting 1 instead of one group; using a neighbor fact without adjusting; treating patterns as magic rules.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can solve 9 x 6 as 10 x 6 minus 6 and explain the adjustment.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for missing adjustment; `multiple-choice` for correct strategy; `text-input` for final product.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show 10 rows of 6 with one row crossed out to make 9 rows of 6.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid long pattern lists; make the removed or added group visible.

### Lesson 6: Division From Related Multiplication

- **Teaching goal:** Use multiplication facts and unknown-factor reasoning to solve division.
- **Student outcome:** The student can solve division facts by naming the related multiplication fact.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A division fact asks for a missing factor in a related multiplication equation.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Multiplication facts, fact families, quotient meaning, and unknown-factor equations.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Recalling division as isolated facts; swapping dividend and divisor; forgetting what the quotient represents.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can solve 48 divided by 6 by stating 6 x 8 = 48 and labeling the quotient.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for division to multiplication; `fill-blank` for missing factors; `text-input` for quotients.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Place a division fact under its multiplication fact and circle the missing factor.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep numbers within known facts; use clear division phrasing rather than dense notation alone.

### Lesson 7: Choose The Most Useful Strategy

- **Teaching goal:** Develop judgment about which multiplication or division strategy fits a fact.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose and justify a useful strategy for a given fact.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Strategy choice depends on the numbers and structure, not on a fixed rule.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Anchor facts, doubles, turnaround facts, decomposition, nines strategies, and related division.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Using the same strategy for every fact; choosing the fastest-looking shortcut without understanding; ignoring division relationships.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects a strategy that fits a fact and explains the first step.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for strategy selection; `match-pairs` for fact to strategy; `constructed-response` for brief justification.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Present three facts and model thinking aloud: "Which known fact is closest?"
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use concise strategy names; limit explanations to one or two sentences.

### Lesson 8: Mixed Fact Retrieval With Meaning

- **Teaching goal:** Practice mixed multiplication and division facts while keeping meaning and relationships visible.
- **Student outcome:** The student can solve mixed facts accurately and identify a supporting related fact or strategy when needed.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Fluency includes accurate recall, flexible strategy use, and retention over time.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Unit 4 strategies and multiplication/division meanings from Units 2-3.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Speed matters more than accuracy; one correct answer proves mastery; division facts do not need labels or related facts.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student answers mixed facts accurately across delayed practice and can explain selected non-automatic facts.
- **Recommended question types:** `flash-card` for retrieval; `text-input` for products and quotients; `multiple-choice` for related-fact support.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Frame practice as "retrieve if you know it, reason if you do not."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep practice bursts short; do not combine speed pressure with new strategy demands.

## Unit 5: Place Value, Rounding, And Whole-Number Operations

### Lesson 1: Nearest Ten On A Number Line

- **Teaching goal:** Teach rounding to the nearest ten through distance to benchmarks.
- **Student outcome:** The student can round two- and three-digit numbers to the nearest ten using a number line.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Rounding chooses the closest ten, with halfway cases handled consistently.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Tens, ones, open number lines, comparison, and benchmark numbers.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Rounding by changing digits randomly; looking at the wrong place; thinking any nearby ten is correct.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can place a number between two tens, choose the nearest ten, and explain the choice.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for nearest ten; `fill-blank` for missing rounded value; `constructed-response` for one-sentence distance reasoning.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Place 47 between 40 and 50 and compare the distance to each endpoint.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use visual number lines first; avoid long contextual prompts until rounding meaning is clear.

### Lesson 2: Nearest Hundred And Reasonableness

- **Teaching goal:** Extend rounding to the nearest hundred and connect rounding to reasonableness.
- **Student outcome:** The student can round to the nearest hundred and use the rounded number to judge approximate size.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Hundreds benchmarks help estimate and check whether an answer is in the right range.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Hundreds and tens, nearest-ten rounding, open number lines, and comparison.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Rounding 349 to 400 because 9 is large; ignoring the midpoint between hundreds; treating rounding as exact computation.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can round a number such as 362 to the nearest hundred and use it to judge a reasonable estimate.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for rounded values; `order-items` for benchmark placement; `constructed-response` for reasonableness checks.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use 300, 350, and 400 on a number line to discuss which side of halfway a number lies on.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep numbers three-digit; avoid combining nearest ten and nearest hundred until each is secure.

### Lesson 3: Add Within 1,000 With Place Value

- **Teaching goal:** Connect multi-digit addition to place-value units and written methods.
- **Student outcome:** The student can add within 1,000 using models, expanded form, or a written method and explain regrouping when it occurs.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Add hundreds with hundreds, tens with tens, and ones with ones; regroup when a place has enough for a larger unit.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Three-digit place value, addition within 100, expanded form, and regrouping readiness.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Adding digits without attending to place; forgetting regrouped tens or hundreds; treating the algorithm as unexplained steps.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student solves addition within 1,000 and identifies how place value supports the method.
- **Recommended question types:** `text-input` for sums; `multi-blank-cloze` for expanded-form addition; `multiple-choice` for regrouping explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Model 246 + 178 with expanded form before showing the compact written version.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Separate computation from word-problem reading at first; align digits clearly.

### Lesson 4: Subtract Within 1,000 With Regrouping

- **Teaching goal:** Connect multi-digit subtraction to decomposing place-value units.
- **Student outcome:** The student can subtract within 1,000 using place-value reasoning and explain necessary regrouping.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Regrouping in subtraction means trading one larger unit for ten smaller units.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Place value, subtraction within 100, base-ten models, and inverse addition checks.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Subtracting smaller digits from larger digits regardless of position; regrouping without changing the value; losing track of zeros.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student solves a subtraction problem with regrouping and can describe at least one trade.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for correct regrouping step; `text-input` for differences; `constructed-response` for a brief trade explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use base-ten drawings to show why 302 - 158 requires decomposing a hundred into tens and ones.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use clear vertical alignment; introduce zeros carefully with visual support.

### Lesson 5: Use Inverse Operations To Check

- **Teaching goal:** Use addition and subtraction as inverse operations to verify answers.
- **Student outcome:** The student can check a sum with subtraction and a difference with addition.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Inverse operations undo each other and can confirm whether a result fits.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Addition/subtraction within 1,000, equation balance, and place-value computation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Repeating the same operation is always a check; inverse checks can use unrelated numbers; a check does not need to match the original quantities.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can select or write a valid inverse check for a completed addition or subtraction problem.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for valid check equation; `match-pairs` for problem to check; `text-input` for missing value in the check.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show 438 - 176 = 262, then ask why 262 + 176 should return to 438.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep checking equations visually near the original; avoid two-step word problems in this lesson.

### Lesson 6: Multiply By Multiples Of 10

- **Teaching goal:** Connect basic multiplication facts to multiplying one-digit numbers by multiples of 10.
- **Student outcome:** The student can solve facts such as 6 x 40 by using 6 x 4 tens.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** 6 x 40 means 6 groups of 4 tens, or 24 tens, which is 240.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Multiplication facts, place value, tens as units, and expanded form.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Adding a zero without understanding; multiplying only the visible nonzero digits and dropping units; treating 6 x 40 as unrelated to 6 x 4.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can explain a multiple-of-10 product using tens language and compute accurately.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for "___ tens"; `multiple-choice` for place-value explanation; `text-input` for products.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Build 4 tens, then make 6 equal groups and count the total tens before writing 240.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use multiples of 10 through 90; avoid multi-digit by multi-digit multiplication.

### Lesson 7: Estimate, Compute, Or Check?

- **Teaching goal:** Help students choose whether a task calls for estimation, exact computation, or checking.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide which whole-number tool fits a situation and use it appropriately.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Estimation predicts or judges size; computation finds exact answers; checking verifies.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Rounding, addition/subtraction within 1,000, inverse checks, and multiples of 10.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Estimation is a mistake or a less careful answer; exact answers are always needed; checks happen only after wrong answers.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can classify a prompt as estimate, compute, or check and complete the selected action.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for tool choice; `constructed-response` for why a tool fits; `text-input` for exact or estimated result.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare three tasks: "About how many?", "Exactly how many?", and "Is this answer reasonable?"
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep contexts direct; do not mix all four operations until students can name the purpose of the tool.

## Unit 6: Four-Operation Word Problems And Equations

### Lesson 1: Read For Structure, Not Key Words

- **Teaching goal:** Shift students from key-word hunting to identifying the mathematical relationship in a situation.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain whether a story involves joining, separating, comparing, equal groups, sharing, or grouping.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Operation choice comes from the relationship among quantities, not a single word.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** All four operation meanings, comparison language, equal groups, and one-step story problems.
- **Likely misconceptions:** "More" always means add; "left" always means subtract; all equal-group stories use the same operation.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can select the operation structure and reject a misleading key-word choice.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for structure choice; `match-pairs` for story to operation meaning; `constructed-response` for a short reason.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Present two stories using the word "more," one addition and one comparison, and discuss the relationship.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short stories with one clear question; avoid irrelevant details while building the routine.

### Lesson 2: One-Step Problems With All Four Operations

- **Teaching goal:** Practice choosing and solving one-step problems across addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division.
- **Student outcome:** The student can match a one-step context to the correct operation, model, equation, and answer.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Different operations answer different relationship questions.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Operation meanings, basic fluency, equations, arrays, number lines, and strip diagrams.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Applying the most recently learned operation to every problem; selecting equations by numbers only; omitting units.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can solve mixed one-step problems and label each answer in context.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for short contexts; `multiple-choice` for operation/equation choice; `text-input` for final numeric answers.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a four-box operation sort and require students to name what is unknown before solving.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep number sizes within current fluency; avoid two-step language in this lesson.

### Lesson 3: Unknowns In Different Places

- **Teaching goal:** Solve equations and word problems where the unknown is not always the final result.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use inverse reasoning or models to find unknown starts, changes, factors, or totals.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A box can represent any missing quantity in the relationship.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Equation balance, inverse operations, fact families, and one-step contexts.
- **Likely misconceptions:** The blank must be the answer after the equals sign; combine all visible numbers; ignore the meaning of the missing quantity.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student solves equations with blanks in varied positions and identifies what the blank means.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for unknowns; `multiple-choice` for meaning of the unknown; `text-input` for missing values.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare 24 + __ = 61, __ + 24 = 61, and 61 - __ = 24 as related equations.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep notation clean; use boxes or blanks rather than letters.

### Lesson 4: Represent Compare Problems

- **Teaching goal:** Use strip diagrams to model additive and multiplicative comparison problems.
- **Student outcome:** The student can represent "more than," "fewer than," and "times as many" relationships with a diagram and equation.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Compare problems relate two quantities, and the unknown may be one quantity or the difference/multiple.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Addition/subtraction comparison, multiplication as groups, strip diagrams, and comparison vocabulary.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating every comparison as subtraction; confusing "times as many" with "more than"; drawing bars that do not match the relationship.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can choose or complete a strip diagram and solve the comparison problem.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for matching diagram; `fill-blank` for missing value in equation; `constructed-response` for diagram explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show two bars for "8 more than" and repeated bars for "3 times as many" to contrast the structures.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Do not mix additive and multiplicative comparison in the same item until after the contrast is taught.

### Lesson 5: Find The Hidden First Step

- **Teaching goal:** Identify the intermediate quantity in accessible two-step problems.
- **Student outcome:** The student can state what must be found first and why it is needed for the final answer.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Two-step problems often have a hidden first answer that becomes an input to the second step.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** One-step mixed problems, operation choice, equations, and representation models.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Answering after one step; combining all numbers at once; not naming what the first result represents.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can identify the first question to answer, compute the intermediate quantity, and label it.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for first step; `order-items` for step sequence; `constructed-response` for intermediate quantity label.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a problem where students must first find a total, then compare it with another quantity.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep numbers friendly; use two short sentences rather than long paragraphs.

### Lesson 6: Write Equations For Two-Step Problems

- **Teaching goal:** Connect two-step models to equations that represent each step.
- **Student outcome:** The student can write or select equations for both steps of an accessible two-step problem.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Each step has its own relationship, and the first result feeds the second equation.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Hidden first steps, one-step equations, operation meanings, and intermediate quantities.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Writing one equation with all numbers in order; using the final answer in the first equation; losing the intermediate label.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects two valid equations in order and solves the final answer.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for equation sequence; `multiple-choice` for equation set; `multi-blank-cloze` for completing two equations.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Annotate a two-step diagram with Step 1 and Step 2 before writing equations.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Limit to two operations; avoid nested or multi-unknown equations.

### Lesson 7: Check Units And Reasonableness

- **Teaching goal:** Make unit labels and reasonableness checks part of problem solving.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether an answer fits the question, units, and approximate size.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A correct number is incomplete if it does not answer the question in context.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Estimation, all four operations, word-problem solving, and answer labels.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Units are optional; any computed result is reasonable; checking means only redoing arithmetic.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can evaluate a worked answer and correct an unreasonable number or missing unit.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for reasonable answer; `error-correction` for missing or wrong units; `constructed-response` for a brief check.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show a problem whose computed answer has the wrong unit and ask what the answer should say.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short explanations; focus on one issue at a time: size, operation fit, or unit.

### Lesson 8: Mixed Modeling Practice

- **Teaching goal:** Practice independent model choice across varied Grade 3 problem types.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose a representation, solve, and explain a one-step or accessible two-step problem.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Good problem solvers choose the model that reveals the structure.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Unit 6 models, all four operations, checking routines, and earlier arithmetic fluency.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Using the same model every time; solving before understanding; stopping after an intermediate answer.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects a fitting model and solves a mixed problem with a labeled, reasonable answer.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for model choice; `passage-question` for compact contexts; `constructed-response` for explanation of model choice.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Offer a small "model menu" and ask students to choose before computing.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Mix problem types, but keep reading load controlled and avoid more than two steps.

## Unit 7: Fractions As Equal Parts And Numbers

### Lesson 1: Equal Parts Make Fractions

- **Teaching goal:** Establish that fractions require equal-size parts of the same whole.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify equal and unequal partitions and explain whether a diagram can represent a fraction.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Fraction parts must be equal in size, even if the shapes of the parts can look different in later examples.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Halves, thirds, fourths, equal shares, shapes, and comparison language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Any cut shape makes fractions; equal number of pieces means equal-size parts; unequal shares can still use fraction names.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can classify partitions as valid or invalid fraction models and state why.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for valid partition; `match-pairs` for examples and non-examples; `constructed-response` for a short equality reason.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare two rectangles cut into four parts, one equal and one unequal, before naming fourths.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use simple shapes; avoid complex area-equivalence cases in the first lesson.

### Lesson 2: Unit Fractions And The Denominator

- **Teaching goal:** Introduce unit fractions and denominator meaning.
- **Student outcome:** The student can name one equal part as a unit fraction and explain what the denominator tells.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** In 1/4, the denominator 4 means the whole is partitioned into 4 equal parts.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Equal partitions, whole, part, and basic fraction notation exposure.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Denominator counts shaded parts; larger denominator means larger part; denominator and numerator are unrelated labels.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can identify 1/b from a partitioned whole and explain the denominator in words.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for naming unit fractions; `fill-blank` for denominator meaning; `match-pairs` for diagrams to unit fractions.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Shade one part in wholes split into 2, 3, 4, 6, and 8 equal parts and compare part size.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep notation large and clear; use "one equal part out of..." language repeatedly.

### Lesson 3: Numerators Count Equal Parts

- **Teaching goal:** Build non-unit fractions as counts of unit fractions.
- **Student outcome:** The student can name and represent fractions such as 3/4 as three one-fourth parts.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** The numerator counts how many equal parts are being named.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Unit fractions, denominator meaning, equal parts, and counting.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Numerator counts all parts in the whole; denominator changes when more parts are shaded; 3/4 means 3 wholes and 4 parts.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can match a shaded model to a non-unit fraction and describe it as repeated unit fractions.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for diagram-to-fraction; `fill-blank` for numerator/denominator roles; `text-input` for fraction names after support.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Build 3/5 by adding one-fifth, one-fifth, one-fifth while keeping the same whole.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use denominators within Grade 3 scope; avoid mixed numbers and fraction operations.

### Lesson 5: Fractions Greater Than One Whole

- **Teaching goal:** Extend fraction number lines beyond 1 in controlled cases.
- **Student outcome:** The student can locate and name fractions greater than 1 when intervals continue past one whole.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Equal fractional intervals continue past 1 just as whole-number intervals continue past each whole.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Fractions from 0 to 1, whole fractions such as 4/4, and number-line intervals.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Fractions cannot be greater than 1; the count restarts after 1; 5/4 must be less than 1 because it is a fraction.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can label points such as 5/4 or 7/6 on a number line with equal intervals.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for missing labels; `multiple-choice` for location; `order-items` for fractions around 1.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Extend fourths from 0 to 2 and count 1/4, 2/4, 3/4, 4/4, 5/4.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep diagrams explicit; avoid improper-fraction terminology unless needed informally.

### Lesson 6: Whole Numbers As Fractions

- **Teaching goal:** Show that whole numbers can be named with fraction notation.
- **Student outcome:** The student can recognize 1 as 2/2, 3/3, 4/4, and similar whole fractions.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** When all equal parts of one whole are counted, the fraction names the whole.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Unit and non-unit fractions, number-line location of 1, and denominator meaning.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A fraction can never equal a whole number; 4/4 is four wholes; denominator always makes the amount less than 1.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can match whole models and number-line points to whole-number fractions.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for whole fractions to models; `multiple-choice` for fractions equal to 1; `fill-blank` for missing numerator in __/5 = 1.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Shade every fourth in a whole and label the total as four fourths and one whole.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Focus on fractions equal to 1 and simple whole-number points; avoid fraction simplification rules.

### Lesson 7: Connect Shapes, Strips, And Lines

- **Teaching goal:** Translate the same fraction across shapes, fraction strips, and number lines.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify the same fraction represented in multiple models.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Different representations can show the same fractional amount when the whole and equal parts match.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Shape fractions, fraction strips, number-line fractions, numerator and denominator meaning.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Fractions are tied to one picture; different-looking models always show different amounts; number-line fractions are unrelated to shaded models.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can match a fraction to a shape model, strip model, and number-line point and explain the shared meaning.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for representation matches; `multiple-choice` for equivalent representation of a named fraction; `constructed-response` for a short comparison.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show 3/4 in a rectangle, strip, and line, and ask what each representation has in common.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use the same whole size or clearly labeled wholes; avoid equivalence comparisons until Unit 8.

### Lesson 8: Explain The Same Whole

- **Teaching goal:** Make same-whole reasoning explicit when naming and interpreting fractions.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain why fractions must refer to the same whole before they are compared or matched.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** The size of a fraction depends on the size of the whole being partitioned.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Equal parts, fraction naming, visual models, and comparison language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Same fraction symbols always mean same physical size; two shaded pieces can be compared without checking the whole; larger picture means larger fraction automatically.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can identify when two fraction models can or cannot be compared and state the same-whole issue.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for valid comparison; `constructed-response` for same-whole explanation; `match-pairs` for compare/not compare cases.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show 1/2 of a small rectangle and 1/2 of a large rectangle and ask what is the same and different.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep visual wholes clear; use sentence frames like "These can be compared because..."

## Unit 8: Equivalent Fractions And Fraction Comparison

### Lesson 1: Equivalent Means Same Amount

- **Teaching goal:** Introduce equivalent fractions as different names for the same amount.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify equivalent fractions using aligned visual models.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Equivalent fractions cover or mark the same quantity, even with different numerators and denominators.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Fraction naming, equal parts, same whole, and shape/strip models.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Different numbers always mean different amounts; equivalent means visually identical pieces; wholes of different sizes can prove equivalence.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can match simple equivalent fractions such as 1/2 and 2/4 with a model-based explanation.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for equivalent models; `multiple-choice` for same amount; `constructed-response` for one-sentence reasoning.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Layer fraction strips for 1/2, 2/4, and 3/6 to show aligned endpoints.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use clear models and same-size wholes; avoid abstract equivalence rules.

### Lesson 3: Compare Same Denominators

- **Teaching goal:** Compare fractions with the same denominator by counting same-size parts.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide which same-denominator fraction is greater and explain why.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** If the denominator is the same, the parts are the same size, so the larger numerator means more parts.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Numerator/denominator meaning, same whole, and non-unit fractions.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Comparing denominators even when equal; thinking the smaller numerator is closer to 1; ignoring same whole.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can compare fractions such as 3/8 and 5/8 using models or reasoning.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for greater/less comparison; `fill-blank` for comparison symbols; `constructed-response` for reasoning about same-size parts.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use two equal fraction strips divided into the same number of parts and shade different numerators.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Compare only two fractions at a time; use same-denominator cases only in this lesson.

### Lesson 4: Compare Same Numerators

- **Teaching goal:** Compare fractions with the same numerator by reasoning about unit fraction size.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide which same-numerator fraction is greater and explain the unit-size reasoning.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** If the numerator is the same, the fraction with fewer equal parts in the whole has larger parts.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Unit fraction size, denominator meaning, same whole, and comparison symbols.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Larger denominator means larger fraction; same numerator means equal amount; ignoring part size.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can compare fractions such as 3/4 and 3/8 by saying fourths are larger than eighths.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for comparison; `match-pairs` for denominator to part size; `constructed-response` for unit-size explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show 1/4 and 1/8 first, then compare three of each on same-size wholes.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep the whole the same size; avoid unlike numerator and unlike denominator comparisons.

### Lesson 5: Use Benchmarks To Compare

- **Teaching goal:** Use 0, 1/2, and 1 as benchmarks for fraction comparison.
- **Student outcome:** The student can compare Grade 3 fractions by locating them near familiar benchmarks when helpful.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Benchmarks help judge fraction size without exact calculation.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Fraction number lines, equivalence to 1/2 in simple cases, and fraction comparison basics.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Benchmark labels are arbitrary; every comparison must use numerator/denominator rules; 1/2 is always one tick mark.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can identify whether a fraction is less than, equal to, or greater than 1/2 in supported cases and use that to compare.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for benchmark relation; `order-items` for fractions by benchmark; `constructed-response` for benchmark reasoning.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Place several fractions on a 0-to-1 line and sort them as near 0, near 1/2, or near 1.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use visual number lines; avoid complex benchmark derivations beyond Grade 3 cases.

### Lesson 6: Same Whole Or Not?

- **Teaching goal:** Ensure students check whether fraction comparisons use the same whole.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide if a fraction comparison is valid and explain the same-whole condition.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Fractions can be compared only when they refer to the same-size whole or a clearly equivalent whole.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Same-whole reasoning, fraction models, and comparison language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Any two shaded diagrams can be compared; same symbols always mean same size; larger drawing always represents a larger fraction.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can identify invalid comparison setups and describe what information is missing.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for valid/invalid comparison; `constructed-response` for same-whole explanation; `error-correction` for flawed reasoning statements.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare 1/2 of a small cake and 1/3 of a large cake, asking what must be known before judging amount.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep diagrams visually unambiguous; avoid trick language.

### Lesson 7: Choose The Comparison Reason

- **Teaching goal:** Help students select the appropriate comparison reasoning for a fraction pair.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify whether to use same denominator, same numerator, benchmark, equivalence, or same-whole reasoning.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Different fraction pairs call for different valid reasoning.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Unit 8 comparison cases and Unit 7 fraction magnitude.
- **Likely misconceptions:** One rule works for every comparison; cross-multiplication is needed; equivalent fractions are comparisons of different amounts.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can match a fraction comparison to the reasoning that justifies it and state the correct comparison.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for comparison to reason; `multiple-choice` for best reasoning; `constructed-response` for a brief justification.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Create a small decision chart: same denominator, same numerator, benchmark, equivalent, or not enough information.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use only Grade 3 comparison types; do not include pairs with both unlike numerators and unlike denominators unless resolved by benchmark or model.

## Unit 9: Measurement, Data, And Applied Quantitative Reasoning

- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Measurement depends on the attribute being measured and the unit used.

### Lesson 3: Measure And Estimate Length

- **Teaching goal:** Connect length measurement, estimation, and appropriate units.

- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Length is measured by equal units laid end to end with no gaps or overlaps.

### Lesson 4: Mass And Liquid Volume Problems

- **Teaching goal:** Solve simple mass and liquid-volume problems with appropriate units.
- **Student outcome:** The student can interpret grams, kilograms, liters, and milliliters in short problem contexts.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Mass and liquid volume measure different attributes and require different units.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Unit choice, addition/subtraction, estimation, and comparison.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Confusing mass with volume; comparing quantities with different units as if labels do not matter; choosing kilogram or liter by object size alone.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses the correct attribute and unit, then solves an arithmetic problem in context.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for attribute/unit; `passage-question` for short applied problems; `text-input` for computed amounts.

- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep units within one system per item; avoid conversions between large and small units.

### Lesson 6: Scaled Bar Graphs And Tables

- **Teaching goal:** Interpret scaled bar graphs and tables using intervals.
- **Student outcome:** The student can read values, compare categories, and answer total/difference questions from scaled displays.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Bars and table values must be read according to the scale or interval.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Skip counting, multiplication facts, subtraction for differences, and graph vocabulary.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Reading unlabeled grid lines as ones; using bar height only without the scale; comparing visually without calculating.

## Unit 10: Area, Arrays, And Multiplication

### Lesson 3: Arrays Inside Rectangles

- **Teaching goal:** Connect rectangular area to multiplication arrays.
- **Student outcome:** The student can write a multiplication equation for the area of a tiled rectangle.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A tiled rectangle is an array of square units; rows times columns gives the total square units.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Arrays, multiplication facts, unit squares, rows and columns.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Multiplying side labels without understanding the square units; adding rows and columns; forgetting square-unit labels.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches a tiled rectangle to a multiplication equation and area statement.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for rectangle to equation; `multiple-choice` for area statement; `text-input` for product.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Place an array from Unit 2 inside a rectangle and relabel the dots as square units.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep dimensions within known facts; use "square units" in answer expectations.

### Lesson 4: Use Side Lengths For Area

- **Teaching goal:** Reason from side lengths to rectangular area without every square drawn.
- **Student outcome:** The student can find area from whole-number side lengths by imagining or using the implied array.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Side lengths tell how many rows and columns of unit squares fit in a rectangle.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Tiled rectangle area, multiplication, side length, and units.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Adding side lengths for area; treating side length and area as the same measure; ignoring units.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student computes rectangular area from labeled side lengths and states the result in square units.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for correct operation; `text-input` for area; `constructed-response` for why multiplication fits.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show a 5 by 7 rectangle first fully tiled, then only with side labels, and connect the two.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use whole-number side lengths; avoid perimeter comparison until the area idea is stable.

### Lesson 5: Unknown Side Or Area In A Rectangle

- **Teaching goal:** Use multiplication or unknown-factor reasoning in rectangular area contexts.
- **Student outcome:** The student can find a missing area or missing side length in supported rectangle problems.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Area, rows, and columns form a multiplication relationship; a missing side can be found as an unknown factor.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Rectangular area, multiplication/division facts, unknown factors, and side labels.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Dividing the wrong numbers; treating missing side as perimeter; guessing a side without checking the product.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can solve for a missing rectangle measure and write a related multiplication or division equation.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for missing side/area; `multiple-choice` for related equation; `text-input` for missing value.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use "4 rows, ? columns, 28 square units" and ask what factor with 4 makes 28.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep one unknown per item; use diagrams with labels.

## Unit 11: Perimeter, Geometry, And Shape Attributes

### Lesson 1: Perimeter Is Distance Around

- **Teaching goal:** Define perimeter as the distance around a shape's boundary.
- **Student outcome:** The student can trace a boundary and find perimeter when side lengths are given or countable.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Perimeter measures around the outside, not the inside covering.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Side length, addition, measurement units, polygons, and area from Unit 10.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Counting inside squares; confusing perimeter with area; omitting one side or counting a side twice.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can trace all sides once and add side lengths to find perimeter with units.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for perimeter path; `text-input` for perimeter; `constructed-response` for a boundary explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a finger-trace around a rectangle and say each side length aloud before adding.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use simple polygons with labeled sides; avoid missing side lengths initially.

### Lesson 2: Add Side Lengths Carefully

- **Teaching goal:** Find perimeter by adding all side lengths, including controlled missing repeated sides.
- **Student outcome:** The student can calculate perimeter for rectangles and simple polygons without skipping sides.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Perimeter is the sum of every side length around the boundary.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Addition within 1,000, equal side reasoning, rectangles, and side labels.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Adding only two sides; multiplying when addition is clearer; assuming opposite sides without evidence in non-rectangles.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student finds perimeter from side labels and explains which side lengths were added.
- **Recommended question types:** `text-input` for perimeter; `fill-blank` for missing side length in controlled cases; `multiple-choice` for complete addition expression.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Put a check mark on each side as it is included in the perimeter sum.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep diagrams large and labels readable; avoid irregular shapes with too many sides.

### Lesson 3: Area Or Perimeter?

- **Teaching goal:** Distinguish area as square-unit covering from perimeter as boundary length.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether a task asks for area or perimeter and choose the correct method.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Area measures inside with square units; perimeter measures around with length units.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Area, perimeter, square units, side lengths, and measurement units.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Using multiplication for every rectangle question; adding sides for area; counting squares for perimeter.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student classifies a prompt as area or perimeter, solves it, and uses correct units.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for area/perimeter decision; `match-pairs` for prompt to method; `constructed-response` for unit reasoning.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use the same rectangle for two questions: "cover it" and "fence around it."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep contrast language direct; avoid composite figures until the distinction is clear.

### Lesson 4: Attributes That Define Shapes

- **Teaching goal:** Classify shapes by defining attributes rather than appearance.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify attributes such as number of sides, straight sides, and equal sides and use them to classify shapes.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Defining attributes determine shape categories; color, size, and orientation do not.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic 2D shape names, sides, vertices, polygons, and comparison language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A rotated shape is a different shape; color or size changes category; all four-sided shapes are rectangles.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can sort shapes and justify a category using defining attributes.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for shape to attribute; `multiple-choice` for category choice; `constructed-response` for attribute evidence.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show several triangles in different sizes and orientations and ask what they all share.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use clear, unambiguous shapes; limit attribute sets in each item.

### Lesson 5: Quadrilateral Categories

- **Teaching goal:** Reason about quadrilaterals and subcategories using attributes.
- **Student outcome:** The student can classify rectangles, squares, rhombuses, and other quadrilaterals by Grade 3 attributes.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Shapes can belong to broad categories and more specific subcategories.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Defining attributes, polygons, side counts, right-angle exposure if already familiar, and equal-side language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A square is not a rectangle; a shape changes category when tilted; every quadrilateral is a square or rectangle.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies quadrilaterals and explains why a square can fit a broader category when attributes match.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for category membership; `match-pairs` for shape to attribute set; `constructed-response` for a classification claim.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use an attribute chart with columns for four sides, opposite sides, equal sides, and square corners when appropriate.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid formal proof language; use "can belong to" and "has these attributes."

### Lesson 7: Geometry And Measurement Mix

- **Teaching goal:** Integrate perimeter, area, shape attributes, and fraction partitioning in mixed tasks.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose the correct geometry or measurement idea for a prompt and explain the choice.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Shape tasks may ask about inside covering, outside boundary, attributes, or equal parts.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Units 10-11 concepts, fraction partitioning, addition, multiplication, and classification language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Applying area or perimeter automatically to every shape question; ignoring the question word; classifying by appearance.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student solves a mixed geometry task and identifies the concept used.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for concept choice; `passage-question` for short shape contexts; `constructed-response` for a brief method explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a four-choice concept sort: area, perimeter, attributes, or fractions.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one diagram per task; keep labels and units clear.
