# Unit 3: Division As Sharing, Grouping, And Unknown Factors

Track: Math
Level: Grade 3
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/grade-03-math/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students understand division as reasoning about equal groups when either the size of each group or the number of groups is unknown. They connect sharing, grouping, arrays, division equations, multiplication facts, unknown-factor reasoning, and fact families.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

Division belongs immediately after multiplication meaning because students can use the equal-group structures they just built. Placing division here makes multiplication and division a connected operation pair before fact-fluency work begins in Unit 4.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: partitive division, measurement division, equal sharing, equal grouping, quotient meaning, inverse relationship, unknown factor, related facts.
- Vocabulary: divide, division, quotient, equal shares, equal groups, share equally, how many groups, how many in each group, unknown factor, related facts, fact family, inverse operation.
- Representations and models: sharing drawings, grouping drawings, arrays with a missing dimension, number lines with equal jumps, multiplication/division fact-family triangles or sets, equations with boxes.
- Texts and forms: short sharing stories, grouping stories, unknown-factor statements, fact-family explanations.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need multiplication as equal groups and arrays, factors and products, skip counting, equal sharing from earlier grades, equations with unknowns, and the idea that groups must be equal to support multiplication or division.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Division always means sharing into a known number of groups.
- The quotient always names the size of a group.
- Division can be reversed the way multiplication can.
- A leftover or unequal group is acceptable in basic division contexts without noticing the mismatch.
- Division facts must be memorized separately from multiplication.
- The answer to a division problem can be interpreted without reference to the story.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with sharing because it is often familiar, but quickly introduce grouping so students do not overgeneralize division as sharing only. Use drawings and arrays before symbolic division equations. Then connect both interpretations to multiplication by asking "What factor is missing?" Finish with fact families and representation matching so students see division as a structural partner to multiplication.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Review Unit 2 equal groups, arrays, factors, products, and multiplication equations. Continue Unit 1 equation and number-line work by using boxes, blanks, and equal jumps to show unknowns.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares Unit 4 strategy-based division fluency and Unit 6 mixed word problems. It also supports later scaled graph work, area side-length reasoning, and cumulative transfer tasks where students must decide whether a problem asks for group size, number of groups, or a total.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Distinguish sharing and grouping division situations.
- Represent division with drawings, arrays, number lines, and equations.
- Interpret what the quotient means in a story.
- Use multiplication to solve division as an unknown-factor problem.
- Build and explain multiplication/division fact families.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Sharing Into Equal Groups**: Model division when the number of groups is known.
2. **Making Equal Groups**: Model division when the size of each group is known.
3. **What Does The Quotient Mean?**: Interpret the answer as group size or number of groups.
4. **Arrays With A Missing Side**: Use array structure to connect division and multiplication.
5. **Division As An Unknown Factor**: Solve division by asking which factor is missing.
6. **Fact Families For Multiplication And Division**: Build related equation sets from one equal-group situation.
7. **Choose Sharing Or Grouping**: Sort and solve mixed division contexts.
