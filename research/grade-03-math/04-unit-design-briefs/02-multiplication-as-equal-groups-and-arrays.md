# Unit 2: Multiplication As Equal Groups And Arrays

Track: Math
Level: Grade 3
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/grade-03-math/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students understand multiplication as a way to represent and reason about equal-size groups, arrays, repeated addition, and contextual totals. They should connect situations, drawings, arrays, skip counting, repeated addition, and multiplication equations before being asked to rely on fact recall.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit follows number readiness because multiplication is the first major new Grade 3 operation. It uses familiar skip-counting and array readiness from Unit 1, then formalizes that structure with factors, products, and equations. Building meaning here protects later fluency work from becoming disconnected memorization.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: equal groups, group size, number of groups, multiplication as repeated equal groups, array structure, row and column interpretation, multiplication equations as situation models.
- Vocabulary: equal groups, groups of, factor, product, multiply, multiplication equation, repeated addition, skip counting, array, row, column, total.
- Representations and models: counters, equal-group drawings, arrays, skip-counting paths, repeated-addition equations, multiplication equations, context-to-diagram matching.
- Texts and forms: short equal-group stories, array descriptions, "___ groups of ___" statements, equation explanations.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need addition within 100, skip counting, row/column language, equal groups, basic equation reading, and willingness to interpret a diagram before calculating.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Any collection can be multiplied, even when groups are unequal.
- The first factor always means rows or always means columns, regardless of context.
- Multiplication is just faster counting by ones.
- Repeated addition and multiplication are unrelated notations.
- The factors can be swapped in an equation without considering how the story is described.
- Arrays are pictures to count, not structures to reason from.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Start with concrete equal groups in familiar contexts, then represent the same structure through drawings and repeated addition. Move from grouped objects to arrays so students can see two-dimensional structure. Introduce multiplication equations only after the story and model are clear. End with matching and explaining tasks that ask students to move among contexts, arrays, repeated addition, and multiplication equations.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Review Unit 1 skip counting, addition strategies, equations with blanks, row/column language, and reasonableness checks. Addition review should support repeated addition and totals rather than distract from the new multiplicative structure.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares Unit 3 division by making equal-size groups explicit. It prepares Unit 4 fluency by giving facts a visual and contextual base. It prepares Unit 6 word-problem modeling, Unit 9 scaled graphs, and Unit 10 area because arrays and equal jumps become recurring representations.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify whether a situation has equal groups.
- Represent a multiplication situation with a drawing, repeated addition, array, and equation.
- Interpret factors and products in context.
- Match arrays to equations and equations to stories.
- Explain why multiplication fits a given diagram or situation.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Equal Groups Or Not?**: Decide whether a situation has the equal-size groups needed for multiplication.
2. **Groups Of A Number**: Represent "groups of" with drawings, counters, and repeated addition.
3. **Skip Counting As Equal Jumps**: Connect repeated equal groups to skip-counting paths.
4. **From Repeated Addition To Multiplication**: Introduce multiplication equations as compact equal-group notation.
5. **Rows And Columns In Arrays**: Use array structure to organize factors and totals.
6. **Match Stories, Arrays, And Equations**: Translate among the main multiplication representations.
7. **Explain The Factors**: Name what each factor means in a story or diagram.
8. **Multiplication In New Contexts**: Apply equal-group reasoning in unfamiliar but controlled contexts.
