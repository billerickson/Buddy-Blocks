# Unit 9: Measurement, Data, And Applied Quantitative Reasoning

Track: Math
Level: Grade 3
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/grade-03-math/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students apply arithmetic, estimation, fractions, units, and representation choice to measurement, data, time, and simple financial contexts. They learn to read scales, interpret data displays, solve measurement problems, and make reasonable quantitative decisions.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit comes after core arithmetic and fraction foundations because measurement and data require students to coordinate numbers, units, scales, and contexts. It gives Grade 3 mathematics a practical setting before the geometric measurement work with area and perimeter.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: measurement attributes, unit choice, elapsed time, scaled data displays, line plots, arithmetic in applied contexts, financial reasonableness.
- Vocabulary: time, elapsed time, hour, minute, length, inch, foot, yard, centimeter, meter, mass, gram, kilogram, liquid volume, liter, milliliter, data, table, scale, interval, picture graph, bar graph, line plot, price, cost, save, spend, earn, reasonable.
- Representations and models: clocks, timelines, rulers, measuring tools, tables, scaled picture graphs, scaled bar graphs, line plots, number lines, simple price lists.
- Texts and forms: measurement word problems, data interpretation questions, "how many more/fewer" prompts, short financial scenarios, answer statements with units.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need all four operations, rounding and estimation, graph vocabulary, ruler and clock readiness, number-line reasoning, fraction number-line understanding, and experience with short applied contexts.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Each picture or bar unit always means 1.
- The scale can be ignored if the display looks simple.
- Units are labels added after math, not part of the quantity.
- Elapsed time works exactly like base-ten subtraction.
- Length, mass, and liquid volume can be compared without attending to the attribute.
- The lowest price or biggest number is always the best financial choice.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Start with measurement attributes and units so students know what is being measured. Use time and elapsed time with timelines because number-line thinking reduces load. Move into scaled graphs and line plots once students are comfortable interpreting units and intervals. Close with applied mixed contexts, including simple financial decisions, where students must choose operations and explain reasonableness.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Review Unit 5 rounding and estimation in measurement contexts, Unit 6 word-problem modeling, Unit 4 multiplication/division through graph scales, and Units 7-8 fractions through line plots and ruler marks.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares Units 10-11 by strengthening the idea that measurement depends on the attribute and unit. It prepares Unit 12 by giving realistic mixed contexts where arithmetic, fractions, data, and reasonableness interact.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Solve elapsed-time problems using clocks or timelines.
- Measure or interpret length, mass, and liquid volume with correct units.
- Read scaled picture graphs, scaled bar graphs, tables, and line plots.
- Use operations and estimation in measurement and data contexts.
- Explain a simple financial decision using quantities and the stated goal.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Choose The Attribute And Unit**: Distinguish length, mass, liquid volume, and time.
2. **Elapsed Time On A Timeline**: Use jumps and benchmarks to reason about time.
3. **Measure And Estimate Length**: Connect rulers, units, and reasonable measurements.
4. **Mass And Liquid Volume Problems**: Interpret grams, kilograms, liters, and milliliters in context.
5. **Scaled Picture Graphs**: Read each symbol by its scale, not as one.
6. **Scaled Bar Graphs And Tables**: Interpret intervals and compare data values.
7. **Line Plots And Fractional Lengths**: Use fraction understanding to read measurement data.
8. **Money Choices With A Goal**: Compare costs, savings, and spending choices using arithmetic.
9. **Mixed Measurement And Data Decisions**: Choose operations and units across varied applied contexts.
