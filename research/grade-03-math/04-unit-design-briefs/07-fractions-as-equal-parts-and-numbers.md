# Unit 7: Fractions As Equal Parts And Numbers

Track: Math
Level: Grade 3
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/grade-03-math/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students understand fractions as equal parts of a whole and as numbers with magnitude on a number line. They build from equal partitioning to unit fractions, non-unit fractions, repeated unit fractions, and whole numbers written as fractions.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

Fractions begin after students have a stronger base in number lines, multiplication/division language, and problem modeling. This placement lets students see fractions as numbers in the same system they already use, while keeping fraction work conceptually grounded before equivalence and comparison.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: equal partitioning, unit fractions, denominator as equal parts in the whole, numerator as count of those parts, non-unit fractions, fractions on a number line, whole numbers as fractions.
- Vocabulary: whole, equal parts, equal shares, fraction, unit fraction, non-unit fraction, numerator, denominator, fraction strip, number line, benchmark, same whole, partition, 1 whole.
- Representations and models: partitioned shapes, fraction strips, folded or tiled rectangles, set models only when carefully controlled, number lines from 0 to 1 and beyond 1, labeled tick marks.
- Texts and forms: short naming prompts, diagram-to-fraction matches, fraction-location explanations, sentence frames using numerator and denominator.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need halves, thirds, fourths, equal-share language, whole-number number lines, partitioned shapes, measurement language, multiplication/division vocabulary for equal parts, and comparison language.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Unequal parts can be named with fraction notation.
- The numerator and denominator are two separate whole-number counts with no relationship.
- Fractions live only in shaded shapes and are not numbers.
- A larger denominator always means a larger amount.
- Fractions cannot name whole numbers.
- Fraction comparisons can ignore whether the whole is the same size.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with equal and unequal partitions so the core constraint is unmistakable. Then introduce unit fractions and denominator meaning, followed by non-unit fractions as repeated unit fractions. Move to number lines early enough that students do not overattach fractions to shaded regions. End by naming whole numbers as fractions and connecting shape, strip, and number-line representations.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Review Unit 1 number lines and Unit 6 modeling language. Lightly connect division as equal sharing from Unit 3 when discussing equal parts, but avoid turning fraction naming into a division procedure. Use multiplication language only to support repeated unit fractions, not fraction operations.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares Unit 8 equivalence and comparison, Unit 9 line plots and measurement fractions, Unit 11 equal-area partitions, and Unit 12 cumulative transfer. It also prepares Grade 4 fraction equivalence and operations by securing magnitude and same-whole reasoning first.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify equal and unequal partitions.
- Name unit fractions and explain denominator meaning.
- Represent non-unit fractions as repeated unit fractions.
- Place fractions on a number line.
- Recognize and explain whole numbers written as fractions.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Equal Parts Make Fractions**: Distinguish valid fraction partitions from unequal shares.
2. **Unit Fractions And The Denominator**: Name one equal part and explain what the denominator tells.
3. **Numerators Count Equal Parts**: Build non-unit fractions from repeated unit fractions.
4. **Fractions On A Number Line**: Place fractions between 0 and 1 using equal intervals.
5. **Fractions Greater Than One Whole**: Extend number-line fractions beyond 1 in controlled cases.
6. **Whole Numbers As Fractions**: Recognize 1 as 2/2, 3/3, 4/4, and related whole fractions.
7. **Connect Shapes, Strips, And Lines**: Translate the same fraction across multiple representations.
8. **Explain The Same Whole**: Use same-whole language when naming or interpreting fractions.
