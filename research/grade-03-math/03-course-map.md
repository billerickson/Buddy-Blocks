# Grade 3 Math Course Map

Track: Math
Level: Grade 3
Sources: Accepted research brief at `research/grade-03-math/01-research-brief.md` and level blueprint at `research/grade-03-math/02-level-blueprint.md`

## Course Design Notes

This course map orders Grade 3 Math from prerequisite stabilization into multiplication and division meaning, strategy-based fluency, whole-number operations, fractions as numbers, measurement and data, area and perimeter, geometry, and cumulative transfer. The map is intentionally uneven: some units need more time because they introduce major Grade 3 ideas, while others are shorter applications or consolidation units. Review and fluency should be cumulative throughout; lesson and question design will happen in later artifacts.

## Unit 1: Grade 3 Number Readiness

### Unit Purpose

Stabilize the Grade 2 arithmetic and representation knowledge students need before the new cognitive load of Grade 3 multiplication, division, fractions, and multi-step problem solving. Students review place value, addition and subtraction strategies, number lines, skip counting, equal groups, arrays, and simple problem models.

### Student Outcomes

- Read, write, compare, and decompose three-digit numbers using hundreds, tens, and ones.
- Add and subtract within 100 fluently and within 1,000 with place-value models or strategies.
- Use open number lines, base-ten drawings, equations, and strip diagrams to represent simple problems.
- Skip count by 2s, 5s, 10s, and 100s, and connect skip counting to equal groups and arrays.
- Check whether an answer is reasonable using estimation, inverse operations, or context.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Place value, digit, value, hundreds, tens, ones, expanded form, compose, decompose, sum, difference, compare, estimate, reasonable, inverse operation, number line, equation, strip diagram, equal groups, row, column, array.

### Prerequisites

Students should have prior experience with two- and three-digit numbers, addition and subtraction within 100, developing fluency within 20, simple word problems, skip counting, arrays as readiness, and basic graph and measurement vocabulary.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit connects Grade 2 place-value and additive reasoning to the multiplicative structure in Units 2-4. It also prepares students for rounding, regrouping, multi-digit operations, fraction number lines, scaled graphs, and area models later in the course.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Treating digits as isolated marks instead of values.
- Counting all from 1 when a place-value or skip-counting structure is available.
- Thinking the equals sign only means "write the answer next."
- Using an operation because of a key word rather than the situation.
- Accepting an unreasonable answer without checking size or units.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

6-8 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Keep addition/subtraction within 100, place-value language, open number lines, and skip counting active in every later unit. Early review should be short and model-rich, with delayed retrieval of facts and representations rather than long isolated drills.

## Unit 2: Multiplication As Equal Groups And Arrays

### Unit Purpose

Introduce multiplication as a structured way to represent equal-size groups, arrays, and repeated addition. Students learn to see multiplication situations in context before they are expected to retrieve multiplication facts.

### Student Outcomes

- Represent equal groups with drawings, counters, arrays, repeated addition, and multiplication equations.
- Interpret factors as the number of groups and the size of each group in context.
- Use rows and columns to describe arrays.
- Connect skip counting and repeated addition to multiplication.
- Explain why a multiplication equation matches a situation or diagram.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Equal groups, groups of, factor, product, multiply, multiplication equation, repeated addition, skip counting, array, row, column, total, context.

### Prerequisites

Students need skip-counting readiness, addition within 100, equal groups, row and column language, and experience matching simple stories to equations.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit extends Unit 1 equal-group and array readiness into formal multiplication. It prepares for division meanings in Unit 3, strategy-based fluency in Unit 4, area models in Unit 10, and multiplication word problems throughout the course.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Counting every object one by one instead of using equal-group structure.
- Treating unequal groups as multiplication without noticing the mismatch.
- Confusing the number of groups with the size of each group.
- Assuming any repeated addition expression can be written in any multiplication order without attending to context.
- Reading rows and columns inconsistently in arrays.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

6-8 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Interleave skip counting, repeated addition, array interpretation, and equation matching. Continue short addition/subtraction and place-value review, especially in word problems that ask students to decide whether a situation is additive or multiplicative.

## Unit 3: Division As Sharing, Grouping, And Unknown Factors

### Unit Purpose

Build division meaning from concrete contexts and connect division to multiplication. Students learn both major division interpretations: finding how many are in each group and finding how many groups can be made.

### Student Outcomes

- Represent division with equal-sharing and equal-grouping models.
- Distinguish "how many in each group?" from "how many groups?"
- Connect division equations to drawings, arrays, and multiplication facts.
- Use unknown-factor reasoning to solve division problems.
- Build multiplication/division fact families from related equations.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Divide, division, quotient, equal shares, equal groups, share equally, how many groups, how many in each group, unknown factor, related facts, fact family, inverse operation.

### Prerequisites

Students need multiplication as equal groups and arrays, skip counting, equal sharing from earlier grades, and comfort with equations that have unknowns in varied positions.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit depends on Unit 2 multiplication meanings and prepares for mixed multiplication/division strategy work in Unit 4. It also supports two-step word problems in Unit 6 and area/perimeter comparison work where students must choose the correct operation.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Thinking division always means sharing and never means finding the number of groups.
- Assuming division is commutative because multiplication is commutative.
- Matching a division equation to a context without tracking what the quotient represents.
- Forgetting that groups in division must be equal-size groups.
- Treating division as unrelated to multiplication facts.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

5-7 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Review multiplication arrays while introducing division so students repeatedly connect the operations. Begin spaced retrieval of related multiplication and division facts only after students have represented the relationship visually and verbally.

## Unit 4: Multiplication And Division Strategies

### Unit Purpose

Develop accuracy, flexibility, and retention for multiplication and division within 100 through meaning-based strategies. Students use properties, decompositions, related facts, and inverse relationships before moving toward more automatic retrieval.

### Student Outcomes

- Use commutativity to relate multiplication facts while recognizing that contexts may differ.
- Decompose factors with the distributive property to solve harder facts.
- Use doubles, fives, tens, nines, and known facts to derive unknown facts.
- Solve division facts with related multiplication facts and unknown-factor reasoning.
- Explain or select an efficient strategy for a given fact.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Commutative property, distributive property, factor, product, quotient, decompose, related fact, known fact, unknown fact, double, multiple, inverse relationship, strategy, efficient.

### Prerequisites

Students need concrete multiplication and division meanings, arrays, equal groups, fact families, skip counting, and addition/subtraction fluency for decomposing and combining partial products.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit consolidates Units 2-3 and supplies the fact fluency needed for multi-step word problems, multiplying by multiples of 10, scaled graphs, area, and perimeter problem solving. Later units should keep these facts alive through mixed retrieval.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Believing fluency means speed only, not accuracy and strategy choice.
- Memorizing isolated facts without understanding their relationships.
- Misusing commutativity for division.
- Applying the distributive property as a digit trick instead of decomposing a factor.
- Thinking a fact is mastered after answering it correctly once.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

7-10 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use short, frequent, mixed practice rather than long single-factor blocks only. Space facts by strategy family first, then interleave multiplication, division, unknown-factor prompts, and word-problem contexts across all later units.

## Unit 5: Place Value, Rounding, And Whole-Number Operations

### Unit Purpose

Extend place-value reasoning into Grade 3 whole-number expectations: rounding, estimation, multi-digit addition and subtraction, regrouping, inverse checks, and multiplying one-digit numbers by multiples of 10.

### Student Outcomes

- Round whole numbers to the nearest ten or hundred using place-value and number-line reasoning.
- Add and subtract within 1,000 using place-value strategies and regrouping.
- Estimate sums and differences and judge whether exact answers are reasonable.
- Use inverse operations to check addition and subtraction.
- Multiply one-digit numbers by multiples of 10 using place value and multiplication facts.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Round, nearest ten, nearest hundred, benchmark, estimate, reasonable, regroup, compose, decompose, expanded form, inverse operation, multiple of 10, place value, digit value.

### Prerequisites

Students need three-digit place-value understanding, addition and subtraction strategies, open number lines, basic multiplication facts and equal groups, and comparison language.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit extends Unit 1 readiness and Unit 4 multiplication strategies. It supports multi-step problems in Unit 6, measurement estimation in Unit 9, area with larger side lengths in Unit 10, and cumulative mixed problem solving in Unit 12.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Thinking rounding is randomly changing a number to one that looks easier.
- Rounding by looking only at a digit instead of magnitude and nearest benchmark.
- Regrouping digits without understanding trades of hundreds, tens, and ones.
- Estimating after calculating instead of using estimation to predict or check.
- Treating 4 x 30 as unrelated to 4 x 3 and place value.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

6-9 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Interleave rounding with estimation and reasonableness checks instead of isolating it. Keep multiplication/division fact retrieval short and cumulative, especially facts needed for multiples of 10 and later area work.

## Unit 6: Four-Operation Word Problems And Equations

### Unit Purpose

Help students model and solve one-step and accessible two-step problems with all four operations. The emphasis is on choosing a representation, identifying quantities, writing or matching equations, and explaining the answer in context.

### Student Outcomes

- Decide whether a situation calls for addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, or a combination.
- Represent problems with equations, strip diagrams, arrays, number lines, tables, or short explanations.
- Solve two-step problems by identifying the intermediate quantity.
- Use equations with unknowns in varied positions.
- Check answers for reasonableness, units, and fit with the story.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Operation, equation, unknown, box or blank, intermediate quantity, total, difference, product, quotient, compare, more than, fewer than, times as many, reasonable, units, model.

### Prerequisites

Students need addition/subtraction within 1,000, multiplication/division meanings and facts within 100, rounding and estimation, inverse checks, and experience with diagrams and equations.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit brings together Units 1-5 and becomes the main modeling routine for fractions, measurement, data, area, perimeter, geometry, financial literacy, and the final transfer unit. It should establish habits that later units can reuse without reteaching from scratch.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Choosing operations from key words rather than mathematical structure.
- Solving only one step of a two-step problem.
- Losing track of what an intermediate answer represents.
- Writing an equation that has correct numbers but the wrong relationships.
- Giving a numerical answer without a unit or context phrase.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

6-8 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use mixed contexts from earlier units so students must choose operations. Continue spaced arithmetic fluency, but embed it in modeling and answer-checking rather than treating computation as the whole task.

## Unit 7: Fractions As Equal Parts And Numbers

### Unit Purpose

Build fraction meaning from equal partitioning into unit fractions, non-unit fractions, whole-number fractions, and number-line quantities. Students learn that fractions are numbers with magnitude, not only shaded parts of shapes.

### Student Outcomes

- Partition shapes into equal parts and name unit fractions.
- Explain that the denominator names the number of equal parts in the whole.
- Represent non-unit fractions as multiple unit fractions.
- Place unit fractions and non-unit fractions on a number line.
- Recognize whole numbers as fractions, such as 1 as 3/3 or 4/4.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Whole, equal parts, equal shares, fraction, unit fraction, non-unit fraction, numerator, denominator, fraction strip, number line, benchmark, same whole, partition, 1 whole.

### Prerequisites

Students need experience with halves, thirds, and fourths; equal shares; number lines; whole-number ordering; measurement language; and precise comparison vocabulary.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit extends Grade 2 equal-share work and Unit 1 number-line reasoning. It prepares for equivalence and comparison in Unit 8, line plots and measurement fractions in Unit 9, shape partitioning in Unit 11, and cumulative transfer in Unit 12.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Believing any parts count as fractions even when the parts are unequal.
- Thinking the numerator and denominator are two unrelated whole numbers.
- Treating fractions only as shaded pieces, not numbers on a number line.
- Assuming a larger denominator always means a larger fraction.
- Ignoring the need for the same whole when naming or comparing fractions.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

7-9 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Return often to equal parts, same whole, and unit fraction size. Continue multiplication/division retrieval in low-load warmups, especially with denominators and repeated unit fractions where the structure is helpful but should not become fraction operations.

## Unit 8: Equivalent Fractions And Fraction Comparison

### Unit Purpose

Extend fraction magnitude reasoning to equivalent fractions and Grade 3 fraction comparisons. Students use models, number lines, benchmarks, same numerator reasoning, same denominator reasoning, and same-whole reasoning.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify equivalent fractions using visual models and number lines.
- Explain that equivalent fractions name the same quantity.
- Compare fractions with the same denominator by reasoning about the number of same-size parts.
- Compare fractions with the same numerator by reasoning about unit fraction size.
- Use benchmarks such as 0, 1/2, and 1 when appropriate.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Equivalent fraction, same quantity, compare, greater than, less than, equal to, same numerator, same denominator, unit size, benchmark, number line, same whole.

### Prerequisites

Students need fraction naming, unit fractions, non-unit fractions, number-line placement, whole-number fractions, and precise understanding of numerator, denominator, and same whole.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit depends on Unit 7 fraction magnitude and supports measurement line plots, shape partitioning, and cumulative comparison tasks in Units 9-12. It also prepares students for Grade 4 fraction equivalence, ordering, and operations without teaching those later procedures yet.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Comparing denominators as if they were whole numbers.
- Assuming fractions are equivalent because diagrams look similar but wholes differ.
- Comparing fractions without checking the same whole.
- Using cross-multiplication or later-grade rules without understanding.
- Thinking equivalent fractions are different amounts because they use different numbers.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

5-7 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use varied diagrams and number lines so students do not memorize a single visual. Revisit Unit 7 concepts after delays, and include brief review of multiplication/division facts only when it supports structure rather than premature fraction shortcuts.

## Unit 9: Measurement, Data, And Applied Quantitative Reasoning

### Unit Purpose

Use measurement and data as applied contexts for arithmetic, fractions, units, scales, and decision-making. Students solve problems involving time, length, mass, liquid volume, line plots, scaled graphs, and simple financial choices.

### Student Outcomes

- Tell and solve problems involving time and elapsed time in Grade 3 contexts.
- Measure and solve problems involving length, mass, and liquid volume with appropriate units.
- Read and interpret scaled picture graphs, scaled bar graphs, tables, and line plots.
- Use addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, and estimation in measurement and data contexts.
- Make simple financial decisions involving earning, saving, spending, prices, and reasonableness.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Time, elapsed time, hour, minute, length, inch, foot, yard, centimeter, meter, mass, gram, kilogram, liquid volume, liter, milliliter, data, table, scale, interval, picture graph, bar graph, line plot, price, cost, save, spend, earn, reasonable.

### Prerequisites

Students need all four operations, rounding and estimation, graph vocabulary, ruler and clock readiness, fraction number-line understanding, and experience reading short applied contexts.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit applies Units 1-8 in concrete contexts. It prepares for area and perimeter units by reinforcing units and measurement attributes, and it supplies transfer contexts for the cumulative mastery work in Unit 12.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Reading each picture or bar as 1 instead of using the scale.
- Confusing the measured attribute, such as length versus mass or volume.
- Dropping or mismatching units.
- Treating elapsed time as ordinary base-ten subtraction without considering 60 minutes in an hour.
- Making financial choices by the largest or smallest number alone without considering the question or goal.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

7-9 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Interleave scaled graphs with multiplication facts, elapsed time with number-line reasoning, and measurement contexts with estimation. Revisit fractions through line plots and measurement marks, but do not introduce fraction operations.

## Unit 10: Area, Arrays, And Multiplication

### Unit Purpose

Develop area as square-unit covering and connect rectangular area to arrays and multiplication. Students learn to count unit squares, tile rectangles, reason from side lengths, and decompose figures when appropriate.

### Student Outcomes

- Explain area as the number of square units needed to cover a plane region.
- Count square units and use tiled rectangles to find area.
- Connect rows and columns in rectangles to multiplication.
- Find the area of rectangles with whole-number side lengths using arrays or multiplication.
- Decompose rectilinear figures into rectangles in supported contexts.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Area, square unit, unit square, tile, cover, rectangle, rows, columns, side length, array, product, square inch, square centimeter, square foot, decompose, compose.

### Prerequisites

Students need multiplication arrays, rows and columns, facts within 100, measurement units, rectangle attributes, and experience with grid diagrams.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit turns Unit 2 arrays and Unit 4 multiplication facts into geometric measurement. It prepares directly for perimeter distinction in Unit 11 and reinforces multiplication in a visual context before cumulative mixed problem solving.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Counting boundary marks instead of square units for area.
- Leaving gaps or overlaps when thinking about tiling.
- Confusing side lengths with total area.
- Adding side lengths when the task asks for area.
- Treating area as a memorized formula without understanding square-unit covering.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Review multiplication facts through arrays and rectangles. Delay heavy area/perimeter contrast until students have a stable idea of area, then begin short comparison prompts near the end of the unit.

## Unit 11: Perimeter, Geometry, And Shape Attributes

### Unit Purpose

Teach perimeter as boundary length, distinguish it from area, and strengthen geometry reasoning through shape attributes, quadrilaterals, and equal-area partitions.

### Student Outcomes

- Find perimeter by adding side lengths or reasoning about equal sides.
- Distinguish area as square-unit covering from perimeter as distance around a boundary.
- Classify shapes by defining attributes, especially quadrilaterals.
- Recognize categories and subcategories of shapes using attributes.
- Partition shapes into equal-area parts and connect partitioning to fraction language.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Perimeter, boundary, distance around, side length, area, square unit, attribute, defining attribute, non-defining attribute, quadrilateral, rectangle, square, rhombus, polygon, partition, equal area, category, subcategory.

### Prerequisites

Students need area as square-unit covering, addition and multiplication fluency, measurement units, shape vocabulary, fraction partitioning, and comparison language.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit depends on Unit 10 area and Unit 7 fraction partitioning. It consolidates geometry and measurement before the final mixed transfer unit and prepares for Grade 4 work with angles, symmetry, factors, multiples, and more complex measurement problems.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Confusing area and perimeter because both involve shapes and numbers.
- Counting squares when the question asks for perimeter.
- Adding side lengths for area.
- Classifying shapes by appearance or orientation instead of attributes.
- Believing equal-area parts must always have identical shapes.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

5-7 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use frequent mixed prompts that ask students to decide whether the task is about covering or boundary length. Revisit fraction vocabulary through equal partitions and keep multiplication/division facts active in perimeter and area contexts.

## Unit 12: Cumulative Modeling And Grade 3 Mastery

### Unit Purpose

Bring the level together through cumulative mixed problem solving. Students choose representations, identify mathematical structures, solve with appropriate operations, check reasonableness, and explain answers across arithmetic, fractions, measurement, data, area, perimeter, geometry, and simple financial contexts.

### Student Outcomes

- Choose an appropriate model or representation without being told which one to use.
- Solve mixed one-step and accessible two-step problems involving all major Grade 3 domains.
- Explain why a strategy, equation, diagram, or answer fits the situation.
- Identify and correct common misconceptions in worked examples or answer choices.
- Demonstrate retained fluency with multiplication/division within 100 and whole-number operations within Grade 3 expectations.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Model, representation, equation, strategy, reasonable, estimate, unit, factor, product, quotient, fraction, equivalent, compare, scale, area, perimeter, attribute, evidence, explain.

### Prerequisites

Students need the full body of Grade 3 concepts and representations from Units 1-11, including arithmetic fluency, fraction magnitude, measurement/data interpretation, area/perimeter distinction, and shape attribute reasoning.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit reviews and integrates the entire Grade 3 course. It prepares students for Grade 4 work with multi-digit multiplication, division with remainders, factors and multiples, expanded fraction equivalence and operations, decimal notation, angle measurement, and more complex multi-step modeling.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Applying the most recent procedure to every problem.
- Choosing operations by key words instead of structure.
- Ignoring units, scale, or the meaning of an intermediate answer.
- Treating a fast answer as evidence of mastery when the reasoning is weak.
- Failing to transfer a known model to a new diagram or context.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use deliberately mixed review with delayed retrieval. Include arithmetic facts, place value, fractions, measurement, data, area, perimeter, and geometry in short cycles so mastery evidence reflects retention and transfer, not same-day pattern matching.
