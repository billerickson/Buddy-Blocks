# Unit 4: Mood, Voice, And Register

Track: Grammar
Level: Level 4
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/grammar-4/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students use grammar to match meaning, responsibility, tone, and audience. By the end of the unit, they can recognize common mood meanings, distinguish active and passive voice effects, and revise register respectfully for formal, informal, or academic contexts.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

After students have practiced sentence shape, balance, precision, and concision, they are ready to make subtler choices about how sentences position the actor, the action, the audience, and the writer's stance.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: sentence purpose, condition, wish, suggestion, command, actor, receiver, responsibility, tone, audience fit, standard academic English as context.
- Vocabulary: indicative, imperative, conditional, subjunctive, active voice, passive voice, agent, receiver, register, formal, informal, academic language, dialect, audience, purpose.
- Representations and models: actor-action-receiver charts, mood meaning labels, register ladders, audience/purpose scenarios.
- Texts and forms: school announcements, emails, lab or history sentences, conversation snippets, civic or academic examples.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need subject and verb recognition, sentence purpose language, active/passive exposure, clause control, tone and audience vocabulary, and respect for language variation.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Passive voice is always wrong.
- The actor is always the subject.
- Formal writing should sound stiff or inflated.
- Informal language or dialect is inferior rather than context-specific.
- Subjunctive and conditional meaning require memorizing many labels before using them.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with mood as sentence meaning so students can recognize statements, commands, conditions, wishes, and suggestions. Then give controlled practice with conditions, wishes, and suggestions because these are common in school and civic writing. Move to active and passive voice as choices about focus and responsibility. End with register so students apply grammar choices to audience and purpose.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Lessons revisit concise revision, sentence openings, modifiers, and clause boundaries. Active/passive and register questions include distractors that are wordy, falsely formal, or unclear.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares Unit 5 by showing how punctuation can shift tone and focus. It prepares Unit 6 by giving students voice and register as explicit targets during focused edit passes.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Match a sentence to its mood meaning in clear cases.
- Complete controlled conditional, wish, or suggestion sentences.
- Identify the actor and receiver in active and passive sentences.
- Choose active or passive voice for a stated purpose.
- Revise register for audience while preserving meaning and respect.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **What Mood Means In A Sentence**: Identify statement, command, condition, wish, and suggestion meanings.
2. **Conditions, Wishes, And Suggestions**: Use controlled verb forms for hypothetical, desired, or recommended situations.
3. **Active Or Passive For A Reason**: Choose voice based on clarity, responsibility, and focus.
4. **Register For Audience And Purpose**: Match usage and tone to context while respecting language variation.
