# Unit 5: Punctuation As Craft

Track: Grammar
Level: Level 4
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/grammar-4/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students use punctuation to show relationships, rhythm, and emphasis. By the end of the unit, they can choose semicolons, colons, commas, dashes, or parentheses in controlled cases and explain how punctuation affects meaning in short author sentences.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

Punctuation appeared earlier as sentence control. It belongs here again after students have studied sentence variety, modifiers, voice, and register, because punctuation choices now have richer effects on rhythm, clarity, and tone.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: relationship between ideas, explanation, example, list, interruption, aside, contrast, pause, emphasis, punctuation restraint.
- Vocabulary: comma, semicolon, colon, dash, parentheses, independent clause, explanation, example, interruption, aside, pause, rhythm, punctuation effect.
- Representations and models: punctuation decision charts, clause relationship labels, before/after sentence pairs, effect labels.
- Texts and forms: literary sentences, historical or civic sentences, short academic examples, revision-choice and punctuation-insertion items.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need independent clause recognition, compound and complex sentence control, list punctuation, appositives and interrupters, parallel list awareness, and register/tone vocabulary.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- A semicolon is just a stronger comma.
- A colon can follow any words before a list.
- Dashes and parentheses are interchangeable.
- More punctuation makes writing more mature.
- Punctuation rules matter only for correctness, not meaning.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Start with semicolons because they depend directly on independent clause control. Then teach colons as forward-pointing punctuation for explanation, example, or list. Next, compare commas, dashes, and parentheses for interruption and emphasis. End with punctuation effects in author sentences so students read grammar choices before using them in cumulative revision.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Lessons revisit Unit 1 boundaries, Unit 2 rhythm, Unit 3 modifiers and parallel lists, and Unit 4 register. Several items ask students to choose no added punctuation when that is the clearest revision.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares Unit 6 by giving students punctuation as both a proofreading target and an author-choice lens. It also supports later writing work where students must justify punctuation choices.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Choose semicolons only between closely related independent clauses.
- Choose colons before explanations, examples, and lists when the setup is complete.
- Compare commas, dashes, and parentheses for interruption or aside.
- Identify punctuation effects in short literary, historical, or civic sentences.
- Avoid punctuation overuse and select a simpler boundary when clearer.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Semicolons For Close Connections**: Use semicolons to join related independent clauses without a conjunction.
2. **Colons That Point Forward**: Use colons after complete setups to introduce explanation, example, or list.
3. **Commas, Dashes, And Parentheses**: Compare punctuation choices for interruption, aside, and emphasis.
4. **Punctuation Effects In Author Sentences**: Read punctuation as part of tone, rhythm, and meaning.
