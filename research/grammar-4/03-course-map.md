# Grammar 4 Course Map

Track: Grammar
Level: Level 4
Sources: Accepted research brief at `research/grammar-4/01-research-brief.md` and level blueprint at `research/grammar-4/02-level-blueprint.md`

## Course Design Notes

This course map moves from sentence control into grammar as choice. The sequence begins with clause and punctuation readiness, then develops sentence variety, parallelism, modifier precision, concision, mood, voice, register, punctuation as craft, and cumulative editing systems. Units are intentionally compact because the level depends on repeated short practice across many grammar targets rather than long isolated drills.

## Unit 1: Sentence Control For Purposeful Revision

### Unit Purpose

Stabilize the sentence-boundary, clause, phrase, punctuation, agreement, and modifier knowledge students need before using grammar for style. Students review correctness as the foundation for meaningful revision.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify independent clauses, dependent clauses, and phrases in short sentences.
- Correct fragments, run-ons, and comma splices in clear cases.
- Choose semicolons, conjunctions, or sentence breaks to connect independent clauses.
- Review pronoun agreement and modifier placement as clarity tools.
- Explain how sentence control supports reader understanding.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Independent clause, dependent clause, phrase, fragment, run-on, comma splice, coordinating conjunction, subordinating conjunction, semicolon, colon, pronoun agreement, antecedent, modifier, sentence boundary, clarity.

### Prerequisites

Students should already know major parts of speech, basic phrase and clause language, common punctuation marks, compound and complex sentence structures, pronoun agreement, and modifier placement in familiar cases.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit reconnects earlier grammar knowledge to revision. It prepares students for sentence variety in Unit 2, parallel and modifier work in Unit 3, punctuation craft in Unit 5, and focused edit passes in Unit 6.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A long sentence is automatically a run-on.
- A fragment is always short.
- A comma can join any two complete thoughts by itself.
- Pronoun agreement matters only in grammar worksheets, not revision.
- Modifiers can sit anywhere if the sentence sounds interesting.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Sentence-boundary checks should recur in every later unit. Pronoun agreement and modifier placement should return as distractors in passage revision items so students keep correctness tied to clarity.

## Unit 2: Sentence Variety, Rhythm, And Emphasis

### Unit Purpose

Help students use sentence structure deliberately. Students revise sentence length, openings, combining, appositives, interrupters, and repeated patterns to make writing clearer, smoother, and more emphatic.

### Student Outcomes

- Compare simple, compound, complex, and compound-complex structures for effect.
- Revise repetitive sentence openings and lengths without creating errors.
- Combine related ideas into fluent sentences while preserving meaning.
- Use appositives and interrupters in controlled cases.
- Identify purposeful repetition and sentence rhythm in literary or civic sentences.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Sentence variety, rhythm, emphasis, flow, simple sentence, compound sentence, complex sentence, compound-complex sentence, sentence opening, sentence combining, appositive, interrupter, repetition, style.

### Prerequisites

Students need reliable sentence-boundary knowledge, clause recognition, punctuation for compound and complex sentences, and basic revision experience.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit uses Unit 1 sentence control for style. It prepares Unit 3 by showing that patterns can guide readers, Unit 5 by linking punctuation to rhythm, and Unit 6 by giving students revision options beyond error correction.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Style means adding fancy words.
- Every sentence in a paragraph should be the same length.
- Combining sentences always improves writing.
- An appositive or interrupter can be added anywhere without punctuation.
- Repetition is always a mistake.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Interleave sentence-boundary review with every sentence-combining task. Later units should ask students to preserve useful sentence variety while editing for parallelism, voice, punctuation, and concision.

## Unit 3: Parallelism, Modifiers, And Concision

### Unit Purpose

Build revision judgment around patterns and precision. Students learn to keep equal ideas in equal forms, place modifiers close to the words they describe, and remove wordiness without stripping important detail.

### Student Outcomes

- Repair faulty parallelism in lists, paired ideas, and comparisons.
- Choose parallel structures that make ideas easier to compare.
- Identify misplaced and dangling modifiers in clear cases.
- Revise wordy sentences for concision while preserving meaning.
- Decide whether a detail should be cut, moved, or kept.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Parallel structure, list, series, paired construction, comparison, modifier, misplaced modifier, dangling modifier, concise, wordy, precise, redundancy, essential detail, revision.

### Prerequisites

Students need sentence control, basic phrase recognition, adjective/adverb and modifier language, and enough reading control to compare sentence versions.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit extends Unit 2 pattern work into grammatical balance and precision. It prepares Unit 4 register work because concision and tone interact, Unit 5 punctuation choices because modifiers and interrupters need punctuation, and Unit 6 portfolio editing.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Parallel structure is optional decoration.
- A list is parallel if all items are about the same topic.
- A modifier can describe any nearby noun.
- Concision means removing all detail.
- Longer sentences always sound more advanced.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Review Unit 1 sentence boundaries and Unit 2 sentence combining while repairing parallelism and concision. Later units should include false-parallel and wordy distractors.

## Unit 4: Mood, Voice, And Register

### Unit Purpose

Teach students to choose grammar based on meaning, responsibility, audience, and context. Students recognize common verb moods, choose active or passive voice deliberately, and revise register respectfully.

### Student Outcomes

- Distinguish statement, command, condition, wish, and suggestion meanings in clear cases.
- Recognize indicative, imperative, conditional, and common subjunctive uses without exhaustive taxonomy.
- Choose active voice when the actor should be clear and passive voice when the receiver or result matters most.
- Revise tone and usage for formal, informal, and academic contexts.
- Explain register as audience fit rather than language superiority.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Indicative, imperative, conditional, subjunctive, statement, command, condition, wish, suggestion, active voice, passive voice, agent, receiver, register, formal, informal, academic language, audience, purpose, dialect, standard academic English.

### Prerequisites

Students need verb, subject, clause, sentence purpose, active/passive recognition, and basic audience/purpose language from writing instruction.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit uses sentence structure from Units 1-3 to make meaning and audience choices. It prepares Unit 5 by showing how punctuation can shape tone and Unit 6 by giving students revision categories for focused edit passes.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Passive voice is always wrong.
- Formal writing means stiff writing.
- Informal or dialect language is "bad grammar" instead of context-dependent language.
- Subjunctive means every unusual verb form must be memorized.
- Register changes should make writing sound impressive rather than appropriate.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Continue sentence-boundary and concision review inside register and voice choices. Use short, respectful contexts that contrast audience and purpose without mocking informal speech.

## Unit 5: Punctuation As Craft

### Unit Purpose

Move punctuation from rule recall to meaning and rhythm. Students use commas, semicolons, colons, dashes, and parentheses to show relationships among ideas and to read author choices in literary, historical, and civic sentences.

### Student Outcomes

- Choose semicolons and colons for clear relationships among independent clauses, explanations, examples, or lists.
- Use commas, dashes, and parentheses to signal interruption, aside, or emphasis in controlled cases.
- Compare punctuation choices for rhythm, tone, and clarity.
- Identify punctuation effects in short literary or civic sentences.
- Avoid punctuation overuse when a simpler sentence boundary would be clearer.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Comma, semicolon, colon, dash, parentheses, independent clause, explanation, example, list, interruption, aside, emphasis, pause, rhythm, contrast, punctuation effect.

### Prerequisites

Students need sentence-boundary control, clause recognition, appositive and interrupter exposure, parallel-list awareness, and experience comparing revision options.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit revisits Unit 1 punctuation control, Unit 2 rhythm, Unit 3 modifiers and parallel lists, and Unit 4 tone. It prepares Unit 6 for polished copyediting and revision justification.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Punctuation is only correctness and has no effect on meaning.
- A semicolon is just a fancy comma.
- A colon can appear after any incomplete phrase before a list.
- Dashes and parentheses mean the same thing in every context.
- More punctuation makes writing sound more mature.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Interleave sentence-boundary repair, parallel lists, appositives, and register choices. Include examples where the best punctuation choice is no added punctuation.

## Unit 6: Editing Systems And Grammar In Context

### Unit Purpose

Bring the level together through focused edit passes, copyediting marks, literary and civic sentence analysis, and short revision portfolios. Students practice fixing one target at a time, then combining targets without losing meaning.

### Student Outcomes

- Use common copyediting marks for delete, insert, transpose, capitalize, lowercase, and punctuation changes.
- Complete focused edit passes for sentence boundaries, punctuation, parallelism, modifiers, voice, register, and concision.
- Analyze grammar choices in literary, historical, or civic sentences.
- Choose revisions that preserve meaning while improving clarity, rhythm, or audience fit.
- Explain the purpose of a revision in brief, constrained formats.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Copyediting mark, delete, insert, transpose, capitalize, lowercase, proofread, revise, focused edit pass, portfolio, grammar in context, author choice, preserve meaning, cumulative review.

### Prerequisites

Students need the full Grammar 4 sequence: sentence control, sentence variety, parallelism, modifier precision, concision, mood, voice, register, punctuation craft, and short passage revision habits.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit is the cumulative transfer unit. It prepares students for later grammar and writing work by building independent edit routines and requiring students to choose among grammar targets rather than responding to a single isolated rule.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Copyediting means fixing everything at once.
- A revision is better just because it is longer or sounds more formal.
- Grammar in literature follows no rules.
- Editing marks are decorative rather than instructions for a change.
- Fixing one error guarantees the whole passage is polished.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use cumulative mixed review in every lesson. Keep passages short and edit targets explicit so students practice disciplined revision instead of scanning randomly for mistakes.
