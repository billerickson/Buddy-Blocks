# Grammar 4 Level Blueprint

Track: Grammar
Level: Level 4
Source: Accepted research brief at `research/grammar-4/01-research-brief.md`

## 1. Level Mission

Grammar 4 helps students move from knowing grammar terms and correcting clear errors to using grammar as a deliberate tool for clarity, emphasis, rhythm, register, and revision. Students review clause and sentence control, then apply that control to sentence variety, parallel structure, concise modifiers, verb mood and voice, punctuation choices, usage/register decisions, literary reading, and focused copyediting. By the end of the level, students should be able to revise short passages with purpose, explain why a grammar choice improves meaning, and respect the difference between home language, informal language, and standard academic English.

## 2. Durable Student Outcomes

By the end of Grammar 4, students should be able to:

1. Identify independent clauses, dependent clauses, phrases, and sentence boundaries in order to revise sentences for correctness and effect.
2. Vary sentence structure and length to improve emphasis, flow, and reader attention.
3. Use parallel structure in lists, comparisons, paired ideas, and repeated patterns.
4. Tighten modifiers and remove wordiness while preserving important meaning and detail.
5. Recognize and revise clear cases of indicative, imperative, conditional, and subjunctive meaning in student-friendly contexts.
6. Choose active or passive voice based on clarity, emphasis, responsibility, and audience.
7. Select punctuation that clarifies relationships among ideas and shapes rhythm in sentences.
8. Choose usage and register that fit audience, purpose, and context without treating nonstandard or informal language as inferior.
9. Read literary, historical, and civic sentences for the grammar choices that shape tone, emphasis, and meaning.
10. Use copyediting marks and focused edit passes to revise short passages without trying to fix everything at once.

## 3. Concept And Skill Progression

The level begins with a short return to sentence control. Students revisit clauses, phrases, fragments, run-ons, comma splices, semicolons, colons, pronoun agreement, and modifier placement as tools for clean revision rather than as isolated labels. This opening stage ensures that later work with style and register rests on reliable sentence boundaries.

Students then move from correctness to choice. They study sentence length, openings, combining, appositives, interrupters, and purposeful repetition to understand how writers create rhythm and emphasis. The main skill is not making sentences "fancier"; it is choosing the structure that makes the idea easiest or most powerful for the reader.

Next, students focus on parallel structure, modifiers, and concision. They repair faulty lists and paired forms, choose modifiers that sit near the words they describe, and cut wordiness without cutting needed detail. This stage builds revision judgment and prepares students to edit short passages instead of only individual sentences.

The middle of the level introduces mood, voice, and register. Students learn to recognize common meanings of statement, command, condition, wish, suggestion, active voice, and passive voice. They practice choosing voice and register for clarity, tone, responsibility, and audience fit.

Punctuation returns as craft. Students review commas, semicolons, colons, dashes, and parentheses in controlled cases, with attention to how punctuation shows relationship, pause, contrast, explanation, and interruption. Literary and civic sentence examples help students see punctuation as meaning-making, not decoration.

The level ends with cumulative editing systems and revision portfolios. Students practice focused passes for sentence boundaries, punctuation, modifiers, parallelism, voice, register, and concision. They use copyediting marks, compare revision options, and justify choices in brief, app-scorable ways.

## 4. Vocabulary, Representation, Model, Text, And Form Progression

Early vocabulary reviews core grammar language: independent clause, dependent clause, phrase, fragment, run-on, comma splice, subject, verb, pronoun, modifier, agreement, conjunction, semicolon, colon, and sentence boundary.

Style vocabulary then expands to sentence variety, simple sentence, compound sentence, complex sentence, compound-complex sentence, opener, appositive, interrupter, emphasis, rhythm, repetition, parallel structure, series, comparison, paired construction, concise, wordy, precise, modifier, misplaced modifier, and dangling modifier.

Mood, voice, and register vocabulary includes indicative, imperative, conditional, subjunctive, active voice, passive voice, agent, receiver, formal register, informal register, academic language, audience, purpose, tone, dialect, standard academic English, respectful revision, and usage choice.

Punctuation and editing vocabulary includes comma, semicolon, colon, dash, parentheses, quotation, interruption, explanation, contrast, pause, copyediting mark, delete, insert, transpose, capitalize, lowercase, indent, focused edit pass, proofread, revise, and justify.

Texts and representations progress from single sentences and paired revisions to short paragraphs, literary and historical sentences, civic or academic examples, and brief student-written passages. Response forms progress from recognition and matching to sentence ordering, fill-blank revision, exact error correction, passage-based choice, and constrained explanations through multiple-choice items.

## 5. Fluency Expectations

Grammar 4 fluency means students can quickly recognize common sentence-boundary errors, obvious parallelism breaks, misplaced modifiers, wordy constructions, active/passive choices, and punctuation patterns in short text. Students should not be expected to memorize exhaustive taxonomy or obscure usage rules. Instead, they should build fluent routines: find the main clause, name the relationship between ideas, check whether forms match, ask what the modifier describes, choose the punctuation that shows the relationship, and reread for audience and purpose.

Short cumulative practice should support automatic recognition of high-value patterns. Fluency is evidenced by accurate, flexible revision decisions, not by speed alone.

## 6. Conceptual Understanding Expectations

Students should understand that grammar describes choices writers make to help readers follow meaning. Correctness matters because it supports clarity, but style also depends on sentence rhythm, emphasis, and audience. Students should understand that a sentence can be grammatically correct and still need revision because it is wordy, awkward, unclear, mismatched in tone, or poorly organized.

Students should understand parallel structure as a pattern that helps readers compare equal ideas. They should understand modifiers as meaning carriers that must sit near what they describe. They should understand active and passive voice as choices with effects, not as a simple good/bad pair. They should understand register as context-sensitive, with standard academic English taught as an additional formal context rather than a judgment of home language.

Students should understand punctuation as a signal of relationship and rhythm. A semicolon joins closely related independent clauses; a colon points forward to explanation, example, or list; commas, dashes, and parentheses can shape interruption and emphasis in different ways.

## 7. Application And Transfer Expectations

Students should apply Grammar 4 work to short student-like passages, literary excerpts, civic or historical sentences, and academic paragraphs. Transfer is visible when students can choose the better revision for a new sentence, explain the reason for that choice, and avoid revisions that accidentally change meaning.

Students should transfer grammar knowledge into reading by noticing how authors use sentence length, punctuation, repetition, parallelism, or passive voice to create tone and emphasis. They should transfer grammar knowledge into writing by completing focused edit passes, using copyediting marks, and making purposeful revisions for clarity, rhythm, and audience fit.

## 8. Review And Spacing Plan

Review should be cumulative and brief. Clause and sentence-boundary work should reappear in every unit because unclear boundaries make later style work unstable. Pronoun agreement, modifier placement, semicolon/colon use, active/passive voice, sentence combining, quotation/dialogue punctuation, and grammar vocabulary should recur in short mixed items.

Each new unit should open with one or two prerequisite ideas from earlier units, then revisit those ideas in a new purpose. Parallelism should return during register and punctuation work. Modifier and concision work should return during passage revision. Mood and voice should return during literary and civic sentence analysis. Copyediting marks should be introduced late but then applied across every prior target.

Question sets should include delayed review through diagnostic distractors: wordiness, false parallelism, tone mismatch, unclear antecedents, punctuation overuse, passive voice used when responsibility should be clear, and revisions that sound polished but alter meaning.

## 9. Mastery Evidence For The End Of The Level

End-of-level mastery should be evidenced by a balanced body of short revision and analysis tasks. A student who has mastered Grammar 4 can correct sentence-boundary errors, choose sentence structures for emphasis and flow, repair faulty parallelism, tighten modifiers, remove wordiness, choose active or passive voice deliberately, select register for audience, and use punctuation to clarify idea relationships.

The strongest evidence includes transfer across short passages. The student can complete focused edit passes, use copyediting marks, identify the purpose of a revision, explain why one revision is stronger than another, and avoid changes that distort the original meaning. The student can also read literary or civic sentences and identify how grammar choices contribute to tone, emphasis, and clarity.
