# Grammar - Level 4 Lesson Briefs

Sources: accepted level blueprint, course map, and unit design briefs in `research/grammar-4/`.

## Unit 1: Sentence Control For Purposeful Revision

### Lesson 1: Clause, Phrase, Or Complete Thought?

- **Teaching goal:** Refresh the difference among phrases, dependent clauses, independent clauses, and complete sentences.
- **Student outcome:** The student can classify a word group and explain whether it can stand alone.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A complete sentence needs an independent clause that expresses a complete thought; phrases and dependent clauses need support.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Subject and verb recognition, basic clause language, and sentence punctuation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Thinking any group with a verb is complete; treating length as proof that a sentence is complete.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given short word groups, the student can sort them and choose the reason a group can or cannot stand alone.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for group-to-category sorting; `multiple-choice` for reason checks; `fill-blank` for completing a sentence label.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "A phrase gives part of an idea. A dependent clause has a subject and verb but still leans on another idea. An independent clause can stand alone as a complete thought."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short word groups with familiar vocabulary; avoid ambiguous fragments where context could change the answer.

### Lesson 2: Repair Fragments And Run-Ons

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to repair sentence-boundary errors with complete, clear revisions.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose a valid repair for a fragment, run-on, or comma splice.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Boundary repairs either complete an unfinished thought, separate complete thoughts, or connect complete thoughts with correct punctuation and joining words.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Independent/dependent clauses, comma and period use, and coordinating conjunctions.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Adding any punctuation fixes a run-on; every long sentence is a run-on; every short sentence is a fragment.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses a repair that creates complete sentences without changing the intended meaning.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for best repair; `error-correction` for finite exact sentence fixes; `order-items` for deciding repair steps.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "A fragment is missing a complete thought. A run-on pushes complete thoughts together without a clear boundary. A comma splice joins complete thoughts with only a comma."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep each item to one target error; do not mix fragment and run-on repairs in the same sentence until after guided practice.

### Lesson 3: Joining Independent Clauses

- **Teaching goal:** Help students join independent clauses with semicolons, coordinating conjunctions, or sentence breaks.
- **Student outcome:** The student can select the clearest joining method for two closely related complete thoughts.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Two independent clauses can stand as two sentences, join with a comma plus coordinating conjunction, or join with a semicolon when closely related.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Independent clauses, comma splices, coordinating conjunctions, and sentence boundaries.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Using a semicolon after any long phrase; using a comma alone between independent clauses; joining unrelated ideas because both are complete.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student recognizes valid and invalid joins and revises a comma splice correctly.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for choosing punctuation or connector; `multiple-choice` for valid join recognition; `error-correction` for exact comma-splice repairs.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Independent clauses are complete thoughts. When two independent clauses are closely related, a semicolon can join them. A comma needs a coordinating conjunction such as and, but, or so."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use two-clause sentences with clear relationships; avoid semicolon cases that require advanced rhetorical judgment.

### Lesson 4: Pronouns And Modifiers For Clarity

- **Teaching goal:** Show how pronoun reference and modifier placement affect reader clarity.
- **Student outcome:** The student can revise unclear pronouns and misplaced modifiers in controlled sentences.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A pronoun should point clearly to one antecedent, and a modifier should sit near what it describes.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Pronouns, antecedents, modifiers, nouns, and basic sentence parts.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Pronouns are clear if the writer knows the referent; modifiers can float because readers will infer the meaning.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the confusion and chooses a revision that makes the sentence unambiguous.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for identifying the clarity problem; `error-correction` for finite exact revisions; `match-pairs` for pronoun-to-antecedent checks.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Readers should not have to guess. A pronoun should clearly name one earlier noun. A modifier should stay close to the word it describes."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use sentences with only two possible antecedents or one movable modifier; avoid humor-based modifier examples that distract from the grammar.

## Unit 2: Sentence Variety, Rhythm, And Emphasis

### Lesson 1: Sentence Length And Rhythm

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to notice how sentence length affects rhythm, pace, and emphasis.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose a sentence-length pattern that fits a purpose in a short passage.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Short sentences can create focus or speed; longer sentences can connect ideas and add flow when boundaries stay clear.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Sentence boundaries, simple/compound/complex sentence exposure, and paragraph reading.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Longer sentences always sound better; short sentences are always childish; rhythm is separate from grammar.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the effect of a short or long sentence and chooses a revision that improves rhythm.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for short paragraph rhythm; `multiple-choice` for effect recognition; `order-items` for arranging sentences for emphasis.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Writers can shape pace by changing sentence length. A short sentence can make an idea stand out. A longer sentence can connect related ideas."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short paragraphs of three to five sentences; ask for one effect at a time.

### Lesson 2: Strong Sentence Openings

- **Teaching goal:** Help students revise repetitive or weak sentence openings without creating fragments.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose a varied opening that keeps the sentence complete and clear.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Sentence openings can begin with the subject, a time or place phrase, a dependent clause, or a transition, but the full sentence must still be complete.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Phrases, dependent clauses, commas after introductory elements, and sentence boundaries.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Any moved phrase makes the sentence stronger; every sentence needs a dramatic opener; introductory clauses can stand alone.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student revises a repetitive paragraph opening and rejects fragment-like alternatives.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for best opener; `order-items` for arranging sentence parts; `fill-blank` for transition or opening phrase selection.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "If every sentence starts the same way, writing can feel flat. You can vary openings with time, place, contrast, or a dependent clause, as long as the sentence still has a complete thought."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep moved phrases short; use commas only in clear introductory cases.

### Lesson 3: Combine Without Crowding

- **Teaching goal:** Teach sentence combining as a choice, not an automatic improvement.
- **Student outcome:** The student can combine related ideas when it improves flow and leave ideas separate when clarity needs space.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Combining works when ideas are closely related and the new sentence does not bury the main point.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Coordinating conjunctions, subordination, sentence boundaries, and appositive exposure.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Combining all sentences is always more mature; every connector means the same thing; a combined sentence can ignore emphasis.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses a combined or separated version that preserves meaning and improves readability.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for best revision; `order-items` for building a combined sentence; `error-correction` for correcting an over-combined sentence.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Sentence combining can make writing smoother when ideas belong together. But some ideas need their own sentence so readers do not miss the main point."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use pairs or trios of sentences only; avoid asking students to combine many ideas at once.

### Lesson 4: Appositives, Interrupters, And Emphasis

- **Teaching goal:** Introduce appositives and interrupters as controlled tools for adding information and emphasis.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify or add an appositive/interrupter with appropriate punctuation in simple cases.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** An appositive renames a noun; an interrupter adds a side comment or extra detail that is set off from the main sentence.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Nouns, phrases, commas, dashes or parentheses as exposure, and sentence completeness.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Extra information can be dropped anywhere; commas, dashes, and parentheses all create the same emphasis; appositives are always needed.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects a clear sentence with a correctly placed appositive or interrupter and explains its effect.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for appositive-to-noun relationships; `fill-blank` for punctuation choices; `multiple-choice` for effect or best revision.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "An appositive is a noun phrase that renames a noun, such as 'Maya, the team captain, called the play.' Interrupters add extra information and must not break the main sentence."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one appositive or interrupter per sentence; avoid nonessential/essential terminology overload.

## Unit 3: Parallelism, Modifiers, And Concision

### Lesson 1: Lists That Stay Parallel

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to make list items use matching grammatical forms.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify and repair faulty parallelism in a series.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Parallel list items use the same kind of structure, such as all nouns, all verbs, or all phrases beginning the same way.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Parts of speech, lists, commas in a series, and sentence reading.
- **Likely misconceptions:** List items are parallel if they share a topic; one longer item can hide a form mismatch.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses a list revision with matching forms and rejects false-parallel alternatives.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for parallel version selection; `multi-blank-cloze` for completing list forms; `error-correction` for exact list repair.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Parallel structure means equal ideas use matching forms. In a list, try to keep the items in the same pattern: 'running, swimming, and biking' or 'to run, to swim, and to bike.'"
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use lists of three items; keep vocabulary familiar so form, not word meaning, carries the challenge.

### Lesson 2: Paired Ideas And Comparisons

- **Teaching goal:** Extend parallel structure to paired constructions and comparisons.
- **Student outcome:** The student can repair comparisons and paired ideas so both sides match.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Paired signals such as either/or, neither/nor, not only/but also, and comparisons need balanced forms.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Parallel lists, conjunctions, comparison language, and phrase recognition.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A comparison is clear if the topic is similar; paired words can connect mismatched structures.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects a parallel paired construction or comparison that compares like with like.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for completing paired signals; `multiple-choice` for best balanced comparison; `error-correction` for finite exact repairs.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Parallel structure also helps with pairs: 'either study quietly or practice aloud.' Both sides should match so readers can compare the ideas easily."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Teach one paired pattern at a time before mixing; avoid overly formal paired constructions.

### Lesson 3: Modifiers Near What They Describe

- **Teaching goal:** Help students place modifiers near the words they describe.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify what a modifier seems to describe and choose a clearer placement.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A modifier's position guides meaning; misplaced or dangling modifiers can make the wrong noun seem described.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Nouns, verbs, adjectives/adverbs, phrases, and sentence subjects.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Readers will always infer the intended meaning; moving a modifier never changes meaning.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student explains the unintended meaning of a misplaced modifier and chooses a revision that fixes it.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for "what does it seem to describe?"; `error-correction` for exact modifier revisions; `order-items` for arranging sentence parts.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "A modifier describes another word or idea. Keep the modifier close to what it describes: 'Wearing a red hat, Lena waved' is clearer than making the hat seem to wave."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use clear, concrete examples; avoid edge cases where more than one placement is acceptable.

### Lesson 4: Concision Without Losing Meaning

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to reduce wordiness while preserving important meaning.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose concise revisions that cut redundancy or filler without removing needed detail.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Concision means saying the needed idea clearly, not making every sentence as short as possible.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Sentence meaning, modifiers, revision comparison, and audience/purpose awareness.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Shorter is always better; formal-sounding phrases improve writing; cutting details is the same as cutting wordiness.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies redundancy and chooses a concise revision that keeps the original meaning.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for best concise revision; `passage-question` for short paragraph tightening; `error-correction` for finite wordy phrase repairs.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Concise writing removes extra words that do not add meaning. Keep details that help the reader; cut repetition and filler."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar wordy phrases and short paragraphs; do not reward revisions that remove necessary context.

## Unit 4: Mood, Voice, And Register

### Lesson 1: What Mood Means In A Sentence

- **Teaching goal:** Introduce mood as the sentence's way of showing statement, command, condition, wish, or suggestion.
- **Student outcome:** The student can match clear sentences to mood meanings without memorizing exhaustive taxonomy.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Mood helps show how the speaker presents an action: as fact, command, possibility, wish, or recommendation.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Verb recognition, sentence purpose, commands, and basic conditional language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Mood means emotion only; every verb form needs a technical label before meaning can be understood.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the meaning of clear indicative, imperative, conditional, and subjunctive-like sentences.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for sentence-to-meaning; `multiple-choice` for mood meaning; `fill-blank` for choosing a controlled verb phrase.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "In grammar, mood is not just feeling. It is how a sentence presents an action: a fact, a command, a condition, a wish, or a suggestion."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use meaning labels more than technical labels at first; avoid rare verb forms.

### Lesson 2: Conditions, Wishes, And Suggestions

- **Teaching goal:** Practice controlled forms for hypothetical, desired, and recommended situations.
- **Student outcome:** The student can complete common condition, wish, and suggestion sentences in standard academic English.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Some contexts use forms such as "were," "would," or a base verb after suggestions to show that something is hypothetical or recommended.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Mood meanings, verbs, if-clauses, and standard academic English context.
- **Likely misconceptions:** "Was" and "were" are always interchangeable; suggestions should always use "to" before the verb; hypothetical language is the same as fact.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses controlled verb forms that fit wishes, conditions, and recommendations.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for form choice; `multi-blank-cloze` for sentence completion; `multiple-choice` for meaning of the form.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Wishes, conditions, and suggestions sometimes use special verb patterns: 'If I were,' 'I would,' and 'I suggest that she bring.' These patterns help show that the idea is not a plain fact."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use a small set of high-frequency patterns; avoid exhaustive subjunctive terminology.

### Lesson 3: Active Or Passive For A Reason

- **Teaching goal:** Teach active and passive voice as choices with effects on clarity, focus, and responsibility.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify active/passive voice and choose the voice that fits a purpose.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Active voice puts the actor before the action; passive voice often puts the receiver or result first and may hide or delay the actor.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Subject, verb, object/receiver as informal language, and active/passive exposure.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Passive voice is always wrong; the subject always performs the action; passive voice is always more formal.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies actor and receiver, rewrites finite voice changes, and selects a voice for a given purpose.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for actor/receiver roles; `multiple-choice` for voice choice; `error-correction` for exact active/passive revisions.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "In active voice, the actor usually comes first: 'The committee approved the plan.' In passive voice, the receiver comes first: 'The plan was approved by the committee.' Writers choose voice for a reason."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use clear transitive verbs and short sentences; avoid passive constructions without an obvious actor until later items.

### Lesson 4: Register For Audience And Purpose

- **Teaching goal:** Help students revise usage and tone for audience while respecting language variation.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose a formal, informal, or academic register that fits a situation without judging other contexts as inferior.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Register is language fit for audience and purpose; standard academic English is useful in formal school contexts but not a measure of a person's worth or intelligence.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Audience, purpose, tone, formal/informal language, and revision comparison.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Formal writing means stiff writing; informal or dialect language is bad grammar; changing register means making writing sound fancy.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses a register revision that fits a context and preserves respectful meaning.
- **Recommended question types:** `dialogue-builder` for audience-appropriate response; `multiple-choice` for best register revision; `passage-question` for tone and audience analysis.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Register means matching language to the situation. A text to a friend, a speech to a school board, and a science report can all be clear, but they use different language choices."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid mocking nonstandard language; use respectful scenarios and clear audience labels.

## Unit 5: Punctuation As Craft

### Lesson 1: Semicolons For Close Connections

- **Teaching goal:** Use semicolons to join closely related independent clauses.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide when a semicolon is valid and when another boundary is clearer.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A semicolon can join two independent clauses that belong closely together; it is not a comma substitute.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Independent clauses, comma splices, coordinating conjunctions, and sentence breaks.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Semicolons can join any two long parts; semicolons make writing automatically sophisticated; a semicolon can follow a dependent clause alone.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects sentences where a semicolon is valid and repairs invalid semicolon use.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for valid semicolon use; `error-correction` for finite repairs; `fill-blank` for choosing punctuation.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "A semicolon joins two independent clauses that are closely related. Both sides should be able to stand alone as complete sentences."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use two-clause examples; avoid conjunctive adverb complexity unless directly taught in the prompt.

### Lesson 2: Colons That Point Forward

- **Teaching goal:** Teach colons as forward-pointing punctuation after a complete setup.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use a colon before an explanation, example, or list when the setup is complete.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A colon tells the reader, "Here comes the explanation, example, or list promised by the first part."
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Complete sentences, lists, explanations, examples, and clause boundaries.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A colon can appear after any phrase before a list; a colon and semicolon do the same job; a colon always means a list is coming.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies complete setups and chooses the colon revision that points forward clearly.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for colon-valid sentences; `fill-blank` for punctuation choice; `error-correction` for exact colon repairs.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "A colon points forward. Use it after a complete setup to introduce an explanation, example, or list: 'Bring three supplies: paper, pencils, and tape.'"
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep setup clauses short; make the introduced material clearly an explanation, example, or list.

### Lesson 3: Commas, Dashes, And Parentheses

- **Teaching goal:** Compare punctuation choices for interruptions, asides, and emphasis.
- **Student outcome:** The student can select commas, dashes, or parentheses based on how strongly the added information should stand out.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Commas gently set off extra information, dashes create stronger interruption or emphasis, and parentheses make an aside feel quieter.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Appositives, interrupters, commas, sentence completeness, and tone.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Dashes and parentheses mean the same thing; every aside needs the strongest punctuation; extra information can interrupt any point in a sentence.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses punctuation that fits the intended effect and maintains a readable sentence.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for effect choice; `fill-blank` for punctuation selection; `passage-question` for comparing tone in context.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Different punctuation can set off extra information in different ways. Commas are smooth, dashes are stronger, and parentheses feel like a quieter aside."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one interruption per sentence; avoid long parenthetical content.

### Lesson 4: Punctuation Effects In Author Sentences

- **Teaching goal:** Help students read punctuation as part of author craft in literary, historical, or civic sentences.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify how punctuation shapes rhythm, emphasis, contrast, or explanation.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Punctuation can guide how a reader hears the sentence and understands relationships among ideas.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Semicolons, colons, dashes, parentheses, sentence rhythm, and tone vocabulary.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Grammar in literature follows no patterns; punctuation effects are only personal preference; any dramatic punctuation has the same effect.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student reads a short sentence or passage and chooses the best explanation of the punctuation effect.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for author-sentence analysis; `multiple-choice` for effect explanation; `match-pairs` for punctuation mark to effect.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "When you read, punctuation gives clues about pace and meaning. A colon may announce an explanation; a dash may interrupt; a semicolon may hold two related thoughts together."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short public-domain or original model sentences; provide enough context for effect questions.

## Unit 6: Editing Systems And Grammar In Context

### Lesson 1: Copyediting Marks And Focused Passes

- **Teaching goal:** Introduce common copyediting marks and the habit of editing for one target at a time.
- **Student outcome:** The student can match copyediting marks to actions and choose a focused edit pass for a problem.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Copyediting marks are instructions, and focused passes help writers avoid trying to fix everything at once.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Sentence boundaries, punctuation, capitalization, insertion/deletion language, and revision categories.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Copyediting marks are decorative; proofreading and revising are the same; good editors scan randomly for all errors at once.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches marks to actions and selects the next focused pass for a short passage.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for mark-to-action; `multiple-choice` for choosing edit pass; `order-items` for sequencing revision passes.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Editors often use marks as quick instructions: delete, insert, move, capitalize, lowercase, or add punctuation. A focused pass means you look for one kind of issue before moving to the next."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use plain-text descriptions of marks when symbol rendering might be unclear; limit pass choices to familiar categories.

### Lesson 2: Literary Sentences Under The Grammar Lens

- **Teaching goal:** Analyze how sentence structure, punctuation, repetition, and voice shape meaning in literary or civic sentences.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a grammar choice and its effect in a short author-style sentence.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Grammar choices can slow a reader down, speed a sentence up, emphasize a contrast, hide or reveal an actor, or create a pattern.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Sentence rhythm, repetition, punctuation effects, active/passive voice, and parallelism.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Literary grammar has no rules; author choices cannot be explained; effect answers are purely personal.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses the grammar feature and effect that best match a short sentence.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for sentence analysis; `multiple-choice` for feature/effect selection; `match-pairs` for feature-to-effect.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Authors use grammar to guide attention. Sentence length, repetition, punctuation, and voice can all shape how a sentence feels and what readers notice."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use brief original or public-domain-style sentences; ask about one or two features at a time.

### Lesson 3: Revise A Passage In Passes

- **Teaching goal:** Apply multiple Grammar 4 targets through ordered revision passes on a short passage.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose and complete sentence-boundary, clarity, style, and punctuation revisions in a sensible order.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Revision improves when writers fix foundations first, then refine style and polish punctuation.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** All prior Grammar 4 units and focused edit-pass language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Editing can happen in any order with the same result; style edits should come before fixing unclear sentences; one corrected sentence means the passage is finished.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the first pass, chooses targeted revisions, and preserves meaning across a short passage.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for passage revision choices; `order-items` for pass sequence; `error-correction` for finite exact sentence repairs.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "When a passage has several problems, start with the foundation: complete sentences and clear meaning. Then revise for style, register, and punctuation."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use passages of four to six short sentences; score one target per question.

### Lesson 4: Portfolio Check: Justify The Revision

- **Teaching goal:** Have students evaluate before/after revisions and identify the purpose of each change.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose the best revision and the clearest justification for it.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A strong revision improves a specific target while preserving the writer's intended meaning.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Full Grammar 4 sequence, revision comparison, and grammar-effect vocabulary.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A revision is better if it is longer, more formal, or more dramatic; justification can be vague; changing meaning is acceptable if the sentence sounds smoother.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects a revision, names the reason it works, and rejects revisions that alter meaning or target the wrong problem.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for best justification; `passage-question` for before/after comparison; `match-pairs` for revision-to-purpose.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "A portfolio revision should have a reason. Ask: What problem did this change fix? Did it preserve the meaning? Did it improve clarity, rhythm, register, or punctuation?"
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep before/after passages short; use answer choices that name concrete grammar targets rather than vague praise.
