# French 3 Research Brief

Track: French  
Level: Level 3  
Intended student profile: continuing learners who can handle Novice Mid exchanges and are ready for a Novice High bridge: recombining language into connected sentences across familiar topics.

This brief defines outcomes and instructional priorities only. It does not define units, lessons, or questions.

## 1. End-Of-Level Outcomes

By the end of French 3, students should be able to participate in short connected conversations on familiar topics; interpret authentic-looking schedules, notes, maps, messages, menus, weather/community texts, and dialogues; produce connected sentences about self, school, routines, hobbies, weather, places, and preferences; use être, avoir, aller, faire, regular -er/-ir/-re verbs in supported present-tense contexts; manage articles, partitive chunks, adjective agreement, negation, and question formation; and compare everyday Francophone cultural practices.

## 2. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should control French 2 vocabulary and routines, predictable questions, simple descriptions, present-time chunks, articles, adjective agreement, basic negation, numbers/time/date, tu/vous, and key interpretive/listening strategies.

## 3. Major Concept Clusters

- **Novice High Recombination:** connected sentences, familiar conversations, and short presentational descriptions.
- **Present-Tense System Awareness:** subject pronouns, être/avoir/aller/faire, regular -er/-ir/-re patterns, and verb endings as meaning clues.
- **French Structure:** articles, partitive chunks, adjective agreement/placement, ne...pas, question formation, and common prepositions.
- **Daily Life And Community:** routines, hobbies, school schedules, weather, community places, directions, menus, and messages.
- **Sound And Listening:** liaison exposure, silent letters, nasal vowels, elision, and accent-aware spelling.

## 4. Recommended Progression

Begin with high-frequency review and listening routines. Teach subject pronouns and present-tense regular patterns as tools for understanding and producing familiar messages, not chart recitation. Spiral être/avoir/aller/faire and ne...pas through real contexts. Add daily routines, hobbies, weather, and community places. Use authentic-looking short texts early and often. End with connected-sentence conversations and short presentational writing.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Verb endings are optional because subject pronouns are present.
- Regular verb patterns apply to all high-frequency verbs.
- `Être` and `avoir` map neatly to English "is" and "has."
- Partitives are just extra articles that can be dropped.
- Adjective placement follows English.
- `Ne...pas` can be placed anywhere near the sentence.
- Connected sentences require translating an English paragraph.
- Listening comprehension requires hearing every word.

## 6. Developmental Constraints And Reading/Load Concerns

French written and spoken forms diverge more than Spanish, so pair text with audio frequently. Keep texts short but connected. Support verb patterns with meaningful subject/action contexts. Do not expect spontaneous accuracy across all subjects and verbs. Maintain accent-tolerant scoring where appropriate, but model standard forms.

## 7. Assessment Implications

Assess interpretation of short texts/audio, question formation/answering, present-tense meaning, connected sentence production, and cultural comparison. Use dialogue-builder, listening-question, passage-question, fill-blank, text-input, multi-blank cloze, and short constructed response. Distractors should reveal subject-verb mismatch, article/partitive confusion, negation-frame error, adjective-placement error, and sound-spelling confusion.

## 8. Spiral Review Priorities

Numbers/time/date, articles/gender, adjective agreement, question words, être/avoir/aller/faire/il y a/aimer, regular present endings, ne...pas, school/food/place vocabulary, directions, weather, tu/vous, cognates, accents, liaison exposure, and Francophone culture.

## 9. Concepts That Should Not Be Taught Yet

Avoid passé composé/imparfait as productive contrast, object pronoun systems, subjunctive, conditional, complex relative clauses, full partitive exceptions, extended essays, and fully unscripted multi-topic conversation.
