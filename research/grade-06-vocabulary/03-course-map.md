# Grade 6 Vocabulary Course Map

Track: Vocabulary  
Level: Grade 6  
Sources: Accepted research brief at `research/grade-06-vocabulary/01-research-brief.md` and level blueprint at `research/grade-06-vocabulary/02-level-blueprint.md`

## Course Design Notes

This map treats Grade 6 Vocabulary as a compact middle-school word-learning course, not a spelling list or a broad ELA replacement. The sequence begins with a transferable word-learning routine, deepens morphology and academic language, then applies those tools to domain vocabulary, word choice, figurative language, argument, media, research, revision, and cumulative transfer. Each unit has four suggested lessons so every strand is taught seriously without turning the level into a long decontextualized list.

## Unit 1: Strategic Word Learning In Context

### Unit Purpose

Establish the routine students will reuse all year: use context, word parts, known words, reference tools, and meaning-fit checks to solve unfamiliar words.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify useful sentence and paragraph clues without assuming context always gives an exact definition.
- Combine context clues with morphology or prior knowledge to form a meaning hypothesis.
- Choose a reference entry that fits the word's part of speech and context.
- Test a proposed meaning by substituting it into the original sentence.
- Explain which clue or strategy supports the meaning.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Context, clue, infer, verify, reference, dictionary entry, glossary, part of speech, definition, example, morphology, prefix, suffix, root, meaning fit, strategy, hypothesis.

### Prerequisites

Students should be able to read short grade-level sentences and paragraphs, recognize basic prefixes and suffixes, use simple dictionaries or glossaries with guidance, and explain a clue in a sentence frame.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit stabilizes elementary context-clue and reference skills while preparing every later unit. Morphology in Unit 2, academic/domain vocabulary in Units 3-4, connotation in Unit 5, figurative language in Unit 6, and source vocabulary in Unit 7 all depend on this routine.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Context clues always give a perfect definition.
- A dictionary definition is useful even if it does not fit the sentence.
- The first definition listed is always correct.
- A word part alone is enough to determine meaning.
- Guessing once is the same as verifying.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use the word-learning routine as a warm-up and feedback frame throughout the level. Later units should ask students to name the evidence behind a meaning, not just choose the right answer.

## Unit 2: Morphology And Word Families

### Unit Purpose

Teach morphology as a useful but limited evidence source for academic word meaning. Students analyze prefixes, suffixes, roots, word families, cognates, and false patterns in meaningful Grade 6 words.

### Student Outcomes

- Use common prefixes and suffixes to infer changes in meaning or part of speech.
- Recognize high-utility Greek and Latin roots in academic words.
- Build and interpret related word families.
- Distinguish helpful morphology from false-looking patterns.
- Check morphology-based guesses against context.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Prefix, suffix, root, base word, affix, word family, part of speech, noun, verb, adjective, adverb, cognate, false pattern, etymology, infer, verify.

### Prerequisites

Students need basic experience with prefixes, suffixes, base words, context clues, and dictionary or glossary use. They should be able to compare related words such as sign, signal, and signature at a simple level.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit applies the routine from Unit 1 and supplies a durable tool for academic verbs, disciplinary vocabulary, argument terms, and research words in later units. It also prepares students to notice connotation and part-of-speech shifts in Unit 5.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Every root or prefix has exactly one meaning.
- Words with the same root always mean the same thing.
- Any familiar-looking letter group is a meaningful word part.
- Adding a suffix changes spelling only, not meaning or part of speech.
- Cognates are always safe translations.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Return to roots and affixes in later contexts instead of treating this as a single morphology block. Review should ask students to combine word-part clues with sentence clues.

## Unit 3: Academic Vocabulary For Reading, Tasks, And Discussion

### Unit Purpose

Build the high-utility Tier Two language students need to understand directions, explain thinking, use evidence, compare ideas, and participate in academic discussion.

### Student Outcomes

- Distinguish common academic verbs such as identify, analyze, infer, evaluate, summarize, cite, and support.
- Use evidence, inference, explanation, and reasoning words accurately.
- Understand compare/contrast, cause/effect, sequence, and relationship language.
- Use discussion moves such as clarify, elaborate, qualify, challenge, and synthesize in supported frames.
- Apply academic vocabulary in short reading and response tasks.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Analyze, infer, evaluate, summarize, cite, support, evidence, reason, explanation, compare, contrast, cause, effect, sequence, relationship, clarify, elaborate, qualify, challenge, synthesize.

### Prerequisites

Students should know basic school words such as answer, explain, compare, different, same, because, first, next, and evidence, though precise use may still be developing.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit uses the Unit 1 routine and Unit 2 morphology when helpful. Its vocabulary becomes the language of later lessons: students will use evidence words in argument, compare/contrast words in connotation and figurative language, and discussion words in research and transfer tasks.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Analyze means "answer quickly."
- Infer means "guess without evidence."
- Evidence and reason are the same thing.
- Compare only means find similarities, and contrast only means find differences.
- Challenge in discussion means argue rudely.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Recycle these words in every later unit's prompts and explanations. Use sentence frames to reduce writing load while requiring accurate word use.

## Unit 4: Disciplinary Vocabulary Across Subjects

### Unit Purpose

Help students recognize that words become more precise in subject-area contexts. Students learn selected math, science, history/social studies, literature, and source-analysis vocabulary through short texts, diagrams, tables, and excerpts.

### Student Outcomes

- Interpret math precision words such as factor, variable, expression, model, and estimate in context.
- Use science process and data words such as observe, variable, evidence, classify, data, and conclusion.
- Understand history, geography, and civics words such as source, region, culture, economy, citizen, and perspective.
- Use context to select the subject-specific meaning of words that also have everyday meanings.
- Explain why a technical word's meaning fits a diagram, table, or source excerpt.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Discipline, domain-specific, technical meaning, model, factor, variable, expression, estimate, observe, classify, data, conclusion, source, region, culture, economy, citizen, perspective, glossary.

### Prerequisites

Students need the strategic word-learning routine, basic academic verbs, experience with simple diagrams or data displays, and enough background knowledge to read short content-area contexts.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit applies Units 1-3 in subject contexts. It prepares students for connotation and precision in Unit 5, author-craft and informational language in Unit 6, source vocabulary in Unit 7, and research transfer in Unit 8.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Academic words belong to only one class.
- Everyday meanings always work in technical contexts.
- Diagrams and tables are separate from vocabulary clues.
- A glossary definition can be copied without checking context.
- Source means any website, regardless of origin or purpose.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Interleave content-area words with context, morphology, and reference checks. Later units should revisit words such as source, evidence, model, perspective, and precise.

## Unit 5: Connotation, Denotation, And Precise Word Choice

### Unit Purpose

Teach students that words with similar dictionary meanings can differ in feeling, intensity, formality, precision, and point of view. Students apply these distinctions to reading and revision.

### Student Outcomes

- Distinguish denotation from connotation.
- Identify positive, negative, neutral, formal, informal, mild, and intense word associations.
- Choose precise synonyms that fit audience, purpose, tone, and context.
- Explain how word choice affects meaning or reader response.
- Revise vague or mismatched word choice in short sentences.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Denotation, connotation, synonym, precise, vague, intensity, formal, informal, neutral, positive, negative, tone, audience, purpose, revise, word choice.

### Prerequisites

Students should understand synonyms and antonyms, basic shades of meaning, dictionary definitions, academic purpose/audience language, and sentence-level revision.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit builds on context and academic vocabulary, then prepares figurative language and author craft in Unit 6 and media framing in Unit 7. Revision language carries into Unit 8.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Connotation means positive or negative only.
- Synonyms can always be swapped.
- Stronger words are always better.
- Formal words are always clearer.
- Revision is only fixing spelling or punctuation.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Spiral connotation and precision through figurative language, argument, media, and revision tasks. Ask students to justify word choice using audience, purpose, tone, or exact meaning.

## Unit 6: Figurative Language And Author Craft

### Unit Purpose

Move students from labeling figurative language to interpreting how nonliteral and craft language shapes meaning, imagery, tone, and reader understanding.

### Student Outcomes

- Distinguish literal and figurative meanings in context.
- Interpret idioms, similes, metaphors, and personification in short literary or informational contexts.
- Connect imagery and word choice to tone and mood.
- Distinguish author attitude from reader feeling.
- Explain how a figurative phrase contributes to meaning.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Literal, figurative, idiom, simile, metaphor, personification, imagery, tone, mood, author craft, word choice, comparison, meaning, effect.

### Prerequisites

Students need context-clue skills, basic figurative-language exposure from earlier grades, connotation and word-choice language, and enough reading stamina for short excerpts.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit applies context, connotation, and academic discussion vocabulary. It prepares students to analyze media framing and word choice in Unit 7 and to complete cumulative transfer in Unit 8.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Figurative language is just decoration.
- A metaphor or idiom should be interpreted word by word.
- Tone and mood mean the same thing.
- The label matters more than the meaning.
- Imagery means any descriptive word, even if it does not appeal to the senses.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Continue quick connotation and precision review inside figurative-language contexts. Later units should ask how word choice affects audience or reader response.

## Unit 7: Argument, Media, And Source Vocabulary

### Unit Purpose

Give students the vocabulary needed to understand basic arguments, evaluate source language, and notice how media word choice can frame information.

### Student Outcomes

- Distinguish claim, reason, evidence, and explanation.
- Evaluate whether evidence is relevant and sufficient for a simple claim.
- Use credible, source, audience, purpose, perspective, and bias accurately.
- Recognize that bias is a perspective or slant to evaluate, not automatic proof that a source is false.
- Explain how headlines or word choices can frame an event.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Claim, reason, evidence, explanation, relevant, sufficient, credible, source, audience, purpose, perspective, bias, frame, media, headline, slant, support, counterclaim readiness.

### Prerequisites

Students need academic evidence vocabulary, connotation and tone language, source vocabulary from domain contexts, and experience reading short arguments or informational excerpts.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit combines academic vocabulary, connotation, precision, and source terms. It prepares the final unit's research, paraphrase, synthesis, and cumulative transfer tasks.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Evidence and reason are the same thing.
- Relevant evidence means any interesting detail.
- Sufficient means "some" evidence.
- Credible means "I agree with it."
- Bias means a source is automatically false.
- A headline simply reports facts without word-choice effects.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Interleave connotation, tone, perspective, and evidence vocabulary. Use short, low-stakes media and argument examples rather than long political texts or advanced rhetoric.

## Unit 8: Research, Revision, And Cumulative Transfer

### Unit Purpose

Bring the level together by using vocabulary as a tool for inquiry, paraphrase, synthesis, revision, portfolio habits, and transfer to unfamiliar contexts.

### Student Outcomes

- Use inquiry, source, keyword, note, paraphrase, cite, synthesize, and conclusion vocabulary accurately.
- Distinguish paraphrase from copying or changing only one word.
- Combine information from two short notes or sources with synthesis language.
- Maintain a word-learning portfolio entry with context, morphology, definition, connotation, example, non-example, and original sentence.
- Transfer vocabulary strategies to unfamiliar short passages, source excerpts, and writing samples.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Inquiry, research, source, keyword, note, paraphrase, quote, cite, synthesize, conclusion, revise, refine, transition, portfolio, context, morphology, definition, connotation, example, non-example, original sentence, transfer.

### Prerequisites

Students need the full level toolkit: context, morphology, reference use, academic verbs, disciplinary vocabulary, connotation, figurative language, argument/source terms, and brief written-response skills.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This capstone unit reviews and integrates Units 1-7. It prepares Grade 7 vocabulary work by requiring more independent strategy choice, explanation, and transfer across subjects.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Research vocabulary is only about finding websites.
- Paraphrase means changing a few words while keeping the original structure.
- Synthesize means list two facts separately.
- Portfolio entries are copied definitions rather than learning tools.
- A strategy learned in one unit only works in that unit.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

This unit should be fully cumulative. Question sets should interleave context, morphology, reference selection, word choice, figurative meaning, evidence/source vocabulary, revision, and portfolio reasoning.
