# Grade 6 Vocabulary Level Blueprint

Track: Vocabulary  
Level: Grade 6  
Source: Accepted research brief at `research/grade-06-vocabulary/01-research-brief.md`

## 1. Level Mission

Grade 6 Vocabulary helps students become independent middle-school word learners instead of short-term definition memorizers. Students learn a durable routine for figuring out unfamiliar words, then apply it to morphology, academic language, content-area vocabulary, word choice, figurative language, argument, media, discussion, research, and revision. By the end of the level, students should be able to use context, meaningful word parts, reference tools, and meaning-fit checks to understand new words in short Grade 6 texts and to use high-value academic words accurately in speech and writing.

## 2. Durable Student Outcomes

By the end of Grade 6 Vocabulary, students should be able to:

1. Use a repeatable word-learning routine: inspect context, analyze meaningful word parts, connect to known words, verify with a reference when needed, and test meaning in the sentence.
2. Determine or clarify meanings of unknown and multiple-meaning words in Grade 6 literary, informational, and content-area contexts.
3. Use common Greek and Latin roots, prefixes, suffixes, and word-family patterns to infer and explain academic word meanings.
4. Choose the reference entry, part of speech, pronunciation, origin note, or definition that best fits a specific context.
5. Acquire and use high-utility academic vocabulary for directions, reading analysis, discussion, argument, research, and revision.
6. Interpret selected domain-specific vocabulary from math, science, history/social studies, literature, media, and research when a short context makes the term meaningful.
7. Distinguish denotation, connotation, intensity, formality, precision, tone, and point of view in word choice.
8. Interpret figurative and author-craft language in context, including idioms, similes, metaphors, personification, imagery, tone, and mood.
9. Explain word relationships such as category, analogy, cause/effect, contrast, part/whole, root family, intensity, evidence/reason, and claim/support.
10. Maintain a small word-learning portfolio habit that records a word, context, morphology, definition, connotation, example, non-example, and original use.

## 3. Concept And Skill Progression

The level begins with strategic word learning because every later unit depends on a shared routine. Students first review sentence and paragraph context clues, learn that context may narrow but not always fully define a word, and practice checking whether a guessed meaning fits the sentence. They also learn when a reference tool is needed and how to choose the entry that matches part of speech and context.

Students then deepen morphology. Rather than memorizing a long root list, they work with small clusters of high-utility prefixes, suffixes, roots, and word families that appear in real academic words. Morphology is treated as evidence, not magic: students learn that word parts can help, but they must test the inferred meaning in context and watch for false patterns.

After the strategy and morphology base is established, the course builds general academic vocabulary. Students learn the verbs and relational words that appear in directions, discussions, reading tasks, explanations, and comparisons. This language is practiced through short prompts and sentence frames so students can use the words, not only recognize definitions.

Domain-specific vocabulary comes next through compact contexts from math, science, history/social studies, literature, media, and research. Students learn to read technical terms inside diagrams, data displays, source excerpts, and explanations. The goal is transfer across subjects, with enough context to make each term meaningful.

The course then moves into connotation, denotation, precision, and word choice. Students compare near-synonyms, identify changes in formality and intensity, and revise sentences to better fit audience, purpose, tone, and exact meaning.

Figurative language and author craft follow because students now have a stronger base in context and word choice. They interpret literal and nonliteral meanings, connect figurative language to imagery and tone, and distinguish author attitude from reader feeling.

Argument, media, and source vocabulary is introduced after students have the tools for academic meaning and word choice. Students distinguish claim, reason, evidence, relevance, sufficiency, credibility, audience, purpose, perspective, bias, and framing. The course ends with research, revision, discussion, portfolio habits, and cumulative transfer to unfamiliar short contexts.

## 4. Vocabulary, Representation, Model, Text, And Form Progression

Early vocabulary centers on the word-learning routine: context, clue, infer, verify, definition, entry, part of speech, example, morphology, prefix, suffix, root, base word, word family, meaning fit, and strategy. Representations include clue charts, dictionary-entry snapshots, word-part maps, and sentence substitution checks.

Morphology vocabulary expands to include common prefixes and roots that support Grade 6 academic language, including examples such as `mis`, `mit`, `scrib`, `script`, `jur`, `jus`, `bene`, `man`, `vac`, and high-utility affixes such as `pre-`, `re-`, `inter-`, `sub-`, `trans-`, `-tion`, `-ity`, `-ous`, `-ive`, and `-able`. Representations include word-family webs, root-plus-affix maps, cognate checks, and false-pattern warnings.

General academic vocabulary includes analyze, infer, evaluate, summarize, compare, contrast, cite, support, elaborate, clarify, qualify, relevant, sufficient, synthesize, revise, refine, transition, conclude, and similar high-transfer words. Text forms include task directions, short discussion turns, evidence statements, revision notes, and reading-response frames.

Domain vocabulary is introduced through compact texts and visual forms: math expressions and graphs, science observations and data tables, maps and source excerpts, literature snippets, media headlines, research notes, and glossary entries. Students should learn that a word such as model, factor, source, structure, or evidence can become more precise in different subjects.

Later vocabulary includes denotation, connotation, tone, mood, intensity, formality, precise, literal, figurative, idiom, simile, metaphor, personification, imagery, claim, reason, evidence, source, credible, audience, purpose, perspective, bias, framing, paraphrase, synthesize, cite, and portfolio. Response forms progress from selected answers and matching to brief explanations, sentence revision, and short constructed responses.

## 5. Fluency Expectations

Vocabulary fluency in this level means flexible, accurate word-solving and word use, not rapid isolated recall. Students should become fluent with the word-learning routine, common academic affixes and roots, dictionary-entry navigation, academic direction verbs, high-value discussion stems, and frequently revisited argument/source terms. Short retrieval practice is appropriate for roots, affixes, academic verbs, and word meanings after contextual exposure. Speed should never replace meaning-fit reasoning, pronunciation support, or accurate use in context.

## 6. Conceptual Understanding Expectations

Students should understand that word meaning comes from a network of evidence: context, word parts, prior knowledge, related words, reference tools, and sentence fit. They should know that no single clue type is always enough and that strong readers often combine clues before deciding.

Students should understand morphology as a meaning tool with limits. A root or affix can suggest meaning, but words can change over time, shift part of speech, or include letter patterns that look meaningful but are not useful in that word. Students should learn to treat morphology as a hypothesis that must be checked.

Students should understand that words carry more than dictionary definitions. Connotation, formality, intensity, precision, tone, point of view, and figurative meaning affect how a reader understands a text or how an audience hears a message. They should also understand that academic words can shift across disciplines and that a source's language can reveal purpose, perspective, or bias without proving the source false.

## 7. Application And Transfer Expectations

Students should apply vocabulary strategies to short unfamiliar contexts across subjects. Transfer should be visible when a student can infer the meaning of a new academic word, justify the clue or word part that helped, choose the correct dictionary definition, select a precise synonym for a particular tone, or explain how a technical term functions in a science, math, history, or media context.

Students should also use new vocabulary expressively. They should complete academic sentence frames, revise imprecise wording, label evidence and reasons, paraphrase a sentence without copying it, and write short original sentences that show accurate meaning. Production tasks should stay brief and supported so the assessment measures vocabulary knowledge rather than long-form essay stamina.

## 8. Review And Spacing Plan

Review should be cumulative from the first unit. The word-learning routine should appear in every unit, even when the focus is morphology, domain vocabulary, connotation, figurative language, argument, or research. Items should repeatedly ask students to name the evidence behind a meaning: context clue, word part, reference entry, connotation, tone clue, source clue, or fit check.

Morphology should be spaced by returning to roots and affixes in new word families rather than teaching each cluster once. General academic verbs should recur in domain contexts and argument/research contexts. Connotation and precision should reappear in figurative language, media framing, and revision tasks. Argument/source vocabulary should be revisited through literature discussion, informational text, research notes, and media examples.

The final unit should interleave all major strategies: context, morphology, reference use, domain meaning, connotation, figurative meaning, source vocabulary, discussion language, revision vocabulary, and portfolio habits. Feedback should name the strategy, not merely identify the correct answer.

## 9. Mastery Evidence For The End Of The Level

End-of-level mastery should be evidenced by a balanced body of work. A student who has mastered Grade 6 Vocabulary can solve unfamiliar words in short grade-level contexts, explain the evidence for a meaning, and revise an initial guess when a reference entry or sentence-fit check shows a better answer.

The student can infer meanings from common roots and affixes while recognizing when a word-part clue is insufficient. The student can use academic verbs, discussion stems, argument/source terms, and revision language in brief, accurate responses. The student can interpret domain-specific vocabulary in compact texts, choose precise words for a context, explain connotation and tone, and interpret figurative language as meaning-making rather than decoration.

The strongest mastery evidence includes transfer: given a new passage, source excerpt, diagram, or writing sample, the student selects appropriate vocabulary strategies, justifies the interpretation, avoids common misconceptions, and records useful words in a portfolio format that supports later independent learning.
