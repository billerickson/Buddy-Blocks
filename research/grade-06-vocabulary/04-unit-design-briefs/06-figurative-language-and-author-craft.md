# Unit 6: Figurative Language And Author Craft

Track: Vocabulary  
Level: Grade 6  
Stage: Unit Design Brief  
Source: `research/grade-06-vocabulary/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students interpret figurative and author-craft language as meaning-making, connecting nonliteral phrases, imagery, tone, and mood to context rather than treating labels as the goal.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

Figurative interpretation depends on context, connotation, and precise word choice. Placing this unit after Unit 5 helps students explain effect, not just identify devices.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: literal versus figurative meaning, comparison, personification, idiom, imagery, tone, mood, author effect.
- Vocabulary: literal, figurative, idiom, simile, metaphor, personification, imagery, tone, mood, author craft, word choice, comparison, meaning, effect.
- Representations and models: literal/figurative T-charts, comparison maps, tone/mood distinction charts, imagery sensory notes.
- Texts and forms: short literary excerpts, single-sentence figurative phrases, brief author-craft explanations.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need basic figurative-language exposure, context skills, connotation and tone vocabulary, and the ability to explain meaning in a short response.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Figurative language should be interpreted word by word.
- The label is more important than the meaning.
- Tone and mood are the same thing.
- Imagery means any interesting description.
- Figurative language is decoration that does not affect comprehension.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Start with literal versus figurative meaning to prevent word-by-word readings. Then study common comparison devices and personification. Next handle idioms as fixed nonliteral phrases that need context. End with tone, mood, and imagery so students connect author craft to reader response and meaning.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Students should continue to use context, connotation, and precision from prior units. Academic verbs such as interpret, explain, compare, and support should appear in prompts and feedback.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

Understanding word choice and effect prepares students for media framing, bias, audience, purpose, and final transfer tasks.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Distinguish literal and figurative meanings.
- Interpret similes, metaphors, personification, and idioms in context.
- Explain how imagery supports meaning.
- Distinguish tone from mood.
- Use evidence from the wording to support an interpretation.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Literal And Figurative Meaning**: Decide when words should be read literally and when they point beyond literal meaning.
2. **Similes, Metaphors, And Personification**: Interpret comparison and human-quality language in short contexts.
3. **Idioms In Context**: Use surrounding clues to understand common nonliteral phrases.
4. **Tone, Mood, And Imagery Words**: Connect author attitude, reader feeling, and sensory word choice.
