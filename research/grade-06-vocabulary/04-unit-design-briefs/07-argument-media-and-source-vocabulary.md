# Unit 7: Argument, Media, And Source Vocabulary

Track: Vocabulary  
Level: Grade 6  
Stage: Unit Design Brief  
Source: `research/grade-06-vocabulary/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students use basic argument, media, and source vocabulary to identify claims, reasons, evidence, relevance, sufficiency, credibility, audience, purpose, perspective, bias, and framing in short contexts.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit relies on academic vocabulary, connotation, precision, and author-craft work. Students are now ready to see how word choice and source context shape arguments and media messages.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: argument structure, evidence quality, source credibility, audience and purpose, perspective, bias, framing through word choice.
- Vocabulary: claim, reason, evidence, explanation, relevant, sufficient, credible, source, audience, purpose, perspective, bias, frame, media, headline, slant, support.
- Representations and models: claim-reason-evidence charts, relevance filters, credibility checklists, audience-purpose maps, headline comparison tables.
- Texts and forms: mini arguments, source notes, media headlines, short informational excerpts, discussion statements.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need evidence/inference language, connotation and tone vocabulary, basic source vocabulary, and the ability to read short argumentative or informational snippets.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Evidence and reason are the same thing.
- Relevant evidence means any interesting detail.
- Sufficient means at least one example.
- Credible means familiar or agreeable.
- Bias automatically means false.
- Headlines do not influence reader interpretation.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with claim, reason, and evidence because they are the argument foundation. Then evaluate relevance, sufficiency, and credibility. Move to audience, purpose, perspective, and bias so students read sources thoughtfully. End with media framing and word choice, applying connotation and tone to headlines and short media contexts.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

This unit reviews academic evidence words, connotation, tone, source vocabulary, and meaning-fit reasoning. Students should be asked to justify each term with a clue from the argument or source context.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

Argument and source vocabulary prepares research, paraphrase, synthesis, citation, and cumulative transfer in Unit 8. It also supports future Grade 7 analysis language.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Distinguish claim, reason, evidence, and explanation.
- Decide whether evidence is relevant or sufficient.
- Identify credibility clues and limitations.
- Use audience, purpose, perspective, and bias accurately.
- Explain how word choice frames a topic.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Claim, Reason, And Evidence**: Separate the main point, support, and proof in a simple argument.
2. **Relevant, Sufficient, And Credible**: Evaluate whether evidence fits, is enough, and comes from a trustworthy source.
3. **Audience, Purpose, Perspective, And Bias**: Interpret why a source was made and how viewpoint can shape it.
4. **Media Framing And Word Choice**: Notice how headlines and loaded words guide reader response.
