# Vocabulary - Grade 6 Lesson Briefs

Sources: accepted level blueprint, course map, and unit design briefs in `research/grade-06-vocabulary/`.

## Unit 1: Strategic Word Learning In Context

### Lesson 1: Build A Word-Learning Routine

- **Teaching goal:** Introduce a repeatable process for figuring out unfamiliar words in middle-school texts.
- **Student outcome:** The student can name and apply the routine: context, word parts, known words, reference, and meaning-fit check.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A five-step word-learning checklist turns guessing into evidence-based reasoning.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic context clues, familiar prefixes/suffixes, dictionary/glossary exposure, and short explanation frames.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Guessing from one clue is enough; reference tools replace context; word parts always settle the meaning.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given an unfamiliar word in a short context, the student chooses the best next strategy and explains why.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `order-items` for the routine sequence; `multiple-choice` for strategy choice or best strategy explanation; `match-pairs` for strategy-to-purpose links.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Teach the five-step routine explicitly: look at sentence and paragraph context, check meaningful word parts, connect to words you already know, verify with a reference if needed, and test the meaning back in the sentence.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short contexts and no more than one unfamiliar target word at a time.

### Lesson 2: Use Context Without Overguessing

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to use sentence and paragraph clues while recognizing that context may narrow rather than fully define a word.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a useful context clue and avoid unsupported over-specific guesses.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Context clues include restatement, example, contrast, cause/effect, and surrounding ideas.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic inference, synonyms/antonyms, and ability to cite a nearby phrase as evidence.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Any nearby word is a clue; context always provides an exact definition; a familiar topic makes every guess valid.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects a meaning supported by context and names the clue that supports it.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `passage-question` for contextual inference; `multiple-choice` for clue selection or choosing the best evidence explanation; `fill-blank` for constrained clue-type vocabulary.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare two possible meanings for one word and ask which is actually supported by the sentence.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep passages under 90 words and limit background knowledge demands.

### Lesson 3: Choose The Right Reference Entry

- **Teaching goal:** Help students select the definition, part of speech, or glossary entry that matches the context.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use part of speech and sentence meaning to choose the correct reference entry.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Reference entries are tools to verify meaning, but the context decides which entry fits.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Alphabetical reference use, noun/verb/adjective awareness, and simple multiple-meaning words.
- **Likely misconceptions:** The first definition is always correct; every definition for a word fits every sentence; part of speech can be ignored.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses the fitting entry for a multiple-meaning word and rejects a mismatched entry.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `multiple-choice` for entry selection; `match-pairs` for part-of-speech clues; `fill-blank` for context-fit checks.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show a two-entry word such as conduct and test each definition in a sentence.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use simplified dictionary entries and avoid more than three definitions per item.

### Lesson 4: Test Meaning For Fit

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to substitute a proposed meaning into the sentence and revise when the meaning does not fit.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether a meaning fits the context and improve an inaccurate first guess.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A strong word meaning must make sense with grammar, topic, and nearby evidence.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Context clues, reference entry selection, and simple paraphrase.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A partly related definition is close enough; a word-part clue cannot be contradicted; checking means repeating the same guess.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student explains why one meaning fits and another does not in the same sentence.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `error-correction` for revising a bad definition; `multiple-choice` for fit decisions; `text-input` for concise replacement words after support.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Write a sentence with a wrong substitution and let students hear how it breaks the meaning.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep substitutions brief and avoid abstract passages until the fit-check habit is secure.

## Unit 2: Morphology And Word Families

### Lesson 1: Prefixes That Shift Meaning

- **Teaching goal:** Use high-utility prefixes to infer direction, repetition, negation, order, or relationship.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain how a prefix changes the meaning of a base word or academic word.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Prefixes add meaning before the base, but the whole word still needs a context check.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Base words, common elementary prefixes, and context-fit checks from Unit 1.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A prefix always has one exact meaning; `un-`, `in-`, and `non-` are interchangeable; prefix meaning replaces sentence context.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student infers and verifies meanings for words such as preview, reconsider, interact, submerge, and transfer.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `match-pairs` for prefix meanings; `fill-blank` for choosing prefixed words in sentences; `multiple-choice` for diagnostic false-prefix choices.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a small prefix map and show how `pre-` changes view into preview.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Limit to four or five prefixes at a time and use transparent examples before academic words.

### Lesson 2: Suffixes And Parts Of Speech

- **Teaching goal:** Show how suffixes can change a word's role, meaning, and fit in a sentence.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify whether a suffix forms a noun, adjective, adverb, or action-related word in context.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Suffixes such as `-tion`, `-ity`, `-ous`, `-ive`, `-able`, and `-ly` often signal part of speech.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic part-of-speech awareness, base words, and sentence-fit reasoning.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Suffixes only make words longer; words in the same family can be swapped without changing grammar; `-ly` always makes a word informal.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses a word form that fits a sentence and explains the suffix clue.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `multi-blank-cloze` for word-form choice; `match-pairs` for suffix to function; `error-correction` for correcting the wrong word form.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare create, creation, creative, and creatively in four short sentences.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one word family per item set before mixing families.

### Lesson 3: Greek And Latin Roots In Academic Words

- **Teaching goal:** Use selected roots to unlock related academic words without turning roots into isolated memorization.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use roots such as `scrib/script`, `mit/miss`, `jur/jus`, `bene`, `man`, and `vac` to support meaning.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Roots carry meaning across word families, but the word's full meaning depends on affixes and context.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Prefixes, suffixes, context checking, and basic word-family comparison.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Same root means same full definition; a root clue is enough by itself; unrelated look-alikes are always root relatives.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches roots to meanings, infers a related word, and checks it in context.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `match-pairs` for root meanings; `passage-question` for root-in-context inference; `multiple-choice` for choosing the best word-part evidence explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Build a word web for `scrib/script` with describe, manuscript, inscription, and transcript.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep root sets small and avoid obscure etymology beyond practical meaning.

### Lesson 4: Word Families, Cognates, And False Patterns

- **Teaching goal:** Build flexible awareness of related words while warning students against misleading look-alikes.
- **Student outcome:** The student can group related words, use reliable cognates cautiously, and reject false morphology clues.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Word families help when meaning and context align; false patterns must be checked.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Roots, affixes, context-fit checks, and part-of-speech shifts.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Every similar spelling signals shared meaning; all cognates mean exactly the same thing; word-family members have identical connotations.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the strongest related-word clue and explains why a tempting look-alike is not useful.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `match-pairs` for family relationships; `multiple-choice` for false-pattern diagnostics or cautious morphology explanations; `passage-question` for word-family context checks.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Contrast helpful relatives such as inspect and inspection with a misleading look-alike that shares letters but not useful meaning.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar multilingual examples only when the cognate is accurate and not essential background knowledge.

## Unit 3: Academic Vocabulary For Reading, Tasks, And Discussion

### Lesson 1: Academic Verbs In Directions

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to decode what common academic task verbs ask them to do.
- **Student outcome:** The student can match verbs such as analyze, infer, evaluate, summarize, cite, and support to the expected action.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Academic verbs are directions for thinking and response, not decorative school words.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic response verbs such as explain, compare, and describe.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Analyze means answer quickly; summarize means copy the first sentence; evaluate means state an opinion only.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses the correct action for a prompt and explains what the verb requires.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `match-pairs` for verb-to-action links; `multiple-choice` for prompt interpretation; `dialogue-builder` for choosing a fitting student response.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Put three prompts side by side and underline the verb that changes the task.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short prompts with one target verb at a time before combining verbs.

### Lesson 2: Evidence, Inference, And Explanation

- **Teaching goal:** Distinguish proof from thinking and from the reasoning that connects them.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify evidence, inference, reason, and explanation in a short response.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Evidence is what the text shows; an inference is what you figure out; explanation connects the two.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic text evidence and because statements.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Evidence and reason are identical; inference is a wild guess; explanation means repeat the evidence.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student labels parts of a response and completes a concise evidence-based sentence frame.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `passage-question` for evidence choice and supported inference checks; `multi-blank-cloze` for sentence frames; `multiple-choice` for distinguishing inference, evidence, and explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a three-part frame: "I infer __ because the text says __. This shows __."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep passages brief and focus on vocabulary roles rather than deep literary analysis.

### Lesson 3: Compare, Contrast, Cause, And Effect

- **Teaching goal:** Use relationship vocabulary to track how ideas are alike, different, or connected by cause and effect.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose and use compare, contrast, cause, effect, sequence, and relationship terms in context.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Relationship words tell what kind of connection a reader or writer should make.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Same/different, because, first/next/last, and basic paragraph reading.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Compare means only similarities; contrast means any disagreement; cause and effect can be reversed.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the relationship signaled by a sentence and chooses vocabulary that fits it.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `match-pairs` for signal word relationships; `order-items` for sequence language; `fill-blank` for choosing precise relationship words.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a simple chart with four relationship types and one example sentence for each.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid long multi-paragraph comparisons; use two related ideas at a time.

### Lesson 4: Discussion Moves For Stronger Thinking

- **Teaching goal:** Teach academic discussion words as purposeful moves in a conversation.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose lines that clarify, elaborate, qualify, challenge, or synthesize respectfully.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Discussion vocabulary names what a speaker is doing with ideas.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic agree/disagree frames, evidence language, and conversational turn-taking.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Challenge means attack; qualify means make an answer weaker; synthesize means repeat the last speaker.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses a discussion move that fits a goal and completes a short academic response.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `dialogue-builder` for fitting conversation turns; `multiple-choice` for naming discussion moves or choosing supported use; `fill-blank` for constrained discussion vocabulary.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Model one claim and several possible next turns, naming the purpose of each.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep conversations to two or three turns and avoid debating sensitive topics.

## Unit 4: Disciplinary Vocabulary Across Subjects

### Lesson 1: Math Words With Precise Meanings

- **Teaching goal:** Interpret common math words whose technical meanings are narrower than everyday meanings.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain words such as factor, variable, expression, model, estimate, and equivalent in math contexts.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Math vocabulary often names relationships, quantities, or representations with exact meanings.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic arithmetic vocabulary, academic directions, and diagram reading.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Factor means anything that affects something; variable means something "varies" in a vague way; model only means a physical object.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses the math-specific meaning and uses a diagram, equation, or context clue to justify it.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `match-pairs` for term-to-meaning; `passage-question` for math contexts; `multiple-choice` for everyday-versus-technical distinctions.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare the everyday word factor with factor in "3 is a factor of 12."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep calculations simple so vocabulary remains the target.

### Lesson 2: Science Process And Data Words

- **Teaching goal:** Build vocabulary for observing, classifying, testing variables, reading data, and drawing conclusions.
- **Student outcome:** The student can interpret science process words in short experiment or data contexts.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Science words often name actions in an investigation or parts of evidence.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic observe/predict language, table reading, and cause/effect vocabulary.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Observe means guess; data is the same as conclusion; variable means any detail in a story.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the role of a word in a lab-note or table context.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `passage-question` for lab-note contexts; `match-pairs` for process terms; `multiple-choice` for distinguishing evidence-based conclusions from unsupported claims.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show a tiny investigation with observation, variable, data, and conclusion labeled.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar science contexts and very small data displays.

### Lesson 3: History, Geography, And Civics Terms

- **Teaching goal:** Interpret social studies vocabulary through source, map, and civic contexts.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use words such as source, region, culture, economy, citizen, and perspective accurately.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Social studies terms often name human groups, places, systems, rights, duties, and viewpoints.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic map/source exposure and academic relationship words.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Culture means only food or clothing; economy means money only; perspective means opinion with no evidence.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches a term to a social studies context and explains the clue that supports it.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `match-pairs` for terms and examples; `passage-question` for source excerpts; `fill-blank` for precise term selection.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a map caption and a source note to show how the same topic can include region, culture, economy, and perspective.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid advanced political terminology and keep contexts historically neutral.

### Lesson 4: Technical Meanings In Short Sources

- **Teaching goal:** Help students choose technical meanings that fit mixed short sources, diagrams, and glossary entries.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether a word is being used in an everyday or discipline-specific way.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** The subject, visual, and sentence all help select the right meaning of a technical word.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Unit 4 vocabulary, reference entry selection, and context-fit checks.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A familiar everyday meaning is always enough; glossary definitions can be copied without checking the source; visuals are optional.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student explains why a technical definition fits a mixed source better than an everyday definition.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `passage-question` for source interpretation; `multiple-choice` for definition choice or technical meaning justification; `match-pairs` for subject-context meaning links.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Present a word like model in a science source and a literature sentence, then compare meanings.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one source feature at a time before combining passage plus visual plus glossary.

## Unit 5: Connotation, Denotation, And Precise Word Choice

### Lesson 1: Denotation And Dictionary Meaning

- **Teaching goal:** Anchor students in denotation as the direct dictionary meaning of a word.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify denotation and choose definitions that fit context.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Denotation is the core meaning; connotation and tone build on it.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Reference entries, multiple meanings, and context-fit checks.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Denotation means the same thing as connotation; all definitions in an entry fit equally; denotation includes every feeling a word suggests.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses a direct definition and distinguishes it from an association.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `multiple-choice` for direct-definition checks; `match-pairs` for word-to-denotation; `fill-blank` for fitting dictionary meanings in sentences.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare the denotation of home and house before discussing associations.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use common words first so the concept, not decoding, is the main load.

### Lesson 2: Connotation, Feeling, And Formality

- **Teaching goal:** Teach connotation as association, feeling, formality, and social effect beyond denotation.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify positive, negative, neutral, formal, and informal connotations in context.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Words with similar denotations can make readers feel differently or fit different audiences.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Denotation, synonyms, tone, and audience/purpose basics.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Connotation is only positive or negative; informal words are always wrong; formal words are always better.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects a word that fits a sentence's audience and explains the connotation.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `multiple-choice` for connotation identification or short justification choice; `order-items` for formality or feeling scales; `match-pairs` for near-synonym connotation contrasts.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare childlike, youthful, and immature as words with related denotations but different effects.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid culturally obscure associations and use context to make connotation clear.

### Lesson 3: Intensity And Precision In Synonyms

- **Teaching goal:** Help students choose among near-synonyms by strength and exact meaning.
- **Student outcome:** The student can rank or select words by intensity and precision for a specific context.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Synonyms are rarely identical; the best choice fits both degree and exact meaning.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Synonyms, shades of meaning, connotation, and context-fit checks.
- **Likely misconceptions:** All synonyms can replace one another; intense words always improve writing; vague words are safer because they fit many contexts.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects the most precise synonym for a context and explains why stronger or weaker choices do not fit.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `order-items` for intensity ladders; `fill-blank` for precise word choice; `error-correction` for replacing vague wording.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Build an intensity ladder such as notice, examine, inspect, scrutinize.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar synonym sets and short contexts with clear purpose.

### Lesson 4: Revise For Exact Word Choice

- **Teaching goal:** Apply denotation, connotation, intensity, formality, and precision to sentence revision.
- **Student outcome:** The student can replace a vague or mismatched word with one that better fits audience, purpose, and tone.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Strong revision changes words to make meaning more exact, not just more impressive.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Unit 5 concepts, basic revision vocabulary, and audience/purpose awareness.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Revision only fixes errors; fancy words are automatically better; tone does not matter in word choice.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student revises a sentence and gives a concise reason for the chosen word.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `error-correction` for replacing imprecise words; `multiple-choice` for best revision or revision explanation; `match-pairs` for revision-to-purpose links.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show two revised versions of one sentence and ask which better fits a school announcement, a story, or a lab note.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep revision at sentence level; avoid asking for paragraph rewrites.

## Unit 6: Figurative Language And Author Craft

### Lesson 1: Literal And Figurative Meaning

- **Teaching goal:** Help students decide when language should be read literally and when it points beyond the literal meaning.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify literal and figurative meanings and explain the contextual clue.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Figurative language uses words to suggest meaning beyond the surface definition.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Context clues, denotation, connotation, and basic idiom exposure.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Figurative language is nonsense if read literally; every descriptive phrase is figurative; literal meaning is always less important.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student explains a figurative phrase in context without relying only on the label.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `multiple-choice` for literal/figurative decisions or concise meaning explanations; `passage-question` for interpretation; `fill-blank` for plain-language paraphrases.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use one phrase literally in one sentence and figuratively in another.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use brief excerpts and common expressions before less familiar literary language.

### Lesson 2: Similes, Metaphors, And Personification

- **Teaching goal:** Interpret comparison and human-quality language as tools for meaning and effect.
- **Student outcome:** The student can interpret a simile, metaphor, or personification in a short context.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** These devices connect ideas through comparison or human qualities to create emphasis or imagery.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Literal/figurative distinction, compare/contrast language, and connotation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Simile and metaphor labels matter more than meaning; personification means any animal action; comparisons must match in every way.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student explains what two ideas are being connected and what quality is emphasized.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `match-pairs` for device to meaning; `passage-question` for interpretation; `multiple-choice` for choosing the best explanation of effect.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Ask what quality is shared in "The hallway was a river of students."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep examples vivid and concrete; avoid advanced literary taxonomy.

### Lesson 3: Idioms In Context

- **Teaching goal:** Use context to understand idioms and fixed nonliteral phrases.
- **Student outcome:** The student can infer an idiom's meaning and choose a plain-language paraphrase.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Idioms usually cannot be understood by defining each word separately.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Figurative meaning, context clues, and paraphrase.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Idioms should be read word by word; idioms mean the same thing in every possible context; all unusual phrases are idioms.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses a paraphrase that fits the sentence and rejects a literal misreading.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `passage-question` for idiom meaning; `match-pairs` for idiom-to-paraphrase; `multiple-choice` for literal-trap diagnostics.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Contrast "spill the beans" as an accident with beans and as revealing a secret.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use common idioms and include enough context for multilingual learners.

### Lesson 4: Tone, Mood, And Imagery Words

- **Teaching goal:** Connect author word choice to tone, mood, and sensory imagery.
- **Student outcome:** The student can distinguish tone from mood and identify imagery that supports a feeling or attitude.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Tone is author attitude; mood is reader feeling; imagery uses sensory details to shape experience.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Connotation, figurative meaning, and evidence language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Tone and mood are interchangeable; imagery means any adjective; one word can prove tone without context.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses a tone or mood word and cites wording that supports it.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `passage-question` for tone/mood evidence and evidence-based explanations; `match-pairs` for tone versus mood examples; `fill-blank` for constrained imagery vocabulary.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Read a two-sentence description and ask how the author's word choice makes the scene feel.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short excerpts with clear word-choice signals; avoid obscure tone labels.

## Unit 7: Argument, Media, And Source Vocabulary

### Lesson 1: Claim, Reason, And Evidence

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to separate the main point, support, and proof in a simple argument.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify claim, reason, evidence, and explanation in a short argument.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Argument vocabulary names different jobs in a response, not just different sentence positions.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Evidence/inference language and basic because statements.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A reason is the same as evidence; the claim is always first; evidence means any detail.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student labels parts of an argument and chooses evidence that supports a claim.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `passage-question` for argument snippets; `match-pairs` for term-to-role; `multi-blank-cloze` for claim/reason/evidence frames.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Color-code a three-sentence argument into claim, reason, evidence, and explanation.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use low-stakes topics and short arguments with one clear claim.

### Lesson 2: Relevant, Sufficient, And Credible

- **Teaching goal:** Evaluate evidence by fit, amount, and source trustworthiness at a Grade 6 level.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether evidence is relevant, sufficient, and credible for a simple claim.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Good evidence must connect to the claim, be enough for the claim, and come from a source worth trusting.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Claim/evidence terms, source vocabulary, and connotation-neutral evaluation language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Interesting details are relevant; one example is always sufficient; credible means personally liked.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student explains which evidence best supports a claim and why another option is weak.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `multiple-choice` for evidence quality or concise evaluation; `passage-question` for source/evidence scenarios; `error-correction` for replacing weak evidence.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a claim about school gardens and compare an expert data point, a personal story, and an unrelated fact.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid complex research methods and formal citation rules.

### Lesson 3: Audience, Purpose, Perspective, And Bias

- **Teaching goal:** Interpret why a source was made, who it addresses, and how viewpoint may shape it.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use audience, purpose, perspective, and bias accurately without treating bias as automatic falsehood.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A source's message is shaped by its creator, intended readers, goal, and viewpoint.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Source, tone, connotation, and evidence vocabulary.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Bias means false; perspective means random opinion; audience is everyone who might read the source.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies audience/purpose clues and explains how perspective or bias may affect wording.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `passage-question` for source notes; `multiple-choice` for audience/purpose identification or bias explanation; `match-pairs` for source-analysis terms.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare a park flyer and a city report about the same event.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use everyday civic or school examples; avoid partisan texts.

### Lesson 4: Media Framing And Word Choice

- **Teaching goal:** Show how headlines and loaded words can frame a topic for readers.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify framing words and explain how they influence reader response.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Media framing often works through connotation, emphasis, selection, and headline wording.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Connotation, tone, audience, purpose, and source vocabulary.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Headlines are neutral labels; framing means lying; changing one loaded word cannot change reader response.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student compares two headlines or summaries and identifies the framing effect of word choice.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `match-pairs` for loaded words to effects; `passage-question` for headline comparisons; `multiple-choice` for short framing explanations.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare "Students request longer lunch" with "Students demand schedule change" and discuss effect.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use nonpartisan school or community topics and avoid advanced media theory.

## Unit 8: Research, Revision, And Cumulative Transfer

### Lesson 1: Inquiry And Source Terms

- **Teaching goal:** Name common research actions and source features students need for Grade 6 inquiry.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use inquiry, keyword, source, note, question, topic, and conclusion vocabulary accurately.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Research vocabulary describes actions for asking, finding, recording, and concluding.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Source vocabulary, academic verbs, and basic note-taking.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Research means copying facts from a website; a keyword is a full question; a conclusion is just the last sentence.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches research terms to actions and chooses useful keywords or notes for a simple inquiry.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `match-pairs` for term-to-action; `multiple-choice` for keyword choice; `passage-question` for source-note interpretation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use one research question and show how it becomes keywords, notes, and a conclusion.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar inquiry topics and avoid formal citation styles.

### Lesson 2: Paraphrase, Synthesize, And Cite

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students vocabulary for restating, combining, and crediting ideas.
- **Student outcome:** The student can distinguish paraphrase, quote, cite, and synthesize in short research contexts.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Paraphrasing restates an idea; quoting repeats exact words; citing gives credit; synthesizing combines ideas.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Source terms, summary language, and sentence-level rewriting.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Paraphrase means changing a few words; synthesize means list facts separately; citing is only punctuation.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the correct action in a research note and chooses a stronger paraphrase or synthesis sentence.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `multiple-choice` for distinguishing actions or choosing brief synthesis; `error-correction` for fixing weak paraphrases; `passage-question` for comparing notes.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare copied, weakly changed, and truly paraphrased versions of the same source sentence.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep source sentences short and avoid requiring formal bibliographic formats.

### Lesson 3: Word-Learning Portfolio Habits

- **Teaching goal:** Teach portfolio entries as durable word-learning tools rather than copied dictionary notes.
- **Student outcome:** The student can complete a portfolio entry with context, morphology, fitting definition, connotation, example, non-example, and original sentence.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A useful portfolio entry records evidence and use so the word can be remembered and applied later.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Context clues, morphology, reference entries, connotation, examples/nonexamples, and original sentence writing.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A copied definition is enough; examples and non-examples are optional decoration; original sentences can ignore the word's actual meaning.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses or completes the strongest portfolio entry for a new word.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `multi-blank-cloze` for entry parts; `multiple-choice` for best example/non-example or accurate original-sentence selection; `error-correction` for fixing weak portfolio sentences.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show a weak portfolio card and revise it into a learning tool with evidence.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one target word at a time and keep original sentences brief.

### Lesson 4: Cumulative Vocabulary Transfer Challenge

- **Teaching goal:** Have students choose among the full set of vocabulary strategies in unfamiliar mixed contexts.
- **Student outcome:** The student can infer, verify, explain, revise, and apply words across context, morphology, reference, connotation, figurative, source, and research tasks.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Independent word learners choose the strategy that fits the word problem in front of them.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** All prior units in the Grade 6 Vocabulary level.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Each strategy works only in its original unit; one clue is enough for every word; transfer means memorizing old answers.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student solves unfamiliar vocabulary problems, names the strategy used, and explains the evidence.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `passage-question` for new contexts; `multiple-choice` for strategy choice or concise transfer explanation; `fill-blank` for fitting reference definitions.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Frame the lesson as a mixed strategy challenge: "What kind of word problem is this?"
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep each item compact; mix strategies one at a time before a final combined item.
