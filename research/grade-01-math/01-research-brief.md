# Grade 01 Math Research Brief

## Track

- Track slug: `grade-01-math`
- Level: Grade 1 mathematics
- Intended student profile: early elementary learners who are moving from counting-based kindergarten work toward reliable addition/subtraction strategies, base-ten structure, simple measurement, and shape reasoning. Lessons must be short, explicit, highly visual, and app-scorable, with contextual exposure before recall and regular spiral review.

## 1. End-of-Level Outcomes

By the end of Grade 1 math, students should be able to:

- Represent and solve addition and subtraction word problems within 20, including add-to, take-from, put-together/take-apart, and compare situations, using drawings, objects, equations, and unknowns in different positions.
- Use counting-on, making ten, decomposing numbers, doubles/near doubles, and the relationship between addition and subtraction to add and subtract within 20; fluently add and subtract within 10.
- Understand the equal sign as a relationship, not an "answer comes next" signal, and determine unknown whole numbers in addition/subtraction equations.
- Count, read, write, and compare whole numbers to 120, starting from numbers other than 1 when appropriate.
- Understand two-digit numbers as tens and ones, including the special structure of 10 to 19 as one ten and some ones.
- Add within 100 using concrete models, drawings, place value, properties of operations, and the relationship between addition and subtraction; mentally find 10 more or 10 less.
- Measure lengths by comparing indirectly and by iterating equal-length units with no gaps or overlaps.
- Tell and write time to the hour and half-hour, organize simple categorical data, and answer simple comparison questions about data displays.
- Distinguish defining from non-defining attributes of shapes, compose/decompose two- and three-dimensional shapes, and partition circles/rectangles into halves and fourths using equal-share language.

## 2. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need kindergarten foundations:

- Stable counting sequence to 100, one-to-one correspondence, cardinality, and comparison of small groups.
- Reading/writing numerals at least to 20 and counting forward from a given number.
- Composing/decomposing numbers up to 10 and teen numbers as 10 plus some ones.
- Addition as putting together/adding to and subtraction as taking apart/taking from, with small-number objects and drawings.
- Recognition of basic two-dimensional and three-dimensional shapes, and informal comparison of length, weight, and capacity.
- Ability to follow short oral or written directions and attend to simple visual models.

## 3. Major Concept Clusters

- Addition and subtraction meanings: situation types, equations, unknown positions, relationship between operations.
- Addition/subtraction strategies within 20: counting on, make ten, decomposing, doubles, inverse relationships, fluency within 10.
- Base-ten number sense: counting to 120, tens and ones, compare two-digit numbers, 10 more/10 less, adding within 100.
- Early measurement and data: length comparison, length iteration, time to hour/half-hour, simple categorical data.
- Geometry and equal shares: attributes, composing shapes, partitioning into halves/fourths, part-whole language.
- Mathematical practices: explain a strategy, choose a representation, notice structure, and check whether an answer makes sense.

## 4. Recommended Progression

1. Begin with counting, comparison, and number relationships to 20, using ten-frames, number paths/number lines, fingers, and short stories. Reinforce that numbers can be decomposed in more than one way.
2. Develop addition and subtraction from contexts before equations: add-to, take-from, put-together/take-apart, and compare stories. Show drawings and equations after the situation is understood.
3. Move from counting all to counting on and counting back, then to make-ten and known-fact strategies. Keep strategy choice explicit: "Which strategy fits this problem?"
4. Introduce equations as balanced relationships, including unknowns at the start or middle, after students have seen the corresponding context or model.
5. Extend counting and place value to 120. Treat two-digit numbers as tens and ones before asking for symbolic comparisons or mental 10-more/10-less work.
6. Add within 100 with base-ten drawings and decompositions. Keep regrouping conceptual and model-based; do not introduce the formal standard algorithm.
7. Teach measurement through direct comparison, indirect comparison, and repeated equal units, then connect length data and simple time.
8. Teach shape attributes and composition through sorting, building, and decomposing. Equal shares should use fair-share language and visual models, not formal fraction computation.
9. Interleave short review: a new lesson on place value can include one retrieval item on make-ten; a measurement lesson can include a small comparison item; a geometry lesson can revisit part-whole language.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Counting all every time: students may restart from 1 instead of counting on from a larger addend.
- Equal sign as command: students may think `8 + 3 = __` is valid but `__ = 8 + 3` or `7 + 2 = 6 + 3` is strange or false.
- Teen-number confusion: students may read 14 as "four and one" or treat the 1 as one rather than one ten.
- Digit comparison without place value: students may think 62 is less than 59 because 2 is less than 9.
- Ten more/ten less as one more/one less: students may change the ones digit instead of the tens digit.
- Subtraction always means "take away": students may miss comparison or missing-addend meanings.
- Measurement gaps and overlaps: students may count units that do not align end to end or use units of different lengths.
- Longer-looking means longer: students may ignore endpoints or alignment when comparing lengths.
- Shape attributes tied to orientation or color: students may reject a triangle if it is rotated or accept a non-triangle because it "looks pointy."
- Equal shares by count rather than size: students may call two pieces halves even when the pieces are unequal.

## 6. Developmental Constraints and Reading/Load Concerns

- Grade 1 students are still developing decoding stamina. Prompts should use short sentences, familiar nouns, and visible models. Avoid unnecessary vocabulary in word problems.
- Working memory is limited; use one new idea per lesson and avoid two-step symbolic tasks until the underlying representation is familiar.
- Fine motor and typed input can interfere with math evidence. Prefer multiple-choice, fill-blank, match-pairs, order-items, and short numeric text input only when typing is not the main challenge.
- Visuals should make the mathematical structure obvious: aligned objects, clear ten-frames, clean number paths, simple base-ten drawings, and equal-size partitions.
- Feedback should name the structure, not just the answer: "14 is 1 ten and 4 ones" or "The pieces are not halves because they are not the same size."
- Timed fluency can be used sparingly after understanding is established; it should not be the first exposure to a strategy.

## 7. Assessment Implications

- Assess concept, strategy, and fluency separately. A student may know that 8 needs 2 to make 10 but still be slow with recall.
- Use short contextual items before naked equations when a concept is new. Move toward abstract equations after students have interpreted the context.
- Include misconception-sensitive distractors: counting-all errors, reversed subtraction, place-value digit errors, unequal shares, and shape orientation errors.
- Ask students to match equations to stories or models, choose the best first step, identify a correct representation, and compute short answers.
- Use app-scorable formats: multiple-choice for strategy choice and misconception checks, fill-blank/text-input for short number answers, match-pairs for model/equation relationships, order-items for measurement steps or counting sequences.
- Mastery evidence should include transfer across representations: story, drawing, number sentence, and verbal explanation choice.

## 8. Spiral Review Priorities

- Counting forward/backward within 120, especially from numbers other than 1.
- Combinations within 10 and make-ten pairs.
- Addition/subtraction relationship and missing-addend equations.
- Teen numbers as ten and ones; two-digit comparisons using tens first.
- 10 more and 10 less.
- Length measurement with equal units and no gaps/overlaps.
- Telling time to hour/half-hour.
- Shape defining attributes and equal-share language.
- Mixed word-problem situations so students do not overfit to one keyword.

## 9. Concepts That Should Not Be Taught Yet

- Standard algorithms for multi-digit addition/subtraction, especially regrouping notation.
- Multiplication and division symbols or fact memorization.
- Formal fraction notation beyond informal halves/fourths language in equal-share contexts.
- Area, perimeter, angle measure, volume, decimals, negative numbers, ratios, variables as algebraic unknowns beyond simple blank boxes.
- Long multi-step word problems that require reading stamina rather than Grade 1 mathematics.
- Memorized tricks for comparison, measurement, or operations without visual/place-value meaning.

## 10. Source Notes

- CCSS-M Grade 1 critical areas prioritize addition/subtraction within 20, tens-and-ones place value, length measurement, and shape composition. Those critical areas define the end-of-level outcomes and the recommended sequence.
- The CCSS-M introduction emphasizes understanding plus procedural skill and notes that standards are not a fixed curriculum order. This brief therefore sequences concepts by prerequisite relationships and Buddy Blocks lesson constraints rather than copying the standards order.
- The Common Core Progressions informed the move from counting and concrete models toward more generalizable strategies, especially in operations/algebraic thinking and base-ten work.
- IES math intervention guidance shaped the instructional priorities: systematic instruction, concise mathematical language, concrete/semi-concrete representations, number lines, deliberate word-problem instruction, and fluency practice after conceptual grounding.
- IES study guidance supports Buddy Blocks features such as spacing, retrieval practice, interleaving worked examples with independent practice, connecting concrete and abstract representations, and asking explanation-oriented questions.
- NCTM sources reinforced focus and coherence: lessons should establish clear goals, connect representations, use purposeful questions, and keep Grade 1 attention on a small number of durable ideas rather than many shallow topics.
