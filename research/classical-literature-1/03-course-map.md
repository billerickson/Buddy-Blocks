# Classical Literature 1 Course Map

Track: Classical Literature
Level: Level 1
Sources: Accepted research brief at `research/classical-literature-1/01-research-brief.md` and level blueprint at `research/classical-literature-1/02-level-blueprint.md`

## Course Design Notes

This course map builds a compact shared story foundation. The sequence begins with the clearest short forms, then broadens into folktale patterns, introductory myths, heroic journeys, poetry, dialogue, comparison, and transfer. The six units are intentionally not a survey of every classical text students will eventually meet. Instead, each unit gives students a durable reading move: retell accurately, connect choices to consequences, recognize genre and pattern, hear poetic language, read speech as action, compare with evidence, and discuss inherited stories respectfully.

## Unit 1: Fables, Morals, And Story Elements

### Unit Purpose

Establish the core habits of Level 1 literary study: retelling a short story in order, naming story elements, and connecting a moral or lesson to specific events. Fables are compact enough for students to practice comprehension, evidence, and feedback without heavy reading load.

### Student Outcomes

- Retell a short fable with beginning, middle, end, and key details.
- Identify character, setting, problem, action, outcome, moral, and evidence.
- Explain how a character choice or mistake leads to the ending.
- Choose a moral that fits the events rather than a lesson that merely sounds nice.
- Use short evidence language such as "because in the story..."

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Fable, moral, lesson, character, setting, problem, action, outcome, consequence, detail, retell, evidence, beginning, middle, end.

### Prerequisites

Students should be able to listen to or read a short story, answer who/what/where questions, and retell familiar events with support.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This opening unit turns prerequisite story comprehension into a repeatable literary routine. Later units keep the same retell-evidence habit while adding folktale patterns, myths, heroic journeys, poetry, dialogue, and comparison.

### Misconceptions To Address

- The moral is any good advice, even if the story does not support it.
- Retelling means mentioning one favorite part instead of the main events in order.
- Characters are simply good or bad, not people or animals whose choices produce consequences.
- Evidence is a quote-like detail copied at random rather than a reason for an answer.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Retelling, story elements, moral, and evidence should reappear in every later unit. Later fable review should mix recognition with new genres so students do not assume every old story works like a fable.

## Unit 2: Folktales, Tricksters, And Story Patterns

### Unit Purpose

Extend fable comprehension into longer oral-tradition patterns. Students recognize repeated scenes, trickster behavior, tests, warnings, and patterned endings while learning that folktales can travel, change, and carry cultural memory.

### Student Outcomes

- Distinguish a folktale or trickster tale from a fable using concrete features.
- Identify repeated phrases, repeated actions, tests, warnings, and clever turns.
- Explain that a trickster can be clever, disruptive, funny, dangerous, or corrective depending on the story.
- Compare two short tales or tale moments using one shared pattern.
- Discuss stories from different traditions with respectful, non-flattening language.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Folktale, oral tradition, trickster, repeated phrase, pattern, test, warning, cleverness, version, culture, tradition, compare, contrast, criterion.

### Prerequisites

Students need Unit 1 retelling, story elements, character-choice-consequence reasoning, and evidence language.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit reviews fable lessons while preparing for mythic and heroic patterns. Students learn to look for recurring structures before they meet quests, transformations, helpers, and tests in later units.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A trickster is only a villain or only a hero.
- All old stories are the same because they have animals, magic, or repeated lines.
- Comparison means writing two summaries side by side.
- A story from a culture stands for everything about that culture.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Interleave fable moral checks with folktale pattern checks. Keep comparison tasks small: one feature, one evidence detail from each story, and one sentence of explanation.

## Unit 3: Myths, Names, And Cultural Meaning

### Unit Purpose

Introduce myths as traditional stories with cultural or sacred significance and as a major source of classical references. Students learn a small number of Greek and Roman names, places, and patterns through story episodes rather than detached memorization.

### Student Outcomes

- Explain that a myth is a traditional story with cultural meaning, not simply a false statement.
- Retell an adapted myth episode with attention to character, motive, event, and outcome.
- Identify mythic patterns such as origin, transformation, divine messenger, gift, warning, and consequence.
- Use pronunciation and relationship supports for unfamiliar names.
- Discuss Greek and Roman myths respectfully as inherited stories from ancient cultures.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Myth, ancient, Greek, Roman, culture, sacred, origin story, transformation, messenger, gift, warning, consequence, motive, Mount Olympus, underworld, sea, city, reference.

### Prerequisites

Students need experience with retelling, morals, folktale patterns, evidence, and respectful comparison from Units 1-2.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit uses earlier pattern work to make myths comprehensible. It prepares students for heroic quests, later epic material, allusions, and version comparison. It also gives poetry and drama units a small bank of shared names and images.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Myth means fake or silly.
- Myths are interchangeable fantasy stories without cultural setting.
- Students must memorize long lists of gods, monsters, or genealogies to understand a myth.
- A myth's lesson can be separated from the story's choices and consequences.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Spiral fable morals and folktale patterns into myth lessons by asking whether a story is teaching, explaining, warning, or dramatizing. Retrieve names only through story context.

## Unit 4: Heroes, Quests, And Consequences

### Unit Purpose

Build readiness for later epic, legend, and medieval literature by helping students track heroic tasks, obstacles, helpers, choices, flaws, and costs. The unit emphasizes that classical heroes are often tested and changed, not simply perfect winners.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify quest or heroic-tale parts: goal, task, obstacle, helper, choice, outcome, and consequence.
- Explain how a hero's strength, flaw, courage, pride, or curiosity affects the story.
- Distinguish admiration from perfection when discussing heroes.
- Compare two heroic or journey episodes using a shared criterion.
- Connect journey patterns to earlier trickster, test, and consequence patterns.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Hero, heroine, quest, journey, task, obstacle, helper, monster, courage, flaw, pride, choice, cost, reward, consequence, legend, epic tale.

### Prerequisites

Students need retelling, character-choice-consequence reasoning, pattern recognition, and basic myth vocabulary from Units 1-3.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

The unit extends mythic patterns into heroic and legendary material. It prepares students for later epic study while also setting up poetry and drama work, where speakers and characters often reveal themselves through choices and speech.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A hero is always perfect or always wins easily.
- A quest is just traveling from one place to another.
- Monsters or obstacles matter only because they are exciting.
- A character's flaw cancels all admirable qualities.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Interleave mythic names and earlier pattern vocabulary. Continue short comparison tasks, especially between trickster cleverness and heroic courage or between fable consequence and heroic cost.

## Unit 5: Poetry, Sound, And Image

### Unit Purpose

Introduce poetry and oral language as literary forms meant to be heard, seen, remembered, and felt. Students learn a small set of poetic terms through short poems, chants, speeches, and image-rich lines rather than through abstract definitions.

### Student Outcomes

- Hear and identify rhyme, rhythm, repetition, line, stanza, image, simile, and personification in short poems or poetic passages.
- Explain how a repeated sound, phrase, or image helps create meaning or mood.
- Distinguish the speaker of a poem from the student or author in a basic way.
- Use oral reading or recitation to notice sound and sense.
- Connect poetic images to earlier story worlds, names, and themes.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Poem, line, stanza, rhyme, rhythm, repetition, image, sound, speaker, simile, personification, mood, recitation, oral language.

### Prerequisites

Students need experience listening closely to short texts, retelling key ideas, and noticing repeated language in folktales and myths.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

The unit reviews repetition from folktales and vivid images from myths and heroic tales. It prepares for dialogue and drama by sharpening attention to speaker, voice, line, and oral performance.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Poetry is only rhyme.
- A poem's speaker is always the author.
- Sound features are decorations with no meaning.
- Figurative language must be read literally.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use brief oral review throughout the unit. Continue to ask for evidence, but evidence may now be a word, phrase, sound pattern, or image rather than only a plot event.

## Unit 6: Dialogue, Comparison, And Final Transfer

### Unit Purpose

Bring the level together through narrator, dialogue, brief drama excerpts, version comparison, and a final transfer task. Students practice reading speech as character action and using the full Level 1 toolkit on new short passages.

### Student Outcomes

- Distinguish narrator, speaker, and character dialogue in short passages.
- Identify how dialogue reveals character, choice, conflict, or consequence.
- Recognize a brief drama excerpt as a scene built from character speech and action.
- Compare two stories, versions, characters, or passages using one clear criterion and evidence.
- Apply Level 1 skills to a new adapted classical or traditional passage.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Narrator, dialogue, speaker, line, scene, drama excerpt, stage direction, character voice, compare, contrast, criterion, evidence, version, transfer.

### Prerequisites

Students need the full prior sequence: retelling, story elements, moral/theme, pattern recognition, myth and hero vocabulary, poetic sound and image, and respectful discussion habits.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This culminating unit reviews every earlier unit while preparing students for Level 2, where they will meet more sustained ancient myth, adapted epic, drama concepts, lyric poetry, and version comparison.

### Misconceptions To Address

- The narrator and every speaker are the same person.
- Dialogue is filler instead of evidence about character and conflict.
- Drama is just a story with quotation marks.
- Comparison means listing everything about two texts rather than using one shared criterion.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use mixed retrieval: genre, pattern, story elements, evidence, poetic features, dialogue, and comparison. The final lesson should ask students to transfer skills to a new passage rather than repeat a known example.
