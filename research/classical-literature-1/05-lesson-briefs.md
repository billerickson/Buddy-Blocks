# Classical Literature 1 Lesson Briefs

Sources: accepted level blueprint, course map, and unit design briefs in `research/classical-literature-1/`.

## Unit 1: Fables, Morals, And Story Elements

### Lesson 1: Retell The Fable In Order

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to retell a compact fable as a sequence of important events rather than as isolated favorite details.
- **Student outcome:** The student can place the beginning, middle, and end of a fable in order and include the key event that makes the ending make sense.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A beginning-middle-end retell frame shows how the problem, action, and outcome connect.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic story listening or reading, first/next/last language, and who/what/where questions.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Retelling only the ending; adding events not in the story; confusing a minor detail with a key event.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a short fable, the student can order the main events and select the sentence that best retells the whole story.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for event sequence; `multiple-choice` for best retell; `passage-question` for evidence from a short fable.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Read a short fable aloud once for enjoyment, then reread and mark what happened first, what changed, and how it ended.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one short fable with few characters; keep event sentences brief; avoid unfamiliar names in this opening lesson.

### Lesson 2: Characters, Settings, Problems, And Outcomes

- **Teaching goal:** Help students identify the basic story elements that make a fable understandable.
- **Student outcome:** The student can name the character, setting, problem, action, and outcome in a short fable.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A story-elements chart separates who, where, what goes wrong, what is done, and what happens.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Retelling in order and basic who/where/what questions.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Naming any place mentioned as the setting; confusing the problem with the outcome; naming a trait instead of a character.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can complete or choose accurate story-element labels for a short fable.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for elements and examples; `multiple-choice` for element identification; `multi-blank-cloze` for completing a story chart.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use one familiar fable and model how each story element answers a different question.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep the passage short; do not ask for moral interpretation before elements are clear.

### Lesson 3: Choices Lead To Consequences

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to connect a character's choice or mistake to what happens next.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain how one choice in a fable leads to a consequence.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A character-choice-consequence chain shows cause and effect in story events.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Story elements, sequence, and basic cause/effect language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating the consequence as random; labeling a character only good or bad; skipping the choice and jumping straight to the moral.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can match choices to consequences and explain one chain with a short because statement.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for choice-to-consequence; `passage-question` for evidence; `multi-blank-cloze` or `multiple-choice` for a brief because/so explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Model a chain such as "The character boasts, so the other characters stop trusting him."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use clear cause/effect language; keep written explanations to one sentence or a short frame.

### Lesson 4: Find The Moral With Evidence

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to choose a moral that fits the story events and support it with evidence.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a supported moral and cite a story event that proves it.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A moral-and-evidence T-chart connects a lesson to the event that supports it.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Retelling, story elements, and choice-consequence reasoning.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Choosing a moral because it sounds kind; confusing topic with moral; citing an unrelated detail as evidence.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can choose the best moral from plausible options and identify the event that supports it.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for moral selection; `passage-question` for evidence; `multiple-choice` for a supported moral explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Contrast two morals: one true-sounding but unsupported, one supported by the fable's ending.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar moral language; limit answer choices to short statements; avoid abstract vocabulary such as "theme" until later.

## Unit 2: Folktales, Tricksters, And Story Patterns

### Lesson 1: What Makes A Folktale?

- **Teaching goal:** Distinguish folktales from fables using concrete features of oral tradition, pattern, and story purpose.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify folktale features and explain how a folktale differs from a fable at an introductory level.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A genre-feature chart compares fable and folktale without treating either category as a rigid formula.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Fable, moral, story elements, retelling, and evidence language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Thinking every animal story is a fable; thinking every old story has an explicit moral; ignoring oral-tradition features.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given short descriptions or passages, the student can choose which features point to folktale or fable.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for genre features; `multiple-choice` for classification; `fill-blank` for precise vocabulary.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare a one-problem fable with a patterned folktale opening, asking what each kind of story seems designed to do.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short excerpts and feature labels; avoid asking students to classify ambiguous examples at first.

### Lesson 2: Repeated Words And Patterned Events

- **Teaching goal:** Show how repeated words, repeated actions, and patterned events help listeners remember and predict a folktale.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a repeated phrase or event pattern and explain its job in the tale.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A pattern ladder tracks repeated scenes or phrases from one part of a tale to the next.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Retelling sequence and basic folktale features.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating repetition as a mistake; noticing repeated words but not repeated actions; assuming prediction means guessing wildly.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can find repeated language or events and use them to predict the next likely step.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for patterned event sequence; `passage-question` for repeated phrase evidence; `multiple-choice` for the purpose of repetition.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Read a short patterned passage aloud and have students tap or mark each repeated phrase.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep patterns obvious; avoid long tales; use read-aloud support for repeated lines.

### Lesson 3: The Trickster's Choice

- **Teaching goal:** Help students analyze trickster choices with care instead of labeling the character as simply good or bad.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a trickster's goal, trick, consequence, and effect on others.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A trickster-choice chart separates what the trickster wants, what the trickster does, and what happens because of it.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Choice-consequence chains, folktale patterns, and respectful discussion language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Trickster means villain; cleverness always deserves praise; funny endings have no consequence.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can explain whether a trickster's choice helps, harms, reveals, or corrects something in the tale.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for trickster evidence; `multiple-choice` for consequence analysis; `multiple-choice` for a short balanced explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Present one trickster action and ask two questions: "What did the character want?" and "Who was changed by the trick?"
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one trick at a time; avoid culturally loaded claims; keep judgment language careful.

### Lesson 4: Compare Two Tale Patterns

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to compare two folktale or trickster moments using one shared criterion and evidence from both.
- **Student outcome:** The student can state one way two tale patterns are alike or different and support the comparison with details.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A one-criterion comparison frame keeps students from writing two unrelated summaries.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Retelling, folktale features, repeated patterns, trickster choices, and evidence.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Comparison means listing everything; one detail from only one story is enough; alike and different can be mixed without a criterion.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can complete a comparison sentence using both/evidence/because language.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for criteria and evidence; `passage-question` for paired text details; `multiple-choice` for a one-sentence comparison.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Model a comparison with a tiny criterion: "Both tales use a warning, but the characters respond differently."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short paired passages; limit comparison to one feature at a time.

## Unit 3: Myths, Names, And Cultural Meaning

### Lesson 1: Myth Is A Traditional Story

- **Teaching goal:** Define myth as a traditional story with cultural meaning and separate it from the everyday use of myth as "false."
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a myth by its literary features and explain that myths should be discussed respectfully.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A myth-feature chart emphasizes tradition, cultural meaning, gods or extraordinary beings, origins, and human questions.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Genre-feature charts, folktale comparison, fable moral evidence, and respectful language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Myth means lie; myths are just fantasy stories; all myths teach in the same way fables do.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can choose the best explanation of myth and reject a disrespectful or inaccurate definition.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for definition; `match-pairs` for features and examples; `multiple-choice` for respectful wording.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Begin with two uses of the word myth and explain that literature class uses the traditional-story meaning.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep examples brief; avoid theological debate; use "traditional story" consistently.

### Lesson 2: Names, Places, And Relationships

- **Teaching goal:** Support students in handling unfamiliar Greek and Roman names, places, and character relationships through story context.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use pronunciation cues and relationship clues to keep track of a small myth cast.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Name cards and a relationship mini-map reduce memory load while preserving story meaning.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Story characters and settings, respectful discussion language, and myth as traditional story.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Understanding a myth requires memorizing long lists; similar-looking names are interchangeable; place names are trivia with no story role.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a short myth setup, the student can match names to roles and use a relationship clue to answer a comprehension question.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for name-role relationships; `fill-blank` for pronunciation or role support; `passage-question` for context clues.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Introduce only three or four names at once and say them aloud before reading the passage.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use pronunciation hints; avoid genealogies; repeat names with roles when needed.

### Lesson 3: Origins And Transformations

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to recognize origin and transformation patterns in adapted myths.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain what changes in a myth and why that change matters to the story.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A before-change-after chart shows transformation as a meaningful story event, not random magic.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Myth features, sequence, cause and consequence, and pattern recognition.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Transformation is only a special effect; origin stories are the same as scientific explanations; the reason for change does not matter.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can identify the before state, cause or warning, transformation, and story meaning.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for transformation sequence; `passage-question` for cause evidence; `multiple-choice` for what the change shows.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Model one mythic change with the question "What was different at the end, and what caused it?"
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use age-appropriate myth episodes; avoid disturbing transformations; keep names limited.

### Lesson 4: Warnings, Gifts, And Consequences

- **Teaching goal:** Help students track warnings, gifts, and consequences as central mythic patterns.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain how a warning or gift shapes a myth's outcome.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A warning/gift-consequence chain connects motive, choice, and result.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Choice-consequence chains, myth features, and name-role support.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A gift is always helpful; warnings are background details; consequences are random punishments rather than tied to choices.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can match a warning or gift to the action and consequence that follow.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for warning/gift to consequence; `passage-question` for evidence; `multiple-choice` for pattern recognition.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Ask students to mark the moment when a character receives a warning, gift, or command before asking about the ending.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep moral judgment careful; avoid graphic myth material; emphasize story cause and effect.

## Unit 4: Heroes, Quests, And Consequences

### Lesson 1: A Quest Has A Goal

- **Teaching goal:** Teach quest structure as more than travel: a goal, path, obstacle, and meaningful outcome.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify the goal and main parts of a short quest or journey episode.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A quest path maps goal, route, obstacle, and outcome.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Mythic names and places, story sequence, and pattern vocabulary.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Any trip is a quest; the obstacle alone is the point; the goal can be ignored once action begins.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can choose the quest goal and arrange quest events in order.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for quest sequence; `multiple-choice` for goal identification; `passage-question` for obstacle evidence.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Draw a simple path and label what the hero wants before reading the episode.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one clear goal; avoid too many stops or names; keep the route visually simple.

### Lesson 2: Obstacles, Helpers, And Tests

- **Teaching goal:** Show how obstacles, helpers, and tests reveal what a hero must learn or do.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain the role of an obstacle, helper, or test in a heroic episode.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** An obstacle-helper chart distinguishes what blocks the hero from what aids the hero.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Quest structure, choice-consequence reasoning, and mythic support vocabulary.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A helper solves the quest for the hero; obstacles are only decorations; tests are always battles.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can match a helper or obstacle to its function and explain how it affects the task.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for role matching; `passage-question` for evidence; `multiple-choice` for function analysis.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Pause in a quest episode and ask whether each new character or event helps, blocks, or tests the hero.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use concrete roles; avoid multi-layered symbolism; keep the number of characters low.

### Lesson 3: Heroes Can Have Flaws

- **Teaching goal:** Help students recognize that a hero can have admirable traits and flaws that both affect the story.
- **Student outcome:** The student can connect a hero's trait or flaw to a choice and consequence.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A trait-choice-outcome chain supports balanced character thinking.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Character traits, consequences, quest parts, and mythic story support.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Heroes are perfect; a flaw makes someone not a hero; courage means never needing help.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can identify a trait or flaw from evidence and explain its effect on one event.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for trait evidence; `passage-question` for choices; `multiple-choice` for balanced explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use two columns, "admirable" and "risky," to show that one character can have both.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar trait words; avoid moral complexity beyond one clear choice and result.

### Lesson 4: Cost, Reward, And Return

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students that a journey ending may include a reward, a cost, and a change in the hero or community.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify what is gained, what is lost or learned, and how the return matters.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A cost-reward-return chart connects ending events to meaning.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Quest structure, obstacles, helpers, traits, flaws, and consequence reasoning.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A happy ending has no cost; reward only means treasure; return is just the final location.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can explain the reward and cost in a short heroic episode and connect them to the story's ending.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for cost/reward examples; `passage-question` for return evidence; `multiple-choice` for a brief cost/reward explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Ask three ending questions: What was gained? What was hard or lost? What changed after the journey?
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use gentle, age-appropriate costs; keep explanations short and concrete.

## Unit 5: Poetry, Sound, And Image

### Lesson 1: Lines, Stanzas, And Sound

- **Teaching goal:** Introduce the visible and audible shape of a poem through lines, stanzas, and sound.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify lines and stanzas and notice one sound feature in a short poem.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Line-and-stanza markup shows that poetry is arranged for hearing and seeing.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Listening to short texts, noticing repeated language, and using evidence.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A poem is just a paragraph broken oddly; every line is a complete sentence; sound does not affect meaning.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can count or identify lines and stanzas and choose a sound feature that stands out.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for line/stanza identification; `fill-blank` for vocabulary; `passage-question` for sound evidence.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show a four-line poem beside the same words in a paragraph and ask what changes when it is arranged as lines.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use very short poems; avoid advanced meter; read aloud before asking labels.

### Lesson 2: Repetition And Rhyme Help Memory

- **Teaching goal:** Show how repeated words and rhyme can help a poem, chant, or speech become memorable and meaningful.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify repetition or rhyme and explain how it supports memory, emphasis, or mood.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Sound and repetition highlighting connects repeated language to meaning.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Lines and stanzas, folktale repetition, and oral listening.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Rhyme is the only feature that makes poetry; repetition is filler; any two similar-looking words rhyme.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can choose repeated words or rhyming words and state their effect in a simple way.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for rhyming words; `passage-question` for repeated language; `multiple-choice` for one-sentence effect.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Read a short repeated line twice and ask why the poet or speaker might want that phrase to stay in memory.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use clear rhymes and repeated phrases; avoid technical stress terminology.

### Lesson 3: Images, Similes, And Personification

- **Teaching goal:** Introduce image, simile, and personification as ways words help readers picture and understand something.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a vivid image, a simile using like or as, and a simple personification.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Image sketch notes and comparison markers help students avoid reading figurative language literally.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Story images from myths and heroes, poem lines, and evidence language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** An image must be a printed picture; similes are literal facts; personification means the thing truly becomes human.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can match a line to image, simile, or personification and explain the picture it creates.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for feature to example; `multiple-choice` for figurative meaning; `multiple-choice` for a short image explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use one line such as "The moon watched over the road" and ask what picture and feeling it creates.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use concrete, gentle images; avoid dense metaphor; keep figurative terms few and tied to meaning.

### Lesson 4: Speaker, Mood, And Recitation

- **Teaching goal:** Help students distinguish speaker from author and use oral reading to notice mood.
- **Student outcome:** The student can infer who is speaking in a short poem or speech and choose a fitting mood for oral recitation.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A speaker-mood-evidence chart connects voice, word choice, and oral performance.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Lines, repetition, image, and basic character voice from stories.
- **Likely misconceptions:** The speaker is always the author; mood is the reader's private feeling only; recitation means saying words quickly from memory.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can identify a likely speaker or mood and cite a word, phrase, or line as evidence.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for speaker evidence; `multiple-choice` for mood; `multiple-choice` for oral reading choice.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Read the same line in two voices and ask which voice fits the words better.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short, clear poems or speeches; no long memorization requirement; keep mood choices age-appropriate.

## Unit 6: Dialogue, Comparison, And Final Transfer

### Lesson 1: Narrator, Speaker, And Dialogue

- **Teaching goal:** Distinguish narrator, speaker, and character dialogue in short narrative and poetic passages.
- **Student outcome:** The student can tell who is telling the story and who is speaking a quoted or scripted line.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Speaker tags and narrator labels help students track voice.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Poem speaker, story characters, dialogue punctuation exposure, and retelling.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Every voice in a passage is the narrator; quotation marks always mean the same speaker; speaker and author are always identical.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can label narrator and character speech in a short passage and answer who said what.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for line to speaker; `passage-question` for narrator/dialogue evidence; `fill-blank` for vocabulary.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Mark one sentence of narration and one line of dialogue in different colors.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use two speakers at most at first; keep dialogue tags clear; avoid nested quotations.

### Lesson 2: Dialogue Shows Character

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to use a line of dialogue as evidence for character, conflict, choice, or consequence.
- **Student outcome:** The student can infer what a line of speech shows about a character or problem.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A dialogue-choice-consequence chain treats speech as action.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Narrator and dialogue labels, character traits, choice-consequence reasoning.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Dialogue is filler; a character's words always tell the whole truth; tone can be guessed without evidence.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can choose what a line reveals and support the inference with the words spoken.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for fitting next line or speech evidence; `multiple-choice` for character inference.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Ask, "What did the character say? What does that show? What might happen next?"
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short lines; keep inference close to text; avoid sarcasm or subtle irony.

### Lesson 3: A Drama Excerpt Is A Scene

- **Teaching goal:** Introduce drama excerpts as scenes made from character lines and stage directions.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a character line, stage direction, and conflict in a brief scene.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A scene map shows speaker names, lines, stage directions, and action.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Dialogue, speaker tracking, character conflict, and oral reading.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Drama is only a story with quotation marks; stage directions do not matter; narrator and stage direction are the same as a character line.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can label parts of a short scene and explain how a stage direction changes understanding.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for scene parts; `passage-question` for stage direction evidence or appropriate line choice.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Read a two-character scene aloud, first ignoring stage directions and then using them, and compare the meaning.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use a tiny scene; keep stage directions simple; avoid performance pressure.

### Lesson 4: Compare, Then Transfer

- **Teaching goal:** Bring Level 1 skills together through one-criterion comparison and a new short passage.
- **Student outcome:** The student can compare with evidence and apply retelling, genre, pattern, character, language, and respectful discussion skills to a new passage.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A final transfer checklist asks: retell, genre, pattern, character choice, evidence, and comparison when needed.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** All prior Level 1 skills.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Comparison is two summaries; transfer means guessing which old lesson the question came from; evidence is optional once the answer feels right.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can answer a mixed set of questions about a new passage and choose a short evidence-based comparison or interpretation.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for new text comprehension; `order-items` for retelling; `multiple-choice` for transfer explanation, genre, and pattern.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Tell students they now have a reader's toolkit and model choosing the tool that fits the question.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use a short adapted passage; include only a few unfamiliar names; keep explanation choices brief and scaffolded.

## Completion Checklist

- Every lesson title comes from the unit design briefs in exact unit and lesson order.
- Every lesson has a distinct instructional job and observable evidence of mastery.
- Question writing should begin with exposure or guided recognition, then move toward application, explanation, comparison, or transfer as appropriate.
