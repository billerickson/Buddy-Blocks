# Unit 3: Myths, Names, And Cultural Meaning

Track: Classical Literature
Level: Level 1
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/classical-literature-1/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn to read introductory Greek and Roman myth episodes as traditional stories with cultural meaning, memorable names, recurring patterns, and consequences tied to choices, warnings, gifts, origins, or transformations.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

Students now have enough retelling and pattern knowledge to handle unfamiliar names and ancient settings. Placing myths after fables and folktales helps students avoid the misconception that myth simply means "false story" or that all older stories work the same way.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: myth as traditional story, cultural meaning, origin, transformation, divine messenger, gift, warning, consequence.
- Vocabulary: myth, ancient, Greek, Roman, culture, sacred, origin story, transformation, messenger, gift, warning, consequence, motive, Mount Olympus, underworld, sea, city, reference.
- Representations and models: name-pronunciation cards, character relationship mini-map, myth-pattern chart, warning-consequence chain.
- Texts and forms: adapted Greek and Roman myth episodes with age-appropriate content and limited names.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need retelling, story elements, moral and lesson evidence, folktale pattern recognition, and respectful discussion language.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Myth means false, silly, or useless.
- A myth can be understood by memorizing a list of gods and places.
- Greek and Roman stories should be treated as interchangeable fantasy.
- A transformation is only magic, not a meaningful story event.
- A warning can be ignored in interpretation if the ending is exciting.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin by defining myth carefully and separating literary meaning from the everyday insult "that's a myth." Then support unfamiliar names and places through story context. Next, focus on origin and transformation patterns. End with warning, gift, and consequence because those patterns connect myths to earlier choice-consequence reasoning and later hero stories.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Students continue to retell, name story elements, identify patterns, connect choices to outcomes, and use evidence. The unit deliberately asks students to compare myth with fable and folktale features so genre terms stay distinct.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

Myth vocabulary and story patterns prepare students for heroic journeys, later epic material, allusion readiness, poetry with classical images, and drama or dialogue drawn from mythic scenes.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Explain myth as a traditional story with cultural meaning.
- Retell an adapted myth episode in order.
- Identify a mythic name, place, relationship, or pattern from context.
- Recognize origin, transformation, warning, gift, and consequence patterns.
- Use respectful language when discussing ancient stories.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Myth Is A Traditional Story**: Define myth in literary and cultural terms, not as "just false."
2. **Names, Places, And Relationships**: Use pronunciation and context supports for unfamiliar mythic names.
3. **Origins And Transformations**: Recognize myths that explain or dramatize change.
4. **Warnings, Gifts, And Consequences**: Track how a warning or gift shapes the outcome of a myth.
