# Unit 6: Dialogue, Comparison, And Final Transfer

Track: Classical Literature
Level: Level 1
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/classical-literature-1/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students bring the level's tools together by distinguishing narrator, speaker, dialogue, and drama; reading speech as character action; comparing texts with one criterion; and transferring Level 1 skills to a new short passage.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

Dialogue and drama require students to track who is speaking, why the line matters, and how speech changes a scene. Those demands are easier after students have practiced story events, character choices, poetic voice, and evidence. The final transfer lesson confirms that students can use the whole toolkit without being told which unit skill to apply.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: narrator versus speaker, dialogue as action, scene, drama excerpt, version comparison, cumulative transfer.
- Vocabulary: narrator, dialogue, speaker, line, scene, drama excerpt, stage direction, character voice, compare, contrast, criterion, evidence, version, transfer.
- Representations and models: speaker tag chart, scene map, dialogue-choice-consequence chain, comparison Venn or two-column chart, final transfer checklist.
- Texts and forms: brief narrative excerpts with dialogue, short drama scenes, paired versions, and one new adapted traditional or classical passage.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need retelling, story elements, moral/theme evidence, pattern recognition, myth and hero vocabulary, poetry speaker awareness, and respectful discussion habits.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- The narrator and every speaker are the same person.
- Dialogue is filler rather than evidence.
- Drama is just narrative with quotation marks.
- Stage directions are not part of meaning.
- Comparison means listing everything about each text.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin by separating narrator, speaker, and character dialogue. Then teach dialogue as a choice that reveals character or conflict. Next, introduce drama excerpts and stage directions. End with comparison and transfer so students demonstrate they can select the right tool for a new passage.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

This unit deliberately reviews all previous work: fable morals, folktale patterns, mythic names and warnings, heroic quests, poetic speaker, sound, image, evidence, and respectful comparison.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

The unit prepares Level 2 work with adapted ancient drama, lyric poetry, myth variants, Homeric episodes, and deeper comparison of gods, mortals, fate, choice, and hero patterns.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Distinguish narrator, speaker, and character dialogue.
- Explain what a line of dialogue reveals about character, conflict, or consequence.
- Identify basic drama features, including scene and stage direction.
- Compare two short texts or versions using one criterion and evidence from both.
- Apply Level 1 tools to a new short passage.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Narrator, Speaker, And Dialogue**: Tell who is telling the story and who is speaking in the text.
2. **Dialogue Shows Character**: Use a line of speech to infer choice, conflict, or trait.
3. **A Drama Excerpt Is A Scene**: Read stage directions and character lines as parts of dramatic action.
4. **Compare, Then Transfer**: Compare with one criterion and apply Level 1 skills to a new passage.
