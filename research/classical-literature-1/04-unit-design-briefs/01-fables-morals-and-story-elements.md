# Unit 1: Fables, Morals, And Story Elements

Track: Classical Literature
Level: Level 1
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/classical-literature-1/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn the basic literary routine for Classical Literature 1: listen or read closely, retell a short work in order, identify story elements, connect character choices to consequences, and choose a moral that is supported by the events.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

Fables are short, memorable, and built around clear consequences, so they let students practice literary thinking without heavy decoding or background demands. This unit establishes the level's core habits before students meet longer folktales, unfamiliar mythic names, poetic language, or drama excerpts.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: retelling, sequence, story elements, cause and consequence, moral, evidence.
- Vocabulary: fable, moral, lesson, character, setting, problem, action, outcome, consequence, detail, retell, evidence, beginning, middle, end.
- Representations and models: beginning-middle-end frame, story-element chart, character-choice-consequence chain, moral-and-evidence T-chart.
- Texts and forms: short animal fables and adapted moral tales with one clear central conflict.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should be able to answer who, what, and where questions about a short story; retell familiar events with support; and understand that a character can make a choice that changes what happens next.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- A retelling is complete if it includes one exciting detail.
- The moral is any good advice, even if the story does not support it.
- Characters are only good or bad rather than characters whose choices have results.
- Evidence means any detail from the story.
- The ending alone tells the whole lesson.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with retelling because students need the events in order before they can interpret them. Then isolate story elements so students can name what they are tracking. Next, connect choices to consequences. End with moral-and-evidence work so students practice making a simple interpretation that is grounded in the story.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

This opening unit reviews prerequisite story comprehension from earlier reading experience: who, where, what happened, first/next/last language, and basic lesson language from familiar stories.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

The retell-evidence routine becomes the foundation for folktale comparison, myth interpretation, hero analysis, poetic evidence, dialogue analysis, and final transfer. The choice-consequence chain prepares students for tricksters, heroes, tests, warnings, and flaws.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Retell a short fable in beginning-middle-end order.
- Identify character, setting, problem, action, outcome, and moral.
- Match a character choice to a consequence.
- Choose a moral supported by the story.
- Cite one event or detail as evidence for a lesson.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Retell The Fable In Order**: Build beginning-middle-end retelling from a compact fable.
2. **Characters, Settings, Problems, And Outcomes**: Name the basic story elements that make a fable understandable.
3. **Choices Lead To Consequences**: Connect character traits and decisions to what happens next.
4. **Find The Moral With Evidence**: Choose and explain a moral using a specific story event.
