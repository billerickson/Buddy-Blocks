# Unit 2: Folktales, Tricksters, And Story Patterns

Track: Classical Literature
Level: Level 1
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/classical-literature-1/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students extend fable comprehension into folktales and trickster tales by recognizing oral-tradition patterns, repeated language, tests, warnings, clever turns, and culturally respectful comparison.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

Folktales are a natural next step after fables because they remain accessible while adding more pattern, repetition, and variation. Trickster tales also challenge the easy "good character/bad character" thinking that can follow early moral work.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: oral tradition, repeated pattern, trickster behavior, test or warning, tale versions, respectful comparison.
- Vocabulary: folktale, oral tradition, trickster, pattern, repeated phrase, test, warning, cleverness, version, culture, tradition, compare, contrast, criterion.
- Representations and models: pattern ladder, repeated-phrase tracker, trickster-choice chart, two-story comparison frame.
- Texts and forms: short folktales, trickster episodes, and adapted world-tradition tale excerpts.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need Unit 1 retelling, story-element vocabulary, character-choice-consequence reasoning, and the ability to use one detail as evidence.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- A trickster is only a villain or only a hero.
- Repetition is accidental or only for decoration.
- All old stories with animals are fables.
- Comparison means writing two unrelated summaries.
- A story from one tradition represents everything about that culture.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Start by distinguishing folktales from fables with visible features. Then examine repetition and pattern as oral-story tools. Move into trickster choices and consequences, where students must reason carefully rather than moralize quickly. End with a small comparison task using one shared feature.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Students continue to retell in order, identify story elements, connect choices to consequences, and use evidence for a lesson. Fable morals are revisited as one kind of lesson, not the only way older stories make meaning.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

Pattern recognition prepares students for mythic origins, transformations, quests, tests, helpers, and repeated poetic language. Respectful comparison prepares them for Greek and Roman myths and version comparison in the final unit.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Classify examples as fable, folktale, or trickster tale using concrete features.
- Identify a repeated phrase, repeated action, warning, test, or pattern.
- Explain a trickster's choice and consequence without reducing the character to one label.
- Compare two tales or moments using one criterion and evidence from both.
- Use respectful language about story traditions.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **What Makes A Folktale?**: Distinguish folktales from fables through oral-tradition features.
2. **Repeated Words And Patterned Events**: Notice repeated language and actions that help listeners remember a tale.
3. **The Trickster's Choice**: Analyze clever, disruptive, or risky choices and their consequences.
4. **Compare Two Tale Patterns**: Use one shared criterion to compare two short tales or tale moments.
