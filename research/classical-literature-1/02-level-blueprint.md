# Classical Literature 1 Level Blueprint

Track: Classical Literature
Level: Level 1
Source: Accepted research brief at `research/classical-literature-1/01-research-brief.md`

## 1. Level Mission

Classical Literature 1 gives beginning students a shared foundation for reading, hearing, retelling, and discussing older stories with care. Students begin with compact fables and moral tales, then move through folktales, trickster tales, introductory myths, heroic journeys, short poems, and brief dialogue or drama excerpts. Across the level they learn to follow what happens, name story elements, connect character choices to consequences, recognize major genres and recurring patterns, hear sound and image in poetry, and compare stories using concrete evidence. By the end of the level, students should approach unfamiliar classical or traditional stories with curiosity, respect, and a small but durable toolkit for understanding plot, genre, theme, language, and cultural context.

## 2. Durable Student Outcomes

By the end of Classical Literature 1, students should be able to:

1. Retell a short fable, myth, folktale, legend, adapted heroic tale, poem, or dialogue excerpt in an accurate beginning-middle-end sequence.
2. Identify characters, setting, problem, important actions, outcome, and lesson or theme in short literary works.
3. Distinguish introductory genres: fable, folktale, trickster tale, myth, legend, heroic or epic tale, poem, and drama excerpt.
4. Explain how a character trait, choice, or mistake leads to a consequence in the story.
5. Recognize recurring story patterns such as trickster, quest, test, transformation, helper, reward, and consequence.
6. Interpret a simple moral, lesson, or theme by connecting it to specific story events rather than to a personal preference alone.
7. Notice basic literary features, including narrator, dialogue, stanza, rhyme, rhythm, repetition, image, simile, and personification, when they support meaning.
8. Compare two stories, characters, versions, or passages using a shared criterion and concrete evidence.
9. Use respectful language when discussing stories from Greek, Roman, biblical, medieval, and world traditions as literature and cultural inheritance.
10. Build habits of rereading, recalling, listening closely, and answering with evidence from the text.

## 3. Concept And Skill Progression

The level begins with fables and brief moral tales because they are short, memorable, and structurally clear. Students first practice retelling the story in order, naming characters and settings, and identifying the problem, action, outcome, and moral. This early work keeps attention on what happened and why before students are asked for broader interpretation.

Students then move to folktales and trickster tales. These stories extend retelling into pattern recognition: repeated scenes, clever characters, tests, warnings, rewards, and consequences. Students learn that a trickster is not simply "bad" and that similar tale patterns can appear in different cultural traditions. Comparison begins here with concrete criteria such as character goal, trick, repeated phrase, or ending.

After story structure is secure, students meet introductory myths. Greek and Roman stories are presented in adapted, age-appropriate episodes with pronunciation support and a small number of names at a time. Students learn that a myth is a traditional story with cultural or sacred significance, not merely a false statement. They practice connecting mythic events to questions about nature, human behavior, memory, or custom without reducing any culture to trivia.

The middle of the level adds heroic, journey, and legend material. Students track tasks, obstacles, helpers, choices, flaws, and costs. They learn that heroes in classical stories are not always perfect and that a journey or quest usually changes the character or community in some way. This unit strengthens cause-and-effect reasoning and prepares students for later epic and medieval material.

Students next study poetry and oral language through short poems, chants, speeches, and lyrical passages that can be heard aloud. They identify stanza, rhyme, rhythm, repetition, image, simile, and personification when those features help create meaning or memory. The emphasis stays on sound, picture, and feeling before formal analysis.

The level closes with narrator, dialogue, drama, comparison, and cumulative transfer. Students distinguish narrator from speaker, read brief dialogue as character action, compare versions or related stories, and respond to a new short passage by retelling, naming the genre or pattern, explaining a lesson, and citing evidence.

## 4. Vocabulary, Representation, Model, Text, And Form Progression

Early vocabulary should stabilize story-comprehension language: character, setting, beginning, middle, end, problem, action, outcome, detail, retell, lesson, moral, and evidence. Representations should include story maps, sequence strips, character-action-consequence chains, and simple beginning-middle-end frames.

Genre and pattern vocabulary should build gradually: fable, folktale, trickster tale, myth, legend, heroic tale, epic tale, quest, test, helper, obstacle, transformation, reward, consequence, version, and tradition. Models should include genre-example charts, pattern maps, comparison tables, and story-path diagrams.

Myth and cultural-context language should include adapted text, ancient, Greek, Roman, tradition, culture, place, name, reference, sacred, explain, dramatize, and respect. Instruction should introduce names and places only as they help students understand a story, not as detached memorization lists.

Poetry and oral-language vocabulary should include poem, line, stanza, rhyme, rhythm, repetition, image, sound, simile, personification, speaker, and recitation. Forms should include short poems, repeated lines, short speeches, and read-aloud passages. Students should hear the language before being asked to label it.

Dialogue and comparison vocabulary should include narrator, dialogue, speaker, scene, drama, character voice, compare, contrast, same, different, both, because, criterion, and evidence. Response forms should progress from selected answers and matching to short constructed responses that use frames such as "Both stories..." and "The evidence is..."

## 5. Fluency Expectations

Fluency in this level is literary fluency rather than speed. Students should become more fluent at retelling a short text in order, recognizing common genres and patterns, using a small set of literary terms accurately, and retrieving important names, motifs, and story features after spaced review. Oral fluency matters: students should practice hearing and saying short lines, repeated phrases, and brief recitations with attention to sense and sound. Retrieval practice should be brief, meaningful, and tied to stories rather than detached trivia.

## 6. Conceptual Understanding Expectations

Students should understand that stories are made from choices about events, characters, language, and form. A moral or theme is supported by story events; it is not any lesson the reader happens to like. A genre is a helpful expectation, not a cage: fables often teach morals, myths carry cultural meaning, folktales travel through retelling, legends attach story to remembered people or places, poems use concentrated language, and drama lets characters speak and act directly.

Students should also understand that older stories come from particular traditions and should be read with historical and cultural imagination. A myth is not taught as "wrong science" or as interchangeable fantasy. A hero may be admirable and flawed. A trickster may reveal cleverness, disorder, justice, or danger. Comparison should be based on a shared feature, such as character, pattern, lesson, or language.

## 7. Application And Transfer Expectations

Students should apply their learning to new, short, age-appropriate passages. Given an unfamiliar fable, folktale, myth episode, poem, or dialogue excerpt, they should identify the genre or pattern, retell the key events, connect choices to outcomes, infer a lesson or theme from evidence, and explain a basic language feature when it supports meaning. They should transfer comparison skills by using one criterion across two stories or versions, rather than producing two unrelated summaries.

Students should also transfer respectful discussion habits. When a story comes from a different time, place, or tradition, students should use careful language, ask what the story shows within its context, and avoid flattening a culture into a single belief or stereotype.

## 8. Review And Spacing Plan

Review should begin in the first unit and remain cumulative. Retelling sequence, story elements, character-choice-consequence reasoning, and evidence language should appear in every unit. Genre terms should be introduced in focused units and then revisited in mixed review after short and longer delays.

Fable morals should return during folktales and myths so students compare "lesson from events" across genres. Trickster, quest, transformation, test, helper, reward, and consequence patterns should be interleaved with myth and heroic material. Names and places should be retrieved through meaningful story prompts, not flash trivia alone.

Poetry terms should reappear during dialogue and final transfer work so students continue noticing sound, image, repetition, and speaker. Comparison frames should be practiced in small, recurring tasks: same/different, both, because, evidence, criterion. Feedback should point back to the story event, wording, or pattern that supports the answer.

## 9. Mastery Evidence For The End Of The Level

End-of-level mastery should be evidenced by a balanced set of short reading, listening, recognition, sequencing, comparison, and explanation tasks. A student who has mastered Classical Literature 1 can retell a new short literary passage in order; name its key story elements; distinguish fable, folktale, myth, legend, poem, and drama excerpt at an introductory level; identify a recurring pattern; and explain a simple lesson or theme with evidence from events or words.

The student can notice character traits and choices, explain consequences, recognize basic literary features when they matter, compare two stories or versions using a shared criterion, and use respectful language about older stories and cultural traditions. The strongest evidence of mastery is transfer: the student meets a new adapted classical or traditional passage and still knows how to slow down, retell, identify the pattern, and support an interpretation with concrete evidence.

This blueprint is derived from the accepted Classical Literature 1 research brief.
