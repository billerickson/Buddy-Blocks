# Memory Works 2 Research Brief

Track: Memory Works  
Level: Level 2  
Intended student profile: students with basic memory routines who can chunk, retrieve, correct, and recite short selections, now ready for more fluent recitation and broader cross-subject memory anchors.

This brief defines outcomes and instructional priorities only. It does not define units, lessons, selections, or questions.

## Sources

- [IES: Organizing Instruction and Study to Improve Student Learning](https://ies.ed.gov/ncee/wwc/PracticeGuide/1), for spaced practice, retrieval, interleaving, feedback, and review.
- [Dunlosky et al., Improving Students' Learning With Effective Learning Techniques](https://doi.org/10.1177/1529100612453266), for practice testing and distributed practice.
- [Roediger and Karpicke, Test-Enhanced Learning](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1467-9280.2006.01693.x), for retrieval practice and long-term retention.
- [Common Core State Standards for ELA/Literacy](https://learning.ccsso.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/11/ELA_Standards1.pdf), for oral presentation, fluency, poetry, language, and knowledge-building expectations.
- [Core Knowledge Sequence 2023](https://www.coreknowledge.org/core-knowledge-sequence/), used as a cross-check for poems, sayings, geography, history, math/science, and cultural knowledge.
- Local product constraints only: `docs/content-creation.md` and `docs/question-types.md`.

## 1. End-Of-Level Outcomes

By the end of Memory Works 2, students should be able to memorize and fluently recite longer poems or passages than Level 1; retrieve Latin maxims, grammar definitions, geography lists/map pegs, timeline anchors, civic terms, and useful formulas; use expressive oral delivery; paraphrase the meaning of memorized material; and maintain spaced cumulative review across multiple content types.

## 2. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should know chunking, cue-to-recall practice, spaced review, error correction, short oral recitation, and meaning checks. They should be able to retrieve short poems, sayings, facts, and sequences with support.

## 3. Major Concept Clusters

- **Fluent Recitation:** accuracy, pacing, expression, volume, and meaning.
- **Longer Chunk Chains:** stanza, sentence, line, phrase, list group, map region, and timeline segment.
- **Cross-Subject Anchors:** Latin maxims, grammar definitions, civic terms, history sentences, geography pegs, formulas, and literary lines.
- **Meaningful Recall:** paraphrase, context match, example/nonexample, and use.
- **Review Management:** old/new mix, weak-chunk repair, and cumulative retrieval.

## 4. Recommended Progression

Begin with Level 1 routine review. Increase target length only through stable chunks. Add expression and oral delivery after accuracy. Interleave content types early. Use meaning questions for every major target. End with mixed delayed recall and a short polished recitation.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Fluent means fast.
- Longer memory targets should be practiced only from the beginning.
- Expression matters more than accuracy.
- Definitions are learned if recited once.
- Map and timeline lists are trivia rather than mental pegs.
- Review is only for material already forgotten.

## 6. Developmental Constraints And Reading/Load Concerns

Keep new material short enough for success. Provide audio/read-aloud support. Do not introduce too many long selections at once. Separate memory accuracy from spelling unless written recall is the goal. Use visual supports for maps, formulas, and timelines. Avoid public performance pressure too early.

## 7. Assessment Implications

Assess recognition, cued recall, independent recall, delayed recall, and oral fluency separately. Use flash-card, fill-blank, match-pairs, order-items, listening, speaking-prompt, and short text-input tasks. Distractors should reflect swapped order, near wording, wrong region, similar term, or formula confusion. Feedback should identify the missed chunk and cue repair.

## 8. Spiral Review Priorities

Short and longer poems, Latin maxims, grammar definitions, geography pegs, timeline anchors, civic terms, math/science formulas, retrieval routines, weak-chunk repair, oral delivery, and meaning paraphrase.

## 9. Concepts That Should Not Be Taught Yet

Avoid very long speeches, high-stakes public recitation, advanced mnemonic systems as required content, disconnected trivia lists, and memory targets students cannot paraphrase at all.

## 10. Source Notes

IES, Dunlosky, and Roediger/Karpicke shaped retrieval, spacing, and delayed recall. Common Core shaped oral fluency and language expectations. Core Knowledge shaped content anchor selection. Buddy Blocks docs shaped compact practice, feedback, and cumulative review.
