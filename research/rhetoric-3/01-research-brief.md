# Rhetoric 3 Research Brief

Track: Rhetoric  
Level: Level 3  
Intended student profile: students who can retell, imitate, amplify, reduce, and explain a point, now ready for refutation, confirmation, commonplace, praise/blame, comparison, evidence, audience, transitions, and multi-paragraph composition.

This brief defines outcomes and instructional priorities only. It does not define units, lessons, or questions.

## 1. End-Of-Level Outcomes

By the end of Rhetoric 3, students should be able to confirm or refute a simple claim using relevant evidence; develop a commonplace or general observation; praise or criticize actions and qualities in age-appropriate, evidence-based ways; compare people, choices, texts, or ideas using criteria; choose evidence for an audience; use transitions; and draft short multi-paragraph compositions with introduction, support, and conclusion.

## 2. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should control fable/narrative retelling, moral/point, paraphrase, imitation, amplification, reduction, oral narration, claim/reason/example, and audience/purpose basics.

## 3. Major Concept Clusters

- **Confirmation And Refutation:** support or challenge a claim with reasons and evidence.
- **Commonplace:** develop a general idea through examples and explanation.
- **Praise And Blame:** evaluate actions, choices, and qualities with evidence, avoiding personal attack.
- **Comparison:** use explicit criteria and balanced structure.
- **Evidence And Audience:** select support that fits purpose and listener/reader.
- **Paragraph Arrangement:** transitions, topic sentences, examples, and conclusion.

## 4. Recommended Progression

Begin with claim/reason/evidence review. Teach confirmation before refutation. Add refutation as respectful testing of a claim, not winning. Introduce commonplace through familiar virtues, habits, or civic/literary ideas. Teach praise/blame as evidence-based evaluation of actions. Move to comparison with criteria. End with short multi-paragraph compositions and oral sharing.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Refutation means being rude.
- Praise means flattering; blame means insulting.
- Evidence is any detail.
- A comparison is two separate descriptions.
- Transitions fix weak organization.
- A paragraph is a certain number of sentences.
- Audience awareness means saying what people want to hear.

## 6. Developmental Constraints And Reading/Load Concerns

Use safe literary, historical, school, and civic examples. Avoid inflammatory topics. Keep compositions short and scaffolded. Require one clear criterion at a time for comparison. Provide sentence frames for refutation and praise/blame to keep tone respectful.

## 7. Assessment Implications

Use passage questions to select evidence, multiple-choice for relevant refutation, order-items for paragraph arrangement, and short constructed responses for one claim with evidence. Distractors should include irrelevant evidence, personal attack, vague praise, unsupported comparison, transition misuse, and audience mismatch.

## 8. Spiral Review Priorities

Audience, purpose, claim, reason, evidence, example, moral, paraphrase, amplification, confirmation, refutation, commonplace, praise, blame, comparison criterion, transition, paragraph.

## 9. Concepts That Should Not Be Taught Yet

Avoid full formal debate, policy proposal, complex rhetorical appeals, advanced fallacies, long research papers, and mature invective/satire as models.
