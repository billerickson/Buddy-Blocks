# Logic 1 Level Blueprint

Track: Logic  
Level: Level 1  
Source: Accepted research brief at `research/logic-1/01-research-brief.md`

## 1. Level Mission

Logic 1 builds the first durable habits of careful reasoning for beginning upper-elementary students. Students learn to tell statements from other sentence jobs, identify claims and reasons, decide whether support is relevant, draw only conclusions that follow from given information, and notice common weak reasoning patterns in plain language. By the end of the level, students should not be memorizing fallacy names or using logic as a debate weapon; they should be able to slow down, name the reasoning move, use evidence, avoid overclaiming, and explain a choice in one clear sentence across reading, discussion, science, math, civics, and everyday decisions.

## 2. Durable Student Outcomes

By the end of Logic 1, students should be able to:

1. Distinguish statements from questions, commands, exclamations, opinions, reasons, and simple claims.
2. Identify a claim, a reason, and a conclusion in short age-appropriate arguments.
3. Distinguish examples and evidence from reasons, while recognizing how each can support a claim.
4. Decide whether a reason or piece of evidence is relevant to a specific claim.
5. Draw supported conclusions from short texts, scenarios, rules, or data without adding unsupported guesses.
6. Interpret simple if-then rules, cause/effect claims, categories, and all/some/none statements.
7. Recognize weak reasoning patterns in plain language: unrelated reason, too little evidence, circular explanation, either/or thinking, broad always/never claim, and personal attack.
8. Use core logic vocabulary accurately: statement, claim, reason, evidence, example, conclusion, support, relevant, clue, because, if, then, cause, effect, all, some, none, always, never.
9. Explain a reasoning choice with a short selected explanation, sentence frame, or brief constructed response.
10. Transfer careful-thinking routines to new reading, academic, civic, and everyday contexts.

## 3. Concept And Skill Progression

The level begins with sentence and idea sorting. Students first learn that not every sentence does the same job: a statement can be true or false, a question asks for information, a command tells someone to do something, an exclamation shows strong feeling, and an opinion tells what someone thinks or likes. This opening work gives students language for later argument parts.

Students then learn a tiny argument structure: a claim says what someone wants others to accept, and a reason tells why. Because-language is used as a bridge, but students also learn that the word `because` does not automatically make a reason useful. They practice separating a claim from a reason and building a short claim-plus-reason argument.

After students can identify argument parts, the level turns to support quality. Students distinguish examples, evidence, and reasons in short contexts, then judge whether support is relevant to the exact claim. They learn that a reason can be true but still not help, and that one example may be useful without proving a broad claim.

The middle of the level develops conclusion habits. Students practice naming what information is given, what conclusion follows, and what would only be a guess. They use clues from short passages, simple rules, tables, or everyday situations and explain the clue that supports the conclusion.

Students then meet common reasoning forms that appear across subjects: if-then rules, cause/effect claims, categories, and quantifier words such as all, some, none, always, and never. These are kept concrete and visual. Students follow rules, sort examples, avoid reversing if-then statements, and distinguish cause from merely happening first.

The final third introduces weak reasoning patterns with plain-language labels and diagnostic contexts. Students learn to notice when support is unrelated, too small, circular, unfairly either/or, personally attacking, or too broad for the evidence. The level ends with mixed transfer tasks where students identify the claim, evaluate support, draw a supported conclusion, and choose a careful explanation in reading, science, math, civics, and everyday settings.

## 4. Vocabulary, Representation, Model, Text, And Form Progression

Early vocabulary should stay close to ordinary language: statement, question, command, opinion, true, false, think, like, reason, because, claim, support. Representations should include sentence cards, two-column sorts, simple claim/reason frames, and short familiar examples.

Support vocabulary should add evidence, example, clue, relevant, unrelated, prove, show, and enough. Representations should progress from single-sentence arguments to short two- or three-sentence scenarios, small evidence tables, picture-free text snippets, and match tasks that connect claims to reasons or evidence.

Conclusion vocabulary should add conclusion, given information, follow, guess, infer, and explain. Models should include clue-to-conclusion arrows, "we know / we do not know" charts, and sentence frames such as "This follows because ___" or "That is only a guess because ___."

Conditional, causal, and category vocabulary should include if, then, rule, condition, result, cause, effect, happened before, category, belongs, all, some, none, always, and never. Representations should include concrete rules, short event chains, category lists, simple set descriptions in words, and comparison tables.

Weak-reasoning vocabulary should remain plain-language and useful: unrelated reason, too little evidence, too broad, circular reason, either-or choice, personal attack, fair, and careful claim. Students should not memorize formal Latin fallacy names. Text forms should grow from one-sentence examples to short mixed passages, but reading load should remain controlled so the reasoning task is the main challenge.

## 5. Fluency Expectations

Logic 1 fluency is not speed debate or rapid fallacy naming. Students should become fluent with recurring reasoning routines: find the claim, find the reason, ask whether the support is relevant, identify what information is given, decide what conclusion follows, and explain the choice briefly. They should also retrieve core vocabulary accurately enough that terms such as claim, reason, evidence, conclusion, relevant, if, then, all, some, and none do not create extra cognitive load.

Short spaced review should build quick recognition of argument parts and common weak reasoning patterns, but fluency evidence should include accuracy, transfer, and explanation. A fast answer that labels the wrong reasoning move is not mastery.

## 6. Conceptual Understanding Expectations

Students should understand that logic evaluates the connection between ideas, not whether they like the speaker or agree with the opinion. A good reason supports a specific claim; evidence and examples help only when they are relevant and strong enough for the claim being made.

Students should understand that conclusions must stay inside the information given. A conclusion can be interesting and still unsupported if it adds facts not in the text, rule, or data. Students should also understand that cause/effect claims need more support than "this happened first," and that broad words such as all, none, always, and never require especially careful evidence.

Students should understand common weak reasoning patterns as fixable thinking problems. The goal is to improve reasoning by asking, "What is wrong with this support?" or "What would make the claim more careful?" rather than using labels to end discussion.

## 7. Application And Transfer Expectations

Students should apply Logic 1 habits to short reading passages, science observations, math explanations, classroom discussions, civic rules, product claims, and everyday choices. They should be able to identify a claim in a text, choose the best supporting evidence, reject an unrelated reason, distinguish a supported conclusion from a guess, and revise a broad claim into a more careful one.

Transfer should be visible when the surface topic changes but the reasoning structure remains familiar. A student might recognize that a playground rule uses if-then reasoning, a science observation does not yet prove cause, a book paragraph supports one conclusion but not another, or a class decision should use relevant and fair reasons rather than personal attacks.

## 8. Review And Spacing Plan

Review should be cumulative from the first unit. Claim, reason, because, and relevant should reappear in every later unit. Support-quality lessons should continue asking students to identify claim and reason, while conclusion lessons should continue asking whether evidence is relevant before drawing an inference.

Conditional, causal, category, and quantifier lessons should space earlier vocabulary by embedding claims and reasons inside rules and examples. For example, a cause/effect prompt can ask whether the evidence supports the claim, and an all/some/none prompt can ask whether the conclusion is too broad.

Weak-reasoning lessons should interleave earlier structures rather than become a separate list of labels. Each weak pattern should be revisited after a delay in mixed contexts. Final review should ask students to choose among multiple reasoning checks: identify the argument part, test relevance, draw a conclusion, follow a rule, judge a causal claim, or name a weak reasoning pattern.

Feedback during review should name the reasoning move and the fix: "This reason is unrelated," "This conclusion adds a guess," "One example is too little for always," or "Attack the claim with evidence, not the person."

## 9. Mastery Evidence For The End Of The Level

End-of-level mastery should be evidenced by mixed reasoning tasks, not by isolated vocabulary recall alone. A student who has mastered Logic 1 can read a short argument, identify its claim and reason, decide whether the support is relevant, and explain the decision in a sentence or selected explanation.

The student can draw a supported conclusion from a short passage, simple rule, category statement, or data table while rejecting unsupported guesses. The student can interpret concrete if-then rules, avoid simple cause/effect confusion, use all/some/none carefully, and revise broad claims into more cautious statements when the evidence is limited.

The strongest mastery evidence includes transfer: the student uses the same careful-thinking routine in a new subject context, notices a weak reasoning pattern without being told which one to look for, and gives feedback that improves the reasoning instead of merely naming a mistake.
