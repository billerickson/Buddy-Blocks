# Logic 1 Course Map

Track: Logic  
Level: Level 1  
Sources: Accepted research brief at `research/logic-1/01-research-brief.md` and level blueprint at `research/logic-1/02-level-blueprint.md`

## Course Design Notes

Logic 1 moves from naming sentence jobs and argument parts into judging support, drawing careful conclusions, following common reasoning forms, recognizing weak reasoning, and transferring the whole routine across subjects. The map is intentionally cumulative: each unit keeps claim, reason, evidence, relevance, and conclusion language alive while adding one new reasoning demand. Lessons should stay compact, use familiar topics, avoid symbolic logic, and treat weak reasoning as a fixable thinking problem rather than a label to memorize.

## Unit 1: Claims, Reasons, And Tiny Arguments

### Unit Purpose

Introduce the basic language students need before they can evaluate reasoning. Students distinguish statements from other sentence jobs, separate claims from reasons, understand because-language, and build very short arguments with a claim and a relevant reason.

### Student Outcomes

- Distinguish statements, questions, commands, exclamations, opinions, claims, and reasons in simple contexts.
- Identify the claim in a short argument.
- Identify the reason that supports a claim.
- Explain how because-language connects a claim and a reason.
- Build or complete a tiny argument using one claim and one reason.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Statement, question, command, exclamation, opinion, claim, reason, because, support, true, false, agree, disagree, argument, tiny argument.

### Prerequisites

Students should be able to read short sentences, recognize everyday opinions, give a simple reason for a preference, and understand ordinary because-language.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This opening unit supplies the vocabulary and argument-part structure needed for every later unit. Unit 2 evaluates the quality of support, Unit 3 draws conclusions from information, and Units 4-6 repeatedly ask students to identify claims and reasons inside richer reasoning contexts.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Treating every opinion as a supported claim.
- Thinking a reason is good just because the student likes it.
- Assuming the word `because` automatically makes a sentence logical.
- Confusing the claim with the reason.
- Treating disagreement as proof that a claim is false.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Claim, reason, and because-language should reappear in every later unit. Early review should ask students to sort sentence jobs and locate claim/reason pairs before judging support quality.

## Unit 2: Support, Evidence, And Relevance

### Unit Purpose

Teach the first major quality check in Logic 1: support must fit the exact claim. Students distinguish reasons, examples, and evidence, then decide whether support is relevant, sufficient for a careful claim, or in need of improvement.

### Student Outcomes

- Distinguish reasons, examples, and evidence in short arguments.
- Decide whether a reason or evidence is relevant to a specific claim.
- Explain why true information may still be unrelated to a claim.
- Recognize that one example may support a small claim but not prove a broad one.
- Improve weak support by choosing a more relevant reason or narrower claim.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Support, reason, evidence, example, clue, relevant, unrelated, fit, enough, prove, show, broad claim, careful claim.

### Prerequisites

Students need Unit 1 vocabulary for claim, reason, because, and support, plus the ability to read short scenarios and compare two pieces of information.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit extends claim/reason identification into support evaluation. It prepares Unit 3 by teaching students to use relevant evidence before drawing conclusions. It also prepares Unit 5, where weak reasoning patterns often involve unrelated or too-small support.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A true sentence is automatically good support.
- An example always proves the whole claim.
- Interesting details are evidence even when they do not fit the claim.
- More words mean stronger support.
- Support is strong if it matches the topic, even if it does not prove the exact claim.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Continue quick review of claim and reason in every lesson. Interleave relevance decisions with example/evidence sorting so students do not treat evidence as a magic word.

## Unit 3: Conclusions From Clues

### Unit Purpose

Build the habit of drawing only conclusions that follow from given information. Students identify what is known, choose supported conclusions, reject unsupported guesses, and explain which clue makes a conclusion reasonable.

### Student Outcomes

- Name facts or information that are given in a short text or scenario.
- Distinguish a supported conclusion from an interesting guess.
- Choose the conclusion that follows from clues in a passage, table, or rule.
- Explain which clue supports a conclusion.
- Avoid adding facts that were not given.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Conclusion, clue, given information, follow, support, guess, infer, know, cannot know yet, text evidence, data, rule.

### Prerequisites

Students need claim/reason/evidence vocabulary, relevance judgment, short-passage reading, and comfort using one or two clues to answer a question.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit depends on Unit 2 relevance work: only relevant information can support a conclusion. It prepares Unit 4 if-then, cause, and category reasoning by giving students a general "what follows?" routine.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A conclusion can include any detail that sounds possible.
- The most interesting answer is the best conclusion.
- A clue supports every related idea.
- Prior knowledge can replace the information in the prompt.
- If a conclusion is true in real life, it must be supported by this text.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Review should mix claim/reason/support checks with conclusion tasks. Include delayed practice where students must say "we cannot know yet" when evidence is missing.

## Unit 4: Rules, Causes, And Categories

### Unit Purpose

Introduce common reasoning forms that students meet across subjects: if-then rules, cause/effect claims, categories, and all/some/none statements. Students learn to follow the information given without reversing rules, confusing sequence with cause, or overreading broad words.

### Student Outcomes

- Interpret simple if-then rules by identifying the condition and result.
- Decide what follows when a rule applies and what does not follow when it does not apply.
- Distinguish cause/effect support from merely happening before.
- Evaluate whether a cause claim has enough evidence.
- Interpret all, some, and none statements using concrete categories.
- Sort examples by category and avoid conclusions that are too broad.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

If, then, rule, condition, result, follows, cause, effect, happened before, evidence, category, group, belongs, all, some, none, always, never.

### Prerequisites

Students need supported-conclusion habits, relevance checks, short scenario reading, basic category sorting, and ordinary understanding of if, then, all, some, and not.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit broadens the reasoning forms used in Units 1-3. It prepares Unit 5 because weak reasoning often misuses cause, all/some/none, always/never, or either/or thinking.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Reversing an if-then rule.
- Thinking a rule says what happens when the condition is not met.
- Assuming that if one event happened first, it caused the next event.
- Treating one example as proof of all.
- Reading some as all, or none as not many.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Interleave rule-following with conclusion language. Review relevance by asking whether the evidence actually supports a cause claim. Revisit all/some/none during later weak-reasoning lessons.

## Unit 5: Weak Reasoning And Careful Fixes

### Unit Purpose

Help students recognize common reasoning errors in plain language and improve them. The unit focuses on diagnosing the support problem and choosing a careful fix, not memorizing formal fallacy labels.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify unrelated reasons, too-little evidence, circular reasons, either/or traps, personal attacks, and too-broad always/never claims.
- Explain what is wrong with a weak reasoning move in student-friendly language.
- Choose a better reason, narrower claim, or fairer response.
- Distinguish criticizing a claim from attacking a person.
- Apply weak-reasoning checks to short academic and everyday contexts.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Weak reasoning, unrelated reason, too little evidence, too broad, always, never, circular reason, same idea again, either-or, choice, personal attack, fair reason, careful fix.

### Prerequisites

Students need claim/reason/evidence/relevance vocabulary, conclusion habits, if-then and cause/effect basics, and careful all/some/none language.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit synthesizes the prior units by showing how arguments break when earlier reasoning moves are misused. It prepares Unit 6 transfer by giving students a checklist for revising reasoning across subject areas.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Naming a problem is enough; no fix is needed.
- The loudest or funniest criticism is the strongest reason.
- A personal attack disproves a claim.
- Two choices are the only choices if they are the only ones named.
- Always and never are safe when one example feels strong.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Each lesson should retrieve earlier terms. For example, an unrelated reason lesson should still identify claim and reason, and a too-broad lesson should revisit evidence, example, all, some, always, and never. Later mixed practice should present multiple weak patterns together.

## Unit 6: Careful Thinking Across Subjects

### Unit Purpose

Consolidate Logic 1 by applying careful-thinking routines in reading, science, civic, math, and everyday contexts. Students choose the right reasoning check, explain briefly, and revise claims or support when needed.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify claims, reasons, evidence, and conclusions in mixed subject contexts.
- Choose relevant evidence from a short reading passage.
- Judge whether a science cause claim has enough support.
- Evaluate fair and relevant reasons in simple civic or classroom decisions.
- Use a final reasoning routine: find the claim, check the support, draw only what follows, and explain the choice.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Review vocabulary from the level: claim, reason, evidence, example, conclusion, relevant, clue, because, if, then, cause, effect, all, some, none, always, never, weak reasoning, careful claim, fair reason.

### Prerequisites

Students need the full Logic 1 toolkit from Units 1-5 and enough reading stamina for brief passages with one clear reasoning task.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This capstone unit reviews the full level and prepares later logic study. Future levels can add stronger argument evaluation, validity/soundness language, probability/statistical reasoning, more formal fallacy names, and longer argumentative texts.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Using only the most recent reasoning check for every problem.
- Choosing evidence because it is interesting rather than relevant.
- Treating science sequence as cause without evidence.
- Believing fair reasoning means everyone agrees.
- Explaining a conclusion without naming the clue that supports it.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use cumulative mixed practice. Lessons should vary the subject context and the reasoning move so students practice selecting the tool, not just repeating a recent pattern.
