# Logic - Level 1 Lesson Briefs

Sources: accepted level blueprint, course map, and unit design briefs in `research/logic-1/`.

## Unit 1: Claims, Reasons, And Tiny Arguments

### Lesson 1: Statements, Questions, And Commands

- **Teaching goal:** Help students sort common sentence jobs before they analyze arguments.
- **Student outcome:** The student can distinguish statements, questions, commands, and exclamations in familiar contexts.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A sentence's job can be identified by what it does: tells, asks, directs, or shows strong feeling.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Reading short sentences, basic punctuation awareness, and oral classroom directions.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating every sentence as a statement; relying only on punctuation; confusing commands with questions when both mention an action.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given short sentences, the student correctly identifies the sentence job and explains one clue.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for sentence-job recognition; `match-pairs` for sentence-to-job sorting; `fill-blank` for supported vocabulary recall.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show four sentences about the same topic, such as a book, and ask what each one is trying to do.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short concrete sentences; avoid sarcasm, rhetorical questions, or embedded clauses.

### Lesson 2: Opinion, Claim, Or Feeling?

- **Teaching goal:** Separate personal preference and feeling from claims that can be supported.
- **Student outcome:** The student can classify a sentence as an opinion/preference, a feeling, or a supportable claim.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A claim says something someone can try to support; a preference or feeling may be real without needing proof.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic emotion and preference language, statements from Lesson 1, and familiar school examples.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Thinking every opinion needs evidence; treating feelings as false because they are not provable; assuming "I think" always makes a sentence weak.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can sort examples and explain whether the sentence can be supported with reasons or evidence.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for classification; `match-pairs` for examples to categories; `constructed-response` for a brief explanation after models.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Contrast "I feel nervous," "I like graphic novels," and "Graphic novels can help readers understand a story."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep topics low-stakes and familiar; avoid controversial values claims.

### Lesson 3: Reasons With Because

- **Teaching goal:** Teach because-language as a connection between a claim and a reason.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify the reason after because and decide whether it is trying to support the claim.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** In a tiny argument, the claim tells what someone thinks, and the because-part tells why.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Opinions and claims from Lesson 2, ordinary because-language, and sentence-level reading.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Believing any because-part is automatically a good reason; identifying the whole sentence as the claim; reversing claim and reason.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student highlights or selects the claim and reason in a because sentence.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for claim/reason vocabulary; `multiple-choice` for identifying the because-part; `multi-blank-cloze` for completing a claim/reason frame.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use "We should water the plant because the soil is dry" and label both parts aloud.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one because clause at a time; avoid sentences with multiple reasons.

### Lesson 4: Find The Claim And Reason

- **Teaching goal:** Identify claim and reason when they appear in either order across one or two sentences.
- **Student outcome:** The student can locate the claim and the supporting reason in a short argument.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Claim and reason are roles, not fixed positions in a sentence.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Claim/reason definitions, because-language, and short two-sentence reading.
- **Likely misconceptions:** The first sentence is always the claim; the sentence with because is always fully the reason; every detail in the argument is support.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student correctly labels claim and reason in varied short arguments.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for claim selection; `match-pairs` for claims to reasons; `passage-question` for short two-sentence arguments.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Present two versions of the same argument with claim first and reason first.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep arguments to one claim and one reason; avoid extra evidence details for now.

### Lesson 5: Build A Tiny Argument

- **Teaching goal:** Have students complete or produce a tiny argument with one claim and one relevant reason.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose or write a reason that supports a simple claim.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A tiny argument is stronger when the reason answers "Why should I accept this claim?"
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Claim/reason identification, because-language, and familiar opinion contexts.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Giving a new claim instead of a reason; giving a reason that is only about personal preference; adding a detail that does not support the claim.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects a relevant reason and completes a claim-because-reason sentence.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for best reason; `multi-blank-cloze` for argument frames; `constructed-response` for a short reason with a checklist.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Model revising "This game is good because I like it" into a reason such as "it teaches players to plan ahead."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use sentence frames and familiar topics; require one reason, not a paragraph.

## Unit 2: Support, Evidence, And Relevance

### Lesson 1: Reasons, Examples, And Evidence

- **Teaching goal:** Distinguish common support types students will meet in short arguments.
- **Student outcome:** The student can tell whether a support sentence is a reason, an example, or evidence.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Reasons explain why, examples show one case, and evidence gives information that can support a claim.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Claim and reason language from Unit 1, short scenario reading, and example vocabulary.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Calling every support sentence evidence; treating examples and reasons as identical; thinking evidence must always be a number.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student sorts support sentences and explains the clue in the support type.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for type definitions; `multiple-choice` for support-type recognition; `passage-question` for short argument contexts.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use one claim about a class garden and compare a reason, an example, and a simple observation.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep support sentences short; introduce one type contrast at a time before mixing all three.

### Lesson 2: Support That Fits The Claim

- **Teaching goal:** Teach relevance as fit between a specific claim and support.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose support that directly helps prove a claim.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Relevant support points at the exact claim, not just the same broad topic.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Claim identification, support types, and ability to compare two ideas.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Choosing support because it shares a keyword; accepting interesting facts; confusing a related topic with direct support.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a claim, the student chooses the support sentence that best fits and rejects unrelated options.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for best support; `match-pairs` for claim-to-support matches; `constructed-response` for one-sentence why.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Ask whether "The library has many books" supports "The library is quiet after lunch."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep claims narrow; use choices that are plausible but clearly different in relevance.

### Lesson 3: True But Unrelated

- **Teaching goal:** Show that true or interesting information can fail to support the claim.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a true detail that is unrelated to the claim's support need.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A support sentence must be both accurate and relevant.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Relevance from Lesson 2, claim/support matching, and familiar factual statements.
- **Likely misconceptions:** True means useful; topic match means support; longer explanations are stronger.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects true-but-unrelated details and explains why they do not help the claim.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for identifying unrelated support; `passage-question` for context-based relevance; `constructed-response` for a brief rejection reason.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a claim about a backpack being heavy and a true detail about its color to show the difference.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use concrete objects and events; do not require students to fact-check outside knowledge.

### Lesson 4: One Example Is Not Always Enough

- **Teaching goal:** Help students notice when evidence is too small for a broad claim.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether one example supports a small careful claim or overreaches to all/always language.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** One example can show that something can happen, but it usually cannot prove what always happens.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Examples and evidence, broad versus narrow claims, and basic all/always language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** One memorable example proves a whole category; always and never are harmless emphasis words; a claim feels stronger when it is broader.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses a careful claim that matches limited evidence.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for careful claim choice; `fill-blank` for all/some vocabulary; `passage-question` for example-to-claim reasoning.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Contrast "This apple is sour" with "All apples are sour."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar categories; avoid statistical language beyond one, some, many, all.

### Lesson 5: Make The Support Stronger

- **Teaching goal:** Have students improve weak support by choosing a better reason, evidence, or narrower claim.
- **Student outcome:** The student can revise a claim-support pair so the support fits better.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Weak support can often be fixed by changing the reason or making the claim more careful.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Support types, relevance, true-but-unrelated details, and one-example limits.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Fixing means adding more words; the claim should never change; any stronger-sounding sentence is an improvement.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects a better support sentence or revises a broad claim into a careful claim.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for strongest revision; `error-correction` for replacing weak support; `constructed-response` for a short careful fix.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Model two fixes: improve the reason, then narrow the claim to match the evidence.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Provide clear revision options before asking for production; keep written responses to one sentence.

## Unit 3: Conclusions From Clues

### Lesson 1: What Information Is Given?

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to separate given information from outside ideas.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify what a short prompt actually says and what it does not say.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Conclusions must start with the information given, not with guesses added from outside.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Short passage reading, relevance, and simple fact/detail identification.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Adding likely background information; assuming unstated details; treating "not given" as false.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student sorts statements into "given" and "not given" based on a short context.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for stated information; `match-pairs` for given/not-given sorting; `multiple-choice` for detail checks.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Read a three-sentence scenario and ask, "What do we know for sure?"
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep passages brief and concrete; avoid ambiguous pronouns.

### Lesson 2: Supported Conclusion Or Guess?

- **Teaching goal:** Distinguish conclusions that follow from clues from guesses that add information.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose a supported conclusion and reject unsupported guesses.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A conclusion follows when the clue points to it; a guess may be possible but not supported.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Given information, relevance, and basic conclusion vocabulary.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Possible means supported; the most detailed answer is best; personal experience can fill evidence gaps.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student correctly classifies choices as supported conclusion or guess.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for supported conclusion; `match-pairs` for conclusion/guess sorting; `constructed-response` for a brief "not enough information" explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a lunchbox scenario where one conclusion follows and one tempting guess does not.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Limit to one or two clues; use explicit "can know" and "cannot know yet" language.

### Lesson 3: Conclusions From Short Passages

- **Teaching goal:** Apply supported-conclusion reasoning to short reading passages.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use passage clues to answer an inference question.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A passage can support a conclusion even when it does not say the exact answer word-for-word.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Given information, conclusion versus guess, and relevance.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Only exact repeated words count as evidence; any related passage detail supports the answer; outside knowledge is stronger than text clues.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses a conclusion and identifies the passage clue that supports it.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for inference; `multiple-choice` for clue choice; `constructed-response` for one-sentence explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Model underlining a clue before choosing the answer.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use two- to four-sentence passages with familiar settings; avoid figurative language as the main challenge.

### Lesson 4: Explain The Clue

- **Teaching goal:** Make students' inference reasoning visible by naming the clue.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain why a conclusion follows using a sentence frame.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A clear explanation connects conclusion and clue: "I know ___ because ___."
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Supported conclusions, clue identification, and claim/reason sentence frames.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Repeating the answer instead of naming evidence; giving a personal reason; using a clue that supports a different conclusion.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student gives or chooses an explanation that names the supporting clue.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for best explanation; `multi-blank-cloze` for explanation frames; `constructed-response` for short supported explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare a strong explanation with one that only says, "I just know."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep constructed responses short; provide frames and sample wording.

## Unit 4: Rules, Causes, And Categories

### Lesson 1: If-Then Rules

- **Teaching goal:** Identify the condition and result in simple if-then rules.
- **Student outcome:** The student can label what must happen first and what follows in an if-then rule.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** In an if-then rule, the if-part is the condition and the then-part is the result.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Sentence parts, conclusion language, and everyday rule-following.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating both parts as equal statements; missing the condition; reversing if and then.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies condition and result in classroom, game, or everyday rules.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for condition/result identification; `match-pairs` for rule parts; `fill-blank` for vocabulary.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use "If the light is red, then cars stop" and mark the two parts.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one condition and one result; avoid unless, except, or multiple conditions.

### Lesson 2: What Follows From A Rule?

- **Teaching goal:** Decide what can be concluded when an if-then rule applies or does not apply.
- **Student outcome:** The student can apply a simple rule without reversing it or adding unstated conclusions.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** If the condition happens, the result follows; if the condition is not shown, the rule may not tell us anything.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Condition/result identification and supported conclusion routines.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Reversing the rule; assuming not seeing the condition proves the result is false; adding a result that the rule did not name.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses what follows from a rule in short scenarios.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for rule scenarios; `multiple-choice` for what follows; `constructed-response` for a brief rule explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare "If it rains, the game moves inside" with "The game is inside" and ask what we can and cannot know.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep rule language explicit; do not introduce formal logic notation.

### Lesson 3: Cause Or Just Happened First?

- **Teaching goal:** Distinguish cause/effect reasoning from sequence alone.
- **Student outcome:** The student can tell when evidence shows only that one event happened before another, not that it caused it.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A cause makes something happen; happening first is only a time clue unless more evidence connects the events.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Given information, supported conclusions, and everyday event sequencing.
- **Likely misconceptions:** First event caused second event; repeated sequence always proves cause; a guess about cause is a conclusion.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies whether a scenario supports cause or only sequence.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for cause/sequence classification; `passage-question` for event contexts; `match-pairs` for cause/effect versus before/after.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Contrast "I put ice in the drink and it got cold" with "I wore blue socks and it rained."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar physical causes before social or emotional causes.

### Lesson 4: Evidence For Cause Claims

- **Teaching goal:** Decide what evidence would make a cause claim stronger.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose evidence that directly connects a possible cause to an effect.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A strong cause claim needs evidence that the cause helped produce the effect, not just a nearby fact.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Cause versus sequence, relevance, evidence, and careful conclusions.
- **Likely misconceptions:** More details equal better evidence; one coincidental example proves cause; unrelated facts about the effect support the cause.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects the evidence that best supports or tests a cause claim.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for best evidence; `passage-question` for science-like observations; `constructed-response` for what evidence is still needed.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use plant growth examples where water, light, and time are controlled in simple language.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid formal experimental design terms; use concrete comparisons.

### Lesson 5: All, Some, And None

- **Teaching goal:** Use all, some, and none accurately in concrete category statements.
- **Student outcome:** The student can interpret and choose statements using all, some, and none without overreading them.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** All means every one, some means at least one but not necessarily all, and none means zero.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic categories, comparison language, and broad/careful claims.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Some means most; none means not many; all can be proven by one example.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses the correct quantifier for a simple group and interprets quantifier statements.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for quantifier choice; `multiple-choice` for interpretation; `match-pairs` for word to meaning.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a visible set of classroom objects or named items and make all/some/none statements about it.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep sets small and explicit; avoid double negatives.

### Lesson 6: Sort The Category Carefully

- **Teaching goal:** Apply category and quantifier reasoning to sorting and avoiding overclaims.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether an item belongs in a category and make a careful statement about the group.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Category conclusions depend on the rule for belonging and the examples actually shown.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** All/some/none, examples, evidence limits, and simple sorting.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Sorting by appearance instead of rule; treating one example as all; ignoring items that do not fit the claim.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student sorts examples and chooses a careful all/some/none statement.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for item-to-category; `multiple-choice` for careful conclusion; `constructed-response` for explaining the category rule.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show items that belong for different reasons and ask students to name the sorting rule first.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use concrete categories and short labels; avoid categories requiring specialized background knowledge.

## Unit 5: Weak Reasoning And Careful Fixes

### Lesson 1: Unrelated Reasons

- **Teaching goal:** Recognize a reason that does not support the claim.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify unrelated reasons and choose a reason that fits better.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A reason can sound positive or true but still fail to answer why the claim should be accepted.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Claim/reason identification, relevance, and true-but-unrelated support.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Any positive detail supports a positive claim; sharing a topic is enough; more enthusiasm means stronger reasoning.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the unrelated reason and selects a relevant replacement.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for unrelated reason; `match-pairs` for claim-to-reason; `error-correction` for replacing support.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Reuse a familiar claim and show one fitting reason beside one unrelated true detail.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep claims specific; avoid choices that require outside fact knowledge.

### Lesson 2: Too Little Evidence

- **Teaching goal:** Recognize claims that are too broad for the amount of evidence.
- **Student outcome:** The student can revise all/always/never claims when evidence is limited.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Small evidence supports small claims; broad claims need broad evidence.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Examples and evidence, one-example limits, all/some/none, and careful claim language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** One example proves all; always and never add strength; the most confident claim is the best claim.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses a narrower claim that matches the evidence.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for careful revision; `fill-blank` for quantifier choice; `constructed-response` for a one-sentence careful claim.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Move from "One student liked the book" to "At least one student liked the book."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid percentages and probability; use one, two, some, many, all.

### Lesson 3: Circular Reasons

- **Teaching goal:** Identify reasons that merely repeat the claim.
- **Student outcome:** The student can tell when a reason does not add new support because it says the same idea again.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A circular reason goes in a loop: the claim is "true" because the claim is "true."
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Claim/reason roles, support, and paraphrase awareness.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Rewording the claim counts as support; repeated words make an idea stronger; "because it is" answers why.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies circular reasons and chooses a reason that adds new support.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for circular reason recognition; `match-pairs` for claim to non-circular reason; `constructed-response` for replacing the loop.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use "This rule is fair because it is the fairest rule" and ask what new support was added.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use obvious paraphrases before subtle ones; avoid abstract claims.

### Lesson 4: Either-Or Traps

- **Teaching goal:** Recognize when an argument unfairly presents only two choices.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify an either-or trap and name another possible option when appropriate.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Some choices really have two options, but weak reasoning pretends there are only two when more may exist.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Careful conclusions, category reasoning, and fair decision contexts.
- **Likely misconceptions:** If two choices are named, they are the only choices; middle options are not allowed; choosing another option avoids reasoning.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student distinguishes a real two-choice situation from a false either-or and chooses a fairer statement.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for either-or identification; `constructed-response` for another option; `passage-question` for decision scenarios.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Contrast "The light is on or off" with "We must play soccer or do no exercise."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar decisions; do not require policy debate or complex tradeoffs.

### Lesson 5: Personal Attacks Are Not Reasons

- **Teaching goal:** Distinguish criticizing a claim from attacking the person who made it.
- **Student outcome:** The student can reject personal attacks as irrelevant and choose a fair reason about the claim.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A person's traits do not automatically prove or disprove a claim; reasons should address the idea.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Relevance, fair reasons, claim/reason identification, and simple classroom discussion norms.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Disliking a speaker disproves the claim; embarrassment is a strong argument; a mean comment counts as evidence.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies a personal attack and replaces it with a claim-focused reason or question.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for personal-attack recognition; `dialogue-builder` for fair response choice; `error-correction` for revising an unfair response.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a low-stakes classroom suggestion and compare "That idea will not work because..." with "You are bad at this."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid sensitive identity traits; keep scenarios school-safe and not emotionally loaded.

### Lesson 6: Fix The Weak Reasoning

- **Teaching goal:** Mix weak-reasoning patterns and focus on choosing a careful fix.
- **Student outcome:** The student can diagnose the support problem and improve the argument with a better reason, narrower claim, or fairer response.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Good reasoning improves when we name the problem and choose a fix that matches it.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** All Unit 5 weak patterns plus Units 1-4 reasoning vocabulary.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Every weak argument has the same fix; labels are enough; adding any evidence solves every problem.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the weak pattern in mixed examples and chooses an appropriate revision.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for weak pattern to fix; `multiple-choice` for best revision; `constructed-response` for a short improved argument.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a three-step chart: claim, problem, careful fix.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Present one weak pattern per item before mixed review; keep constructed responses to one or two sentences.

## Unit 6: Careful Thinking Across Subjects

### Lesson 1: Logic In Reading

- **Teaching goal:** Apply claim, evidence, and conclusion routines to a short reading passage.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a passage-supported claim or conclusion and choose relevant text evidence.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Reading evidence should answer the exact question or support the exact claim.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Claim/evidence/relevance and supported-conclusion routines.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Choosing a sentence because it is interesting; using outside knowledge instead of passage evidence; repeating the claim as evidence.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects the relevant evidence and explains how it supports the claim or conclusion.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for text evidence; `multiple-choice` for best support; `constructed-response` for a short explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Model reading the question first, then hunting for the sentence that actually supports the answer.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep passages to one short paragraph; avoid unfamiliar vocabulary unrelated to logic.

### Lesson 2: Logic In Science

- **Teaching goal:** Apply observation, cause/effect, and careful-conclusion routines to simple science contexts.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether observations support a cause claim or only a cautious conclusion.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Science reasoning depends on observations that fit the claim and avoid guessing beyond the evidence.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Cause/effect, evidence relevance, supported conclusions, and enough/too-little evidence.
- **Likely misconceptions:** One observation proves cause; sequence proves cause; a science-sounding claim is automatically strong.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses the careful conclusion and identifies what evidence would strengthen a cause claim.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for observation scenarios; `multiple-choice` for careful conclusion; `constructed-response` for next evidence needed.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use plant, shadow, magnet, or weather observations with one clear evidence gap.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid technical science terms; make the reasoning task more important than science background.

### Lesson 3: Logic In Fair Decisions

- **Teaching goal:** Apply relevance and fairness to classroom or civic decision-making.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose fair, relevant reasons and reject personal attacks or unrelated preferences.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A fair decision should use reasons that fit the question and treat people respectfully.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Relevant support, fair reasons, personal attacks, and either-or traps.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Fair means everyone gets their first choice; popular preferences are automatically best; attacking a person helps decide the issue.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects the fair and relevant reason for a decision and explains why another response is weak.
- **Recommended question types:** `dialogue-builder` for fair response; `multiple-choice` for relevant reason; `constructed-response` for one-sentence decision support.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a class choice such as where to place supplies or how to schedule shared equipment.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep civic contexts local and noncontroversial; avoid emotional debates.

### Lesson 4: Choose, Check, Explain

- **Teaching goal:** Consolidate the Logic 1 routine in mixed contexts where students choose the reasoning check.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether to check claim/reason, relevance, conclusion, rule, cause, category, or weak reasoning, then explain the choice.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Careful thinkers choose the reasoning tool that fits the problem, check the support, and explain with evidence.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Full Logic 1 vocabulary and routines from Units 1-5.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Using the newest tool for every problem; naming a term without applying it; explaining with a guess instead of support.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student solves mixed reasoning items and gives a brief explanation tied to the correct clue or support.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for choosing the reasoning check; `match-pairs` for problem to tool; `constructed-response` for final explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Present a checklist: What is the claim? What support fits? What follows? Is the reasoning weak? What is the clue?
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short mixed prompts; keep final written explanations brief and scaffolded.
