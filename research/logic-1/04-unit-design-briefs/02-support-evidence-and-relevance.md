# Unit 2: Support, Evidence, And Relevance

Track: Logic  
Level: Level 1  
Stage: Unit Design Brief  
Source: `research/logic-1/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn to evaluate whether support fits the exact claim. By the end of the unit, they can distinguish reasons, examples, and evidence; identify relevant support; reject true-but-unrelated information; notice when one example is too little for a broad claim; and choose a stronger support move.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit follows argument-part identification because relevance is the first practical quality check for young reasoners. It gives students a concrete question to ask before later inference and weak-reasoning work: "Does this support actually help this claim?"

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: support quality, relevance, evidence, examples, broad versus careful claims.
- Vocabulary: reason, example, evidence, clue, relevant, unrelated, support, prove, show, enough, broad claim, careful claim.
- Representations and models: claim-support matching, evidence tables, "fits / does not fit" sorts, one-example claim ladders, claim revision frames.
- Texts and forms: short classroom arguments, book recommendations, weather observations, simple survey or data snippets, and everyday decision scenarios.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need Unit 1 knowledge of claim, reason, because, and support. They should be able to compare two short sentences and notice whether they are about the same exact idea.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- True information is always good support.
- A related topic is the same as relevant support.
- One example proves an always or never claim.
- A longer reason is stronger than a shorter relevant reason.
- Evidence and example mean the same thing in every context.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Start by distinguishing support types so students do not use reason, example, and evidence as interchangeable labels. Then teach relevance directly with clear matches. After that, separate true-but-unrelated details from actual support. The fourth lesson narrows broad claims based on limited examples, and the final lesson asks students to improve support by choosing a better reason or revising the claim.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Every lesson should begin by identifying the claim and possible reason from Unit 1. Because-language should be revisited as useful only when the second idea actually supports the first.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares Unit 3 by making evidence and clues useful for supported conclusions. It prepares Unit 5 by introducing two major weak-reasoning sources: unrelated support and too little evidence.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Sort reasons, examples, and evidence.
- Match claims with relevant support.
- Identify true information that does not support the claim.
- Choose whether an example supports a small claim or overreaches.
- Improve support by selecting a more relevant reason or narrower claim.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Reasons, Examples, And Evidence**: Distinguish the main types of support students will see.
2. **Support That Fits The Claim**: Choose support that matches the exact claim.
3. **True But Unrelated**: Reject information that is true or interesting but not relevant.
4. **One Example Is Not Always Enough**: Notice when a claim is too broad for the evidence.
5. **Make The Support Stronger**: Improve a weak claim-support pair.
