# Unit 5: Weak Reasoning And Careful Fixes

Track: Logic  
Level: Level 1  
Stage: Unit Design Brief  
Source: `research/logic-1/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn to recognize and fix common weak reasoning patterns in plain language. By the end of the unit, they can identify unrelated reasons, too little evidence, circular reasons, either-or traps, personal attacks, and overbroad always/never claims, then choose a fairer or more careful revision.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit belongs after students have worked with claims, support, conclusions, rules, causes, and categories. Weak reasoning is meaningful only when students can explain what went wrong with the support or conclusion.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: reasoning diagnosis, support problems, claim revision, fair criticism, careful fixes.
- Vocabulary: weak reasoning, unrelated reason, too little evidence, too broad, always, never, circular reason, same idea again, either-or, personal attack, fair reason, careful claim.
- Representations and models: weak-pattern sort cards, claim/support/fix charts, broad-to-careful claim ladders, fair response frames.
- Texts and forms: short student arguments, product-like claims, classroom decisions, reading discussions, simple civic choices, and science observations.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need vocabulary and routines from Units 1-4, especially claim/reason identification, relevance, evidence amount, supported conclusions, cause/effect caution, and all/some/none precision.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- A label is a complete explanation.
- The funniest or loudest response is the strongest criticism.
- Attacking a person disproves the person's claim.
- If two choices are named, no other choices exist.
- Always and never are fine when one example is memorable.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

The unit starts with unrelated reasons and too-little evidence because these directly extend Unit 2. It then introduces circular reasons, which require students to notice that a reason merely repeats the claim. Either-or traps come next as a common overnarrowing of choices. Personal attacks are saved until students can distinguish criticizing a claim from criticizing a person. The final lesson mixes patterns and emphasizes choosing a careful fix.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Every lesson should identify the claim and support before naming the weak reasoning. Too-little-evidence lessons should review example/evidence and all/some/none. Personal attack lessons should review relevance and fair reasons.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares the final transfer unit by giving students a practical reasoning checklist. It also prepares later logic levels where formal fallacy names, stronger argument evaluation, and longer texts can be introduced.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify a weak reasoning pattern in a short argument.
- Explain the support problem in plain language.
- Choose a better reason or narrower claim.
- Reject personal attacks as irrelevant to the claim.
- Distinguish a real choice from a false either-or.
- Apply multiple weak-reasoning checks in mixed practice.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Unrelated Reasons**: Notice reasons that do not support the claim.
2. **Too Little Evidence**: Avoid broad claims from one or two examples.
3. **Circular Reasons**: Recognize reasons that only repeat the claim.
4. **Either-Or Traps**: Notice when more than two choices may exist.
5. **Personal Attacks Are Not Reasons**: Criticize the claim with evidence, not the person.
6. **Fix The Weak Reasoning**: Choose a better reason, narrower claim, or fairer response.
