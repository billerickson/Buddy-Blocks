# Unit 6: Careful Thinking Across Subjects

Track: Logic  
Level: Level 1  
Stage: Unit Design Brief  
Source: `research/logic-1/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students consolidate Logic 1 by applying careful-thinking routines across subject areas and everyday contexts. By the end of the unit, they can choose the reasoning check that fits a situation, use relevant evidence, avoid unsupported conclusions, and explain the choice briefly.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit is the capstone because the level goal is transfer, not isolated vocabulary. Students now need mixed practice where the prompt does not announce whether it is about claims, relevance, conclusions, causes, categories, or weak reasoning.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: transfer, mixed reasoning checks, evidence selection, fair decision-making, final explanation.
- Vocabulary: review of claim, reason, evidence, example, conclusion, relevant, clue, because, if, then, cause, effect, all, some, none, weak reasoning, careful claim, fair reason.
- Representations and models: mixed reasoning checklists, passage evidence charts, cause evidence tables, fair-decision charts, choose-check-explain frames.
- Texts and forms: short reading passages, science observations, class or community decisions, math explanation snippets, and everyday choices.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need the full Logic 1 progression from Units 1-5 and enough reading stamina for compact passages with one or two reasoning decisions.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- The most recent reasoning tool is the right one for every problem.
- Evidence is best when it is interesting, not when it is relevant.
- A cause claim is proven by sequence alone.
- Fair reasoning means everyone gets what they want.
- An explanation is complete without naming the supporting clue or reason.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with reading because students already have evidence routines from ELA. Move to science, where cause claims and observation evidence matter. Then use fair decisions to apply relevance and personal-attack cautions in civic or classroom contexts. End with a mixed choose-check-explain lesson that asks students to select the reasoning tool themselves.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

This unit reviews every earlier unit. Each lesson should include at least two reasoning moves, such as identifying a claim and choosing relevant evidence, or judging cause and explaining what conclusion follows.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares future logic study by making careful reasoning portable. Later levels can introduce stronger argument chains, deductive validity, probability, source credibility, formal fallacy names, and longer written explanations.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify claims and evidence in a short reading passage.
- Evaluate whether observations support a science cause claim.
- Choose fair, relevant reasons for simple civic or classroom decisions.
- Select the appropriate reasoning check in a mixed prompt.
- Explain a final reasoning choice with a clue, reason, or evidence.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Logic In Reading**: Use text evidence to support a claim or conclusion.
2. **Logic In Science**: Evaluate observations, causes, and careful conclusions.
3. **Logic In Fair Decisions**: Use relevant and fair reasons in classroom or civic choices.
4. **Choose, Check, Explain**: Apply the full Logic 1 routine in mixed contexts.
