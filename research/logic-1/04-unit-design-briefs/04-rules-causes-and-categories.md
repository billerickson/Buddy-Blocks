# Unit 4: Rules, Causes, And Categories

Track: Logic  
Level: Level 1  
Stage: Unit Design Brief  
Source: `research/logic-1/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn to reason carefully with common forms: if-then rules, cause/effect claims, and category statements using all, some, and none. By the end of the unit, they can follow rules, decide what follows, evaluate simple cause support, and sort category statements without overreading them.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit comes after conclusions because if-then, cause, and category work all ask students to decide what follows from information. It broadens the contexts before the weak-reasoning unit asks students to diagnose how these forms can go wrong.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: condition and result, rule application, cause/effect support, sequence versus cause, concrete category reasoning, quantifier precision.
- Vocabulary: if, then, rule, condition, result, follows, cause, effect, happened before, evidence, category, belongs, all, some, none, always, never.
- Representations and models: rule cards, condition/result charts, event chains, cause evidence tables, category lists, all/some/none sorts.
- Texts and forms: classroom rules, game rules, simple science observations, everyday event sequences, and concrete category examples.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need supported-conclusion routines, relevance checks, short scenario reading, everyday category sorting, and basic understanding of if, then, all, some, and not.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Reversing an if-then rule.
- Assuming a rule says what happens when the condition is not met.
- Treating "happened first" as proof of cause.
- Thinking one piece of weak evidence proves a cause.
- Reading some as all or none as not many.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

The unit begins with simple if-then language, then asks what follows when a rule applies or does not apply. It then shifts into cause/effect, first separating cause from sequence and then asking what evidence a cause claim needs. The final two lessons focus on all/some/none and category sorting because quantifier precision supports later weak-reasoning checks.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Rule and cause lessons should review conclusion language: what follows, what cannot be known yet, and which evidence is relevant. Category lessons should review broad versus careful claims from Unit 2.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares Unit 5 by surfacing common reasoning errors: reversed rules, unsupported cause claims, broad all/always claims, and either/or overreading. It prepares Unit 6 science and civic transfer contexts.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify the condition and result in an if-then rule.
- Decide what follows when a rule applies.
- Distinguish cause/effect from sequence alone.
- Choose evidence that would support a cause claim.
- Interpret all, some, and none statements accurately.
- Sort examples by category without making too-broad conclusions.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **If-Then Rules**: Identify the condition and result in simple rules.
2. **What Follows From A Rule?**: Decide what can be concluded when a rule applies.
3. **Cause Or Just Happened First?**: Separate cause/effect from sequence.
4. **Evidence For Cause Claims**: Decide what evidence would make a cause claim stronger.
5. **All, Some, And None**: Use quantifier words carefully in concrete categories.
6. **Sort The Category Carefully**: Sort examples and avoid category overclaims.
