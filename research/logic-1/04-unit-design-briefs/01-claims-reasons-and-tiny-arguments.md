# Unit 1: Claims, Reasons, And Tiny Arguments

Track: Logic  
Level: Level 1  
Stage: Unit Design Brief  
Source: `research/logic-1/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn the basic language of beginning argument analysis. By the end of the unit, they can distinguish common sentence jobs, identify a claim and a reason in a short argument, understand how because-language connects ideas, and build a tiny argument with one claim and one relevant reason.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit comes first because later reasoning checks depend on stable argument-part vocabulary. Students cannot judge relevance, evidence, conclusions, or weak reasoning until they can slow down and name what a sentence is doing.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: sentence jobs, claims, reasons, because connections, support, tiny argument structure.
- Vocabulary: statement, question, command, exclamation, opinion, claim, reason, because, support, agree, disagree, true, false.
- Representations and models: sentence-sort cards, claim/reason T-charts, because frames, two-sentence arguments, "Claim: ___ / Reason: ___" organizers.
- Texts and forms: familiar school, hobby, food, game, reading, and family contexts; one-sentence and two-sentence prompts.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should be able to read short sentences, recognize questions by meaning and punctuation, understand simple preferences and opinions, and give a basic reason in everyday conversation.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Every opinion is already a reasoned claim.
- A sentence with `because` is automatically logical.
- The reason is the same thing as the claim.
- A reason is good if the student likes it.
- Disagreeing with a claim proves the claim is wrong.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin by sorting sentence jobs so students can tell what kind of idea they are handling. Then separate opinions, claims, and feelings to keep "I like it" from becoming the whole argument. Next, introduce because-language as a bridge from claim to reason. The final two lessons ask students to identify claim/reason pairs and build a tiny argument from controlled parts.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

This is the first unit, so review draws from everyday language rather than prior Logic 1 content. Prompts should activate familiar school discussion routines: asking a question, telling a fact, giving an opinion, and saying why.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares Unit 2 by making support visible. It prepares Unit 3 by establishing the difference between what someone claims and what information supports the claim. Units 4-6 continue to use claim, reason, because, and support as the recurring anchor language.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Sort statements, questions, commands, and exclamations.
- Distinguish opinions, claims, and feelings in simple sentences.
- Identify the claim in a two-sentence argument.
- Identify the reason in a because sentence or short argument.
- Complete a tiny argument with a relevant reason.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Statements, Questions, And Commands**: Sort common sentence jobs before formal argument work begins.
2. **Opinion, Claim, Or Feeling?**: Distinguish personal preference and feeling from a claim someone can support.
3. **Reasons With Because**: Use because-language to connect a claim with a reason.
4. **Find The Claim And Reason**: Identify claim and reason in short familiar arguments.
5. **Build A Tiny Argument**: Complete or produce a one-claim, one-reason argument.
