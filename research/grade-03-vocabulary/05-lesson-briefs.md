# Vocabulary - Grade 3 Lesson Briefs

Sources: accepted level blueprint, course map, and unit design briefs in `research/grade-03-vocabulary/`.

## Unit 1: Word-Learning Routine And Context Clues

### Lesson 1: Stop And Try The Word-Learning Routine

- **Teaching goal:** Establish a repeatable routine for approaching an unknown word instead of guessing immediately.
- **Student outcome:** The student can put the routine steps in order and use them to make a supported meaning guess.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Pause, reread, look for clues, try a meaning, and check whether the meaning fits.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic sentence reading, rereading, familiar classroom vocabulary, and willingness to explain a clue orally or in writing.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Guessing from the first nearby word; skipping rereading; treating the first guess as final; thinking a reference must be used before looking at context.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a short sentence with one target word, the student chooses a likely meaning and identifies one clue or step from the routine.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for routine sequence; `multiple-choice` for choosing a useful first step; `passage-question` for guided context use; `constructed-response` for a brief "I know because..." explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Model meeting the word `drenched` in "After the rainstorm, my drenched socks dripped on the floor" and think aloud through the routine.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use single sentences or two-sentence contexts; keep target words decodable or supported by context; ask for one clue at a time.

### Lesson 2: Definition And Restatement Clues

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to recognize direct definitions and restatements as context clues.
- **Student outcome:** The student can infer a word meaning when the sentence explains the word in nearby language.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Some sentences define or restate an unfamiliar word with clue words such as means, or, called, and that is.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Unit 1 routine, basic sentence punctuation, and ability to match two phrases with similar meaning.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Ignoring the explanation after a comma or dash; choosing a nearby but unrelated word; thinking restatement clues must use exactly the same words.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects a meaning and points to the definition or restatement phrase that supports it.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for meaning recognition; `fill-blank` for clue-signal words; `match-pairs` for word-to-restatement matches; `passage-question` for short sentence contexts.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show a sentence such as "A habitat, or place where an animal lives, gives an animal food and shelter" and underline the explaining words.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep restatements close to the target word; avoid long appositives with several commas.

### Lesson 3: Example Clues

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to use examples to infer a category or general meaning.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify examples in a sentence and use them to choose a reasonable meaning.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Example clues name members of a group, often after words such as such as, including, and for example.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Category language, Unit 1 routine, and basic understanding that examples can reveal a larger idea.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating one example as the full definition; missing example clue words; choosing a meaning that fits only one example but not all.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student infers the category meaning from two or more examples and names at least one example clue.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for choosing category meaning; `match-pairs` for clue words to examples; `fill-blank` for example signal words; `constructed-response` for naming a clue.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a familiar sentence such as "Utensils, such as forks, spoons, and chopsticks, help people eat."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use concrete examples before abstract academic examples; keep lists to two or three items.

### Lesson 4: Contrast Clues

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to use contrast words and opposite ideas as clues to unfamiliar meanings.
- **Student outcome:** The student can infer a word meaning when a sentence contrasts it with a familiar word or idea.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Words such as but, unlike, instead of, however, and although can signal a contrast clue.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Antonym and opposite readiness, Unit 1 routine, and ability to notice signal words.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Ignoring contrast words; choosing the same meaning as the familiar word; treating every but sentence as a direct opposite.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a contrast sentence, the student chooses a meaning that fits the opposite or different idea and identifies the signal word.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for meaning choice; `fill-blank` for signal words; `match-pairs` for contrast clue to meaning; `passage-question` for short contexts.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare "The first trail was smooth, but the second trail was rugged" and ask how but changes the meaning.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep contrast sentences short; use familiar comparison words before however and although.

### Lesson 5: Cause And Effect Clues In Short Passages

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to use cause/effect relationships across short passages to infer word meaning.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use one or two nearby sentences to explain why a word means what it does.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A cause or result can show what an unfamiliar word means, even when the clue is not in the same sentence.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Unit 1 routine, basic cause/effect language, and experience reading two- to four-sentence passages.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Looking only inside the target sentence; confusing cause with effect; choosing a meaning that fits one word but not the whole passage.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects a meaning, identifies whether the clue is a cause or effect, and checks the meaning against the passage.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for short passage inference; `multiple-choice` for clue identification; `order-items` for cause/effect sequence; `constructed-response` for a brief explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a short science-style passage where an observable result reveals the target word, such as a plant `wilted` after days without water.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep passages under four sentences; include one target word and one clear relationship.

## Unit 2: Reference Tools And Meaning Fit

### Lesson 1: Read A Glossary Entry

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students how a glossary entry gives a word, definition, and sometimes an example connected to a text.
- **Student outcome:** The student can read a simple glossary entry and match the entry to a context sentence.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A glossary helps readers understand important words from a specific text.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Alphabet order readiness, Unit 1 context routine, and familiarity with textbooks or informational text features.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating a glossary as an unrelated word list; skipping the example sentence; assuming a glossary word cannot appear in other subjects.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the definition and uses it to explain the word in a short context.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for word-to-definition; `multiple-choice` for entry part identification; `passage-question` for context plus entry; `text-input` for a supported term.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show a mini-glossary for a short animal passage and connect one entry to a sentence in the passage.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use two- or three-entry glossaries; keep definitions short and concrete.

### Lesson 2: Choose The Definition That Fits

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to choose among multiple definitions by using the sentence context.
- **Student outcome:** The student can select the definition that fits and reject a tempting definition that does not.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A word may have more than one definition, so the sentence decides which one fits.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Unit 1 context clues and Lesson 1 glossary-entry reading.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Picking the first definition; choosing the meaning they already know; ignoring the sentence after reading the entry.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a context and a simplified entry with two or three meanings, the student chooses the fitting definition and explains the clue.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for definition choice; `passage-question` for context-with-entry prompts; `match-pairs` for sentence to definition; `constructed-response` for rejecting a non-fit.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use `bark` or `seal` to show how the same word can fit different sentences.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Limit entries to two or three definitions; avoid obscure meanings.

### Lesson 3: Use Syllables And Pronunciation Supports

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students that syllable and pronunciation supports help them read a word, while definitions explain meaning.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use syllable breaks or simple pronunciation cues without confusing them with the word's meaning.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Reference entries may show how to say a word and how many syllables it has; those supports help readers access the definition and context.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic syllable awareness, glossary/dictionary entry parts, and decoding of multisyllabic Grade 3 words with support.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating pronunciation as a definition; thinking a longer word must mean a harder idea; ignoring syllable breaks when decoding.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies which entry part helps with saying the word and which part gives meaning.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for entry-part function; `fill-blank` for syllable count or word part; `match-pairs` for entry part to purpose; `text-input` for a supported syllable count.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show a friendly entry for `delicate` with syllable breaks and ask which part helps you say it and which helps you understand it.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use common academic or content words; avoid advanced pronunciation symbols.

### Lesson 4: Decide When To Check A Reference

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to decide when context is enough and when a reference check is useful.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose a strategy path for a word: use context, check a reference, or use both.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Strong readers use context first, then verify when the meaning is uncertain or when a word has more than one possible meaning.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Unit 1 context routine and Unit 2 entry-reading skills.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Looking up every unknown word before thinking; refusing to check a reference when context is weak; treating reference use as a sign of failure.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student explains why a word should or should not be checked in a reference and uses the reference when needed.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for strategy choice; `passage-question` for context strength; `order-items` for context-then-reference routine; `constructed-response` for a one-sentence decision.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare one sentence with a clear restatement clue and another where the context only hints at the meaning.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short, high-support contexts; make the decision about strategy, not independent dictionary navigation.

## Unit 3: Word Relationships And Precision

### Lesson 1: Sort Words Into Categories

- **Teaching goal:** Strengthen category reasoning so students group words by meaning rather than by surface features.
- **Student outcome:** The student can sort words into categories and identify a word that does not belong.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A category names a group; members belong because they share a meaningful feature.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Common category words, oral explanation, and Unit 1 context habits.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Sorting by first letter or sound; treating a category label as a member; ignoring a word's meaning.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student sorts a short word set and explains the shared meaning.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for word to category; `multiple-choice` for odd-one-out; `fill-blank` for category labels; `constructed-response` for a brief reason.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Sort words such as robin, eagle, salmon, and sparrow and discuss why one does not belong.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar concrete categories before abstract academic groups.

### Lesson 2: Synonyms In Context

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to choose synonyms that preserve sentence meaning.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a same-or-similar meaning word that fits a sentence.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Synonyms have similar meanings, but the sentence still decides whether the replacement fits.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic synonym readiness, context clues, and category reasoning.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Believing every synonym pair is interchangeable; matching by topic instead of meaning; choosing a word with the wrong feeling or strength.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student replaces a word with a fitting synonym and explains why it keeps the sentence meaning.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for synonym choice; `match-pairs` for word pairs; `fill-blank` for sentence replacement; `passage-question` for context-based synonym work.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare `happy`, `glad`, `proud`, and `excited` in one sentence frame to show similar but not identical meanings.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep target words common; avoid advanced connotation labels.

### Lesson 3: Antonyms And Contrasts

- **Teaching goal:** Teach antonyms as meaning opposites or strong contrasts, especially in sentence context.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify an antonym or contrast word and use it to understand a sentence.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Antonyms help show how ideas differ, but the best opposite depends on the word's meaning in context.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic opposites, Unit 1 contrast clues, and synonym awareness.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Choosing any different word as an antonym; missing degree words such as tiny versus enormous; treating contrast as always exact opposite.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses an antonym that fits the sentence and identifies the contrast clue.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for antonym choice; `match-pairs` for word to opposite; `fill-blank` for contrast sentences; `constructed-response` for explaining a contrast.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a sentence pair such as "The pond was shallow near the edge but deep in the middle."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar adjective and verb pairs before abstract words.

### Lesson 4: Shades Of Meaning

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to compare related words by strength, feeling, or precision.
- **Student outcome:** The student can order or choose near-synonyms based on intensity and sentence fit.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Related words can have different strengths, such as warm, hot, and scorching.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Synonyms, antonyms, context clues, and basic comparison language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Thinking all synonyms are equal; always choosing the strongest word; ignoring clues about feeling or amount.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student orders a word line or chooses a word whose strength fits the sentence.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for intensity scales; `multiple-choice` for best-fit word; `fill-blank` for sentence choice; `constructed-response` for explaining word strength.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Build a word line with `walk`, `hurry`, `race` and act or imagine the difference.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use three- or four-word scales; keep contexts brief and concrete.

### Lesson 5: Revise Vague Words

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to replace vague words with more precise vocabulary in short sentences.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose or produce a clearer word that improves a sentence while keeping the meaning accurate.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Precise words help readers picture the action, feeling, size, or idea more clearly.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Categories, synonyms, shades of meaning, and sentence reading.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Using a fancy word even if it does not fit; changing the meaning while revising; thinking precise words must be long.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student revises a sentence with a precise word and explains what became clearer.
- **Recommended question types:** `error-correction` for replacing vague word choices; `multiple-choice` for best revision; `text-input` for supported production; `constructed-response` for a short revised sentence.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare "The dog went across the yard" with "The dog dashed across the yard" and ask what changed.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Require one-word revisions or one short sentence; do not assess spelling beyond recognizable vocabulary.

## Unit 4: Word Parts And Word Families

### Lesson 1: Base Words And Meaning Parts

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to identify a base word and use it as the meaning center of a longer word.
- **Student outcome:** The student can find the base word in familiar words and explain the related meaning.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A base word can stand alone and carries the main meaning in many longer words.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Compound-word and ending readiness, Unit 3 related-word reasoning, and context checking.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating any small spelling chunk as a base word; ignoring spelling changes; assuming look-alike words are related.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies a base word and uses it to infer or explain a derived word's meaning.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for base-word identification; `match-pairs` for derived word to base meaning; `fill-blank` for word-part boxes; `constructed-response` for explaining the base.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show `teach`, `teacher`, and `teaching` and ask what meaning stays the same.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use transparent, familiar examples before words with spelling changes.

### Lesson 2: Prefixes That Change Meaning

- **Teaching goal:** Teach common prefixes as meaning parts at the beginning of words.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use `un-`, `re-`, `pre-`, and `mis-` to infer meaning in familiar and near-transfer words.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A prefix is added before a base word and can change the word's meaning.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Base words, context clues, and basic negation or repetition language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Seeing a prefix where none exists; assuming a prefix gives the whole definition; confusing `pre-` and `re-`; missing context fit.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student explains what the prefix contributes and chooses a meaning that fits the sentence.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for prefix to meaning; `multiple-choice` for word meaning; `fill-blank` for prefix choice; `passage-question` for context application.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Build a quick chart: `un-` not, `re-` again, `pre-` before, `mis-` wrong or badly.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid advanced etymology; use familiar base words such as pack, read, view, spell, and place.

### Lesson 3: Suffixes That Change Meaning Or Use

- **Teaching goal:** Teach common suffixes as endings that can change meaning or how a word is used in a sentence.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use suffixes such as `-ful`, `-less`, `-ly`, `-er`, `-est`, and `-ness` to infer meaning or word use.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A suffix is added after a base word and may show amount, quality, comparison, or manner.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Base words, prefix lesson, adjectives/adverbs as informal word-use ideas, and context checking.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Thinking all suffixes do the same job; treating `-er` only as a person ending; ignoring how the word works in the sentence.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects or explains the meaning of a suffixed word in context.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for suffix to meaning; `multiple-choice` for derived word meaning; `fill-blank` for best suffixed word; `text-input` for a supported word completion.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare `careful`, `careless`, and `carefully` in short sentences.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one suffix focus at a time inside a question; avoid dense grammar terminology.

### Lesson 4: Word Families And Known Roots

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to connect related words by shared base or known root meaning.
- **Student outcome:** The student can group word-family members and infer a related word's meaning from the family.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Words in a family share a meaningful part, but they must also make sense in context.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Base words, prefixes, suffixes, categories, and semantic relationships.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Grouping words by spelling only; assuming every look-alike word is related; missing meaning changes when affixes are added.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student groups related words and explains the shared meaning.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for word to family meaning; `multiple-choice` for related word choice; `fill-blank` for family word in context; `constructed-response` for explaining shared meaning.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Build a family web around `act`: act, action, active, actor, and react, using context for each.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use a few high-utility known roots or bases; do not turn this into formal root memorization.

### Lesson 5: Check Word Parts With Context

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to test morphology guesses against sentence meaning and reject false word-part clues.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide when a word-part clue helps and when context shows the guess is wrong or incomplete.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Word parts are evidence, but the final meaning must fit the sentence.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Prefixes, suffixes, base words, context clues, and definition-fit habits.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Trusting a word part even when the sentence disagrees; splitting words into false parts; using the prefix meaning but ignoring the base.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student explains a word-part guess and checks it with context or rejects a false word-part claim.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for word-part-in-context prompts; `multiple-choice` for valid/invalid word-part reasoning; `error-correction` for fixing a wrong explanation; `constructed-response` for a short check.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare a helpful word such as `reread` with a false split such as `corner` not meaning `corn + er`.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep false-part examples clear and not tricky; target the reasoning, not memorizing exceptions.

## Unit 5: Multiple Meanings, Homophones, And Homographs

### Lesson 1: Multiple-Meaning Words In Context

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to choose the intended meaning of a word with more than one meaning.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use sentence clues to select the fitting meaning of a multiple-meaning word.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** The same word can mean different things in different sentences; context decides.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Context clues, definition fit, and common multiple-meaning words from earlier grades.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Choosing the most familiar meaning; assuming a word has one real meaning; ignoring clues after the target word.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches context sentences to correct definitions and explains the clue.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for sentence to meaning; `multiple-choice` for meaning choice; `passage-question` for context inference; `constructed-response` for clue explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use `light` in "not heavy" and "lamp light" contexts to show how sentence fit changes meaning.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar words with two clear meanings; avoid more than one confusing word per prompt.

### Lesson 2: Homophones Need Sentence Clues

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to use sentence meaning and spelling to choose among homophones.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose the sound-alike word that fits the sentence.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Homophones sound alike but have different spellings and meanings; the sentence tells which word is needed.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Common homophone exposure, context clues, and sentence reading.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Choosing by sound only; ignoring spelling; using the more common word even when the meaning does not fit.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects the correct homophone and explains the meaning clue.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for homophone choice; `multiple-choice` for meaning clue; `error-correction` for fixing wrong homophones; `match-pairs` for word to meaning.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Contrast `there`, `their`, and `they're` only if the sentence clues are strong; otherwise use simpler pairs such as `see/sea` and `hear/here`.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use common Grade 3 homophones; do not make spelling the only evidence without meaning.

### Lesson 3: Homographs Can Shift Meaning

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students that some same-spelled words can have different meanings, and sometimes different pronunciations.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use context to interpret a common homograph.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Homographs share spelling, but context reveals meaning and may suggest how the word is pronounced.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Multiple-meaning words, context clues, and reference-entry awareness.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Same spelling means same meaning; pronunciation cannot change; sentence meaning is not needed if the word is familiar.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses the intended meaning of a homograph and identifies the context clue.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for meaning choice; `passage-question` for context; `match-pairs` for sentence to definition; `constructed-response` for a short explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use accessible examples such as `wind` as moving air and `wind` a string, or `bow` on a gift and `bow` after a performance.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid requiring phonetic notation; focus on meaning in context.

### Lesson 4: Use Entries To Confirm Word Meanings

- **Teaching goal:** Combine context and reference entries to confirm confusing word meanings.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose the fitting definition from an entry after reading a sentence or short passage.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Context gives a meaning guess, and the entry confirms which definition fits.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Unit 2 reference skills, multiple meanings, homophones, homographs, and context clues.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Reading the entry without returning to context; picking a known definition; confusing homophones with multiple definitions of one word.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student uses both context and entry information to select and justify a meaning.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for context plus entry; `multiple-choice` for fitting definition; `match-pairs` for entry definition to sentence; `constructed-response` for verification explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show a two-definition entry for `scale` and ask which definition fits a map sentence.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Limit entries to two or three definitions; keep contexts brief and explicit.

## Unit 6: Literal, Nonliteral, And Author-Craft Language

### Lesson 1: Literal Or Nonliteral?

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to decide whether words mean exactly what they say or have a phrase-level meaning.
- **Student outcome:** The student can classify a phrase as literal or nonliteral and explain the meaning in simple words.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Literal language means the words say exactly what happened; nonliteral language means the phrase has a meaning beyond the exact words.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Multiple meanings, context clues, and oral familiarity with common expressions.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Nonliteral means meaningless; every silly sentence is an idiom; literal meaning always fits if every word is known.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies literal/nonliteral use and states the intended meaning.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for classification; `match-pairs` for phrase to meaning; `passage-question` for context; `constructed-response` for simple explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare "The backpack is heavy" with "The homework was a heavy load" in a supported conversation.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use common expressions and short contexts; avoid advanced figurative labels.

### Lesson 2: Idioms In Short Contexts

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to infer common idiom meanings from context.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose the meaning of an idiom and reject a literal distractor.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** An idiom is a phrase whose meaning usually cannot be solved word by word.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Literal/nonliteral contrast and context-clue routine.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Defining each word separately; choosing the funniest literal picture; missing emotional or situation clues.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student interprets an idiom in a short context and identifies the clue that supports the meaning.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for context-based idioms; `multiple-choice` for meaning choice; `match-pairs` for idiom to meaning; `constructed-response` for a short explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use idioms such as `spill the beans`, `under the weather`, and `hit the books` in brief realistic scenes.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use common idioms; keep cultural assumptions minimal and context-rich.

### Lesson 3: Simple Sayings And Their Lessons

- **Teaching goal:** Introduce simple sayings as whole ideas that often teach a lesson or give advice.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose the meaning of a common saying from a short situation.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A saying can express a general idea that applies to a situation.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Idiom interpretation, context clues, and basic moral/lesson language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Taking the saying word by word; treating every saying as a command; choosing a meaning unrelated to the situation.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches a saying to a fitting situation and explains the lesson in simple words.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for saying to meaning; `passage-question` for situation-based interpretation; `multiple-choice` for lesson choice; `constructed-response` for a brief explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use "Practice makes progress" or "Many hands make light work" in a short class situation.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep sayings common or self-explaining in context; do not require formal adage/proverb labels as mastery.

### Lesson 4: Images, Sounds, And Similes

- **Teaching goal:** Teach simple author-craft words that help students describe how language makes meaning.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify an image, sound word, simple simile, or dialogue cue when it supports understanding.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Writers choose words to help readers picture, hear, compare, or follow what characters say.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Literal/nonliteral language, comparison words, and short literary sentence reading.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Every comparison with like or as is meaningful; sound words are only fun noises; dialogue is separate from meaning.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student names the craft feature and explains what it helps the reader understand.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for craft feature; `match-pairs` for feature to example; `passage-question` for short literary snippets; `constructed-response` for explaining effect.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare "The leaves moved" with "The leaves whispered like paper" and ask what the reader can picture or hear.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one craft feature per item; avoid metaphor/personification terminology.

## Unit 7: Academic And Content-Area Vocabulary

### Lesson 1: Words For Thinking And Explaining

- **Teaching goal:** Teach high-utility academic task words students see in directions and questions.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose what a direction asks them to do when it uses words such as identify, describe, explain, compare, contrast, summarize, and conclude.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Academic verbs tell the kind of thinking or response a question expects.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Context clues, synonym/precision work, and classroom direction experience.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating all direction words as "answer"; confusing compare with contrast; giving a long explanation when asked to identify.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches an academic verb to the action it requires and selects a fitting response.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for verb to action; `multiple-choice` for response choice; `fill-blank` for sentence stems; `constructed-response` for a short academic response.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Display a few question stems and ask what each one is really asking the student to do.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use school-familiar tasks; assess the vocabulary of the direction, not deep content knowledge.

### Lesson 2: Evidence, Support, And Inference

- **Teaching goal:** Teach academic words that connect a claim or answer to text clues.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use words such as infer, evidence, support, clue, and because in short reading responses.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Evidence is the clue that supports an answer; an inference uses clues plus what the reader knows.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Context-clue evidence, basic reading comprehension, and sentence frames.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Evidence is any sentence nearby; inference means wild guess; support means agreeing without a clue.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses evidence for a simple inference and completes a sentence frame explaining it.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for short text evidence; `multiple-choice` for evidence choice; `multi-blank-cloze` for sentence stems; `constructed-response` for a short inference explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a two-sentence mini-passage and model "I infer ___ because the text says ___."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep passages very short; make the inference obvious enough that vocabulary is the focus.

### Lesson 3: Text Features: Captions, Diagrams, Maps, And Tables

- **Teaching goal:** Teach vocabulary for common text features and how those features help meaning.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a caption, diagram, map, table, glossary, or timeline and explain what information it gives.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Text features are part of the text and can give clues about word meanings and ideas.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Glossary use, content-area reading exposure, and basic visual-feature awareness.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Skipping text features; confusing table as furniture with table as a data display; treating captions as optional labels only.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches a feature to its purpose and uses feature information to answer a vocabulary question.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for feature to purpose; `multiple-choice` for feature identification; `passage-question` for caption/diagram context; `constructed-response` for one-sentence explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show a simple animal diagram with a caption and ask what each feature tells a reader.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Describe visuals in text rather than relying on unavailable images; keep feature examples simple.

### Lesson 4: Cause, Effect, Sequence, And Classify

- **Teaching goal:** Teach relationship words that organize informational reading and subject-area tasks.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use cause, effect, sequence, classify, and related words to understand short contexts.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Some academic words name relationships among ideas, events, or objects.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Cause/effect clues, category sorting, and sequence language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Confusing cause and effect; thinking sequence means importance; classifying by appearance rather than shared feature.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the relationship word and applies it to a short context or sort.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for sequence; `match-pairs` for term to meaning; `multiple-choice` for cause/effect/classify choice; `passage-question` for informational context.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a seed-growing sequence and ask which sentence names the cause, effect, and order.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar science and classroom examples; avoid multi-step causal chains.

### Lesson 5: Academic Words Across Subjects

- **Teaching goal:** Help students transfer high-utility words across reading, math, science, and social studies contexts.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose and use academic/content words in different subject settings.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A useful academic word may appear in many subjects, but the context shows exactly how it is being used.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Academic task words, content-feature words, multiple meanings, and context clues.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A word has the same subject meaning every time; math and science vocabulary do not connect to reading vocabulary; using the word without sentence fit.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student interprets and uses a high-utility word in at least two subject contexts.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for short subject contexts; `multiple-choice` for meaning transfer; `match-pairs` for word to subject use; `constructed-response` for a short original sentence.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Track a word like `evidence` across a reading question, a science observation, and a social studies source.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep subject knowledge light and familiar; make vocabulary transfer the learning target.

## Unit 8: Cumulative Word-Solving Transfer

### Lesson 1: Choose The Best Word-Solving Strategy

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to decide which vocabulary strategy should lead in a mixed situation.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose a strategy such as context clue, word part, reference check, relationship, or nonliteral interpretation and explain why it fits.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Strong word learners choose the strategy that matches the problem.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Units 1-7 vocabulary strategies and short explanation frames.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Using the newest strategy for every word; choosing a reference check when context is clear; forcing a word-part clue that does not help.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects a strategy and gives a reason tied to the word or context.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for strategy choice; `match-pairs` for problem to strategy; `passage-question` for mixed contexts; `constructed-response` for one-sentence reasoning.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Present three words: one with a clear context clue, one with a prefix, and one with multiple definitions, then compare the best first move.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep each prompt focused on strategy choice; avoid long passages.

### Lesson 2: Combine Clues, Word Parts, And References

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to combine evidence sources when one clue is not enough.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use context plus word parts or a reference entry to choose and verify a meaning.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Sometimes a reader needs more than one kind of evidence before a meaning is secure.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Context clues, morphology, reference entries, and meaning-fit checks.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Stopping after one weak clue; ignoring an entry because a word part seems familiar; choosing a definition that does not fit the sentence.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student explains how two evidence sources work together to support the meaning.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for context-plus-entry tasks; `multiple-choice` for combined evidence; `multi-blank-cloze` for explanation frames; `constructed-response` for short verification.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use `misplace` in a sentence plus a mini-entry to show how prefix and context agree.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Provide all needed evidence in the prompt; do not require independent dictionary searching.

### Lesson 3: Explain Your Evidence

- **Teaching goal:** Strengthen students' ability to explain the evidence behind a vocabulary answer.
- **Student outcome:** The student can write or select a short explanation connecting the meaning to a clue, word part, reference definition, or phrase meaning.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A good vocabulary explanation names the meaning and the evidence that supports it.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** All prior strategy work and sentence frames such as "The clue ___ tells me ___."
- **Likely misconceptions:** Repeating the answer without evidence; naming an unrelated clue; writing a long response that does not explain meaning fit.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses or produces a concise evidence explanation for a vocabulary decision.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for best explanation; `error-correction` for fixing weak explanations; `multi-blank-cloze` for explanation frames; `constructed-response` for short production.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare three explanations for the same word and discuss which one actually proves the meaning.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Require brief explanations; spelling and punctuation should not be scored as the main target.

### Lesson 4: Cumulative Vocabulary Transfer

- **Teaching goal:** Assess and deepen end-of-level vocabulary transfer in mixed new contexts.
- **Student outcome:** The student can solve and use vocabulary across context clues, references, word parts, relationships, multiple meanings, nonliteral language, and academic words.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Vocabulary mastery means applying the right evidence to a new word or phrase and using it clearly.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Complete Grade 3 Vocabulary sequence.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating the unit as separate review questions; choosing familiar meanings without evidence; using a new word in an unclear sentence.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student answers mixed strategy questions and produces at least one clear sentence or explanation using target vocabulary.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for transfer contexts; `multiple-choice` for meaning and strategy; `match-pairs` for mixed review; `constructed-response` and `text-input` for supported production.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Frame the lesson as a word-detective challenge: the student decides which evidence solves each word best.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep passages compact and varied; do not combine too many unfamiliar words in one item.
