# Grade 3 Vocabulary Course Map

Track: Vocabulary
Level: Grade 3
Sources: Accepted research brief at `research/grade-03-vocabulary/01-research-brief.md` and level blueprint at `research/grade-03-vocabulary/02-level-blueprint.md`

## Course Design Notes

This course map treats Grade 3 Vocabulary as a compact academic-language and word-learning track, not a full ELA course. The sequence begins with a repeatable routine and context clues, adds reference-tool verification, deepens semantic precision, then teaches morphology, multiple meanings, figurative language, and cross-subject vocabulary. The final unit asks students to choose among strategies in unfamiliar contexts. Review should be frequent and cumulative: context clues, meaning fit, word parts, reference checks, and short expressive use recur across the level.

## Unit 1: Word-Learning Routine And Context Clues

### Unit Purpose

Establish the core habit of solving unknown words from evidence. Students learn a repeatable routine, then use sentence and short-passage clues to infer meaning and identify the clue that supports the inference.

### Student Outcomes

- Use a word-learning routine: pause, reread, find clues, try a meaning, and check whether it fits.
- Identify definition, example, restatement, contrast, and cause/effect clues in grade-appropriate sentences.
- Use nearby sentences in a short passage to narrow a word's meaning.
- Explain which clue supports a meaning choice.
- Recognize when context gives only partial help and a reference check may be needed.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Unknown word, context, clue, meaning, reread, infer, evidence, definition clue, example clue, restatement, contrast, cause, effect, fits, sentence, passage.

### Prerequisites

Students need Grade 2 readiness with picture and sentence clues, basic oral vocabulary, complete-sentence discussion, and enough decoding fluency to read short Grade 3 sentences with support.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit turns Grade 2 context-clue readiness into a flexible routine. It prepares Unit 2 reference verification, Unit 4 morphology-in-context, Unit 5 multiple-meaning decisions, Unit 6 idiom interpretation, and Unit 8 mixed strategy transfer.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Any nearby word is automatically a clue.
- The first meaning that comes to mind is good enough.
- Context always gives a complete definition.
- A clue has to be in the same sentence.
- Guessing a meaning is the same as proving it from evidence.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

5-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

The routine should reappear in every later unit. Keep context-clue review short but frequent, shifting from clear single-sentence clues to mixed passage clues and strategy choice.

## Unit 2: Reference Tools And Meaning Fit

### Unit Purpose

Teach students to use glossaries, beginning dictionaries, and simplified digital-style entries as verification tools. Students practice selecting the definition that fits a sentence rather than choosing the first or shortest entry.

### Student Outcomes

- Use alphabet order and entry parts to find or read a simple reference entry.
- Interpret glossary and dictionary information, including definition, example, syllables, and pronunciation support when supplied.
- Choose the definition that fits a context sentence.
- Decide when context is enough and when a reference check is useful.
- Explain how a reference entry confirms or corrects a meaning guess.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Dictionary, glossary, entry, definition, example sentence, syllable, pronunciation, guide word, multiple definitions, reference tool, verify, check, fit.

### Prerequisites

Students should know alphabet order, basic print-reference habits, and the Unit 1 word-learning routine. They should be able to compare a sentence meaning to a short definition.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

Unit 2 builds directly on Unit 1 by adding verification. It supports later work with multiple-meaning words, homographs, content-area glossaries, and cumulative transfer where students must decide whether context, word parts, or references give the strongest evidence.

### Misconceptions To Address

- The first definition is always the correct definition.
- A dictionary gives one fixed meaning for every word.
- Looking up a word replaces reading the sentence.
- Pronunciation and syllable information are the same as meaning.
- A glossary is only for hard science words, not useful reading vocabulary.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Interleave reference checks with context-clue prompts. Later units should include simplified entries when a word has multiple meanings or when context narrows but does not fully define the word.

## Unit 3: Word Relationships And Precision

### Unit Purpose

Deepen students' understanding of word relationships so they compare meanings rather than memorize isolated synonym and antonym pairs. Students learn categories, related words, shades of meaning, and precise word choice in sentences.

### Student Outcomes

- Sort words by category, attribute, or relationship.
- Identify synonyms and antonyms in context.
- Compare near-synonyms by intensity, feeling, and sentence fit.
- Choose a precise word to replace a vague word.
- Explain why two related words are not always interchangeable.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Synonym, antonym, category, member, related word, shade of meaning, precise, vague, stronger, milder, intensity, feeling, formal, context, revise.

### Prerequisites

Students need everyday synonym/antonym readiness, category language, Unit 1 clue habits, and the ability to read short sentences with familiar contexts.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit reviews context and reference habits while adding precision. It prepares morphology work by strengthening word-family reasoning, supports multiple-meaning decisions by foregrounding sentence fit, and prepares academic/content vocabulary where words such as compare, contrast, infer, and evidence have precise classroom meanings.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Synonyms can always replace each other.
- An antonym is simply "not the word."
- Words that feel similar always mean the same thing.
- Stronger words are always better words.
- A category label and a member of the category are interchangeable.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

5-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use spaced word lines, category sorts, and sentence revisions. Later units should keep precision active by asking students to choose the word that best fits a context, not just a dictionary definition.

## Unit 4: Word Parts And Word Families

### Unit Purpose

Teach meaning-oriented morphology for Grade 3: base words, common prefixes and suffixes, known roots, and related word families. Students learn to use word parts as evidence while checking context so they avoid false word-part guesses.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify a base word and explain how a prefix or suffix changes meaning in familiar words.
- Use common prefixes such as `un-`, `re-`, `pre-`, and `mis-` to infer meaning.
- Use common suffixes such as `-ful`, `-less`, `-ly`, `-er`, `-est`, and `-ness` to infer meaning or word use.
- Connect related words in a word family by shared meaning.
- Check a word-part guess against the sentence context.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Base word, root, prefix, suffix, affix, word part, word family, syllable, meaning, `un-`, `re-`, `pre-`, `mis-`, `-ful`, `-less`, `-ly`, `-er`, `-est`, `-ness`.

### Prerequisites

Students need familiarity with compound words, inflectional endings, simple prefixes and suffixes from earlier grades, Unit 1 context habits, and Unit 3 related-word reasoning.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit depends on context and semantic precision because word parts must be checked for meaning fit. It prepares students for academic/content words with meaningful parts, later Grade 4-5 morphology, and cumulative transfer tasks where morphology is one strategy among several.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Every beginning or ending is a prefix or suffix.
- Words that look alike must be related.
- A prefix or suffix always changes meaning in exactly the same way.
- The base word can be ignored once a prefix or suffix is found.
- Word-part clues are always stronger than sentence context.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

5-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Space high-utility affixes across later units. Review should mix base-word identification, meaning changes, and context checks rather than isolated affix labels.

## Unit 5: Multiple Meanings, Homophones, And Homographs

### Unit Purpose

Help students resolve words that share spelling or sound while differing in meaning. Students use context, spelling, pronunciation clues when available, and meaning fit to decide what a word means in a sentence.

### Student Outcomes

- Select the meaning of a multiple-meaning word that fits a sentence.
- Use sentence clues to distinguish homophones.
- Use context to interpret homographs with different meanings or pronunciations.
- Explain why a tempting meaning, spelling, or sound-alike word does not fit.
- Use reference entries to confirm context-specific meanings.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Multiple meaning, homophone, homograph, spelling, sound, pronounce, context, definition, meaning fit, sentence clue, entry, same word, different meaning.

### Prerequisites

Students need Unit 1 context reasoning, Unit 2 reference entry habits, and Grade 2 exposure to everyday multiple-meaning words and common homophones.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit applies context, reference, and precision to confusing word forms. It prepares students for nonliteral language, where literal word meanings may not solve a phrase, and for content vocabulary where words such as table, map, and scale can shift by subject.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A word has one real meaning.
- The first definition or most familiar meaning is always right.
- Words that sound the same mean the same thing.
- Spelling does not matter when two words sound alike.
- A homograph's pronunciation always stays the same.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Include multiple-meaning words in later content-area and cumulative lessons. Keep distractors tied to likely wrong meanings, sound-alike spellings, or overreliance on the first definition.

## Unit 6: Literal, Nonliteral, And Author-Craft Language

### Unit Purpose

Introduce common nonliteral language and simple author-craft vocabulary in short, concrete contexts. Students learn that some phrases must be interpreted as whole expressions and that writers choose words to create pictures, sounds, comparisons, and dialogue.

### Student Outcomes

- Distinguish literal and nonliteral meanings in common idioms and sayings.
- Use context to infer the meaning of an idiom or nonliteral phrase.
- Explain why word-by-word interpretation can be misleading.
- Identify simple author-craft language such as image, sound word, simile, phrase, and dialogue when it supports meaning.
- Use a nonliteral or craft word accurately in a short sentence or explanation.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Literal, nonliteral, idiom, saying, phrase, author, craft, image, sound word, simile, compare, like, as, dialogue, meaning, word choice.

### Prerequisites

Students need context reasoning, multiple-meaning awareness, oral language experience with common sayings, and enough sentence comprehension to notice when literal meaning does not fit.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit uses the meaning-fit habits built in Units 1-5 and prepares students to read literary language in later grades. It also reinforces academic words such as compare, image, phrase, and evidence before the content-area vocabulary unit.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Idioms can always be solved by defining each word.
- Nonliteral means nonsense or impossible.
- Every comparison is automatically a simile.
- Author-craft words are labels to memorize rather than tools for explaining meaning.
- A sound word or image is only decorative and does not affect understanding.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Review literal/nonliteral decisions in later mixed passages. Keep examples concrete and common; avoid advanced figurative-language taxonomy reserved for later grades.

## Unit 7: Academic And Content-Area Vocabulary

### Unit Purpose

Build high-utility academic and domain-specific vocabulary that helps students understand directions, text features, and short subject-area passages. Students connect vocabulary to maps, diagrams, captions, tables, science observations, math contexts, and social studies texts.

### Student Outcomes

- Understand and use academic task words such as infer, explain, evidence, compare, contrast, support, classify, summarize, and conclude.
- Interpret text-feature and content words such as diagram, caption, table, map, timeline, glossary, observe, cause, effect, sequence, estimate, and measure.
- Use context and text features together to determine meaning.
- Choose academic sentence stems that fit a reading, science, math, or social studies task.
- Use a new academic or content word in a short oral or written sentence.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Academic word, direction word, infer, evidence, explain, support, compare, contrast, classify, summarize, conclude, observe, cause, effect, sequence, diagram, caption, table, map, timeline, estimate, measure, glossary.

### Prerequisites

Students need the word-learning routine, context clues, reference habits, precision work, and short expressive-use practice from earlier units.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit applies earlier vocabulary strategies to cross-subject reading. It prepares Unit 8 cumulative transfer and later Grade 4-5 work with more complex informational text, morphology, text structure, and academic discussion.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Academic words are hard only because they are rare.
- A word like table or map has the same meaning in every subject.
- Text features are pictures to skip rather than sources of meaning.
- Explain, infer, support, and summarize all ask for the same response.
- Content vocabulary should be memorized separately from the sentence or diagram where it appears.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

5-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Recycle academic words across subjects. Ask students to use sentence stems and cite clues from captions, diagrams, maps, or short passages so vocabulary becomes usable language.

## Unit 8: Cumulative Word-Solving Transfer

### Unit Purpose

Bring the level's strategies together in mixed, unfamiliar contexts. Students decide whether context, word parts, reference entries, relationships, multiple-meaning reasoning, nonliteral interpretation, or text features provide the best evidence.

### Student Outcomes

- Choose an effective strategy for an unfamiliar word in a short context.
- Combine context, word parts, and reference information when one strategy is not enough.
- Explain the evidence that supports a meaning choice.
- Revise a sentence or response to use vocabulary more precisely.
- Demonstrate end-of-level mastery through mixed strategy transfer and short production.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Strategy, word-learning routine, clue, context, word part, reference, definition, fit, evidence, precise, multiple meaning, nonliteral, academic word, transfer, review.

### Prerequisites

Students need the full set of Units 1-7 strategies and experience with short explanations, sentence frames, and mixed review.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit reviews all prior units and prepares Grade 4 vocabulary work, where students will handle longer paragraph context, more complex affixes, reference tools, idioms, adages, proverbs, similes, metaphors, and stronger discussion vocabulary.

### Misconceptions To Address

- The most recently taught strategy should be used for every word.
- One clue is enough even when it conflicts with the sentence.
- A correct definition matters more than whether the word fits the text.
- Explaining evidence is extra work rather than part of vocabulary mastery.
- Production means using a big word, even if the sentence is unclear.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use mixed sets and delayed review. Include short contexts from literature, science, social studies, math, and classroom directions, with prompts that ask students to name the strategy and justify the meaning choice.
