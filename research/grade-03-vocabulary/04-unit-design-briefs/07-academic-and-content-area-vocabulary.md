# Unit 7: Academic And Content-Area Vocabulary

Track: Vocabulary
Level: Grade 3
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/grade-03-vocabulary/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn high-utility academic and content-area words that help them understand directions, text features, and short subject-area passages. By the end of the unit, students should use vocabulary from reading, math, science, and social studies in short explanations and sentence frames.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit comes late because students can now combine context, references, word relationships, word parts, and multiple-meaning reasoning. Academic and content words are valuable precisely because they transfer across subjects, so the unit acts as a bridge between vocabulary skill practice and authentic school reading.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: academic task words, evidence language, text features, domain-specific words, cross-subject meaning, sentence stems.
- Vocabulary: infer, evidence, explain, support, compare, contrast, classify, summarize, conclude, observe, cause, effect, sequence, diagram, caption, table, map, timeline, estimate, measure, glossary.
- Representations and models: sentence-stem banks, diagram and caption pairs, map and table examples, cause/effect charts, sequence strips, classification charts.
- Texts and forms: short informational paragraphs, math and science directions, social studies map snippets, text-feature prompts, oral and written academic frames.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need the word-learning routine, context clues, reference habits, semantic precision, common morphology, multiple-meaning awareness, and short expressive-use practice from Units 1-6.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Academic words are difficult only because they are rare.
- Direction words such as explain, infer, and summarize all mean "answer the question."
- Text features are pictures to skip.
- A word such as table or map means the same thing in every subject.
- Content vocabulary can be memorized without using the sentence, diagram, or caption.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with thinking and explaining words because they support every subject. Then deepen evidence and inference language. Move to text features so students use captions, diagrams, maps, and tables as meaning sources. Teach relationship words such as cause, effect, sequence, and classify before ending with mixed academic words across subjects.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

This unit should repeatedly ask students to use context, reference entries, word parts, and precision. Multiple-meaning review appears naturally with words such as table, map, scale, and measure. Nonliteral work is not central, but phrase-level meaning still matters in directions and sentence stems.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares Unit 8 cumulative transfer and later Grade 4 vocabulary work with longer informational texts, more advanced morphology, academic discussion, and subject-specific vocabulary.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Choose meanings of academic task words in directions.
- Use evidence and inference language accurately.
- Interpret text-feature vocabulary in context.
- Use cause/effect, sequence, classify, compare, and contrast words correctly.
- Complete or produce short academic sentence stems.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Words For Thinking And Explaining**: Understand academic verbs used in questions and directions.
2. **Evidence, Support, And Inference**: Use words that connect a claim to clues from text.
3. **Text Features: Captions, Diagrams, Maps, And Tables**: Interpret common text-feature words and their roles.
4. **Cause, Effect, Sequence, And Classify**: Use relationship words that organize information.
5. **Academic Words Across Subjects**: Apply high-utility words in reading, math, science, and social studies contexts.
