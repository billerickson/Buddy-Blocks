# Unit 6: Literal, Nonliteral, And Author-Craft Language

Track: Vocabulary
Level: Grade 3
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/grade-03-vocabulary/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students distinguish literal and nonliteral language in common Grade 3 contexts and use simple author-craft vocabulary to explain meaning. By the end of the unit, students should interpret idioms and sayings from context and recognize basic craft words such as image, sound word, simile, phrase, and dialogue when they support reading.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

Nonliteral language comes after multiple-meaning work because students now understand that meaning can shift with context. This unit keeps figurative language concrete and useful, avoiding advanced literary taxonomy while preparing students for later author-craft study.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: literal meaning, phrase-level meaning, nonliteral expression, comparison, image, sound, dialogue as meaning support.
- Vocabulary: literal, nonliteral, idiom, saying, phrase, author, craft, image, sound word, simile, compare, like, as, dialogue.
- Representations and models: literal/nonliteral T-charts, idiom context cards, comparison frames, simple craft-label examples.
- Texts and forms: short story snippets, dialogue exchanges, sentence pairs, brief poem-like lines, one-sentence explanations.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need context reasoning, multiple-meaning awareness, oral familiarity with common sayings, and the ability to notice when a word-by-word meaning does not fit a sentence.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Idioms can be solved by defining each word.
- Nonliteral means the phrase has no meaning.
- Every sentence with like or as is a useful simile.
- Author-craft words are labels to memorize without explaining meaning.
- Sound words and images are decorations rather than meaning clues.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with literal versus nonliteral decisions so students have a clear contrast. Then focus on idioms in short contexts, followed by simple sayings that carry a lesson or general idea. End with author-craft vocabulary that directly supports meaning, especially image, sound word, simile, and dialogue.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Context clues and meaning fit from Units 1-2 remain central. Multiple-meaning awareness from Unit 5 helps students avoid over-literal interpretations. Precision from Unit 3 helps students choose words that describe a phrase's meaning accurately.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares Unit 7 academic and text vocabulary by teaching craft labels as tools for reading. It prepares Grade 4 work with more explicit idioms, adages, proverbs, similes, metaphors, and craft analysis.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify whether a phrase is literal or nonliteral.
- Infer an idiom's meaning from context.
- Explain the general lesson or meaning of a simple saying.
- Identify image, sound word, simile, or dialogue when the label helps meaning.
- Use a nonliteral or craft word in a short explanation.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Literal Or Nonliteral?**: Decide whether words mean exactly what they say.
2. **Idioms In Short Contexts**: Use context to understand common idioms.
3. **Simple Sayings And Their Lessons**: Interpret sayings as whole ideas, not word-by-word definitions.
4. **Images, Sounds, And Similes**: Use simple author-craft words to describe how language makes meaning.
