# Unit 1: Word-Learning Routine And Context Clues

Track: Vocabulary
Level: Grade 3
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/grade-03-vocabulary/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn a repeatable routine for solving unfamiliar words and apply it to sentence and short-passage context clues. By the end of the unit, students should infer a meaning, name the clue that helped, and check whether the meaning fits the sentence.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit comes first because every later vocabulary skill depends on evidence-based meaning making. Third graders may know some context-clue habits from earlier grades, but this unit turns those habits into a deliberate routine that can be reused with references, morphology, multiple meanings, figurative language, and content-area words.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: word-solving routine, context as evidence, clue patterns, meaning fit, partial evidence, rereading.
- Vocabulary: unknown word, context, clue, meaning, reread, infer, evidence, definition clue, example clue, restatement, contrast, cause, effect, fits.
- Representations and models: clue-underlined sentences, meaning-and-clue charts, sentence pairs, short paragraphs, routine checklists.
- Texts and forms: one-sentence contexts, two-sentence contexts, short informational or story passages, one-sentence explanations using "I know because..."

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should have basic Grade 2 context-clue readiness, oral vocabulary, category and comparison language, the ability to reread a sentence, and enough decoding fluency to handle short Grade 3 passages with one target word.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Any nearby word is a useful clue.
- Guessing a meaning is the same as proving it.
- The clue must come after the unknown word.
- Context always gives the full dictionary definition.
- A word's familiar meaning is always the meaning in the sentence.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with the routine itself so students know what to do when a word is unfamiliar. Then teach the clearest clue patterns first: definition and restatement, followed by examples. Move to contrast clues because they require noticing signal words such as but and unlike. End with cause/effect clues in short passages, where students must use more than one sentence and check meaning fit.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

This is the opening unit, so review draws from Grade 2: picture and sentence clues, common synonyms and antonyms, simple categories, and familiar school vocabulary. Review should be light and immediately connected to the new routine.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares Unit 2 by giving students a meaning guess to verify in a reference tool. It prepares Units 3-6 by making context and clue evidence central to precision, word parts, multiple meanings, and nonliteral language. It prepares Unit 8 by establishing strategy-choice language.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Use the word-learning routine in order.
- Match clue types to example sentences.
- Infer meaning from definition, restatement, example, contrast, and cause/effect clues.
- Identify the words or sentences that support a meaning.
- Explain why a meaning fits or does not fit the context.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Stop And Try The Word-Learning Routine**: Establish the steps students use when they meet an unknown word.
2. **Definition And Restatement Clues**: Use direct explanations and same-idea wording to infer meaning.
3. **Example Clues**: Use examples in a sentence to figure out a category or meaning.
4. **Contrast Clues**: Use contrast words and opposite ideas to infer meaning.
5. **Cause And Effect Clues In Short Passages**: Use sentence relationships across a compact passage to choose a fitting meaning.
