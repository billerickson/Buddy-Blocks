# Grade 3 Vocabulary Level Blueprint

Track: Vocabulary
Level: Grade 3
Source: Accepted research brief at `research/grade-03-vocabulary/01-research-brief.md`

## 1. Level Mission

Grade 3 Vocabulary builds durable word-learning habits for students who are becoming more independent readers across literature, informational text, math, science, and social studies. Students learn to pause at unfamiliar words, reread, use sentence and passage clues, examine meaningful word parts, consult beginning reference tools, and test whether a meaning fits the text. The level also deepens word relationships, precision, multiple meanings, homophones, homographs, literal and nonliteral language, and high-utility academic and content vocabulary. By the end of the level, students should not merely remember practiced definitions; they should transfer a small set of flexible strategies to new words and use newly learned words in short, clear oral or written language.

## 2. Durable Student Outcomes

By the end of Grade 3 Vocabulary, students should be able to:

1. Use a repeatable word-learning routine: pause, reread, notice clues, check word parts, try a meaning, and verify that the meaning fits.
2. Infer the meaning of unfamiliar words from sentence and short-passage context, including definition, example, contrast, restatement, cause/effect, and appositive clues.
3. Use common prefixes, suffixes, base words, known roots, and related word families to infer meaning without overclaiming false word parts.
4. Use beginning dictionaries, glossaries, and digital-style entries to check meaning, pronunciation, syllabication, and the definition that fits a sentence.
5. Explain and use word relationships, including synonyms, antonyms, categories, related words, and shades of meaning.
6. Resolve multiple-meaning words, homophones, and homographs by choosing the meaning, spelling, or pronunciation that fits the context.
7. Distinguish literal from nonliteral meanings in common idioms, sayings, and simple author-craft language.
8. Understand and accurately use high-utility academic, text-feature, and content-area words in short reading, speaking, and writing tasks.
9. Produce short sentences or explanations that use new vocabulary precisely enough that the meaning is clear.
10. Apply mixed word-learning strategies to unfamiliar but grade-appropriate contexts, including unseen words and new subject-area passages.

## 3. Concept And Skill Progression

The level begins by establishing a compact word-learning routine and metacognitive habits: students ask what they know, what clue they have, whether a word part helps, and whether the meaning they chose fits the sentence. Early lessons keep decoding and reading load light so students can focus on strategy choice rather than long comprehension demands.

Students then learn context clues in an intentional sequence. They begin with sentence-level definition, restatement, and example clues, then move to contrast and cause/effect clues in sentence pairs and short paragraphs. Context work should also include cases where nearby words only narrow the meaning, prompting students to check with a reference tool or test the meaning against the full sentence.

Reference skills appear early as a verification routine, not as a detached dictionary unit. Students practice alphabet order, glossary and beginning dictionary entries, guide-word-like habits when appropriate, pronunciation and syllable supports, and selecting the definition that fits the context rather than assuming the first definition is correct.

Semantic relationship work deepens Grade 2 readiness. Students review categories, synonyms, and antonyms, then compare near-synonyms by intensity, formality, feeling, and context. They revise vague words into more precise ones and explain why two related words cannot always be swapped.

Morphology develops after context and reference routines are active. Students connect familiar base words to common prefixes and suffixes, then use related word families and known roots to infer meaning. Morphology should stay meaning-oriented and connected to multisyllabic word reading, with regular checks against context to avoid false word parts.

Students next address words that look or sound alike: multiple meanings, homophones, and homographs. They use spelling, pronunciation when visible from context, sentence fit, and meaning clues to choose the intended word or definition. This work prepares students to recognize that a word may not have one permanent meaning.

Literal and nonliteral language comes after students have enough context and meaning-fit habits to reason at the phrase level. Common idioms and simple sayings are taught through short, concrete contexts. Simple author-craft vocabulary such as image, sound word, simile, phrase, and dialogue appears only when it helps students explain meaning.

The final stretch broadens transfer into academic and content-area language. Students use words such as infer, evidence, compare, classify, observe, cause, effect, sequence, diagram, caption, table, map, glossary, estimate, and support in short subject-area passages and sentence frames. The level ends with mixed cumulative word solving in which students must choose the strategy that fits the word and context.

## 4. Vocabulary, Representation, Model, Text, And Form Progression

Early vocabulary centers on the word-learning process: unknown word, clue, context, meaning, reread, base word, word part, glossary, dictionary, definition, example, and fits. Representations should be simple: clue-marked sentences, two-column meaning-and-clue charts, word-part boxes, and short routine checklists.

Context work adds clue-pattern language: definition clue, example clue, restatement, contrast, cause, effect, nearby words, sentence, paragraph, and evidence. Texts begin as single sentences, then sentence pairs, then short paragraphs of two to four sentences with one primary target word.

Reference work introduces entry, guide word when useful, pronunciation, syllable, part of speech only when it clarifies meaning, glossary, caption, diagram, and multiple definitions. Students should see simplified dictionary and glossary entries as usable texts.

Semantic relationship vocabulary includes synonym, antonym, related word, category, member, shade of meaning, precise, vague, stronger, milder, formal, feeling, and intensity. Representations include word lines, intensity scales, category maps, and sentence frames for explaining word choice.

Morphology language includes prefix, suffix, base word, root, word family, syllable, affix, and meaning part. Models include word sums such as `re + read = reread`, meaning maps for word families, and paired context sentences that confirm or reject a word-part guess.

Multiple-meaning and figurative-language vocabulary includes multiple meaning, homophone, homograph, literal, nonliteral, idiom, saying, phrase, simile, image, and sound word. Text forms include short dialogue, brief story excerpts, informational captions, and sentence pairs that contrast meanings.

Academic and content vocabulary moves across subject forms: directions, diagrams, captions, maps, tables, timelines, short science observations, simple social studies passages, and math explanations. Response forms progress from choosing a meaning to explaining a clue, completing a sentence, revising word choice, and writing or saying an original sentence.

## 5. Fluency Expectations

Vocabulary fluency in Grade 3 means efficient and accurate strategy use, not speeded definition recall. Students should become fluent with the word-learning routine, common clue-pattern recognition, everyday reference-tool habits, and frequent academic words used in classroom directions and short texts. They should quickly recognize common affixes such as `un-`, `re-`, `pre-`, `mis-`, `-ful`, `-less`, `-ly`, `-er`, `-est`, and `-ness` in familiar words, while still checking the base word and context.

Short retrieval practice is appropriate for high-utility words and recently taught relationships, especially when spaced across units. Fluency evidence should include accurate meaning selection, explanation of the clue or word part, and later use in a new context. Timed or list-only recall should not be the primary measure of vocabulary mastery.

## 6. Conceptual Understanding Expectations

Students should understand that word meaning is built from evidence. A good vocabulary answer depends on how the word is used in a sentence or passage, what meaningful parts the word may contain, what a reference entry confirms, and whether the chosen meaning fits the whole idea.

Students should understand that word relationships are structured but not automatic. Synonyms can differ by intensity or context, antonyms may be opposites or contrasts, words can belong to categories or families, and related words can change meaning when a prefix or suffix is added.

Students should understand that a word can have more than one meaning and that sound or spelling alone is not enough. Context, spelling, pronunciation clues, and sentence fit work together when resolving multiple-meaning words, homophones, and homographs.

Students should understand that nonliteral language often works at the phrase level. Idioms and sayings may not be solved word by word, and simple author-craft language helps readers describe how words make meaning, pictures, sounds, or comparisons.

## 7. Application And Transfer Expectations

Students should apply vocabulary strategies in short, varied contexts: classroom directions, literary sentences, informational paragraphs, captions, diagrams, maps, tables, science observations, math explanations, and social studies snippets. They should choose a strategy because it fits the word and text, not because it was the most recent skill taught.

Transfer should be visible when students meet unfamiliar but grade-appropriate words and can explain how they know, even if the word was not practiced before. They should be able to select a fitting definition, reject a tempting but wrong definition, identify the clue or word part that helped, and use the word in a sentence that preserves its meaning.

Production expectations should stay compact. Students can complete sentence frames, revise vague words, give one-sentence explanations, participate in short dialogue choices, and write brief original sentences. Long compositions, spelling accuracy, and formal literary analysis should not be treated as the main evidence of vocabulary mastery.

## 8. Review And Spacing Plan

Review should be cumulative from the first unit. The word-learning routine should recur in every cluster: context clues, reference tools, morphology, word relationships, multiple meanings, figurative language, and academic/content vocabulary. Context clue practice should continue after the context unit by appearing in morphology, idiom, reference, and content-area lessons.

Common affixes, word families, and high-utility academic words should be retrieved in short spaced doses over days and weeks. Review should mix relationship types so students learn to decide whether a task is asking about a synonym, antonym, shade of meaning, category, multiple meaning, or word part.

Academic and content words should return in new subject contexts. For example, `evidence` can appear in reading, science, and social studies; `compare` can appear in vocabulary, math, and literature; `table` can appear as furniture, a data display, and a text feature. Review prompts should ask students to name the strategy, clue, word part, or meaning fit rather than simply recall a label.

## 9. Mastery Evidence For The End Of The Level

End-of-level mastery should be evidenced by a mixed body of work across unfamiliar contexts. A student who has mastered Grade 3 Vocabulary can infer meanings from clear context clues, select the fitting definition from a reference entry, use common prefixes and suffixes to support meaning, explain semantic relationships and shades of meaning, resolve multiple meanings and sound-alike words, interpret common nonliteral phrases, and use academic/content vocabulary in short responses.

The strongest evidence includes transfer and explanation: the student can choose the strategy that fits, identify the clue or word part, reject plausible distractors, revise an imprecise word, and produce a short original sentence or explanation. Mastery should not require long written composition, advanced etymology, formal connotation analysis, or large decontextualized word lists.
