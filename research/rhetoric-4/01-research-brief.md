# Rhetoric 4 Research Brief

Track: Rhetoric  
Level: Level 4  
Intended student profile: students who can support and refute claims, compare using criteria, and write short compositions, now ready for public argument, arrangement, impersonation, description, thesis, policy/law proposals, appeals, and delivery.

This brief defines outcomes and instructional priorities only. It does not define units, lessons, or questions.

## 1. End-Of-Level Outcomes

By the end of Rhetoric 4, students should be able to use impersonation/character voice responsibly; craft vivid description or ekphrasis; state a thesis; analyze or propose a simple rule, law, or policy; arrange an argument with introduction, narration/background, proof, refutation, and conclusion; identify and use ethos, pathos, and logos in age-appropriate ways; and deliver a short organized oral argument.

## 2. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should know confirmation, refutation, commonplace, praise/blame, comparison, evidence selection, transitions, audience awareness, and short multi-paragraph structure.

## 3. Major Concept Clusters

- **Impersonation And Voice:** speaking or writing from a role, with textual/contextual evidence.
- **Description/Ekphrasis:** vivid, ordered description that serves an argument or interpretation.
- **Thesis And Policy:** a debatable central claim and reasoning about rules, proposals, benefits, harms, and tradeoffs.
- **Classical Arrangement:** introduction, narration, proof, refutation, conclusion.
- **Rhetorical Appeals:** ethos, pathos, logos as audience-facing resources.
- **Delivery:** voice, pacing, emphasis, gesture, visuals, and listener adaptation.

## 4. Recommended Progression

Begin with audience and evidence review. Teach description and voice through literary/historical models before policy argument. Introduce thesis as a claim that requires support. Teach arrangement as a flexible structure for helping the audience follow reasoning. Add ethos/pathos/logos after students can identify claim, evidence, and audience. End with short oral/written arguments and policy proposals.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Impersonation means making up anything.
- Description is decoration.
- A thesis is just a topic.
- A policy proposal only needs good intentions.
- Ethos means bragging, pathos means manipulating, logos means facts only.
- Refutation belongs only at the end.
- Delivery can compensate for weak reasoning.

## 6. Developmental Constraints And Reading/Load Concerns

Use nonpartisan, local, literary, historical, or classroom policy contexts. Keep proposals bounded. Teach appeals ethically. Avoid assigning students to impersonate traumatic or demeaning perspectives. Oral presentation should allow rehearsal and should not grade charisma over clarity.

## 7. Assessment Implications

Assess arrangement, thesis clarity, relevant proof, respectful refutation, appeal identification, and delivery planning. Use passage questions for appeals and arrangement, order-items for speech structure, and constructed responses for concise thesis/proof/refutation. Distractors should show topic-as-thesis, emotional manipulation, irrelevant ethos, missing tradeoff, and unsupported policy.

## 8. Spiral Review Priorities

Audience, purpose, claim, thesis, evidence, example, confirmation, refutation, comparison, commonplace, description, voice, introduction, narration, proof, conclusion, ethos, pathos, logos, delivery.

## 9. Concepts That Should Not Be Taught Yet

Avoid advanced rhetorical theory, partisan campaign strategy, full formal debate tournaments, complex legal reasoning, manipulative persuasion techniques, and long research speeches as default assessment.

## 10. Source Notes
