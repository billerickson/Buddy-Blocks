# Logic 2 Course Map

Track: Logic  
Level: Level 2  
Sources: Accepted research brief at `research/logic-2/01-research-brief.md` and level blueprint at `research/logic-2/02-level-blueprint.md`

## Course Design Notes

Logic 2 moves from argument vocabulary into evaluation and transfer. The sequence deliberately delays fallacy labels until students have practiced the underlying checks: What is the claim? What supports it? Does the support fit? Is it enough? What else could explain it? The map uses five units with uneven instructional jobs: early units build tools, middle units test evidence quality, and the final unit integrates the tools in mixed informal-logic scenarios.

## Unit 1: Argument Parts And Evidence

### Unit Purpose

Refresh and extend argument-part knowledge so students can read short scenarios as arguments rather than as disconnected sentences. Students distinguish statements, questions, claims, premises, conclusions, evidence, examples, explanations, and objections.

### Student Outcomes

- Distinguish statements, questions, and claims.
- Identify premises and conclusions in short arguments.
- Separate evidence, examples, and explanations by the job each one does.
- Recognize an objection as a reason to test or limit a claim.
- Use a simple claim/support/objection organizer.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Statement, question, claim, reason, premise, conclusion, evidence, example, explanation, support, objection, argument.

### Prerequisites

Students should already know claim, reason, evidence, conclusion, relevant, because, and supported versus unsupported conclusion from Logic 1 or equivalent reasoning work.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit stabilizes the vocabulary needed for every later unit. Definitions, counterexamples, relevance checks, cause claims, source checks, and fallacy work all depend on knowing what the argument is trying to prove and what support has been offered.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Treating every sentence as a claim.
- Confusing the conclusion with the first sentence.
- Thinking evidence, example, and explanation are interchangeable.
- Treating objections as rude disagreement instead of useful tests.
- Looking for fallacy labels before understanding the argument parts.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Return to claim/support identification in every later unit. Keep conclusion placement varied so students do not rely on first-sentence or last-sentence patterns.

## Unit 2: Definitions, Classification, And Counterexamples

### Unit Purpose

Teach students to test the boundaries of terms, rules, and categories. Students learn that definitions can be clear, too broad, too narrow, or unclear, and that examples, nonexamples, and counterexamples can reveal the problem.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify the job of a definition.
- Classify definitions as clear, too broad, too narrow, or unclear in simple cases.
- Use examples and nonexamples to test a category.
- Recognize counterexamples that disprove or limit a rule.
- Improve a weak definition by choosing a clearer boundary.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Definition, category, example, nonexample, clear, too broad, too narrow, boundary, rule, counterexample, exception.

### Prerequisites

Students need Unit 1 argument parts, basic category language, and comfort comparing simple examples and nonexamples.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit uses Unit 1 claims and support to test category claims. It prepares students for generalization, analogy, source, and fallacy work because many reasoning errors depend on weak category boundaries or ignored counterexamples.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A definition is good if it sounds official.
- A favorite example is the same as a definition.
- Too broad and too narrow mean "wrong in the same way."
- A counterexample is just a weird exception that can be ignored.
- One word can be used clearly without a shared meaning.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Continue quick retrieval of claim, support, evidence, and objection. Reuse counterexamples in later generalization, cause, and fallacy lessons.

## Unit 3: Testing Reasons, Examples, And Analogies

### Unit Purpose

Help students evaluate whether support actually helps a claim. Students learn to check relevance, sufficiency, representative examples, analogy strength, and cautious generalizations before accepting a conclusion.

### Student Outcomes

- Decide whether a reason is relevant to a claim.
- Judge whether the amount and kind of evidence is sufficient for a conclusion.
- Distinguish a useful example from an unrepresentative example.
- Compare similarities and differences to judge analogy strength.
- Revise overgeneralized claims into more careful claims.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Relevant, irrelevant, sufficient, enough, representative, example, analogy, similarity, difference, generalization, overgeneralization, cautious conclusion.

### Prerequisites

Students need Unit 1 argument parts and Unit 2 example, nonexample, and counterexample thinking.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit turns earlier tools into evidence-quality checks. It prepares students for source reliability and fallacy analysis by making them explain the weakness before naming it.

### Misconceptions To Address

- More reasons always make an argument stronger.
- A true reason is automatically relevant.
- One example proves a claim about all cases.
- Any similarity makes a strong analogy.
- A careful conclusion is weaker because it is less dramatic.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Interleave definition and counterexample review with generalization lessons. Use mixed prompts that ask whether a problem is relevance, sufficiency, analogy, or overgeneralization.

## Unit 4: Cause, Correlation, And Source Reliability

### Unit Purpose

Build cautious reasoning about cause claims and sources. Students distinguish cause, sequence, correlation, coincidence, and alternative explanations, then evaluate whether a source is reliable for the question being asked.

### Student Outcomes

- Distinguish cause from "came before."
- Treat correlation as a clue that needs more investigation, not proof by itself.
- Suggest or choose plausible alternative explanations.
- Evaluate simple source reliability using expertise, direct observation, evidence, fairness, date, and bias.
- Match evidence and sources to the claim they can actually support.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Cause, effect, sequence, before, after, correlation, coincidence, alternative explanation, source, reliable, expert, eyewitness, evidence, fair, bias, date.

### Prerequisites

Students need argument parts, relevance, sufficiency, examples, and cautious conclusion language.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit applies relevance and sufficiency to cause and source claims. It prepares students for red herring, appeal to popularity, hasty generalization, and mixed argument analysis in Unit 5.

### Misconceptions To Address

- If one event came before another, it caused it.
- If two things happen together, one must cause the other.
- A confident source is always reliable.
- A source is reliable if it agrees with me.
- Bias means a source is always useless rather than something to consider.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Keep counterexamples and sufficiency checks active. Revisit source reliability during fallacy lessons so students learn to connect evidence quality and reasoning errors.

## Unit 5: Informal Fallacies And Mixed Argument Analysis

### Unit Purpose

Name common informal fallacies after students understand the reasoning problems behind them, then integrate the full Logic 2 toolkit in mixed scenarios. The goal is careful analysis, not memorizing labels as debate weapons.

### Student Outcomes

- Explain fallacies as reasoning errors.
- Identify hasty generalization, circular reasoning, false choice, personal attack, appeal to popularity, red herring, and weak analogy in plain-language contexts.
- Distinguish similar fallacies by the kind of support problem they contain.
- Choose a better reason, source, counterexample, or cautious conclusion.
- Apply a mixed argument-check routine to new short arguments.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Fallacy, reasoning error, hasty generalization, circular reasoning, false choice, personal attack, appeal to popularity, red herring, weak analogy, mixed analysis, fair conclusion.

### Prerequisites

Students need all prior units: argument parts, definition and counterexample checks, relevance, sufficiency, analogy, generalization, cause/correlation, and source reliability.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit is the level integration point and prepares students for later logic work involving more formal argument structure, longer texts, and more independent explanation.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Naming a fallacy is enough.
- Fallacy labels are insults.
- Every weak argument has exactly one possible label.
- A popular belief must be true.
- Rejecting a person's character proves the claim false.
- A mixed argument can be judged from one sentence without checking support.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use cumulative mixed practice. Every lesson should retrieve at least two earlier reasoning moves, and the final lesson should require students to choose which check matters most before selecting an answer.
