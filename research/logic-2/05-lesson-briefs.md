# Logic 2 Lesson Briefs

Sources: accepted level blueprint, course map, and unit design briefs in `research/logic-2/`.

## Unit 1: Argument Parts And Evidence

### Lesson 1: Statements, Questions, And Claims

- **Teaching goal:** Help students distinguish sentences that tell, ask, or make a supportable claim.
- **Student outcome:** The student can classify a short sentence as a statement, question, or claim and explain which ones can be supported with reasons.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A question asks; a statement tells; a claim is a statement someone can support, challenge, or prove with reasons.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic sentence meaning, claim/reason vocabulary from Logic 1, and supported versus unsupported conclusions.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating every sentence as a claim; thinking questions can be true or false; missing that claims can be about opinions when reasons are offered.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given mixed sentences, the student can identify which are questions, ordinary statements, and supportable claims.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for classification; `match-pairs` for sentence-to-type sorting; `fill-blank` for vocabulary in simple frames.
- **Student-facing intro for config.intro:** "Logic starts by noticing what a sentence is doing. A question asks for information. A statement tells something. A claim is a statement someone can support with reasons or evidence."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one-sentence prompts; avoid abstract claims with adult civic or political content.

### Lesson 2: Premises And Conclusions

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to identify the support and the point of a short argument.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify the conclusion and the premise or reason in a simple argument, even when the conclusion appears first.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** The conclusion is what the argument wants you to accept; premises are the reasons offered as support.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Claims, reasons, because statements, and basic sentence order.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Assuming the conclusion is always last; calling every reason "evidence"; confusing the topic with the conclusion.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a two-sentence argument, the student selects the conclusion and the premise and can complete a claim/support map.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for short arguments; `match-pairs` for argument part labels; `order-items` for premise-to-conclusion flow.
- **Student-facing intro for config.intro:** "An argument has a point and support. The conclusion is the point the speaker wants you to accept. A premise is a reason that supports that conclusion."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep arguments to one or two sentences and use clear cue words such as because, so, since, and therefore without relying only on them.

### Lesson 3: Evidence, Examples, And Explanations

- **Teaching goal:** Separate three support-related moves that students often blend together.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether a sentence gives evidence, gives an example, or explains how or why something works.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Evidence supports a claim, an example shows one case, and an explanation tells how or why something happens.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Claim, reason, premise, conclusion, and example language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Calling any extra detail evidence; thinking an example always proves a general claim; treating explanation as the same thing as proof.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student sorts support sentences by job and chooses the support type that best fits a claim.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for support type matching; `multiple-choice` for best label; `passage-question` for support role in context.
- **Student-facing intro for config.intro:** "Support can do different jobs. Evidence gives information that supports a claim. An example shows one case. An explanation tells how or why something works."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar contexts and avoid scientific explanations with unfamiliar background knowledge.

### Lesson 4: Objections That Test A Claim

- **Teaching goal:** Frame objections as fair reasoning checks rather than as personal disagreement.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose an objection that directly tests a claim or its support.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A useful objection points to a possible weakness, missing evidence, exception, or alternative view.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Claims, support, relevance, and evidence/example/explanation distinctions.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating objections as rude; choosing objections about side details; thinking one objection automatically proves the claim false.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a claim and support, the student chooses a relevant objection and rejects off-topic objections.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for best objection; `passage-question` for objection role; `order-items` for fair check sequence.
- **Student-facing intro for config.intro:** "An objection is a fair question or reason that tests a claim. A strong objection stays on the claim and asks what might be missing, weak, or not always true."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep scenarios low-stakes and model respectful wording; avoid social conflict that distracts from reasoning.

## Unit 2: Definitions, Classification, And Counterexamples

### Lesson 1: What Makes A Definition Clear?

- **Teaching goal:** Teach definitions as boundary tools rather than official-sounding phrases.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose a clear definition that helps decide what belongs in a category.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A clear definition gives the important features of a thing, not just a favorite example or vague description.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Category language, examples, claims, and support.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Thinking a definition is clear because it sounds fancy; giving only an example; defining a word with the same word.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses clear definitions and identifies why weak definitions do not help classification.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for best definition; `match-pairs` for term-to-definition; `fill-blank` for definition feature vocabulary.
- **Student-facing intro for config.intro:** "A definition helps people decide what a word means. A clear definition gives important features, not just one example or a fancy-sounding phrase."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use concrete categories such as tool, game, mammal, schedule, or rule before abstract categories.

### Lesson 2: Too Broad, Too Narrow, Or Just Right

- **Teaching goal:** Help students diagnose direction of definition errors.
- **Student outcome:** The student can classify a definition as too broad, too narrow, or just right for a simple category.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Too broad lets in things that should stay out; too narrow leaves out things that should belong.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Clear definition, category, example, nonexample, and claim testing.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating any weak definition as "too broad"; confusing broad/narrow with easy/hard; ignoring nonexamples.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a category and proposed definition, the student identifies the boundary problem and picks a better definition.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for diagnosis; `match-pairs` for definition-to-problem; `passage-question` for classification in a short context.
- **Student-facing intro for config.intro:** "A definition can make the boundary too wide or too small. Too broad includes things that do not belong. Too narrow leaves out things that do belong."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Present one category at a time; keep answer choices parallel.

### Lesson 3: Examples And Nonexamples

- **Teaching goal:** Teach examples and nonexamples as active tests of a definition or category.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use examples and nonexamples to decide whether a category boundary is working.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Examples show what fits; nonexamples show what does not fit; both help test the definition.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Definition, category, too broad, too narrow.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating nonexamples as unhelpful; choosing examples by surface similarity only; ignoring the stated definition.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student sorts cases into example and nonexample groups and identifies what feature made the difference.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for case sorting; `multiple-choice` for deciding feature; `multi-blank-cloze` for completing an example/nonexample explanation.
- **Student-facing intro for config.intro:** "To test a definition, look at both sides. Examples should fit the definition. Nonexamples should not fit. Together they show where the boundary is."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short item descriptions; avoid categories requiring specialized science or history knowledge.

### Lesson 4: Counterexamples That Change The Rule

- **Teaching goal:** Show how one clear counterexample can disprove or limit an overstrong claim.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose a counterexample to an "all," "always," or "never" claim and select a more careful revision.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A counterexample is a case that fits the situation but makes the claim false.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Claim, definition, example, nonexample, all/some/none language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating counterexamples as random exceptions; thinking "almost always" can prove "always"; choosing a case that does not actually match the claim.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies a valid counterexample and revises an overstrong rule into a more accurate claim.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for valid counterexample; `passage-question` for claim revision; `order-items` for testing a rule.
- **Student-facing intro for config.intro:** "A counterexample is one case that shows a claim is not always true. If someone says 'all,' 'always,' or 'never,' one real counterexample can change the rule."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use simple universal claims and avoid trick wording; make the counterexample relationship explicit.

## Unit 3: Testing Reasons, Examples, And Analogies

### Lesson 1: Relevant Reasons

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to check whether a reason is about the claim.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose a relevant reason and reject reasons that are true but do not support the claim.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A relevant reason connects directly to the claim; a true sentence can still be irrelevant.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Claim, reason, premise, conclusion, evidence, objection.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating any true or interesting fact as support; choosing reasons that match the topic but not the claim; counting more reasons as stronger automatically.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a claim, the student selects the reason that directly supports it and explains why distractors miss the point.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for relevant reason choice; `match-pairs` for claim-to-support; `passage-question` for reason evaluation.
- **Student-facing intro for config.intro:** "A relevant reason is about the exact claim. A sentence can be true and still not support the claim if it answers a different question."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep claims short; make irrelevant distractors plausible but clearly about another claim.

### Lesson 2: Enough Evidence?

- **Teaching goal:** Help students judge whether the amount and kind of support is enough for the conclusion.
- **Student outcome:** The student can distinguish strong, weak, and cautious conclusions based on available evidence.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Evidence can support a small careful claim without being enough for a big certain claim.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Relevant reasons, examples, counterexamples, all/some/most language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Thinking one example proves an "all" claim; treating many weak reasons as enough; expecting perfect proof for every everyday claim.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses a conclusion that matches the evidence strength and rejects overclaims.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for evidence-to-conclusion matching; `multiple-choice` for sufficiency judgment; `order-items` for cautious reasoning steps.
- **Student-facing intro for config.intro:** "Ask whether the support is enough for the size of the claim. One example might support 'this happened once,' but it is usually not enough for 'this always happens.'"
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use small data sets or short observations; avoid percentages or probability calculations.

### Lesson 3: Strong And Weak Analogies

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to judge analogies by important similarities and differences.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify whether an analogy is strong or weak for a specific claim and name the relevant comparison feature.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** An analogy is stronger when the compared things are alike in the part that matters to the claim.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Similarity/difference language, relevance, definitions, examples.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Any similarity makes a good analogy; fun comparisons are automatically strong; differences never matter if one similarity is true.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects the analogy with the important shared feature and rejects analogies based on surface similarities.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for strong analogy choice; `match-pairs` for important versus unimportant similarity; `passage-question` for analogy critique.
- **Student-facing intro for config.intro:** "An analogy compares two things. It is strong only if the things are alike in the part that matters to the claim. A silly or surface similarity is not enough."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar school, sports, household, and nature comparisons; avoid long analogy chains.

### Lesson 4: Generalizations That Need Care

- **Teaching goal:** Teach careful generalization from examples without overclaiming.
- **Student outcome:** The student can revise overgeneralized claims using cautious words such as some, many, may, or based on this evidence.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A generalization is a claim about a group; it should match the number, variety, and quality of examples.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Examples, counterexamples, sufficient evidence, all/some language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** One example proves all cases; counterexamples do not matter; cautious wording makes a claim useless.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given limited evidence, the student chooses the fairest generalization and identifies an overgeneralization.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for fair claim selection; `fill-blank` for cautious wording; `passage-question` for evidence interpretation.
- **Student-facing intro for config.intro:** "A generalization says something about a group. Good logic keeps the claim as strong as the evidence allows: maybe 'some,' 'many,' or 'in this sample' instead of 'all.'"
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid statistical notation; use plain sample descriptions and clear quantifier words.

## Unit 4: Cause, Correlation, And Source Reliability

### Lesson 1: Cause, Sequence, And Coincidence

- **Teaching goal:** Help students distinguish cause from events that only happen before or together.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether a scenario shows a cause, a sequence, or a coincidence.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A cause helps make something happen; coming before something is not enough by itself.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Claim, evidence, sufficient support, cautious conclusion.
- **Likely misconceptions:** If B happened after A, A caused B; if two events happen together, one caused the other; coincidence means something mysterious happened.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses a cautious conclusion about cause in short scenarios and rejects post hoc reasoning.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for cause/sequence classification; `passage-question` for fair conclusion; `match-pairs` for vocabulary.
- **Student-facing intro for config.intro:** "A cause helps make an effect happen. But not everything that happens before an effect caused it. Sometimes events only happen in order or by coincidence."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar everyday sequences; avoid complex science mechanisms.

### Lesson 2: Correlation Is A Clue, Not Proof

- **Teaching goal:** Teach correlation as a pattern that invites investigation but does not prove cause alone.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a correlation claim and choose a fair next step or cautious conclusion.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Correlation means two things are connected in a pattern; more evidence is needed to show cause.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Cause, sequence, evidence sufficiency, generalization.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Correlation proves causation; all patterns are fake; a pattern needs no further checking if it feels obvious.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student distinguishes pattern evidence from cause proof and selects a next investigation question.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for pattern interpretation; `multiple-choice` for next evidence; `order-items` for correlation-check routine.
- **Student-facing intro for config.intro:** "Correlation means two things show a pattern together. A pattern can be an important clue, but it does not prove that one thing caused the other."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use small verbal data summaries instead of charts that require calculation.

### Lesson 3: Alternative Explanations

- **Teaching goal:** Build the habit of asking what else could explain the same observation.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose a plausible alternative explanation for a causal claim.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Before accepting a cause claim, ask whether another cause, hidden factor, or coincidence could explain it.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Cause versus sequence, correlation, objections, relevance.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Asking for another explanation is the same as denying the first claim; any wild possibility counts as an alternative explanation; one alternative proves the first cause false.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a cause claim and observation, the student selects a plausible alternative explanation and rejects off-topic alternatives.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for plausible alternative; `passage-question` for causal caution; `match-pairs` for claim-to-alternative.
- **Student-facing intro for config.intro:** "An alternative explanation is another possible reason for what happened. It helps us stay careful before saying we know the cause."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep alternatives realistic and concrete; avoid medical, legal, or emotionally intense scenarios.

### Lesson 4: Trustworthy Sources And Fair Use Of Sources

- **Teaching goal:** Teach source reliability as fit between the source, evidence, and question.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose the most reliable source for a simple claim and explain why a source may be limited.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A reliable source has the right knowledge or evidence for the claim; confidence, popularity, or agreement is not enough.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Evidence, relevance, sufficiency, cause claims, cautious conclusions.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A source is reliable if it agrees with me; a famous source can answer anything; bias means a source has no value; eyewitness always beats expertise.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches sources to claims and identifies source strengths and limits.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for source-to-claim fit; `multiple-choice` for reliability judgment; `passage-question` for fair use of a source.
- **Student-facing intro for config.intro:** "Ask whether a source is reliable for this claim. Good checks include: Does the source know about the topic? Did they see evidence? Are they fair? Is the information current?"
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use simple source descriptions; avoid requiring web literacy beyond the stated information.

## Unit 5: Informal Fallacies And Mixed Argument Analysis

### Lesson 1: Fallacies As Reasoning Errors

- **Teaching goal:** Introduce fallacies as names for support problems, not as insults.
- **Student outcome:** The student can match a fallacy label with the reasoning error it names and explain why the label alone is not enough.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A fallacy is a common reasoning error; good logic identifies the error and explains the problem.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Claim, support, relevance, sufficiency, analogy, generalization, source reliability.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Calling something a fallacy proves the claim false; fallacy labels are debate weapons; one weak part means the opposite claim is true.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches fallacy names to plain-language errors and chooses explanations over label-only responses.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for label-to-error; `multiple-choice` for best explanation; `fill-blank` for vocabulary.
- **Student-facing intro for config.intro:** "A fallacy is a common reasoning error. Naming the fallacy can help, but the important part is explaining what went wrong with the support."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Introduce only the fallacy labels used in this level; keep examples low-stakes and nonpartisan.

### Lesson 2: Spot Hasty, Circular, And False-Choice Reasoning

- **Teaching goal:** Teach three fallacies tied to weak evidence, repeated claims, and bad choice boundaries.
- **Student outcome:** The student can distinguish hasty generalization, circular reasoning, and false choice in short arguments.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Hasty generalization uses too little evidence, circular reasoning repeats the claim as support, and false choice pretends there are only two options when more exist.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Generalization, sufficient evidence, claim/support, definitions and category boundaries.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Any generalization is hasty; repeated wording counts as evidence; every either/or claim is false choice.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies which of the three reasoning errors appears and chooses a better reasoning move.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for fallacy identification; `match-pairs` for error type; `passage-question` for best fix.
- **Student-facing intro for config.intro:** "Three common fallacies are easy to mix up. Hasty generalization jumps from too little evidence. Circular reasoning uses the claim as its own support. False choice hides other reasonable options."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use clear, short examples with one main error at a time before mixed choices.

### Lesson 3: Popularity, Personal Attack, And Red Herring

- **Teaching goal:** Teach three fallacies that distract from relevant support.
- **Student outcome:** The student can distinguish appeal to popularity, personal attack, and red herring in short arguments.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Popularity is not proof, attacking a person does not answer a claim, and a red herring changes the subject.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Relevant reasons, source reliability, claim/support, objections.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Popular opinions are automatically true; a person's flaw proves their claim false; any interesting detail is relevant.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies which distraction is present and chooses a relevant way to evaluate the claim.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for fallacy identification; `passage-question` for relevance check; `match-pairs` for fallacy-to-error.
- **Student-facing intro for config.intro:** "Some fallacies distract from the claim. Appeal to popularity says many people believe it. Personal attack goes after a person instead of the claim. Red herring changes the subject."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid mean-spirited attack examples; keep personal-attack scenarios mild and clearly about reasoning.

### Lesson 4: Mixed Argument Clinic

- **Teaching goal:** Integrate the full Logic 2 toolkit in mixed short scenarios.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose the best reasoning check for a new argument and select a fairer conclusion, better evidence, or better objection.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Good reasoning begins by identifying the claim and support, then choosing the check that fits the weakness.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** All prior Logic 2 concepts and vocabulary.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Using the most recent label for every weak argument; naming a fallacy without checking argument parts; accepting a dramatic conclusion from weak support.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student analyzes unfamiliar scenarios using claim/support identification, definition testing, relevance, sufficiency, source, cause, analogy, or fallacy checks.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for mixed scenarios; `multiple-choice` for best next check; `order-items` for argument-check routine; `match-pairs` for weakness-to-fix.
- **Student-facing intro for config.intro:** "In a mixed argument, first find the claim and support. Then choose the best check: definition, counterexample, relevance, enough evidence, analogy, cause, source, or fallacy."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep each scenario short; ask for one primary reasoning check at a time; avoid combining more than two weaknesses in one item.
