# Unit 2: Definitions, Classification, And Counterexamples

Track: Logic  
Level: Level 2  
Stage: Unit Design Brief  
Source: `research/logic-2/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn to test the boundaries of definitions, categories, and rules. By the end of the unit, students can tell whether a definition is clear, too broad, too narrow, or unclear; use examples and nonexamples to classify; and recognize counterexamples that disprove or limit a claim.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

After students can identify claims and support, they need tools for checking what words and rules mean. Many arguments fail because a term is vague, a category is drawn badly, or a counterexample is ignored. This unit gives students concrete testing tools before they evaluate more abstract reason quality.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: definitions set boundaries, categories need shared criteria, nonexamples clarify meaning, counterexamples test "all" and "always" claims.
- Vocabulary: definition, category, example, nonexample, clear, too broad, too narrow, boundary, rule, counterexample, exception.
- Representations and models: example/nonexample charts, definition test tables, category boundary diagrams, "one counterexample" checklists.
- Texts and forms: simple class rules, object categories, word definitions, "all/some/none" claims, short correction choices.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need Unit 1 argument parts, basic category and comparison language, and enough reading stamina to compare a definition with several short cases.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- A definition is good if it sounds official.
- Naming one example is the same as defining the category.
- Too broad and too narrow are interchangeable labels for "bad."
- Nonexamples are distractions instead of useful boundary tests.
- A counterexample can be ignored if the original claim is mostly true.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Start with the purpose of a definition: it should help people decide what belongs. Then separate too-broad and too-narrow definitions so students can name the direction of the boundary problem. Next, use examples and nonexamples as active tests. End with counterexamples because students are ready to see how one well-chosen case can disprove or limit an overstrong rule.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Every lesson reviews claim/support language. Definition statements are treated as claims about a category, and examples, nonexamples, and counterexamples are treated as support or tests.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares Unit 3 by giving students a concrete way to evaluate examples and generalizations. It prepares Unit 5 because hasty generalization, weak analogy, and false choice often depend on poor category boundaries.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify the job of a definition.
- Classify weak definitions as too broad, too narrow, or unclear.
- Match examples and nonexamples to a definition.
- Choose a counterexample that tests an overstrong claim.
- Revise a weak category statement into a more careful one.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **What Makes A Definition Clear?**: Learn that a definition should help decide what belongs and what does not.
2. **Too Broad, Too Narrow, Or Just Right**: Diagnose common definition boundary problems.
3. **Examples And Nonexamples**: Use cases that fit and do not fit to test a category.
4. **Counterexamples That Change The Rule**: Use one clear counterexample to reject or limit an overstrong claim.
