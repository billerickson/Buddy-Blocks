# Unit 4: Cause, Correlation, And Source Reliability

Track: Logic  
Level: Level 2  
Stage: Unit Design Brief  
Source: `research/logic-2/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn to reason cautiously about cause claims and source claims. By the end of the unit, students can distinguish cause, sequence, correlation, coincidence, and alternative explanations; they can also evaluate whether a source is reliable for a particular question.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

Cause and source claims are common in science, history, media, and everyday decisions. Students already know how to check claim, support, relevance, sufficiency, examples, and generalizations, so they are ready to apply those checks to more specific reasoning problems.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: cause versus came before, correlation as a clue, alternative explanations, source reliability for a specific claim, bias as a factor to consider.
- Vocabulary: cause, effect, sequence, before, after, correlation, coincidence, alternative explanation, source, reliable, expert, eyewitness, evidence, fair, bias, date.
- Representations and models: cause/correlation charts, "what else changed?" organizers, source-check cards, evidence-to-claim matching tables.
- Texts and forms: simple science observations, school data, weather and routine examples, historical witness snippets, review or recommendation claims.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need argument parts, relevance, sufficiency, analogy, generalization, counterexample, and cautious conclusion language.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- If one event came before another, it caused it.
- If two things happen together, one must cause the other.
- A source is reliable if it is confident, famous, or agrees with me.
- Bias makes every source useless.
- One source can answer every kind of question equally well.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Start with cause versus sequence because "after this, therefore because of this" is a common developmental error. Then introduce correlation as a useful clue that still needs more evidence. Next, ask students to generate or choose alternative explanations, which deepens causal caution. End with source reliability so students can judge where evidence comes from and whether that source fits the claim.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Cause lessons review relevance and sufficiency. Alternative explanations review objections and counterexamples. Source lessons review evidence, examples, and generalizations by asking what kind of support a source can provide.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares Unit 5 by grounding several fallacy types: hasty generalization, appeal to popularity, red herring, and weak source-based support. It also prepares future logic levels for longer inquiry and evidence evaluation.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify whether a scenario shows cause, sequence, correlation, or coincidence.
- Choose a fairer conclusion when causation has not been shown.
- Select plausible alternative explanations.
- Match sources to the claims they can best support.
- Evaluate reliability using expertise, direct observation, evidence, fairness, date, and bias.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Cause, Sequence, And Coincidence**: Distinguish "caused it" from "came before it" or "happened at the same time."
2. **Correlation Is A Clue, Not Proof**: Treat patterns as questions to investigate rather than proof of cause.
3. **Alternative Explanations**: Look for other plausible reasons before accepting a causal claim.
4. **Trustworthy Sources And Fair Use Of Sources**: Decide whether a source is reliable for the claim being made.
