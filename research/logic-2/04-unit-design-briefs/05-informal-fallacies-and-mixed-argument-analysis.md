# Unit 5: Informal Fallacies And Mixed Argument Analysis

Track: Logic  
Level: Level 2  
Stage: Unit Design Brief  
Source: `research/logic-2/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn common informal fallacy names as labels for reasoning errors they can already explain. By the end of the unit, students can identify hasty generalization, circular reasoning, false choice, personal attack, appeal to popularity, red herring, and weak analogy in short scenarios, then use the full Logic 2 toolkit to choose better reasoning.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

Fallacy names are useful only after students understand the underlying logic. Placing this unit last prevents memorized labels from replacing analysis. The final mixed lesson lets students show level mastery by selecting the right reasoning check without being told which unit skill applies.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: fallacy as reasoning error, label plus explanation, similar fallacies with different support problems, mixed argument checking.
- Vocabulary: fallacy, reasoning error, hasty generalization, circular reasoning, false choice, personal attack, appeal to popularity, red herring, weak analogy, mixed analysis, fair conclusion.
- Representations and models: fallacy-to-error match charts, claim/support/error tables, "better next move" cards, mixed argument checklists.
- Texts and forms: short conversations, school proposals, advertisements, rumors, recommendation claims, simple media-like statements.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need all prior Logic 2 skills: argument parts, definition and counterexample checks, relevance, sufficiency, analogy, generalization, cause/correlation, alternative explanations, and source reliability.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Naming a fallacy proves the other person is wrong.
- Fallacy labels are insults rather than reasoning tools.
- Every weak argument has exactly one possible label.
- A popular claim is probably true because many people repeat it.
- Attacking the person answers the claim.
- The first visible weakness is always the most important weakness.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin by defining fallacies as reasoning errors and separating label from explanation. Then teach three fallacies tied to earlier sufficiency and definition work: hasty generalization, circular reasoning, and false choice. Next teach popularity, personal attack, and red herring as failures of relevance or source fit. End with a mixed argument clinic that retrieves every major Logic 2 tool.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Every lesson reviews argument parts, relevance, sufficiency, counterexamples, source checks, or analogy strength. The final lesson intentionally mixes all prior units.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares future logic study by building a habit of explaining reasoning errors in plain language before using technical labels. It also prepares longer reading and inquiry work where students must evaluate multiple pieces of evidence.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Match fallacy names to underlying reasoning errors.
- Identify common fallacies in short kid-friendly contexts.
- Distinguish relevance failures from sufficiency failures.
- Choose a better next question, better evidence, or fairer conclusion.
- Apply a mixed argument-check routine to unfamiliar scenarios.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Fallacies As Reasoning Errors**: Learn that a fallacy name should point to a specific support problem.
2. **Spot Hasty, Circular, And False-Choice Reasoning**: Identify three common errors tied to weak evidence or bad boundaries.
3. **Popularity, Personal Attack, And Red Herring**: Identify support that distracts from the claim or uses the wrong kind of reason.
4. **Mixed Argument Clinic**: Choose the best logic check for new arguments and revise weak reasoning.
