# Unit 1: Argument Parts And Evidence

Track: Logic  
Level: Level 2  
Stage: Unit Design Brief  
Source: `research/logic-2/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn to see a short text as an argument with parts that do different jobs. By the end of the unit, students can distinguish statements, questions, claims, premises, conclusions, evidence, examples, explanations, and objections, then use those parts to understand what is being argued.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

Logic 2 depends on students being able to identify the claim and the support before they judge quality. This unit comes first because relevance, sufficiency, definitions, source reliability, cause claims, and fallacies all become guesswork if students cannot name what the argument is trying to prove.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: argument as claim plus support, conclusion placement, evidence as support, explanation as meaning or process, objection as a test.
- Vocabulary: statement, question, claim, reason, premise, conclusion, evidence, example, explanation, support, objection, argument.
- Representations and models: claim/support charts, premise-to-conclusion arrows, evidence/example/explanation sort tables, objection test cards.
- Texts and forms: one- and two-sentence everyday arguments, brief classroom conversations, simple notices, short "because" statements.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should know claim, reason, evidence, conclusion, relevant, because, and supported versus unsupported conclusion. They should be able to read a short scenario and choose or explain a reasoning choice in one sentence.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Every statement is a claim in an argument.
- A question can be true or false like a claim.
- The conclusion must be the last sentence.
- Evidence, example, and explanation all mean "extra detail."
- An objection is rude or off-task instead of a way to test a claim.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin by separating statements, questions, and claims because students need to know what can be argued. Then introduce premises and conclusions as the backbone of an argument. Next, separate evidence, examples, and explanations so students do not treat all support as identical. End with objections so students practice testing a claim without jumping into fallacy labels too early.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

This is the opening unit, so review draws from Logic 1 expectations: claim, reason, evidence, conclusion, relevant support, because, and supported versus unsupported conclusions.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares Unit 2 by making definitions and category rules visible as claims that can be tested. It prepares Unit 3 by distinguishing support types before evaluating relevance and sufficiency. It prepares Units 4 and 5 by giving students a stable way to identify cause claims, source claims, and fallacy targets.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Classify short sentences as statements, questions, or claims.
- Identify premises and conclusions in varied sentence order.
- Sort evidence, examples, and explanations by purpose.
- Recognize a useful objection to a claim.
- Complete a simple claim/support/objection map.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Statements, Questions, And Claims**: Separate things that ask, tell, or make a claim that can be supported.
2. **Premises And Conclusions**: Identify what an argument wants us to accept and what support it gives.
3. **Evidence, Examples, And Explanations**: Distinguish support types by the job each one does.
4. **Objections That Test A Claim**: Use objections as fair checks on a claim or its support.
