# Unit 3: Testing Reasons, Examples, And Analogies

Track: Logic  
Level: Level 2  
Stage: Unit Design Brief  
Source: `research/logic-2/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn to evaluate whether support actually helps a claim. By the end of the unit, students can decide whether reasons are relevant, whether evidence is sufficient, whether examples are representative, whether analogies are strong, and whether generalizations need more careful wording.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

Students now know how to identify argument parts and test definitions. The next step is judging support quality. This unit turns examples, counterexamples, and claim/support maps into active evaluation tools before students face cause claims, sources, and fallacy names.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: true but irrelevant reasons, enough support for the size of the claim, representative versus cherry-picked examples, important similarities and differences, cautious conclusions.
- Vocabulary: relevant, irrelevant, sufficient, enough, representative, example, analogy, similarity, difference, generalization, overgeneralization, cautious conclusion.
- Representations and models: relevance check cards, evidence-strength ladders, analogy comparison charts, generalization revision frames.
- Texts and forms: short recommendation arguments, survey snippets, classroom comparisons, everyday "all/most/some" claims.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need Unit 1 argument parts and Unit 2 definition, example, nonexample, and counterexample skills. They should be able to identify a claim before judging a reason.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- A true reason always supports the claim.
- A long list of weak reasons is strong support.
- One example proves a claim about all or most cases.
- Any shared feature makes a strong analogy.
- Careful wording such as "some" or "might" is less logical than a bold claim.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with relevance because students first need to ask whether a reason is about the claim. Then add sufficiency so they ask whether the total support is enough. Next, focus on analogies as useful but limited comparisons. End with generalizations because students can now combine example quality, counterexamples, and cautious wording.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

This unit repeatedly reviews claim/support identification, examples, nonexamples, and counterexamples. It also returns to definition boundaries when students decide which details matter in an analogy or generalization.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares Unit 4 by treating cause and source claims as claims that need relevant and sufficient support. It prepares Unit 5 because many fallacies are specific failures of relevance, sufficiency, analogy, or generalization.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Choose a relevant reason for a claim.
- Reject true but irrelevant support.
- Decide whether evidence is enough for a strong or cautious conclusion.
- Identify important similarities and differences in analogies.
- Revise overgeneralized claims using "some," "many," "may," or "based on this evidence."

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Relevant Reasons**: Decide whether a reason actually supports the claim.
2. **Enough Evidence?**: Judge whether the support is sufficient for the strength of the conclusion.
3. **Strong And Weak Analogies**: Compare similarities and differences that matter to a claim.
4. **Generalizations That Need Care**: Use examples and counterexamples to make fair, cautious claims.
