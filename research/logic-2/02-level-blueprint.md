# Logic 2 Level Blueprint

Track: Logic  
Level: Level 2  
Source: Accepted research brief at `research/logic-2/01-research-brief.md`

## 1. Level Mission

Logic 2 helps students move from noticing simple claims and reasons to evaluating short arguments with care. Students learn to identify argument parts, test definitions, use examples and counterexamples, judge whether reasons and sources are strong enough, separate cause from coincidence, and recognize common informal reasoning errors in kid-friendly contexts. By the end of the level, students should be able to slow down, ask "What is the claim?", "What supports it?", "Is that support good enough?", and "What might be another explanation?" without treating logic as a set of debate tricks or fallacy names to throw at other people.

## 2. Durable Student Outcomes

By the end of Logic 2, students should be able to:

1. Distinguish statements, questions, claims, premises, conclusions, evidence, examples, explanations, objections, and counterexamples in short texts.
2. Identify the main claim and the support in a simple argument, even when the conclusion comes first or last.
3. Evaluate definitions by checking for clarity, useful boundaries, examples, nonexamples, and too-broad or too-narrow wording.
4. Use examples and counterexamples to test general claims, rules, categories, and definitions.
5. Judge whether a reason is relevant to a claim and whether the total support is sufficient for a cautious conclusion.
6. Distinguish strong analogies and careful generalizations from weak analogies and overgeneralizations.
7. Separate cause, sequence, correlation, coincidence, and alternative explanations in simple scenarios.
8. Evaluate source reliability by considering expertise, direct observation, evidence, fairness, date, and possible bias.
9. Recognize common informal fallacies as reasoning errors: hasty generalization, circular reasoning, false choice, personal attack, appeal to popularity, red herring, and weak analogy.
10. Apply a short argument-check routine to mixed everyday, school, science, history, and media scenarios.

## 3. Concept And Skill Progression

The level begins with a careful review of argument parts. Students distinguish statements from questions, claims from facts to be checked, and premises from conclusions. They learn that evidence, examples, explanations, and objections each have different jobs in reasoning. Early tasks use very short scenarios so the vocabulary load does not crowd out the reasoning.

The next stage focuses on definition and classification. Students test whether a definition is clear, whether it includes too many or too few things, and how examples and nonexamples help make a category precise. Counterexamples are introduced as useful tests, not as "gotcha" exceptions.

Students then evaluate reason quality. They decide whether reasons are relevant, whether one example is enough, whether an analogy is strong, and whether a generalization is careful. This stage delays fallacy labels until students can already explain the reasoning problem in plain language.

The course then adds causal and source reasoning. Students distinguish cause from "came before," correlation from proof, and evidence from source confidence alone. They practice looking for alternative explanations before accepting a causal claim.

The level ends with informal fallacies and mixed analysis. Students learn common names only after they have practiced the underlying error. The final work asks them to combine argument-part identification, definition testing, relevance, sufficiency, source reliability, cause/correlation reasoning, and fallacy recognition in short, low-stakes scenarios.

## 4. Vocabulary, Representation, Model, Text, And Form Progression

Early vocabulary should stabilize statement, question, claim, reason, evidence, premise, conclusion, support, example, explanation, objection, and relevant. Students should see short argument maps with boxes labeled "claim," "support," and "possible objection."

Definition and classification vocabulary should include definition, category, example, nonexample, too broad, too narrow, clear, rule, and counterexample. Representations should include two-column example/nonexample charts and simple "test the rule" tables.

Reason-quality vocabulary should include relevant, sufficient, enough, representative, analogy, similarity, difference, generalization, overgeneralization, and cautious conclusion. Models should include reason-rating tables, analogy comparison charts, and "one example / several examples / mixed evidence" organizers.

Causal and source reasoning vocabulary should include cause, effect, sequence, correlation, coincidence, alternative explanation, source, reliable, expert, eyewitness, evidence, bias, date, and fair. Models should include cause/correlation comparison charts, source-check cards, and "what else could explain it?" prompts.

Fallacy vocabulary should stay plain and connected to reasoning: hasty generalization, circular reasoning, false choice, personal attack, appeal to popularity, red herring, and weak analogy. Texts should be brief everyday arguments, classroom conversations, notices, simple media claims, and short inquiry scenarios. Response forms should favor selected explanations, matching, sorting, ordering reasoning steps, and short exact terms rather than long written essays.

## 5. Fluency Expectations

Logic 2 fluency is not speed debate. Students should become fluent with a small set of reasoning questions: identify the claim, identify the support, check relevance, ask whether the support is enough, look for counterexamples, compare similarities and differences, ask whether cause has been shown, and check the source. Students should also retain core vocabulary well enough to use it in mixed lessons without re-teaching every term.

Short retrieval practice is appropriate for terms and fallacy names after concepts have been introduced. Mixed review should prioritize accurate reasoning over fast labeling. A student who quickly names a fallacy but cannot explain the reasoning problem has not met the fluency goal.

## 6. Conceptual Understanding Expectations

Students should understand that an argument is not just people disagreeing; it is a claim supported by reasons or evidence. They should understand that the conclusion is what the argument wants the reader to accept, while premises are the support offered for that conclusion.

Students should understand that definitions make boundaries. A strong definition helps decide what belongs and what does not, while a weak definition may be too broad, too narrow, unclear, or based only on a favorite example.

Students should understand that evidence quality depends on fit and amount. A reason can be true but irrelevant. A single example can be useful but not always enough. A source can be confident but still unreliable. A correlation can suggest a question without proving a cause.

Students should understand that fallacy names are shortcuts for reasoning errors. The important work is explaining why the support fails, not winning an argument by using a label.

## 7. Application And Transfer Expectations

Students should apply logic in familiar school, home, media, science, history, and friendship contexts without relying on emotionally loaded or partisan examples. They should be able to analyze a book-club claim, a class rule, a science observation, a product claim, a survey result, or a rumor by asking structured reasoning questions.

Transfer should appear when students meet a new short scenario and can choose the right lens: argument parts, definition test, counterexample, relevance, sufficiency, analogy strength, generalization quality, cause/correlation, source reliability, or fallacy type. Students should also be able to revise an overstrong conclusion into a cautious one when the evidence is limited.

## 8. Review And Spacing Plan

Review should be cumulative and interleaved. Claim, reason, evidence, premise, conclusion, relevant, sufficient, example, counterexample, definition, analogy, generalization, cause/correlation, source reliability, and informal fallacies should return after short and longer delays.

Each new unit should begin with a small retrieval of earlier reasoning moves. Definition work should continue when students judge analogies and fallacies. Counterexamples should return in generalization, cause, and mixed-analysis lessons. Source checks should reappear when students evaluate evidence and fallacy scenarios.

Feedback should name the reasoning move: "This reason is true but not relevant," "One example is not enough for all," "That source saw the event directly," or "The two things differ in the part that matters." Later question sets should use diagnostic distractors based on real misconceptions rather than random wrong labels.

## 9. Mastery Evidence For The End Of The Level

End-of-level mastery should be evidenced by mixed short scenarios. A student who has mastered Logic 2 can identify claims, premises, conclusions, and evidence; test definitions with examples and nonexamples; use counterexamples; judge reason relevance and sufficiency; compare analogies; revise overgeneralizations; distinguish cause from correlation; evaluate simple source reliability; and recognize common informal fallacies with a plain-language explanation of the reasoning error.

The strongest evidence is transfer: the student can meet a new everyday argument, choose an appropriate checking routine, reject a tempting but weak reason, and select a fairer conclusion or better next question.
