# Logic 2 Research Brief

Track: Logic  
Level: Level 2  
Intended student profile: students who can identify simple claims and reasons and are ready for informal logic, definitions, evidence quality, source reliability, and common reasoning errors.

This brief defines outcomes and instructional priorities only. It does not define units, lessons, or questions. There is no single official elementary "Logic 2" standard; this brief triangulates from ELA/social studies reasoning standards and high-quality logic references.

## 1. End-Of-Level Outcomes

By the end of Logic 2, students should be able to distinguish statements, questions, claims, premises, conclusions, evidence, examples, and explanations; evaluate whether reasons are relevant and sufficient in simple cases; identify good and weak definitions; use examples and counterexamples; judge analogy strength; distinguish generalization from overgeneralization; separate cause from correlation; notice source reliability; and recognize common informal fallacies in kid-friendly contexts.

## 2. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should know claim, reason, evidence, conclusion, relevant, because, if/then, all/some/none, cause/effect, and supported versus unsupported conclusions. They should be able to read short scenarios and explain a reasoning choice in one sentence.

## 3. Major Concept Clusters

- **Argument Parts:** claim, premise, conclusion, evidence, example, explanation, and objection.
- **Definition And Classification:** clear definitions, too broad/too narrow definitions, examples, nonexamples, and counterexamples.
- **Reason Quality:** relevance, sufficiency, representativeness, analogy strength, and source reliability.
- **Causal Reasoning:** cause, correlation, sequence, coincidence, and alternative explanations.
- **Informal Fallacies:** hasty generalization, circular reasoning, false choice, personal attack, appeal to popularity, red herring, and weak analogy in plain language.

## 4. Recommended Progression

Begin with statement/question and claim/evidence review. Introduce premise and conclusion in short arguments, then definitions and counterexamples. Add relevance and sufficiency before fallacy labels. Teach analogy and generalization as useful but limited forms. Introduce cause/correlation and source reliability through everyday, science, history, and school examples. End with mixed informal-logic analysis of short arguments.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Evidence, example, and explanation are the same.
- A definition is good if it sounds official.
- One counterexample is just an exception that can be ignored.
- Any similarity makes a strong analogy.
- More reasons always make an argument stronger.
- Cause means "came before."
- A source is reliable if it agrees with me.
- Naming a fallacy is enough; no explanation is needed.

## 6. Developmental Constraints And Reading/Load Concerns

Use short, low-stakes scenarios. Avoid partisan or emotionally loaded examples. Teach fallacies as reasoning problems, not as debate insults. Keep definitions concrete before abstract. Use tables or graphic organizers for argument parts, source checks, and cause/correlation comparisons.

## 7. Assessment Implications

Use multiple-choice and passage questions to identify argument parts, relevant evidence, counterexamples, weak definitions, and fallacies. Use match-pairs for fallacy-to-error relationships. Use constructed response only for brief explanations. Distractors should reveal common errors: irrelevant reason, too-broad definition, post hoc cause, popularity appeal, source bias confusion, and weak analogy.

## 8. Spiral Review Priorities

Claim, reason, evidence, premise, conclusion, relevant, sufficient, example, counterexample, definition, analogy, generalization, cause/correlation, source reliability, and informal fallacies.

## 9. Concepts That Should Not Be Taught Yet

Avoid formal symbolic notation, truth tables, categorical syllogism taxonomy, square of opposition, probability calculations, formal debate strategy, and fallacy catalogs with Latin names as memorization targets.
