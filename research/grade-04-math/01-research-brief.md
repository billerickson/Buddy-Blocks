# Grade 04 Math Research Brief

## Track

- Track slug: `grade-04-math`
- Level: Grade 4 mathematics
- Intended student profile: upper elementary learners moving from foundational multiplication/division and unit fractions into multi-digit computation, fraction equivalence and operations with like denominators, decimal notation, measurement conversion, and geometric classification. Lessons should be compact but rigorous, with explicit modeling, visual representations, retrieval practice, and misconception-based feedback.

## 1. End-of-Level Outcomes

By the end of Grade 4 math, students should be able to:

- Use the four operations with whole numbers to solve multi-step word problems, including problems with remainders that must be interpreted in context.
- Understand factors, multiples, prime/composite distinctions within 1 to 100, and generate/analyze number or shape patterns.
- Generalize place value to whole numbers through 1,000,000; read, write, compare, and round multi-digit whole numbers.
- Fluently add and subtract multi-digit whole numbers using the standard algorithm.
- Multiply up to four-digit by one-digit numbers and two-digit by two-digit numbers using place-value strategies, properties of operations, equations, arrays, and area models.
- Divide up to four-digit dividends by one-digit divisors using place value, properties, and multiplication/division relationships, explaining with equations, arrays, or area models.
- Generate and recognize equivalent fractions; compare fractions with different numerators and denominators using common denominators/numerators, benchmarks, and visual fraction models.
- Add and subtract fractions and mixed numbers with like denominators; decompose fractions in multiple ways; multiply a fraction by a whole number and solve related word problems.
- Understand decimal notation for fractions with denominators 10 and 100; compare decimals to hundredths.
- Solve measurement conversion problems within one measurement system; use area/perimeter formulas for rectangles; represent fractional measurement data on line plots.
- Measure and sketch angles, solve addition/subtraction angle problems, and classify two-dimensional figures by lines, angles, and symmetry.

## 2. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need Grade 3 foundations:

- Multiplication/division meanings, properties, and fluency within 100.
- Relationship between multiplication and division, including unknown-factor problems.
- Place-value understanding for multi-digit addition/subtraction within 1,000.
- Area as covering with unit squares, area models, and distributive reasoning.
- Fractions as numbers built from unit fractions, represented on number lines and area models.
- Basic fraction equivalence and comparison with same numerator or same denominator.
- Perimeter, elapsed measurement ideas, line plots, and shape categories.
- Ability to explain a strategy with a drawing, equation, or short verbal statement.

## 3. Major Concept Clusters

- Whole-number operations and problem solving: multiplicative comparison, multi-step problems, remainders, factors/multiples, patterns.
- Multi-digit place value and computation: numbers to 1,000,000, rounding, standard addition/subtraction, multiplication/division strategies.
- Fraction equivalence and comparison: visual models, number lines, benchmarks, common denominators/numerators, same-whole constraint.
- Fraction operations: decomposing, adding/subtracting like denominators and mixed numbers, multiplying fractions by whole numbers.
- Decimal fractions: tenths/hundredths, fraction-decimal relationships, decimal comparison.
- Measurement: conversions within a system, area/perimeter, line plots with fractions, angle measurement.
- Geometry: points/lines/rays, parallel/perpendicular, angle types, symmetry, shape classification.

## 4. Recommended Progression

1. Start with Grade 3 retrieval: multiplication/division facts, area models, place-value addition/subtraction, and unit-fraction meaning. This is prerequisite repair, not a separate remedial unit.
2. Develop multiplicative comparison and multi-step word problems using tape diagrams, equations, and explicit contrast with additive comparison.
3. Teach factors, multiples, and patterns as structure in multiplication. Connect factor pairs to arrays and area models.
4. Extend whole-number place value to 1,000,000, then rounding and comparison. Keep digit value language precise.
5. Move into multi-digit multiplication using area models, partial products, and place value before compact procedures. Require explanation of why partial products combine.
6. Develop division with partial quotients, area/array models, and multiplication checks. Teach remainders through context decisions: discard, round up, report as leftover, or convert.
7. Build fraction equivalence with area models and number lines, emphasizing that the whole must stay the same. Use equivalence to compare fractions with benchmarks before procedural common denominators.
8. Teach fraction addition/subtraction with like denominators as joining/separating same-size parts. Decompose mixed numbers visually before symbolic work.
9. Teach fraction-times-whole as repeated groups of unit fractions and connect to multiplication structure.
10. Introduce tenths/hundredths as fractions first, then decimal notation and comparison.
11. Teach measurement conversions, line plots, angles, and geometry after students have enough fraction/place-value structure to interpret units and scales.
12. Spiral review should mix whole-number operations, fraction size, and measurement contexts so students choose operations by meaning rather than by recent topic.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Additive vs multiplicative comparison: interpreting "4 times as many" as "4 more."
- Factor/multiple reversal: saying 6 is a multiple of 24 or treating factors as the result of skip-counting only.
- Place-value zeros: reading 30,405 as 345 or ignoring zero as a placeholder.
- Rounding by changing all digits or rounding from the wrong place.
- Multiplication partial-product errors, especially forgetting place value in two-digit by two-digit multiplication.
- Division as equal sharing only, not measurement/grouping, leading to weak interpretation of quotients and remainders.
- Remainder always rounds up or is always ignored, instead of depending on context.
- Equivalent fractions as changing the amount rather than renaming the same point/area.
- Comparing fractions by larger numerator or denominator without attending to unit size or same whole.
- Adding fractions by adding denominators.
- Mixed numbers treated as two unrelated numbers rather than a whole number plus a fraction.
- Decimal comparison by number of digits: thinking 0.9 < 0.12 because 9 < 12.
- Angle size confused with side length or visual "openness" distorted by ray length.
- Shape classification by prototype only, such as rejecting a rotated square or not seeing a square as a rectangle.

## 6. Developmental Constraints and Reading/Load Concerns

- Grade 4 students can handle multi-step reasoning, but text-heavy word problems can still mask the math. Keep contexts concise and numbers purposeful.
- Multi-digit algorithms carry high working-memory load. Lessons should isolate one decision at a time: estimate, decompose, compute, then check.
- Fractions require strong visual grounding. Use number lines and area models before symbolic comparison or decomposition.
- Decimal notation is easy to overgeneralize from whole numbers; pair decimals with fraction names and place-value charts.
- Geometry vocabulary must be introduced explicitly with examples and non-examples: parallel, perpendicular, ray, angle, line of symmetry.
- App tasks should ask for model interpretation, strategy selection, equation matching, and short computations rather than long written proofs.
- Feedback should identify the property or unit involved: same whole, same denominator, ten times as much, one-degree units, etc.

## 7. Assessment Implications

- Use assessment items that require representation transfer: area model to equation, fraction model to comparison, word problem to operation, decimal to fraction.
- Include distractors that diagnose additive/multiplicative confusion, denominator errors, place-value misalignment, and remainder misinterpretation.
- Assess standard algorithm addition/subtraction fluency, but assess multiplication/division through strategy and explanation as well as answer accuracy.
- For fractions, require same-whole reasoning and benchmark estimates before exact symbolic work.
- For decimals, compare using place value and fraction equivalents; avoid digit-length-only tasks.
- Use angle and shape items with varied orientations and non-prototype examples.
- App-scorable formats should include multiple-choice, fill-blank, match-pairs, order-items for procedure steps, passage-question for short word problems, and numeric text-input.

## 8. Spiral Review Priorities

- Multiplication/division facts and fact families.
- Area models and distributive property.
- Multi-digit place value, rounding, and estimation.
- Additive vs multiplicative comparison.
- Remainder interpretation in context.
- Unit fractions, number-line fractions, equivalent fractions, and benchmark 1/2.
- Fraction addition/subtraction with like denominators and mixed-number decomposition.
- Tenths/hundredths as both fractions and decimals.
- Measurement unit conversions within one system.
- Angle types, parallel/perpendicular lines, and shape hierarchy.

## 9. Concepts That Should Not Be Taught Yet

- Adding/subtracting fractions with unlike denominators as a Grade 4 mastery target.
- Multiplying a fraction by a fraction or dividing fractions.
- Decimal operations beyond comparison and decimal-fraction notation to hundredths.
- Ratios, rates, proportional relationships, percent, negative numbers, coordinate plane graphing, algebraic equation solving.
- Volume formulas and coordinate geometry.
- Formal proofs or theorem-based geometry.
- Cross-multiplication for fraction comparison; use visual models, common units, and benchmarks instead.

## 10. Source Notes

- CCSS-M Grade 4 critical areas identify multi-digit multiplication/division, fraction equivalence and like-denominator operations, fraction-times-whole, and geometric classification as the level core. The outcomes and progression follow those priorities.
- CCSS-M standards require standard algorithm fluency for addition/subtraction in Grade 4, but multiplication/division standards emphasize place value, properties, arrays, area models, equations, and explanations. This shaped the computation sequence.
- Common Core Progressions informed the transition from Grade 3 area/fraction foundations to Grade 4 fraction equivalence, comparison, and operations.
- IES fractions guidance shaped the insistence that fractions be treated as numbers, located on number lines, and computed with procedures that make sense.
- IES math intervention guidance shaped explicit language, systematic examples, visual representations, number lines, word-problem structures, and timed fluency only after understanding.
- IES study guidance supports delayed review, quizzing for retrieval, interleaving worked examples with independent practice, and connecting concrete/semi-concrete models with abstract notation.
- NCTM sources reinforced coherent grade-level focus, clear goals, multiple representations, and purposeful misconception checks.
