# Grammar 2 Level Blueprint

Track: Grammar
Level: Level 2
Source: Accepted research brief at `research/grammar-2/01-research-brief.md`

## 1. Level Mission

Grammar 2 helps students move from basic sentence awareness into deliberate control of sentence parts, word forms, punctuation, and focused editing. Students learn that grammar is not a list of labels to memorize; it is a set of choices that makes meaning clear for a reader. By the end of the level, students should be able to name and use common, proper, plural, and possessive nouns; choose clear pronouns; identify action, helping, and linking verbs; keep simple verb tense consistent; expand sentences with direct objects, compound parts, prepositional phrases, adjectives, and adverbs; punctuate lists and simple dialogue; and use small sentence diagrams or editing routines to explain how words work together.

## 2. Durable Student Outcomes

By the end of Grammar 2, students should be able to:

1. Identify complete sentences and explain the jobs of subjects and predicates in short natural sentences.
2. Use common, proper, singular, plural, and possessive nouns with appropriate capitalization and apostrophes.
3. Choose personal, possessive, and reflexive pronouns that clearly match their antecedents and sentence jobs.
4. Identify action, helping, and linking verbs, then keep simple present, past, and future verb forms consistent in short contexts.
5. Recognize direct objects, compound subjects, compound predicates, and prepositional phrases as ways sentences can expand.
6. Use adjectives and adverbs to add precise detail without confusing their jobs.
7. Apply commas in series and introductory dialogue punctuation to show structure and speaker meaning.
8. Read and build simple sentence diagrams as visual evidence of subject, verb, object, complement, and modifier relationships.
9. Edit short sentences for one named target, then revise a sentence for clearer meaning or fuller detail.
10. Transfer grammar knowledge into mixed review by choosing the relevant grammar tool instead of guessing from surface clues.

## 3. Concept And Skill Progression

The level begins with a short reset on complete sentences, subjects, predicates, and the difference between naming words and action words. This protects later work from becoming label-only grammar. Students then deepen noun control: common and proper nouns, singular and plural forms, and possessive nouns are taught as choices writers make to name exactly who or what they mean.

Pronoun work follows nouns because students need a clear antecedent idea before they can judge whether a pronoun is clear. Students first replace nouns with personal pronouns, then choose subject and object pronoun forms, use possessive pronouns and possessive adjectives, and finally use reflexive pronouns only when they point back to the same person or thing.

The verb strand then expands from action verbs to helping and linking verbs. Students compare what a verb does in a sentence: show action, help another verb, or link the subject to a describing or naming word. After that, students practice simple tense consistency across two-sentence contexts and short passages.

Sentence expansion comes next. Students identify direct objects only after subject and verb recognition are stable. They then learn compound subjects and compound predicates as sentence-building tools, followed by prepositional phrases, adjectives, and adverbs that answer where, when, how, and which questions. Diagramming appears as a visual support during this expansion work, not as a separate advanced system.

Punctuation is taught with the structures it marks. Commas in a series are connected to lists of three or more items. Quotation marks, commas, capitalization, and end punctuation in simple dialogue are connected to who spoke and which exact words were spoken. The level ends with cumulative editing and sentence building so students practice choosing the relevant target: noun form, pronoun clarity, verb consistency, expansion, punctuation, or diagram relationship.

## 4. Vocabulary, Representation, Model, Text, And Form Progression

Early vocabulary should review sentence, complete sentence, fragment, subject, predicate, noun, verb, adjective, adverb, capital letter, and end punctuation. Noun language then expands to common noun, proper noun, singular noun, plural noun, possessive noun, owner, and apostrophe.

Pronoun language should include antecedent, personal pronoun, subject pronoun, object pronoun, possessive pronoun, possessive adjective, reflexive pronoun, clear reference, and unclear reference. Students should see pronouns in sentence pairs before they are asked to produce them independently.

Verb language should include action verb, helping verb, linking verb, main verb, verb phrase, tense, present, past, future, regular verb, irregular verb, and consistency. Representations should include underlined verb phrases, sentence frames, two-sentence mini-passages, and contrast pairs such as "is running" versus "runs."

Expansion language should include direct object, compound subject, compound predicate, conjunction, preposition, prepositional phrase, object of the preposition, modifier, adjective, adverb, detail, and clarity. Visual models should progress from simple subject-predicate frames to labeled sentences and then simple diagrams with a subject line, verb line, object or complement position, and modifier branches.

Punctuation language should include comma, series, quotation marks, dialogue, speaker tag, exact words, capitalization, and punctuation inside quotation marks. Text forms should begin with isolated short sentences, then paired sentences, then three- to five-sentence passages with one clear editing target. Mixed review should remain short enough that reading load does not hide the grammar target.

## 5. Fluency Expectations

Grammar 2 fluency means students can notice and apply common sentence patterns without needing every step re-explained. Students should become fluent at identifying a complete sentence, finding the subject and predicate in short sentences, distinguishing nouns and verbs, recognizing common versus proper nouns, forming regular plurals and common possessives, choosing common pronoun forms, identifying basic verb phrases, and applying commas in simple series.

Fluency should be built through short cumulative retrieval, focused editing, and repeated contrast examples. Speed is not the main goal. A student who answers slowly but accurately, explains the clue, and edits the target convention is showing better mastery than a student who labels quickly without understanding the sentence relationship.

## 6. Conceptual Understanding Expectations

Students should understand that nouns and pronouns are connected: a pronoun is clear only when the reader can identify its antecedent. They should understand that a plural noun names more than one, while a possessive noun shows ownership or relationship, even though both may end with `s`.

Students should understand that a verb is the center of the predicate and that verb phrases can include helpers. They should know that linking verbs do not show action; they connect the subject to a describing or naming word. Tense should be understood as a time signal that must stay consistent unless the meaning changes.

Students should understand sentence expansion as meaning-making. Direct objects answer whom or what after many action verbs; compound subjects and predicates combine related ideas; prepositional phrases, adjectives, and adverbs add controlled detail. Students should also understand that punctuation marks structure: commas separate list items, and dialogue punctuation separates a speaker tag from exact spoken words.

Simple diagramming should help students see relationships among words. The goal is not complex diagram notation; it is recognizing that subject, verb, object, complement, and modifiers have different jobs.

## 7. Application And Transfer Expectations

Students should apply Grammar 2 concepts in short, natural school, family, reading, science, history, and daily-life sentences. They should be able to choose a correct form in a sentence, explain the clue, correct a focused error, and select a stronger revision when a sentence needs clearer naming or more detail.

Transfer should appear when students meet mixed prompts and decide what is being tested. For example, they should know whether an error is plural or possessive, whether a pronoun is unclear or simply repeated, whether a verb form shifts tense, whether a phrase tells where or when, or whether a comma is marking a list or dialogue. The strongest transfer evidence is focused editing: students identify the grammar target, make the correction, and explain why it improves meaning.

## 8. Review And Spacing Plan

Review should be cumulative and brief. Complete sentences, subjects, predicates, noun/verb/adjective/adverb recognition, capitalization, and end punctuation should reappear across all units. Noun and pronoun work should be spaced together so antecedent clarity does not become a one-unit skill. Plural and possessive noun contrasts should reappear in punctuation and editing lessons.

Verb lessons should include delayed review of subjects and predicates because verb choice depends on sentence structure. Tense consistency should be revisited during dialogue and cumulative editing. Sentence expansion lessons should continue to retrieve noun, pronoun, and verb identification while adding direct objects, compounds, phrases, adjectives, and adverbs.

Punctuation review should be interleaved with the structures punctuation marks: commas in lists should return when students write expanded noun or verb series, and dialogue punctuation should return in cumulative editing. Simple diagramming should appear in several units as a quick model for relationships, not only in one isolated lesson.

Feedback and hints should name the useful clue: owner or more than one, noun replaced by pronoun, verb time, direct object question, phrase starting with a preposition, list of three or more, or exact words spoken.

## 9. Mastery Evidence For The End Of The Level

End-of-level mastery should be visible across focused and mixed tasks. A student who has mastered Grammar 2 can identify sentence parts, choose correct noun and pronoun forms, recognize verb phrases and tense consistency, expand sentences with meaningful details, punctuate series and simple dialogue, and use a simple diagram or labeled sentence to explain a word relationship.

The student can edit a short sentence or mini-passage when given a target such as "fix the possessive noun," "make the pronoun clear," "keep the tense the same," or "punctuate the dialogue." The student can also handle mixed review by deciding which grammar concept is relevant before choosing an answer. Strong mastery includes explaining the reason in age-appropriate language, such as "The apostrophe shows ownership," "The pronoun could mean either person," or "The verb changed from past to present without a reason."
