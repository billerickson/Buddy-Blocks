# Grammar 2 Course Map

Track: Grammar
Level: Level 2
Sources: Accepted research brief at `research/grammar-2/01-research-brief.md` and level blueprint at `research/grammar-2/02-level-blueprint.md`

## Course Design Notes

Grammar 2 moves from stable sentence and noun control into pronouns, verb phrases, sentence expansion, punctuation, visual grammar, and cumulative editing. The sequence deliberately starts with clear naming before replacement forms, then strengthens verb recognition before asking students to identify objects and modifiers. Punctuation appears after students know the structures punctuation marks. Simple diagramming is used as a relationship model and returns at the end as a cumulative way to see how sentence parts work together.

## Unit 1: Sentences And Nouns That Name Clearly

### Unit Purpose

Stabilize complete sentence awareness and deepen noun control. Students review subjects and predicates, then use common, proper, singular, plural, and possessive nouns to name people, places, things, and owners clearly.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify complete sentences with subjects and predicates.
- Distinguish common and proper nouns and capitalize proper nouns.
- Form and recognize regular plural nouns in short sentences.
- Distinguish plural nouns from possessive nouns.
- Use possessive nouns to show ownership or relationship.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Sentence, complete sentence, fragment, subject, predicate, noun, common noun, proper noun, singular noun, plural noun, possessive noun, owner, apostrophe, capitalization.

### Prerequisites

Students should know basic nouns and verbs, simple sentence boundaries, capitalization at the start of a sentence, and end punctuation.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit reviews Level 1 sentence knowledge and supplies the noun control needed for Unit 2 pronouns. Plural and possessive noun contrasts return during punctuation and cumulative editing.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A sentence is complete if it is long.
- Every important noun should be capitalized.
- Adding `s` always means more than one.
- An apostrophe always means a contraction.
- Possessive `-'s` means plural.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Keep subject/predicate and noun/verb recognition active in every later unit. Revisit proper noun capitalization during dialogue and editing lessons.

## Unit 2: Pronouns And Clear Reference

### Unit Purpose

Teach pronouns as reader-facing replacement words, not shortcuts that only make sense to the writer. Students connect pronouns to antecedents and choose forms that match sentence job and meaning.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify a pronoun and the noun it replaces or points to.
- Choose subject and object pronouns in short sentences.
- Use possessive pronouns and possessive adjectives without adding apostrophes.
- Use reflexive pronouns only when the pronoun points back to the same subject.
- Revise unclear pronoun references in focused sentences.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Pronoun, antecedent, personal pronoun, subject pronoun, object pronoun, possessive pronoun, possessive adjective, reflexive pronoun, clear reference, unclear reference.

### Prerequisites

Students need Unit 1 noun control, subject/predicate recognition, and basic sentence meaning.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

Pronouns depend on clear noun antecedents from Unit 1. Pronoun form and reference support later sentence expansion, dialogue, and cumulative editing.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A pronoun is clear if the writer knows what it means.
- Any pronoun can replace any noun.
- `I` and `me` can be swapped by sound.
- Possessive pronouns need apostrophes.
- A reflexive pronoun is just a fancier object pronoun.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Spiral noun and antecedent identification across the unit. Later units should include brief pronoun-clarity edits, especially in two-sentence contexts.

## Unit 3: Verb Phrases And Time

### Unit Purpose

Build flexible verb recognition and control. Students identify action, helping, and linking verbs, then use tense as a time signal that should stay consistent unless meaning changes.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify action verbs and verb phrases in complete sentences.
- Recognize helping verbs that work with main verbs.
- Recognize linking verbs that connect a subject to a describing or naming word.
- Choose simple present, past, and future verb forms.
- Keep verb tense consistent across short contexts.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Verb, action verb, helping verb, main verb, verb phrase, linking verb, subject complement, tense, present, past, future, regular verb, irregular verb, consistent.

### Prerequisites

Students need subject/predicate recognition, basic verb recognition, and noun/pronoun knowledge from Units 1-2.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

Verb recognition prepares students to find direct objects and compound predicates in Unit 4. Tense consistency returns in dialogue and cumulative editing.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Helping and linking verbs are not "real" verbs.
- Every verb shows visible action.
- A verb phrase is only the last word in the predicate.
- Past tense always ends in `ed`.
- Tense shifts are acceptable if each sentence sounds familiar alone.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Review subject/predicate and pronoun agreement in quick items. Use short two-sentence passages for delayed tense review after this unit.

## Unit 4: Expanding Sentences With Purpose

### Unit Purpose

Show students how sentences grow while still staying clear. Students identify direct objects, compound subjects, compound predicates, prepositional phrases, adjectives, and adverbs as meaningful expansion tools.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify a direct object by asking whom or what after an action verb.
- Combine related subjects or predicates without creating run-ons.
- Identify prepositional phrases and their objects in short sentences.
- Use adjectives and adverbs to add precise detail.
- Explain how an expansion changes or sharpens meaning.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Direct object, action verb, whom, what, compound subject, compound predicate, conjunction, preposition, prepositional phrase, object of the preposition, adjective, adverb, modifier, detail, clarity.

### Prerequisites

Students need stable subject and verb recognition, noun/pronoun control, and basic adjective/adverb awareness.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit depends on Units 1-3 and prepares students for commas in series, dialogue editing, diagramming, and cumulative revision. Expanded sentences give punctuation a real structure to mark.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Any word after a verb is the direct object.
- A compound subject makes the sentence compound.
- Adding detail always improves a sentence.
- A preposition is any small word.
- Adjectives and adverbs do the same job.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

5-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Interleave noun, pronoun, and verb identification as students expand sentences. Return to earlier plural, possessive, and pronoun clarity in expansion prompts.

## Unit 5: Punctuation That Shows Structure

### Unit Purpose

Teach punctuation as a signal for structure and speaker meaning. Students learn commas in a series and simple dialogue punctuation with quotation marks, speaker tags, capitalization, and end punctuation.

### Student Outcomes

- Use commas to separate three or more items in a series.
- Identify exact spoken words and place quotation marks around them.
- Use commas with simple speaker tags before or after quoted words.
- Capitalize the first word of a quotation when needed.
- Correct focused punctuation errors in dialogue.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Comma, series, item, quotation marks, dialogue, speaker tag, exact words, capitalization, end punctuation, punctuation inside quotation marks.

### Prerequisites

Students need sentence boundaries, proper noun capitalization, noun/verb recognition, and sentence expansion from earlier units.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

Commas in series build on expanded noun and verb lists from Unit 4. Dialogue punctuation prepares students for cumulative editing and clear reader-facing writing.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A comma belongs wherever a reader pauses.
- A list of two items needs the same commas as a list of three or more.
- Quotation marks go around everything in a sentence with speaking.
- End punctuation belongs outside quotation marks in simple dialogue.
- Speaker tags and spoken words do the same job.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Include noun, proper noun, pronoun, and tense review inside punctuation contexts. Use focused punctuation targets before mixed dialogue editing.

## Unit 6: Visual Grammar And Cumulative Editing

### Unit Purpose

Bring the level together through simple diagramming, focused editing, and mixed grammar transfer. Students use visual relationships and named targets to decide what a sentence needs.

### Student Outcomes

- Read simple diagrams that show subject, verb, object, complement, and modifier relationships.
- Build a simple diagram from a short sentence.
- Edit one named grammar target at a time in short sentences.
- Choose the relevant grammar tool in mixed review.
- Explain why a correction or revision improves clarity.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Sentence diagram, subject line, verb line, direct object, complement, modifier, prepositional phrase, target, edit, revise, clear, specific, mixed review.

### Prerequisites

Students need the full Grammar 2 sequence: nouns, pronouns, verbs, expansion, punctuation, and focused editing experience.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This final unit reviews every prior unit and prepares students for later grammar levels that add clauses, more complex punctuation, advanced sentence patterns, and fuller revision.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A diagram is a drawing separate from sentence meaning.
- Editing means fixing every possible issue at once.
- Mixed review can be solved by guessing the most recent rule.
- A revision is better just because it is longer.
- Grammar labels matter more than reader clarity.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Mix targets deliberately: noun forms, pronoun clarity, verb tense, sentence expansion, series commas, dialogue punctuation, and simple diagrams. Keep passages short and focused so transfer remains grammar work rather than reading endurance.
