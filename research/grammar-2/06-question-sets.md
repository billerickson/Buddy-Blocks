## Unit 1: Sentences And Nouns That Name Clearly

### Lesson 1: Complete Sentences Need Subjects And Predicates

The lesson checks whether students can see the subject-predicate frame behind a complete sentence.

```question
key: u01_l01_q01_complete_sentence
type: multiple-choice
prompt: "Which group of words is a complete sentence?"
choices:
  - "The bright kite."
  - "Flew above the park."
  - "The bright kite flew above the park."
  - "Above the park on a windy day."
correctAnswer: "The bright kite flew above the park."
explanation: "A complete sentence has a subject and a predicate. The bright kite is the subject, and flew above the park is the predicate."
hint: "Look for both who or what the sentence is about and what happened."
questionGoal: "Identify a complete sentence with both subject and predicate."
misconception: "Thinking a capital letter and end mark make a fragment complete."
```

```question
key: u01_l01_q02_subject_predicate_jobs
type: match-pairs
prompt: "Match each sentence part to its job."
pairs:
  - left: "subject"
    right: "names who or what the sentence is about"
  - left: "predicate"
    right: "tells something about the subject"
  - left: "fragment"
    right: "is missing a complete sentence part"
  - left: "complete sentence"
    right: "has a subject and predicate"
explanation: "Subjects and predicates work together to make complete sentences."
hint: "Ask what each part does for the reader."
questionGoal: "Connect subject, predicate, fragment, and complete sentence to their roles."
misconception: "Memorizing labels without knowing the sentence job."
```

```question

hint: "Ask who or what cleaned the brushes."
```
