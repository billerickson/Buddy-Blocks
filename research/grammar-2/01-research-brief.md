# Grammar 2 Research Brief

Track: Grammar  
Level: Level 2  
Intended student profile: students with basic sentence awareness who are ready to build expanded sentences, control common word forms, and edit focused conventions.

This brief defines outcomes and instructional priorities only. It does not define units, lessons, or questions.

## 1. End-Of-Level Outcomes

By the end of Grammar 2, students should be able to identify and use common, proper, plural, and possessive nouns; personal, possessive, and reflexive pronouns; action, helping, and linking verbs; simple verb tense consistency; direct objects; compound subjects and predicates; prepositional phrases; expanded sentence patterns; commas in series and quotations; introductory dialogue punctuation; and simple expanded sentence diagrams. Students should edit short sentences for a named target and revise sentences for clarity and detail.

## 2. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should know basic parts of speech, subject/predicate, sentence types, fragments/run-ons in clear cases, basic capitalization and end punctuation, and simple sentence combining. They should be able to read short sentences and write several connected sentences with support.

## 3. Major Concept Clusters

- **Noun And Pronoun Control:** common/proper nouns, singular/plural, possessive nouns, personal/possessive/reflexive pronouns, and clear antecedents.
- **Verb Phrases:** action, helping, and linking verbs; regular and common irregular tense forms; tense consistency in short contexts.
- **Sentence Expansion:** direct objects, compound subjects/predicates, prepositional phrases, adjectives, and adverbs as meaningful expansion tools.
- **Punctuation For Structure:** commas in series, quotation/dialogue conventions, capitalization with names/titles, and punctuation that marks who spoke.
- **Visual Grammar:** simple diagramming as a way to see sentence relationships, not as a separate worksheet skill.

## 4. Recommended Progression

Start with Level 1 review in complete sentences, then deepen nouns/pronouns and verb phrases. Add direct objects and compound sentence parts before prepositional phrases, because students need stable subject/verb recognition before adding modifiers. Teach punctuation alongside the structures it marks. End with short cumulative editing and sentence-building tasks that ask students to choose the relevant grammar tool.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Possessive `-'s` means plural.
- Pronouns are clear if the writer knows the referent.
- Any word after a verb is the direct object.
- Helping and linking verbs are not "real" verbs.
- A compound subject makes the whole sentence compound.
- A comma belongs wherever a reader pauses.
- Dialogue punctuation is decoration rather than a signal for speaker and words spoken.

## 6. Developmental Constraints And Reading/Load Concerns

Use short, natural sentences. Avoid ambiguous words whose part of speech changes by context until students have a stable pattern. Keep editing targets narrow; mixed editing should come after focused practice. Sentence diagrams should remain simple and visually supportive. Do not let handwriting or spelling load obscure the grammar target.

## 7. Assessment Implications

Use recognition and correction before production. Good item types include multiple-choice for identifying sentence jobs, error-correction for one convention, fill-blank for pronoun/verb forms, order-items for dialogue punctuation sequence, and constructed response for brief sentence expansion. Distractors should reflect real errors: plural/possessive confusion, wrong pronoun case, tense shift, missing comma, and mistaken direct object.

## 8. Spiral Review Priorities

Complete sentences, subject/predicate, noun/verb/adjective/adverb, capitalization, end punctuation, fragments/run-ons, plural and possessive nouns, pronouns and antecedents, tense consistency, prepositional phrases, and sentence expansion.

## 9. Concepts That Should Not Be Taught Yet

Avoid formal subordinate-clause analysis, complex sentence diagramming, active/passive voice as a full system, semicolons/colons, advanced modifier errors, gerunds/participles/infinitives, and long paragraph copyediting as primary assessment.
