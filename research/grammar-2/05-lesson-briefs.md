# Grammar 2 Lesson Briefs

Sources: accepted level blueprint, course map, and unit design briefs in `research/grammar-2/`.

## Unit 1: Sentences And Nouns That Name Clearly

### Lesson 1: Complete Sentences Need Subjects And Predicates

- **Teaching goal:** Rebuild the idea that a complete sentence has a subject and a predicate that work together to express a complete thought.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify whether a short group of words is complete and name the subject or predicate that is missing.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A subject tells who or what the sentence is about; a predicate tells something about the subject.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic noun and verb recognition, capitalization, and end punctuation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A capital letter and end mark make any word group complete; the subject must be the first word; a long fragment is a sentence.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given short examples, the student classifies complete sentences and repairs focused fragments by adding a subject or predicate.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for complete versus fragment; `match-pairs` for subject/predicate roles; `fill-blank` for adding a missing sentence part; `error-correction` for exact fragment repairs.
- **Student-facing `config.intro`:** "A complete sentence needs two working parts: a subject and a predicate. The subject names who or what the sentence is about. The predicate tells what the subject does or is."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short concrete sentences; avoid unusual word order; keep fragment repairs constrained to one obvious missing part.

### Lesson 2: Common And Proper Nouns Name Precisely

- **Teaching goal:** Distinguish common nouns from proper nouns and connect proper nouns to capitalization.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose whether a noun is common or proper and capitalize proper nouns in short sentences.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Common nouns name any person, place, thing, or idea; proper nouns name a specific one.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Nouns name people, places, things, or ideas; sentences begin with capital letters.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Any important noun is proper; all words naming people should be capitalized; a proper noun can stay lowercase inside a sentence.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student sorts nouns, chooses precise proper nouns, and corrects capitalization for names of people, places, days, and titles.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for common/proper pairs; `multiple-choice` for noun classification; `error-correction` for capitalization; `text-input` for constrained capitalization corrections.
- **Student-facing `config.intro`:** "A common noun is a general name, like city or teacher. A proper noun is a specific name, like Dallas or Ms. Chen. Proper nouns begin with capital letters."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar names and places; avoid titles with complex punctuation; do not mix in possessives yet.

### Lesson 3: Singular And Plural Nouns

- **Teaching goal:** Teach singular and plural noun forms as number signals in sentences.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify and form common singular and plural nouns in short contexts.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A singular noun names one; a plural noun names more than one. Many plural nouns add `s` or `es`, while some change spelling.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Common nouns, proper nouns, and basic sentence meaning.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Every plural adds only `s`; any noun ending in `s` is plural; irregular forms such as children are mistakes.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses a singular or plural noun that fits the number clue and corrects common regular plural forms.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for noun choice from context; `multiple-choice` for plural rule recognition; `match-pairs` for singular/plural pairs; `text-input` for exact regular forms.
- **Student-facing `config.intro`:** "A singular noun names one: one fox. A plural noun names more than one: three foxes. Look for number clues such as one, two, many, several, or a count."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use high-frequency regular plurals first; include only a few common irregular forms; avoid possessive apostrophes until the next lesson.

### Lesson 4: Possessive Nouns Show Ownership

- **Teaching goal:** Use possessive nouns to show ownership or close relationship and distinguish possessive forms from plurals.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose and correct singular and regular plural possessive nouns in short sentences.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A possessive noun shows that something belongs to or is connected to someone or something.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Singular and plural nouns, common/proper nouns, and apostrophe awareness.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Possessive `-'s` means more than one; plural nouns always need apostrophes; every noun ending in `s` should get `-'s`.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects the noun that owns something and writes the correct possessive form in constrained contexts.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for identifying the owner; `fill-blank` for choosing possessive forms; `error-correction` for focused apostrophe errors; `text-input` for exact possessive forms.
- **Student-facing `config.intro`:** "A possessive noun shows ownership or a close connection. The girl's backpack means the backpack belongs to the girl. The dogs' bowls means the bowls belong to more than one dog."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep possessive targets singular or regular plural; avoid compound possession and irregular plural possessives.

## Unit 2: Pronouns And Clear Reference

### Lesson 1: Personal Pronouns And Antecedents

- **Teaching goal:** Connect personal pronouns to the nouns they replace or point to.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a pronoun and its antecedent in a short sentence pair.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** An antecedent is the noun a pronoun refers to; clear writing lets the reader tell which noun the pronoun means.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Nouns, complete sentences, and basic pronouns such as he, she, it, they.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A pronoun is clear if the writer knows the noun; the nearest noun is always the antecedent; pronouns can refer to any noun in the passage.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches pronouns to antecedents and identifies unclear pronoun references.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for pronoun-to-antecedent matches; `multiple-choice` for clear reference; `passage-question` for two-sentence antecedent checks; `error-correction` for replacing an unclear pronoun with a noun.
- **Student-facing `config.intro`:** "A pronoun can stand in for a noun. The noun it points to is called the antecedent. A reader should be able to tell exactly which noun the pronoun means."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use two-sentence contexts with no more than two possible nouns; avoid ambiguous gendered names where possible.

### Lesson 2: Subject And Object Pronouns

- **Teaching goal:** Choose subject and object pronouns by sentence job.
- **Student outcome:** The student can select pronouns such as I, he, she, we, they for subjects and me, him, her, us, them for objects.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Pronoun form depends on the job in the sentence: subject forms do the action or are described; object forms receive action or follow many prepositions.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Subjects, predicates, personal pronouns, and antecedents.
- **Likely misconceptions:** `Me` can be a subject if it sounds familiar; `I` is always more correct than `me`; pronoun job does not matter.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses the correct pronoun form in subject and object positions and explains the clue.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for form choice; `multiple-choice` for sentence job; `multi-blank-cloze` for paired subject/object choices; `error-correction` for exact pronoun corrections.
- **Student-facing `config.intro`:** "Use subject pronouns for the subject: I, he, she, we, they. Use object pronouns after an action or after words like to, with, and for: me, him, her, us, them."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep sentences short; avoid compound pronoun subjects such as "Maya and I" until later levels.

### Lesson 3: Possessive Pronouns And Possessive Adjectives

- **Teaching goal:** Distinguish possessive pronouns and possessive adjectives from possessive nouns.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose ownership words such as my, your, his, her, our, their, mine, yours, ours, and theirs without adding apostrophes.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Possessive pronouns and possessive adjectives show ownership, but they do not use apostrophes.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Possessive nouns, pronouns, and antecedents.
- **Likely misconceptions:** `Its`, `hers`, `ours`, and `theirs` need apostrophes; `their` and `there` do the same job; a possessive adjective can stand alone after the noun is removed.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses the correct possessive form for a sentence and corrects apostrophe transfer errors.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for ownership word choice; `multiple-choice` for possessive pronoun versus adjective; `error-correction` for apostrophe errors; `match-pairs` for owner to possessive form.
- **Student-facing `config.intro`:** "Possessive words show ownership. Some come before a noun, like my book and their game. Some stand alone, like mine and theirs. These pronoun forms do not need apostrophes."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid dense homophone instruction; include only context-supported their/there contrasts when needed.

### Lesson 4: Reflexive Pronouns Point Back

- **Teaching goal:** Use reflexive pronouns only when they point back to the subject of the sentence.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose and correct reflexive pronouns such as myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself, ourselves, yourselves, and themselves.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A reflexive pronoun reflects back to the same person or thing named by the subject.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Subject pronouns, object pronouns, antecedents, and sentence subjects.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Reflexive pronouns are fancy object pronouns; `hisself` or `theirselves` are standard forms; any sentence can use myself.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies when a reflexive pronoun is needed and corrects nonstandard or unnecessary reflexive forms.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for reflexive form choice; `multiple-choice` for same-subject reasoning; `error-correction` for nonstandard forms; `match-pairs` for subject to reflexive pronoun.
- **Student-facing `config.intro`:** "A reflexive pronoun points back to the subject. Maya taught herself means Maya did the teaching and Maya received the teaching. Use reflexive forms only when they point back."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one-clause sentences; avoid intensive pronoun terminology; keep names and subjects explicit.

## Unit 3: Verb Phrases And Time

### Lesson 1: Action Verbs And Verb Phrases

- **Teaching goal:** Identify action verbs and simple verb phrases in complete sentences.
- **Student outcome:** The student can find the word or words that tell what the subject does.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A verb phrase can be one action verb or a main verb with helpers.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Subject, predicate, noun, pronoun, and basic action words.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Only physical actions count as verbs; the last word in the sentence is the verb; a verb phrase cannot have more than one word.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects the action verb or full verb phrase in a sentence and distinguishes it from nouns and modifiers.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for verb identification; `fill-blank` for choosing action verbs; `match-pairs` for subjects to fitting actions; `text-input` for exact verb phrase in constrained sentences.
- **Student-facing `config.intro`:** "The verb is the heart of the predicate. It tells what the subject does or helps tell what is happening. Sometimes the verb has more than one word, like is jumping or will read."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use concrete and mental actions; keep verb phrases to one helper plus one main verb in this lesson.

### Lesson 2: Helping Verbs Work With Main Verbs

- **Teaching goal:** Recognize helping verbs as words that work with a main verb to form a verb phrase.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a helping verb and main verb in common verb phrases.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Helping verbs such as is, are, was, were, has, have, had, will, can, and should support the main verb.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Action verbs and simple verb phrases.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Helping verbs can be ignored when naming the verb; the main verb alone always shows the full time or meaning; all small words before verbs are helpers.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies complete verb phrases and chooses a helping verb that fits the sentence.
- **Recommended question types:** `multi-blank-cloze` for helper/main verb pairs; `multiple-choice` for complete verb phrase; `fill-blank` for helping verb choice; `match-pairs` for helper meanings.
- **Student-facing `config.intro`:** "A helping verb works with a main verb. In will visit, will helps the main verb visit. In has finished, has helps the main verb finished."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use common helpers only; avoid perfect/progressive labels; keep explanations about meaning simple.

### Lesson 3: Linking Verbs Connect The Subject

- **Teaching goal:** Distinguish linking verbs from action verbs and identify the word that describes or renames the subject.
- **Student outcome:** The student can recognize common linking verbs and explain what they connect.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A linking verb links the subject to a describing word or naming word; it does not show an action done to an object.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Subjects, predicates, action verbs, helping verbs, adjectives, and nouns.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Every verb shows action; `is` is not a verb; the word after a linking verb is a direct object.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student classifies action versus linking verbs and identifies the complement after a linking verb.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for action/linking classification; `match-pairs` for subject to complement; `fill-blank` for linking verb choice; `error-correction` for mislabeling direct object after a linking verb.
- **Student-facing `config.intro`:** "Some verbs link instead of act. In The soup is hot, is links soup to hot. The subject is not doing hot; hot describes the subject."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use common linking verbs such as am, is, are, was, were, seem, became; avoid advanced complements.

### Lesson 4: Keep Verb Tense Consistent

- **Teaching goal:** Keep simple verb tense consistent in short contexts unless the meaning clearly changes.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose present, past, or future verb forms that match the time clue in a sentence or two-sentence passage.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Verb tense shows time. In a short passage about one time, the verbs should usually stay in that same time.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Action verbs, helping verbs, linking verbs, and common present/past/future forms.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A verb form is correct by itself even when it shifts the passage time; all past tense forms use `ed`; future tense can be shown without a helping verb.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student corrects a tense shift and chooses a verb form that matches a stated time clue.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for tense choice; `passage-question` for short context decisions; `error-correction` for exact tense repair; `order-items` for time-signal sequence.
- **Student-facing `config.intro`:** "Verb tense tells when something happens: now, before, or later. If a passage is telling about one time, keep the verbs in the same time unless the meaning changes."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use two- or three-sentence contexts; avoid rare irregular verbs; include clear time words such as yesterday, today, and tomorrow.

## Unit 4: Expanding Sentences With Purpose

### Lesson 1: Direct Objects Receive The Action

- **Teaching goal:** Identify direct objects after action verbs in short sentences.
- **Student outcome:** The student can ask whom or what after an action verb to find the direct object.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A direct object receives the action of an action verb.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Subjects, action verbs, verb phrases, nouns, and pronouns.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Any word after a verb is a direct object; linking verbs have direct objects; prepositional phrase objects are direct objects.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies direct objects and distinguishes them from complements and prepositional phrase objects in controlled examples.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for direct object identification; `fill-blank` for asking "verb + whom/what"; `match-pairs` for action verbs to direct objects; `error-correction` for invalid direct-object labels.
- **Student-facing `config.intro`:** "After an action verb, ask whom or what. In Ava carried the lantern, carried what? the lantern. The lantern is the direct object."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use action verbs with clear receivers; avoid sentences with both direct objects and prepositional phrases until late in the set.

### Lesson 2: Compound Subjects

- **Teaching goal:** Combine and identify compound subjects that share one predicate.
- **Student outcome:** The student can recognize two or more subjects joined by and or or that do the same predicate.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A compound subject has more than one subject sharing the same predicate.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Subject/predicate, nouns, pronouns, and complete sentences.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A compound subject makes two sentences; the predicate is part of the compound subject; every sentence with and has a compound subject.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies compound subjects and combines two related sentences without changing the predicate meaning.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for identifying the compound subject; `order-items` for combining sentence parts; `multi-blank-cloze` for subject/conjunction choices; `error-correction` for incomplete combinations.
- **Student-facing `config.intro`:** "A compound subject has two or more subjects joined together. In Leo and Nora painted the sign, Leo and Nora share the predicate painted the sign."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use two-part compound subjects; avoid compound subjects with interrupting phrases.

### Lesson 3: Compound Predicates

- **Teaching goal:** Combine and identify compound predicates that share one subject.
- **Student outcome:** The student can recognize two or more actions or verb phrases joined for the same subject.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A compound predicate has one subject doing or being more than one thing.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Subjects, predicates, action verbs, helping verbs, and conjunctions.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Every sentence with two verbs has two subjects; a compound predicate must repeat the subject; a list of nouns after a verb is a compound predicate.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies compound predicates and combines two related sentences with the shared subject written once.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for compound predicate identification; `order-items` for combined sentence order; `multi-blank-cloze` for shared subject and verbs; `error-correction` for repeated or missing subject errors.
- **Student-facing `config.intro`:** "A compound predicate has one subject and two or more predicate parts. In The kite rose and dipped, one subject, the kite, did two actions."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use two action parts joined by and; avoid compound predicates mixed with compound subjects at first.

### Lesson 4: Prepositional Phrases Add Where And When

- **Teaching goal:** Identify prepositional phrases and their objects as detail chunks that often tell where or when.
- **Student outcome:** The student can find a common preposition, its object, and the whole phrase in a short sentence.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A prepositional phrase begins with a preposition and ends with a noun or pronoun object.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Nouns, pronouns, subjects, verbs, and sentence expansion.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Every small word starts a prepositional phrase; the object of a preposition is the sentence's direct object; a phrase can stand alone as a complete sentence.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the phrase and explains whether it adds where, when, or another relationship.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for phrase identification; `match-pairs` for preposition to meaning; `fill-blank` for completing phrases; `error-correction` for fragments made of only prepositional phrases.
- **Student-facing `config.intro`:** "A prepositional phrase starts with a preposition such as in, on, under, after, before, with, or beside. It ends with an object: under the table, after lunch."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use common prepositions; limit sentences to one prepositional phrase before adding contrast examples.

### Lesson 5: Adjectives And Adverbs Add Precise Detail

- **Teaching goal:** Use adjectives and adverbs as modifiers that add precise but useful detail.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose whether a modifier describes a noun or a verb and select a detail that improves clarity.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Adjectives usually describe nouns; adverbs often describe verbs by telling how, when, or where.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Nouns, verbs, prepositional phrases, and sentence meaning.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Adjectives and adverbs do the same job; more modifiers always improve a sentence; an adverb must end in `ly`.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies what a modifier describes and chooses a detail that makes a sentence clearer without changing its meaning.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for modifier to word described; `multiple-choice` for best detail; `fill-blank` for adjective/adverb choice; `error-correction` for misplaced or wrong-type modifiers.
- **Student-facing `config.intro`:** "Modifiers add detail. An adjective describes a noun, as in bright star. An adverb can describe a verb, as in whispered softly. Good details make meaning clearer."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar adjectives and adverbs; avoid comparative and superlative forms; keep revision choices short.

## Unit 5: Punctuation That Shows Structure

### Lesson 1: Commas In A Series

- **Teaching goal:** Use commas to separate three or more items in a series.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a series and place commas between the items.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A series is a list of three or more items; commas help readers see the items separately.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Nouns, verbs, adjectives, sentence expansion, and commas as punctuation marks.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Any pause needs a comma; two items need a series comma; commas can go after every word in a sentence.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student punctuates series of nouns, adjectives, or short phrases and avoids adding commas to two-item lists.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for identifying a series; `error-correction` for exact comma placement; `fill-blank` for choosing the correctly punctuated sentence; `order-items` for item sequence with punctuation.
- **Student-facing `config.intro`:** "A series is a list of three or more items. Use commas to separate the items: We packed socks, maps, snacks, and water."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use simple list sentences; avoid coordinate adjectives and complex series structures.

### Lesson 2: Quotation Marks For Exact Words

- **Teaching goal:** Place quotation marks around only the exact words a speaker says.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify quoted words and choose correct quotation mark placement in simple dialogue.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Quotation marks hug the speaker's exact words, not the speaker tag.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Sentence boundaries, capitalization, subjects and verbs, and simple speaker tags.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Quotation marks go around the whole sentence; quotation marks go only around the speaker's name; any sentence with said needs quotation marks around every word.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies exact spoken words and selects or corrects quotation mark placement.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for exact words; `fill-blank` for quotation mark placement decisions; `error-correction` for exact punctuation repair; `dialogue-builder` for choosing a spoken line that matches context.
- **Student-facing `config.intro`:** "Quotation marks show the exact words someone says. In Mom said, \"Bring your coat,\" the exact words are Bring your coat."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one quoted sentence at a time; avoid split quotations.

### Lesson 3: Speaker Tags And Dialogue Commas

- **Teaching goal:** Use commas to connect simple speaker tags with quoted words.
- **Student outcome:** The student can punctuate dialogue when the speaker tag comes before or after the quotation.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A speaker tag tells who spoke; a comma often separates the tag from the exact words in simple dialogue.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Quotation marks, exact spoken words, sentence boundaries, and commas.
- **Likely misconceptions:** The comma belongs outside all quotation marks; the speaker tag is part of the exact words; the comma can be skipped because quotation marks are enough.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses correctly punctuated dialogue with tag-before and tag-after patterns.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for correct punctuation; `error-correction` for exact dialogue repair; `order-items` for arranging tag, comma, quote, and words; `dialogue-builder` for connecting speaker and line.
- **Student-facing `config.intro`:** "A speaker tag tells who spoke, such as Lena said. When the tag comes before the exact words, use a comma before the opening quotation mark: Lena said, \"Wait here.\""
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use said and asked as speaker tags; avoid exclamation-heavy dialogue and split quotations.

### Lesson 4: Capitalization And Punctuation In Dialogue

- **Teaching goal:** Combine capitalization, end punctuation, quotation marks, and simple speaker tags in dialogue.
- **Student outcome:** The student can correct a focused dialogue sentence with the first word of a quotation capitalized and end punctuation inside the quotation marks.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** In simple dialogue, the spoken sentence begins with a capital letter and its end punctuation stays inside the closing quotation mark.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Quotation marks, speaker tags, commas, end punctuation, and capitalization.
- **Likely misconceptions:** End punctuation belongs outside the quotation marks; the first word of quoted speech can stay lowercase; punctuation can be fixed without checking the exact words.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student repairs a simple dialogue sentence and explains which punctuation mark shows the speaker's exact words.
- **Recommended question types:** `error-correction` for exact dialogue edits; `multiple-choice` for correct sentence; `multi-blank-cloze` for punctuation choices; `dialogue-builder` for choosing the correctly punctuated line.
- **Student-facing `config.intro`:** "When a quotation is a full sentence, begin the quoted words with a capital letter. Put the period or question mark inside the closing quotation mark: Ben asked, \"May I help?\""
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep dialogue to one sentence; include only periods and question marks; avoid split or interrupted quotations.

## Unit 6: Visual Grammar And Cumulative Editing

### Lesson 1: Read A Simple Sentence Diagram

- **Teaching goal:** Interpret simple sentence diagrams as visual models of sentence relationships.
- **Student outcome:** The student can match a simple diagram to a sentence and name the subject, verb, direct object or complement, and one modifier.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** A simple diagram places the main subject and verb on the main line, then shows objects, complements, and modifiers in their own positions.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Subjects, verbs, direct objects, linking verbs, complements, adjectives, adverbs, and prepositional phrases.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A diagram is unrelated to meaning; the longest word must be the subject; modifiers belong on the main line.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student reads a labeled diagram and chooses the sentence or word relationship it represents.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for diagram part to sentence job; `multiple-choice` for matching sentence to diagram; `fill-blank` for naming diagram positions; `order-items` for main-line order.
- **Student-facing `config.intro`:** "A sentence diagram is a picture of word jobs. The main line shows the subject and verb. Other words branch from the words they describe or complete."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use text-based diagram labels, not complex drawn notation; limit to one object, complement, or modifier at a time.

### Lesson 2: Build A Simple Sentence Diagram

- **Teaching goal:** Build a simple diagram for familiar sentence patterns.
- **Student outcome:** The student can place subject, verb, direct object or complement, and one modifier into a simple diagram structure.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Diagramming begins by finding the main subject and verb, then placing completing or describing words where they belong.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Reading simple diagrams, subjects, verbs, direct objects, linking complements, and modifiers.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Start with modifiers before the main line; place every word in sentence order; treat direct objects and complements as the same relationship.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student orders or matches diagram parts for a short sentence and distinguishes action-verb and linking-verb patterns.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for diagram-building steps; `match-pairs` for words to diagram roles; `multiple-choice` for correct diagram structure; `multi-blank-cloze` for subject/verb/object slots.
- **Student-facing `config.intro`:** "To build a simple diagram, find the subject first, then the verb. Ask whether the verb has a receiver, a describing word, or a modifier connected to another word."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short sentences of five to eight words; avoid multiple modifiers or compound parts in diagram-building prompts.

### Lesson 3: Edit One Target At A Time

- **Teaching goal:** Use a named grammar target to make focused sentence corrections without changing unrelated correct parts.
- **Student outcome:** The student can correct one target type, such as possessive noun, pronoun clarity, verb tense, or punctuation, in a short sentence.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Focused editing starts by naming the target, finding the evidence, and changing only what needs to change.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Noun forms, pronouns, verb tense, sentence expansion, commas, and dialogue punctuation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Editing means rewriting everything; any sentence with an error must have many errors; a correction is better if it changes more words.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the named target and selects or types the exact correction.
- **Recommended question types:** `error-correction` for exact edits; `multiple-choice` for target identification; `fill-blank` for corrected forms; `passage-question` for focused mini-passage edits.
- **Student-facing `config.intro`:** "When you edit, first ask: What target am I checking? Then find the clue and change only the part that needs repair."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep each item to one named target; avoid requiring students to fix several unrelated errors at once.

### Lesson 4: Choose The Grammar Tool In Mixed Review

- **Teaching goal:** Transfer Grammar 2 knowledge by choosing the relevant tool for a mixed sentence or mini-passage.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether a prompt requires noun form, pronoun clarity, verb tense, expansion, punctuation, diagram reasoning, or revision for clarity.
- **Key idea, model, or representation:** Mixed review begins with diagnosis: name the grammar job before choosing the answer.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** All prior Grammar 2 concepts.
- **Likely misconceptions:** The newest rule is always the one being tested; a familiar-looking mark such as apostrophe or comma always has the same job; longer answers are automatically better revisions.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects the grammar concept being tested, corrects or completes the sentence, and explains the clue in concise feedback.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for choosing the grammar tool; `passage-question` for short mixed contexts; `error-correction` for focused final edits; `match-pairs` for concept-to-clue review.
- **Student-facing `config.intro`:** "In mixed review, pause before answering. Ask, What grammar job is this? Look for the clue: ownership, more than one, pronoun reference, verb time, sentence part, list, dialogue, or diagram relationship."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short mixed prompts; keep each question focused on one best diagnosis even when the sentence includes other correct grammar features.
