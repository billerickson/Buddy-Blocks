# Grammar 1 Level Blueprint

Track: Grammar
Level: Level 1
Source: Accepted research brief at `research/grammar-1/01-research-brief.md`

## 1. Level Mission

Grammar 1 gives beginning formal grammar students a clear, usable language for how English sentences work. Students learn to see a sentence as a complete thought with a subject and predicate, identify the major parts of speech by the jobs words do, use foundational capitalization and punctuation to make meaning clear, and revise short sentences with purpose. By the end of the level, grammar should feel like a practical writing tool: students can explain a choice, fix a targeted error, combine or expand a sentence, and carry those habits into reading and writing rather than merely naming labels in isolation.

## 2. Durable Student Outcomes

By the end of Grammar 1, students should be able to:

1. Distinguish complete sentences from fragments and run-ons in straightforward examples.
2. Identify simple subjects and predicates and explain how they work together to make a complete thought.
3. Identify and use nouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs, pronouns, prepositions, conjunctions, articles, and determiners by their sentence jobs.
4. Use correct capitalization for sentence beginnings, the pronoun `I`, proper nouns, days, months, titles, and familiar place names.
5. Choose appropriate end punctuation for statements, questions, exclamations, and commands.
6. Use common noun, pronoun, verb, adjective, and adverb forms accurately in short sentences.
7. Use apostrophes for common possessive nouns and avoid confusing possessives with simple plurals.
8. Use commas in simple series, dates, place phrases, and introductory dialogue tags when the structure is clear.
9. Expand and combine short sentences to add detail, connect related ideas, and improve sentence variety.
10. Edit a short sentence or compact passage for one or two targeted conventions while preserving the intended meaning.

## 3. Concept And Skill Progression

The level begins with sentence sense. Students first decide whether a word group expresses a complete thought, then identify the subject and predicate that carry the sentence's meaning. This opening work keeps examples short and concrete so students can separate sentence completeness from sentence length.

After sentence boundaries are stable, students learn the first grammar labels as sentence jobs. Nouns and verbs come first because they anchor the subject and predicate. Articles, determiners, pronouns, plurals, and possessives are introduced through the noun system, with capitalization connected to proper nouns from the beginning. Students then study verbs as actions, states of being, and time markers in simple sentences.

Next, students add precision through modifiers. Adjectives modify nouns; adverbs modify verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs. Comparative and superlative forms are taught as meaning choices, not only spelling patterns. Students practice deciding whether a sentence needs a noun detail, a verb detail, or a clearer word form.

The middle of the level adds connecting and locating structures: prepositions, prepositional phrases, conjunctions, and simple sentence expansion. Students learn that some word groups add information without being complete sentences on their own, while conjunctions can join related words, phrases, or simple ideas. Formal clause taxonomy is kept light; the goal is functional awareness that supports sentence combining and editing.

Mechanics are interleaved throughout but receive focused treatment after sentence boundaries and parts of speech are secure. Students connect punctuation and capitalization to meaning: sentence type, names, list separation, dates, place phrases, ownership, and dialogue. Commas are taught as signals for specific structures rather than as places to pause.

The level ends with cumulative editing and revision. Students choose the grammar target before fixing it, compare sentence versions, expand sparse sentences, combine choppy ones, and explain a small number of choices in age-appropriate language. Mixed practice is compact and targeted so working memory is not overwhelmed.

## 4. Vocabulary, Representation, Model, Text, And Form Progression

Early vocabulary should include sentence, complete thought, fragment, run-on, subject, predicate, statement, question, exclamation, command, capital letter, and end punctuation. Representations should include simple sentence frames such as `Who or what?` plus `What happens or is true?`, two-part subject/predicate splits, and punctuation choice charts.

Parts-of-speech vocabulary should grow from high-utility terms to more specific labels: noun, proper noun, common noun, singular, plural, possessive, article, determiner, pronoun, antecedent, verb, action verb, being verb, tense, adjective, compare, superlative, adverb, preposition, prepositional phrase, conjunction, and compound.

Mechanics vocabulary should include uppercase, lowercase, title, month, day, comma, series, date, place, quotation marks, speaker tag, apostrophe, ownership, contraction only as exposure, edit, revise, expand, combine, and sentence variety.

Text forms should begin with isolated words and short sentences, then move to pairs of related sentences, brief dialogue snippets, and compact two- to four-sentence editing passages. Response forms should progress from recognition and matching to selected correction, short typed corrections, simple sentence combining, and brief explanations using frames such as `This is a sentence because...`, `The adjective describes...`, and `I used a comma because...`.

## 5. Fluency Expectations

Grammar 1 fluency means quick, accurate recognition of high-frequency sentence jobs and conventions in simple contexts. Students should become efficient at finding the subject and predicate in straightforward sentences, identifying nouns and verbs, choosing statement/question/exclamation punctuation, capitalizing obvious proper nouns and the pronoun `I`, forming common plurals and possessives, and checking a short sentence for one named target.

Fluency should not be measured mainly by speed. Short retrieval and editing bursts are useful after instruction, but the strongest evidence is flexible accuracy: the student can apply a convention after a delay, in a new sentence, or while also preserving meaning.

## 6. Conceptual Understanding Expectations

Students should understand that grammar labels name jobs words and marks perform in sentences. A noun can name a person, place, thing, or idea; a verb tells what the subject does or what is true about the subject; modifiers add information to another word; and punctuation helps a reader hear sentence meaning and boundaries.

Students should understand that a sentence is complete because it expresses a complete thought, not because it is long or contains impressive words. They should understand that subjects and predicates work together, that pronouns need clear noun referents, that commas mark particular structures, and that apostrophes for ownership differ from plural endings.

Students should also understand that grammar supports writing choices. A revised sentence may be clearer because it adds a specific adjective, shifts a vague pronoun to a noun, combines related ideas, fixes a run-on, or uses punctuation to show how ideas belong together.

## 7. Application And Transfer Expectations

Students should apply grammar knowledge in short, meaningful sentences drawn from everyday life, literature-style narration, history and science contexts, and student-writing-like drafts. They should not rely on surface tricks such as looking for the longest sentence, putting commas where they pause, or choosing an operation because a word sounds grammatical.

Transfer is visible when students can meet an unfamiliar sentence, identify the relevant grammar job, and choose a correction or revision that preserves meaning. A student might capitalize `Monday`, replace unclear `it` with `the kite`, combine two choppy sentences with `because`, choose `ran quickly` over `ran quick`, or explain why `The tall tree` is a fragment even though it has several words.

By the end of the level, students should be prepared for Grammar 2 work with more detailed noun/pronoun forms, helping and linking verbs, compound subjects and predicates, direct objects, expanded prepositional phrases, quotation punctuation, and simple diagramming.

## 8. Review And Spacing Plan

Review should begin immediately and stay cumulative. Sentence completeness, subject/predicate recognition, capitalization, and end punctuation should reappear in nearly every later unit because they support all other grammar decisions.

Parts of speech should be spaced by job and then mixed. Nouns and verbs should be revisited while teaching modifiers; pronouns should return in verb and editing lessons; prepositions and conjunctions should review sentence completeness so students do not mistake every word group for a sentence.

Mechanics should be interleaved with writing tasks. Capitalization and end punctuation should appear in early sentence work, commas should return during combining and dialogue, and apostrophes should be reviewed in cumulative editing after plurals and possessives are taught.

Question design should move from exposure and recognition toward correction, explanation, and production. Later review should use diagnostic distractors: fragment versus sentence, noun versus adjective, plural versus possessive, adjective versus adverb, comma in a list versus comma splice, and unclear pronoun reference.

## 9. Mastery Evidence For The End Of The Level

End-of-level mastery should be shown through a balanced body of compact tasks. A student who has mastered Grammar 1 can identify complete sentences, subjects, predicates, and major parts of speech in simple sentences; choose and explain common punctuation and capitalization choices; correct targeted errors in plurals, possessives, pronouns, verb forms, modifiers, commas, and sentence boundaries; and revise short sentences for detail, clarity, and connection.

The strongest evidence includes transfer into writing. The student can improve a short draft-like passage without changing its meaning, choose which grammar target needs attention, and explain the reason for one or two edits using accurate age-appropriate language.

## Source And Product Constraint Note

This blueprint is derived from the accepted Grammar 1 research brief. The V3 product constraints shape the artifact level and authoring stance: build top-down from research, keep lessons compact but instructionally serious, teach grammar in meaningful sentence and writing contexts, use exposure before unsupported recall, choose question types by learning job, write diagnostic distractors, include useful feedback and optional second-attempt hints, and reserve final runtime curriculum implementation for a later accepted stage.
