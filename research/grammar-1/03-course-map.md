# Grammar 1 Course Map

Track: Grammar
Level: Level 1
Sources: Accepted research brief at `research/grammar-1/01-research-brief.md` and level blueprint at `research/grammar-1/02-level-blueprint.md`

## Course Design Notes

Grammar 1 moves from sentence awareness into word jobs, word forms, mechanics, and cumulative revision. The map is intentionally uneven: sentence foundations and core word classes need more time because they carry the rest of the level, while the final unit is a compact transfer unit. Mechanics are introduced early in context, then taught more directly after students have stable sentence boundaries and parts-of-speech language.

## Unit 1: Sentences, Subjects, And Predicates

### Unit Purpose

Build the foundation for all later grammar work: a sentence expresses a complete thought, usually with a subject and predicate, and punctuation marks the sentence's purpose. Students learn to recognize complete sentences before they are asked to edit fragments, run-ons, or more detailed word jobs.

### Student Outcomes

- Distinguish complete sentences from fragments in straightforward cases.
- Identify the simple subject and simple predicate in short sentences.
- Choose end punctuation for statements, questions, exclamations, and commands.
- Recognize common run-ons and repair them with a period or a conjunction.
- Explain sentence completeness using age-appropriate language.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Sentence, complete thought, fragment, run-on, subject, predicate, statement, question, exclamation, command, period, question mark, exclamation point, capital letter, conjunction as exposure.

### Prerequisites

Students should read and write simple sentences, understand word order in common English sentences, and recognize basic end punctuation in familiar text.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit turns informal sentence knowledge into explicit language. It prepares Unit 2 noun and pronoun work because subjects are often nouns or pronouns, Unit 3 verb work because predicates need verbs, Unit 6 mechanics because punctuation depends on sentence boundaries, and Unit 7 editing because fragments and run-ons are high-priority cumulative targets.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A long word group must be a sentence.
- A subject is always the first word.
- A predicate is any group of words after a noun, even if there is no verb.
- Every sentence should end with a period.
- A run-on is fixed by adding a comma anywhere.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Notes On Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving

Return to complete sentences, subject/predicate splits, capitalization, and end punctuation in every later unit. Later parts-of-speech lessons should use complete sentences as the default context and occasionally ask why a word group is not yet complete.

## Unit 2: Nouns, Articles, Pronouns, And Ownership

### Unit Purpose

Teach the noun system as a set of useful sentence tools: naming words, noun markers, pronouns that stand for nouns, plural forms, and possessive forms. Students connect nouns to subjects and capitalization, then learn how word forms change meaning.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify common and proper nouns in simple sentences.
- Use articles and determiners such as `a`, `an`, `the`, `this`, `that`, `these`, and `those` to point to nouns.
- Form common singular and plural nouns, including a small set of irregular plurals.
- Use apostrophes to show simple singular ownership.
- Choose pronouns with clear noun referents in short sentences.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Noun, common noun, proper noun, person, place, thing, idea, article, determiner, singular, plural, irregular plural, possessive noun, apostrophe, ownership, pronoun, antecedent, clear referent.

### Prerequisites

Students need basic sentence completeness, subject awareness, capitalization at the beginning of sentences, and informal familiarity with naming words.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit extends Unit 1 by showing that subjects are often nouns or pronouns. It prepares Unit 3 because verbs must agree with noun and pronoun subjects in simple cases, Unit 4 because adjectives modify nouns, and Unit 7 because unclear pronouns and plural/possessive confusions are common editing targets.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Only people are nouns.
- Proper nouns are important words rather than specific names.
- Adding apostrophe-s makes a word plural.
- Pronouns are always clear because the writer knows what they mean.
- `a`, `an`, and `the` are unimportant small words instead of noun signals.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Notes On Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving

Review sentence subjects while identifying nouns. Spiral capitalization of proper nouns, end punctuation, and sentence completeness. Possessives should return during comma and dialogue editing so apostrophes are not isolated in one lesson only.

## Unit 3: Verbs, Time, And Agreement

### Unit Purpose

Make verbs visible as the engine of the predicate. Students identify action and being verbs, use simple verb tenses, choose common irregular forms, and maintain basic subject-verb agreement in short sentences.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify verbs that show action or state of being in simple sentences.
- Distinguish past, present, and future time in common verb phrases.
- Choose regular past-tense and present-tense forms in short sentences.
- Use common irregular forms such as `went`, `saw`, `ran`, `came`, `is`, `are`, `was`, and `were`.
- Choose simple subject-verb agreement with singular and plural subjects.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Verb, action verb, being verb, predicate, tense, past, present, future, helping word as exposure, regular verb, irregular verb, subject-verb agreement, singular, plural.

### Prerequisites

Students should know sentence, subject, predicate, noun, pronoun, singular, plural, and basic time words such as yesterday, today, and tomorrow.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit depends on Units 1-2 and prepares Unit 4 because adverbs modify verbs. It also prepares sentence expansion, combining, and cumulative editing because students need to preserve verb time and agreement when revising.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A verb is only a visible action.
- The word closest to the verb always controls agreement.
- Past tense always adds `-ed`.
- Future time always needs a complicated phrase.
- A sentence can have a subject and still be incomplete if it lacks a verb.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Notes On Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving

Keep noun/pronoun subjects active in every verb lesson. Interleave verb tense with sentence type and end punctuation, and delay dense tense comparisons until students can identify the verb confidently.

## Unit 4: Adjectives, Adverbs, And Precise Description

### Unit Purpose

Show how modifiers make sentences more precise. Students identify adjectives and adverbs by what they modify, choose comparative and superlative forms, and revise short sentences by adding or changing details without overloading the sentence.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify adjectives that describe or limit nouns.
- Identify adverbs that tell how, when, or where about verbs.
- Distinguish adjective jobs from adverb jobs in clear examples.
- Use common comparative and superlative adjective forms.
- Revise a bare sentence by adding precise but relevant modifiers.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Adjective, noun, describe, which one, what kind, how many, compare, comparative, superlative, adverb, verb, how, when, where, precise, detail, revise.

### Prerequisites

Students need noun and verb identification, simple sentence completeness, and basic understanding of singular/plural noun forms.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit builds on Units 2-3 and prepares Unit 5 sentence expansion because many details enter through modifiers and phrases. It also supports Unit 7 revision by giving students a way to improve clarity rather than only correct errors.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Any interesting word is an adjective.
- Adjectives describe verbs.
- All adverbs end in `-ly`.
- `More` and `most` can be added to any adjective form.
- Adding many modifiers always makes writing better.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

3-4 lessons.

### Notes On Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving

Review nouns and verbs through modifier questions: `Which word is described?` and `Which action is described?` Interleave precise modifier choice with capitalization and punctuation so sentences remain complete and readable.

## Unit 5: Prepositions, Conjunctions, And Sentence Expansion

### Unit Purpose

Teach students how word groups add location, time, and connection. Prepositions create phrases that add information, while conjunctions connect related words or ideas. Students use this knowledge to expand and combine sentences without creating fragments or run-ons.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify common prepositions and the noun or pronoun that completes a prepositional phrase.
- Use prepositional phrases to add where or when information.
- Identify and use coordinating conjunctions `and`, `but`, and `or` in clear cases.
- Use `because` to connect a reason to a simple sentence when the sentence remains complete.
- Combine or expand short sentences while preserving meaning and punctuation.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Preposition, prepositional phrase, object of the preposition as exposure, where, when, phrase, conjunction, connect, and, but, or, because, combine, expand, complete thought, run-on.

### Prerequisites

Students should identify complete sentences, subjects, predicates, nouns, pronouns, verbs, adjectives, and adverbs in clear examples.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit extends all prior grammar work into sentence building. It prepares Unit 6 commas because lists, dialogue tags, and some place/date phrases depend on grouping, and it prepares Unit 7 cumulative revision because sentence combining is a major transfer skill.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A prepositional phrase can stand alone as a complete sentence.
- Any word that connects ideas can fix a run-on without punctuation.
- `And` is the best connector for every relationship.
- `Because` always starts a complete sentence by itself.
- Longer sentences are automatically stronger.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Notes On Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving

Review fragment detection whenever students add phrases. Return to subject/predicate splits after combining so students see that the new sentence still needs a complete core. Spiral adjective/adverb work by comparing single-word details with phrase details.

## Unit 6: Capitalization, Commas, And Dialogue Signals

### Unit Purpose

Give focused attention to high-utility mechanics once students can recognize sentence structures. Students learn capitalization rules, commas in specific simple structures, and introductory dialogue punctuation as signals that help readers understand names, groups, time, place, and speakers.

### Student Outcomes

- Capitalize sentence beginnings, the pronoun `I`, proper nouns, days, months, titles, and familiar place names.
- Use commas to separate three or more items in a series.
- Use commas in simple dates and place phrases.
- Use commas and quotation marks in a short dialogue sentence with a speaker tag.
- Explain a mechanics choice by naming the structure it marks.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Capitalization, uppercase, lowercase, proper noun, title, day, month, comma, series, list, date, place, address as exposure, quotation marks, dialogue, speaker tag, exact words, edit.

### Prerequisites

Students need sentence boundaries, common/proper nouns, basic punctuation, phrase awareness, conjunctions, and short editing experience.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit consolidates mechanics introduced across Units 1-5. It prepares Unit 7 mixed editing, where students must decide which convention is relevant instead of applying the most recent rule.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Capital letters mark words that feel important, not specific names or sentence beginnings.
- A comma belongs wherever the reader pauses.
- A list of two items needs the same comma pattern as a list of three or more.
- Quotation marks go around every sentence about speaking.
- Dialogue punctuation can ignore who is speaking.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Notes On Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving

Every mechanics lesson should continue to review sentence completeness and parts of speech. Use short, targeted edits rather than long passages. Spiral proper nouns, possessives, conjunctions, and phrase boundaries through punctuation practice.

## Unit 7: Cumulative Editing And Grammar For Writing

### Unit Purpose

Bring the level together through targeted editing, revision, and explanation. Students practice choosing the grammar issue, making a correction or improvement, and explaining how the change helps the reader.

### Student Outcomes

- Edit compact sentences and short passages for one or two named targets.
- Distinguish fixing an error from revising for clarity or variety.
- Combine and expand sentences to reduce choppiness without making run-ons.
- Explain grammar choices using accurate, age-appropriate terms.
- Transfer Grammar 1 skills into a short capstone editing task.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Edit, revise, target, convention, clarity, variety, complete thought, fragment, run-on, noun, pronoun, verb, modifier, prepositional phrase, conjunction, capitalization, punctuation, explain, evidence.

### Prerequisites

Students need all earlier Grammar 1 concepts: sentence boundaries, subjects and predicates, major parts of speech, word forms, punctuation, capitalization, commas, and simple combining.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit reviews and applies all prior units. It prepares Grammar 2 by strengthening the habits students will need for more detailed noun/pronoun forms, verb work, compound structures, direct objects, expanded phrases, dialogue punctuation, and simple diagramming.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Editing means fixing every possible issue at once.
- Revision only means making a sentence longer.
- A correction can change the meaning as long as the grammar looks better.
- Grammar explanations are just labels, not reasons.
- Mixed review should be solved by guessing the most recent rule.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

3-4 lessons.

### Notes On Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving

Use cumulative sets with delayed review of all high-priority targets. Keep passages short enough that grammar, not reading stamina, is the assessment focus. Include both selected corrections and brief written production so transfer is visible.
