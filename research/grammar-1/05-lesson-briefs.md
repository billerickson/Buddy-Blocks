# Grammar 1 Lesson Briefs

Sources: accepted level blueprint, course map, and unit design briefs in `research/grammar-1/`.

## Unit 1: Sentences, Subjects, And Predicates

### Lesson 1: Complete Thoughts

- **Teaching goal:** Teach that a sentence must express a complete thought, not merely contain several words.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether a word group is a complete sentence and explain what is missing when it is not.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Use the frame `Who or what?` plus `What happens or is true?` to test completeness.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Reading short word groups, recognizing capital letters and end marks, and understanding familiar subject-action sentences.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating a long phrase as a sentence; assuming end punctuation alone makes a sentence complete; rejecting short commands because the subject is not stated.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a mix of complete sentences and fragments, the student correctly classifies each and names the missing idea in clear cases.
- **Recommended question types and why each fits:** `multiple-choice` for first recognition; `match-pairs` for sentence/non-sentence sorting; `error-correction` for adding missing words; `constructed-response` for one-sentence explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Contrast `The bright red kite` with `The bright red kite flew over the fence.` Ask which one tells a whole idea.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep word groups short and concrete; avoid advanced fragments, implied subjects except simple commands, and ambiguous poetic examples.

### Lesson 2: Subjects And Predicates

- **Teaching goal:** Show that most sentences have a subject and predicate working together.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify the simple subject and simple predicate in straightforward sentences.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Split the sentence into `who or what the sentence is about` and `what is said about it`.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Complete sentence recognition and basic understanding of naming and action words.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Choosing the first word as the subject even when it is an article; selecting the whole sentence as the predicate; missing being verbs.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can mark or choose the subject and predicate and explain that both are needed for the sentence to work.
- **Recommended question types and why each fits:** `multiple-choice` for identifying parts; `multi-blank-cloze` for completing subject/predicate frames; `match-pairs` for matching subjects to predicates; `constructed-response` for explaining a split.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use two colors or brackets to split sentences such as `The small boat / drifted across the pond.`
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use simple declarative sentences; avoid compound subjects/predicates and long introductory phrases.

### Lesson 3: Sentence Types And End Marks

- **Teaching goal:** Connect sentence purpose to end punctuation.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify statements, questions, exclamations, and commands and choose a fitting end mark.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Sentence type is determined by the job the sentence does: tell, ask, show strong feeling, or give direction.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Complete sentence recognition and familiarity with period, question mark, and exclamation point.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Using an exclamation point for every important sentence; using a period for all commands; identifying questions only by word order, not meaning.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can choose the end punctuation and name the sentence type in clear examples.
- **Recommended question types and why each fits:** `fill-blank` for punctuation insertion; `multiple-choice` for sentence type; `match-pairs` for type-to-end-mark relationships; `error-correction` for replacing a wrong end mark.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Read the same base sentence with different purposes and show how the end mark changes reader voice.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one-sentence prompts; avoid indirect questions and quotations.

### Lesson 4: Fragments And Run-Ons

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to repair incomplete and overconnected sentences in straightforward cases.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a fragment or run-on and choose a correction that creates one or two complete sentences.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A fragment is missing a complete thought; a run-on pushes two complete thoughts together without correct separation or connection.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Complete thoughts, subjects/predicates, end punctuation, and basic conjunction exposure.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Fixing a fragment by adding punctuation only; fixing a run-on with a comma alone; combining ideas without checking each complete thought.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can correct common fragments and run-ons while preserving meaning.
- **Recommended question types and why each fits:** `multiple-choice` for diagnosis; `error-correction` for sentence repair; `order-items` for separating sentence parts; `constructed-response` for explaining the fix.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Place `The rain stopped we went outside.` beside `The rain stopped. We went outside.` and ask what helped the reader.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep run-ons to two short complete thoughts; avoid semicolons, comma-splice terminology, and complex clauses.

## Unit 2: Nouns, Articles, Pronouns, And Ownership

### Lesson 1: Common And Proper Nouns

- **Teaching goal:** Teach nouns as naming words and distinguish common nouns from specific proper nouns.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify common and proper nouns and capitalize proper nouns in simple sentences.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Common nouns name general people, places, things, or ideas; proper nouns name specific ones.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Complete sentences, subjects, capital letters, and familiar names.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Capitalizing any important noun; thinking only people are nouns; missing place and idea nouns.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies nouns, sorts common/proper examples, and corrects proper noun capitalization.
- **Recommended question types and why each fits:** `match-pairs` for category sorting; `multiple-choice` for recognition; `error-correction` for capitalization; `constructed-response` for explaining common versus proper.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Pair `city` with `Dallas`, `girl` with `Maya`, and `month` with `April` to show general versus specific naming.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar people, pets, places, months, and school objects; avoid abstract nouns until after concrete examples.

### Lesson 2: Articles, Determiners, And Noun Signals

- **Teaching goal:** Show that articles and determiners often signal or clarify nouns.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use words such as `a`, `an`, `the`, `this`, `that`, `these`, and `those` to find and choose nouns accurately.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Small pointing words help readers know which noun or how many nouns the sentence means.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Noun recognition, singular/plural meaning as exposure, and simple sentence reading.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Ignoring articles as meaningless; using `a` before vowel sounds; using `this` with plural nouns or `these` with singular nouns.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses an article or determiner that fits the noun and uses noun signals to identify the noun.
- **Recommended question types and why each fits:** `fill-blank` for choosing signals; `multiple-choice` for identifying the noun after a signal; `match-pairs` for singular/plural determiner relationships; `error-correction` for mismatched signal+noun pairs.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Read `a apple`, `an apple`, `this books`, and `these books` aloud and ask which sounds and meanings fit.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use common nouns and short phrases; do not over-teach phonetic exceptions beyond vowel-sound awareness.

### Lesson 3: Singular, Plural, And Irregular Nouns

- **Teaching goal:** Teach plural nouns as forms that show more than one, including common regular and irregular patterns.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose singular, regular plural, and common irregular plural forms in short contexts.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Plural forms change the noun to show number; some plurals follow common spelling patterns and some must be learned.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Nouns, articles/determiners, singular/plural meaning, and sentence context clues.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Adding `s` to every plural; using apostrophes for plurals; using plural determiners with singular nouns.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student forms or chooses plurals such as `cats`, `boxes`, `babies`, `children`, `feet`, and `mice` in clear contexts.
- **Recommended question types and why each fits:** `fill-blank` for form choice; `match-pairs` for singular/plural pairs; `text-input` for supported production; `error-correction` for apostrophe and irregular-form errors.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Sort plural patterns by sound and spelling before asking students to type any forms.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Limit irregular plurals to high-frequency examples; avoid possessive plural apostrophes.

### Lesson 4: Possessives And Clear Pronouns

- **Teaching goal:** Teach singular possessive nouns and clear pronoun reference as relationship tools.
- **Student outcome:** The student can show simple ownership with apostrophe-s and choose pronouns that clearly refer to a noun.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Possessives show whose; pronouns replace nouns only when the reader can tell which noun is meant.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Nouns, singular/plural, proper nouns, sentence completeness, and basic pronouns such as he, she, it, they.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Confusing plural `s` with possessive `'s`; putting apostrophes in every word ending with `s`; using `it`, `he`, `she`, or `they` when several nouns could fit.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student corrects simple possessives and revises unclear pronouns in short two-sentence contexts.
- **Recommended question types and why each fits:** `multiple-choice` for ownership meaning; `error-correction` for possessive and pronoun fixes; `passage-question` for pronoun reference in context; `constructed-response` for explaining what a pronoun refers to.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Draw an ownership arrow from `Maya's pencil` to Maya, then draw a pronoun arrow from `she` back to a named noun.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use singular possessives only; avoid contractions and plural possessives except as non-target exposure.

## Unit 3: Verbs, Time, And Agreement

### Lesson 1: Action Verbs And Being Verbs

- **Teaching goal:** Expand verb recognition beyond visible action to include being verbs.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify action verbs and simple being verbs in predicates.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Verbs can show what the subject does or what the subject is or is like.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Subject/predicate, nouns/pronouns, complete sentence.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Missing `is`, `are`, `was`, and `were` as verbs; choosing every exciting word as the verb; confusing nouns that name actions with verbs.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the verb and states whether it shows action or being in simple sentences.
- **Recommended question types and why each fits:** `multiple-choice` for verb identification; `match-pairs` for action/being sorting; `fill-blank` for choosing a verb; `constructed-response` for explaining the verb job.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare `The dog runs` and `The dog is tired`; both predicates need a verb.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one verb per sentence at first; avoid helping-verb chains and linking-verb edge cases.

### Lesson 2: Past, Present, And Future Verbs

- **Teaching goal:** Connect verb forms and time words to past, present, and future meaning.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose or identify verb forms that show when an action happens.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Time clues such as yesterday, today, and tomorrow help readers choose verb forms.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Verb recognition, time words, and sentence context.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Using present forms with past time words; assuming future time always needs `will` plus any verb form; ignoring context clues.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student classifies and completes sentences in past, present, or future time.
- **Recommended question types and why each fits:** `fill-blank` for verb-form choice; `order-items` for timeline ordering; `multiple-choice` for tense recognition; `error-correction` for mismatched time clues.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Build a three-column timeline: yesterday, today, tomorrow.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use common verbs and direct time clues; avoid perfect tenses and progressive tense terminology.

### Lesson 3: Regular And Irregular Verb Forms

- **Teaching goal:** Teach that some past-tense verbs use `-ed` while common irregular verbs change in other ways.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose regular and common irregular past-tense forms in short sentences.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** English has patterns and memorized common forms; `goed` and `runned` are understandable but not standard.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Past tense, verbs, sentence context, and common oral language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Adding `-ed` to every past verb; treating irregular forms as random with no need to learn; using two past markers such as `did went`.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student corrects or selects forms such as `walked`, `played`, `went`, `saw`, `ran`, `came`, `was`, and `were`.
- **Recommended question types and why each fits:** `match-pairs` for present/past pairs; `fill-blank` for context choice; `error-correction` for overregularized forms; `text-input` for supported production.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Sort past verbs into `adds -ed` and `changes another way`.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Limit the irregular set; avoid spelling rules beyond high-frequency `y` to `ied` if used.

### Lesson 4: Subject-Verb Agreement

- **Teaching goal:** Teach simple agreement between subjects and present-tense verbs.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose a verb that fits a singular or plural subject in clear sentences.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** The verb must fit the subject, not the nearest noun or the writer's guess.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Subject identification, singular/plural nouns and pronouns, present-tense verbs.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Matching the verb to the closest noun; omitting `s` with singular present-tense verbs; adding `s` with plural subjects.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses forms such as `runs/run`, `has/have`, `is/are`, and corrects simple agreement errors.
- **Recommended question types and why each fits:** `fill-blank` for agreement choice; `error-correction` for targeted edits; `multiple-choice` for subject identification before agreement; `multi-blank-cloze` for paired subject+verb decisions.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Circle the subject first, then test the verb aloud: `The bird sings`; `The birds sing`.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid interrupting phrases between subject and verb until mastery is visible; keep sentences short.

## Unit 4: Adjectives, Adverbs, And Precise Description

### Lesson 1: Adjectives Describe Nouns

- **Teaching goal:** Teach adjectives as words that modify nouns by telling which one, what kind, or how many.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify adjectives and the nouns they describe, then choose a precise adjective for meaning.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Draw an arrow from the adjective to the noun it helps.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Noun identification, complete sentences, and basic description.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating any nice-sounding word as an adjective; choosing the noun instead of its modifier; adding irrelevant adjectives.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies adjective+noun pairs and revises a sentence with a relevant adjective.
- **Recommended question types and why each fits:** `multiple-choice` for recognition; `match-pairs` for adjective-to-noun relationships; `fill-blank` for precise choice; `constructed-response` for a short revision.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use `The dog barked` and ask which word could tell what kind of dog without changing the sentence core.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar concrete nouns first; limit sentences to one or two adjectives.

### Lesson 2: Comparatives And Superlatives

- **Teaching goal:** Teach adjective forms for comparing two or more nouns.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose comparative and superlative forms in common cases.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Use `-er` or `more` for comparing two; use `-est` or `most` for one among three or more.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Adjectives, nouns, comparison language, and number words.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Using `most` for two things; using both `more` and `-er`; using `gooder` or `bestest`.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses forms such as `taller`, `tallest`, `more careful`, `most careful`, `better`, and `best` in clear contexts.
- **Recommended question types and why each fits:** `fill-blank` for form choice; `multiple-choice` for number-of-things reasoning; `error-correction` for double comparisons; `match-pairs` for base/comparative/superlative relationships.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Place two pencils and then three pencils in order to show how comparison changes.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use high-frequency adjectives; avoid nuanced usage debates.

### Lesson 3: Adverbs Describe Actions

- **Teaching goal:** Teach adverbs as words that tell how, when, or where about verbs in clear examples.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify adverbs, tell what verb they modify, and choose an adverb that fits the action.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Ask `How? When? Where?` after the verb.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Verb identification, adjective identification, and simple sentence structure.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Believing all adverbs end in `-ly`; using adjectives in adverb jobs; confusing where-phrases with single-word adverbs in first exposure.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the adverb and verb relationship and corrects simple adjective/adverb usage errors.
- **Recommended question types and why each fits:** `multiple-choice` for adverb recognition; `fill-blank` for how/when/where choice; `error-correction` for adjective/adverb fixes; `constructed-response` for adding an action detail.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare `The turtle moved slowly` with `The slow turtle moved` to show different modifier jobs.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use clear one-adverb sentences; avoid intensifiers and adverbs modifying adjectives until later.

## Unit 5: Prepositions, Conjunctions, And Sentence Expansion

### Lesson 1: Prepositional Phrases Add Where And When

- **Teaching goal:** Teach common prepositional phrases as word groups that add location or time information.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a prepositional phrase and explain what where/when detail it adds.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A preposition begins a phrase that usually ends with a noun or pronoun.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Nouns, pronouns, verbs, adverbs as where/when details, and complete sentences.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating a prepositional phrase as a complete sentence; choosing the noun alone as the phrase; confusing every small word with a preposition.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student brackets phrases such as `under the table`, `after lunch`, and `near the school` and tells what they add.
- **Recommended question types and why each fits:** `multiple-choice` for phrase identification; `match-pairs` for preposition-to-meaning; `fill-blank` for completing phrases; `constructed-response` for adding a where/when phrase.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Move an object and describe it: `on the desk`, `under the desk`, `beside the desk`.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use concrete prepositions and one phrase per sentence at first.

### Lesson 2: Conjunctions Connect Words And Ideas

- **Teaching goal:** Teach conjunctions as connectors with different meanings.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose `and`, `but`, `or`, or `because` to connect words or simple ideas clearly.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Connectors show addition, contrast, choice, or reason.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Complete sentences, nouns, verbs, adjectives, and basic sentence meaning.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Using `and` for every relationship; using `because` without a complete main idea; treating connectors as punctuation replacements.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects a conjunction that matches the relationship between two words or ideas.
- **Recommended question types and why each fits:** `fill-blank` for connector choice; `multiple-choice` for relationship meaning; `match-pairs` for conjunction-to-job; `error-correction` for unclear connector use.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare `I wanted tea, but the kettle was empty` with `I wanted tea because I was cold`.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short clauses; do not formalize coordinating versus subordinating conjunction terminology.

### Lesson 3: Expand Sentences With Useful Details

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to expand a base sentence with relevant details.
- **Student outcome:** The student can add a where, when, how, or describing detail that makes a sentence clearer without making it cluttered.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Start with a complete sentence core, then add one useful detail at a time.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Complete sentences, adjectives, adverbs, prepositional phrases, and punctuation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Adding random details; making fragments by adding only a phrase; thinking the longest sentence is always best.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses or writes an expanded sentence that keeps the original meaning and adds useful information.
- **Recommended question types and why each fits:** `multiple-choice` for best expansion; `order-items` for arranging core plus detail; `constructed-response` for writing an expanded sentence; `error-correction` for removing clutter or restoring completeness.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Begin with `The bird sang.` Add where, when, or how details and discuss which one helps most.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Ask for one added detail at a time; avoid long multi-phrase sentences.

### Lesson 4: Combine Sentences Without Run-Ons

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to combine related sentences while preserving sentence boundaries.
- **Student outcome:** The student can combine two short related sentences with a fitting conjunction or punctuation choice.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Combining needs both meaning and boundary control; simply removing the period creates a run-on.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Fragments/run-ons, conjunctions, complete sentence cores, and end punctuation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Joining sentences with a comma alone; using an unfitting conjunction; changing meaning while combining.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses or writes a combined sentence that is grammatical and keeps the original meaning.
- **Recommended question types and why each fits:** `multiple-choice` for best combined sentence; `error-correction` for run-on repair; `order-items` for arranging combined parts; `constructed-response` for writing a combined sentence.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show two choppy sentences and three possible combinations, then ask which one keeps meaning and reads smoothly.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Combine only two short sentences; avoid semicolons and complex punctuation.

## Unit 6: Capitalization, Commas, And Dialogue Signals

### Lesson 1: Capital Letters For Names And Beginnings

- **Teaching goal:** Consolidate high-frequency capitalization rules.
- **Student outcome:** The student can capitalize sentence beginnings, the pronoun `I`, proper nouns, days, months, titles, and familiar place names.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Capital letters signal specific starts and specific names, not general importance.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Complete sentence boundaries, proper nouns, titles/month/day vocabulary, and editing marks as exposure.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Capitalizing any exciting word; missing `I`; lowercasing days and months; capitalizing all common nouns in a title-like phrase.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student corrects capitalization in short sentences and explains which rule applied.
- **Recommended question types and why each fits:** `error-correction` for editing; `multiple-choice` for rule recognition; `fill-blank` for choosing capitalized forms; `passage-question` for context in a short draft.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Sort examples into `sentence beginning`, `specific name`, and `not capitalized`.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep sentences short; avoid title capitalization rules beyond simple titles before names.

### Lesson 2: Commas In A Series

- **Teaching goal:** Teach commas as separators in lists of three or more.
- **Student outcome:** The student can place commas in a simple series and explain that each comma separates list items.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A series is a list of three or more words or phrases; commas help readers see the items.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Nouns, adjectives, conjunction `and`, sentence boundaries, and simple lists.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Putting commas in two-item lists; putting commas after every word including after `and`; using a comma wherever the reader pauses.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student punctuates lists of three or more items and avoids unnecessary commas in two-item pairs.
- **Recommended question types and why each fits:** `error-correction` for comma insertion/removal; `multiple-choice` for choosing correctly punctuated lists; `order-items` for list item awareness; `constructed-response` for explaining the comma job.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Place list items in boxes, then put commas between boxes, not randomly inside them.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use simple noun and adjective lists; avoid complex phrases with internal commas.

### Lesson 3: Commas In Dates And Places

- **Teaching goal:** Teach simple comma patterns in dates and place phrases.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use commas in dates such as `April 5, 2026` and places such as `Austin, Texas`.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Commas group date and place information so readers can see each part.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Days, months, proper nouns, capitalization, and comma as separator.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Leaving out the comma between city and state; putting commas after every date word; confusing month/day/year order.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student corrects simple date and place sentences with capitalization and commas.
- **Recommended question types and why each fits:** `fill-blank` for comma placement choices; `error-correction` for full sentence editing; `match-pairs` for pattern-to-example; `multiple-choice` for correctly punctuated dates/places.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use pattern frames: `Month Day, Year` and `City, State`.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar U.S. place examples; avoid full mailing addresses and international date formats.

### Lesson 4: Dialogue Tags And Quotation Marks

- **Teaching goal:** Introduce quotation marks and speaker tags in simple dialogue sentences.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify exact spoken words, the speaker tag, and the punctuation around a short quotation.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Quotation marks go around exact words someone says; a speaker tag tells who said them.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** End punctuation, commas, verbs such as said/asked, capitalization, and complete sentences.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Putting quotation marks around the speaker tag; forgetting the comma before the quotation in simple tag-first sentences; lowercasing the first word of the quotation.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses or corrects simple dialogue such as `Mia said, "I found it."`
- **Recommended question types and why each fits:** `multiple-choice` for identifying exact words; `error-correction` for quotation punctuation; `fill-blank` for punctuation marks; `dialogue-builder` for choosing a fitting spoken line.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Color the speaker tag one way and the exact spoken words another way before adding marks.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one-sentence dialogue with tag first; avoid split quotations and paragraphs of dialogue.

## Unit 7: Cumulative Editing And Grammar For Writing

### Lesson 1: Focused Editing Targets

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to choose and apply a specific editing target instead of trying to fix everything at once.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify the likely grammar target in a short sentence or passage and make the targeted correction.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Focused editing asks, `What am I checking for right now?`
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** All prior sentence, parts-of-speech, word-form, capitalization, and punctuation targets.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Guessing the newest rule; changing meaning while editing; overcorrecting sentences that are already correct.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student names a target and makes a correction that addresses that target without adding unrelated edits.
- **Recommended question types and why each fits:** `passage-question` for choosing the target; `error-correction` for focused fixes; `multiple-choice` for identifying the error type; `constructed-response` for explaining one edit.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Give a short sentence and three possible targets; model checking only one target at a time.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one or two errors per item; keep passages to two or three short sentences.

### Lesson 2: Revise For Clarity And Variety

- **Teaching goal:** Teach revision as improving clarity, detail, and sentence variety rather than merely lengthening.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose or produce a revision that improves a sentence while preserving meaning.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Good revision asks whether the sentence is clearer, more precise, or better connected.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Sentence expansion, combining, modifiers, prepositional phrases, conjunctions, and run-on repair.
- **Likely misconceptions:** The longest revision is best; adding details always helps; combining sentences automatically improves them.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects or writes a revision that adds relevant detail, combines related ideas, or removes clutter.
- **Recommended question types and why each fits:** `multiple-choice` for best revision; `constructed-response` for writing a revision; `order-items` for sentence arrangement; `error-correction` for fixing a faulty revision.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare three revisions of the same base sentence and discuss clarity, precision, and meaning.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar contexts; require one revision move at a time.

### Lesson 3: Grammar 1 Capstone Edit

- **Teaching goal:** Assess cumulative Grammar 1 transfer in compact mixed editing and explanation tasks.
- **Student outcome:** The student can apply sentence, word, and mechanics knowledge to a short mixed review and explain selected choices.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Read for meaning first, choose a grammar target, edit carefully, then check that the sentence still says what it should.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** All Grammar 1 units.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Editing mechanically without reading meaning; fixing only punctuation while ignoring word forms; changing correct sentences because a mixed set feels suspicious.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student completes a short cumulative edit, chooses accurate corrections, and explains at least one sentence-boundary, word-form, or mechanics choice.
- **Recommended question types and why each fits:** `passage-question` for mixed context; `error-correction` for direct editing; `multi-blank-cloze` for multiple targeted fixes; `constructed-response` for explanation and transfer.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a compact student-writing-style passage and model a checklist: sentence, word job, word form, capitalization, punctuation.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep the capstone passage short; avoid long paragraphs and advanced grammar not taught in this level.
