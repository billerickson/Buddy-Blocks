# Rhetoric 5 Research Brief

Track: Rhetoric  
Level: Level 5  
Intended student profile: advanced foundation students ready for rhetorical analysis, research-supported argument, style imitation, debate case structure, public presentation, and a capstone persuasive or ceremonial oration.

This brief defines outcomes and instructional priorities only. It does not define units, lessons, or questions.

## 1. End-Of-Level Outcomes

By the end of Rhetoric 5, students should be able to analyze rhetorical situation, audience, purpose, speaker, exigence, appeals, arrangement, style, and evidence in literary/civic speeches and prose; plan a speech or argument; evaluate sources; build a research-supported claim; address objections; imitate selected style features ethically; revise through cycles; use visual/oral presentation choices deliberately; and complete a polished persuasive or ceremonial oration.

## 2. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should control thesis, proof, refutation, arrangement, ethos/pathos/logos, description, voice, policy proposal, evidence selection, multi-paragraph organization, and short oral delivery.

## 3. Major Concept Clusters

- **Rhetorical Analysis:** situation, speaker, audience, purpose, occasion/exigence, constraints, appeals, arrangement, and style.
- **Research-Supported Argument:** inquiry question, source quality, claim, evidence, reasoning, counterargument, and citation awareness.
- **Speech Planning And Delivery:** occasion, structure, transitions, emphasis, visuals, rehearsal, and audience adaptation.
- **Debate Case Structure:** resolution, definitions, constructive case, evidence, rebuttal, and weighing.
- **Style Imitation And Revision:** diction, syntax, figures, tone, rhythm, and revision cycles.
- **Capstone Oration:** persuasive, deliberative, forensic readiness, or ceremonial speech in age-appropriate form.

## 4. Recommended Progression

Begin with rhetorical situation and analysis of short speeches/prose. Review arrangement and appeals, then add source evaluation and research-supported claims. Teach debate case structure as organized reasoning, not adversarial performance. Add style imitation from literary/civic models. Build revision cycles and presentation choices. End with a capstone oration or polished argument supported by evidence.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Rhetorical analysis is listing ethos/pathos/logos.
- Strong rhetoric hides weak evidence.
- Research means finding a quote that agrees.
- Counterargument weakens my case.
- Style imitation means copying content.
- Debate means defeating people rather than testing claims.
- Visuals are decoration.
- Ceremonial speech has no argument or structure.

## 6. Developmental Constraints And Reading/Load Concerns

Use short, high-quality models. Keep research scope bounded and source sets manageable. Teach source credibility explicitly. Avoid partisan simulations as the default. Require ethical persuasion and accurate representation of opposing views. Capstone work can accumulate gradually, but individual Buddy Blocks lessons should stay compact.

## 7. Assessment Implications

Use passage questions for rhetorical situation, appeals, arrangement, style, and evidence. Use source-evaluation items for credibility and relevance. Use order-items for speech structure and argument maps. Use constructed responses for concise analysis, thesis, evidence explanation, counterargument, and revision choice. Distractors should show appeal-labeling without analysis, weak source, quote dumping, straw man, style/content confusion, and audience mismatch.

## 8. Spiral Review Priorities

Audience, purpose, speaker, occasion, claim, thesis, evidence, reasoning, arrangement, proof, refutation, ethos, pathos, logos, style, diction, syntax, source, credibility, counterargument, rebuttal, delivery, revision.

## 9. Concepts That Should Not Be Taught Yet

Avoid college-level rhetorical theory, full forensic/debate tournament procedures, complex citation systems as primary content, manipulative persuasion tactics, partisan campaign strategy, and long research papers as the main in-app assessment.
