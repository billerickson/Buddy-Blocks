# Classical Literature 3 Lesson Briefs

Sources: accepted level blueprint, course map, and unit design briefs in `research/classical-literature-3/`.

## Unit 1: Reading Like A Literary Analyst

### Lesson 1: Objective Summary Before Interpretation

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to summarize what a passage says before adding an interpretation.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose an objective summary and separate it from a claim about meaning.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A summary names major events or ideas in neutral language; interpretation explains what those events may mean.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Retelling episodes, identifying major events, and recognizing character actions.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Adding opinions to a summary; copying minor details; treating a theme claim as a summary.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a brief adapted passage, the student selects or completes a neutral summary and rejects interpretive or detail-heavy alternatives.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for summary selection from a shared passage; `match-pairs` for summary versus interpretation examples; `order-items` for event sequence; `multiple-choice` for identifying why a response is not objective.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Before you interpret a story, make sure you know what happened. An objective summary is neutral: it names the main events without adding a lesson, opinion, or extra detail."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one short passage at a time; keep summaries one sentence; avoid unfamiliar names unless the passage gives clear relationships.

### Lesson 2: From Plot To Theme

- **Teaching goal:** Help students turn plot events and motives into supported theme statements.
- **Student outcome:** The student can distinguish a plot event, a topic, and a theme statement.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A theme is a claim about what the passage suggests about a human concern; it is broader than one event but must fit the event.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Objective summary, character motive, consequence, and topic words such as courage or loyalty.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Naming one word as a theme; choosing a moral that does not fit the passage; confusing plot with theme.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects a theme statement that is broad enough to transfer but specific enough to fit the passage.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for best theme statement; `passage-question` for evidence-based theme; `match-pairs` for plot/topic/theme sorting; `fill-blank` for completing a theme frame.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "A plot event tells what happens. A topic names what the passage is about. A theme is a full idea about that topic, such as 'Pride can make a wise person ignore warnings.'"
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep topic words familiar; use short answer choices that differ by real interpretive errors, not tiny wording tricks.

### Lesson 3: Claim, Evidence, Explanation

- **Teaching goal:** Build the habit of connecting evidence to a claim with an explanation.
- **Student outcome:** The student can match a claim to relevant evidence and choose an explanation that shows how the evidence supports the claim.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A strong literary response has three parts: claim, evidence, and explanation.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Summary, theme, quotation or paraphrase, and basic passage comprehension.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Choosing evidence because it is dramatic but irrelevant; stopping after a quotation; restating the claim instead of explaining.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student completes or evaluates a claim-evidence-explanation frame for a short passage.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for claims to evidence; `multiple-choice` for best explanation; `multi-blank-cloze` for completing a frame; `error-correction` for a finite weak response repair.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "A claim says what you think the passage shows. Evidence points to a line or detail. Explanation tells how that detail supports the claim."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short evidence snippets; avoid requiring students to produce open-ended essays; keep explanations constrained and concrete.

### Lesson 4: Context As A Reading Tool

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to use brief historical or cultural context only when it clarifies the passage.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether a context note helps interpret a specific line or scene.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Context can explain names, customs, settings, and stakes, but the passage still supplies the evidence for interpretation.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Claim-evidence-explanation, basic setting, speaker/narrator, and passage details.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating context as proof without textual evidence; ignoring context entirely; using context to excuse or condemn without interpretation.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies a useful context note and chooses an interpretation that combines context with textual evidence.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for helpful versus distracting context; `passage-question` for applying a note; `match-pairs` for context notes to passage clues; `order-items` for the reading process from passage to context to claim.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Context is background that helps you read. Use it like a lamp: it can light up a detail, but it should not replace the words in the passage."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep context notes one sentence; avoid dense history; assess one context use at a time.

## Unit 2: Heroes, Journeys, And Epic Signals

### Lesson 1: Journey Episodes And Trials

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to track a heroic journey episode as a sequence of tests and choices.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify departure, trial, temptation, help, cost, and return or delay in a short episode.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A journey episode is more than travel; it reveals character through obstacles and responses.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Objective summary, event sequence, motive, consequence, and theme.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating every movement as an important trial; ignoring the cost of success; reading adventure as action only.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student sequences a journey episode and explains which trial reveals the most about the hero.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for episode sequence; `passage-question` for trial interpretation; `multiple-choice` for identifying temptation or cost; `match-pairs` for journey terms to examples.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "In heroic journeys, travel often works like a test. Watch what the hero wants, what blocks the way, who helps, and what the hero gains or loses."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use a compact episode with clear stages; avoid multiple unfamiliar character names in one passage.

### Lesson 2: Heroic Code: Honor, Loyalty, And Cost

- **Teaching goal:** Help students analyze how a heroic code shapes choices and consequences.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain how honor, loyalty, duty, or fame influences a character's decision.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A heroic code is a set of values that can guide action but also create costs.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Journey trials, motive, consequence, theme, and evidence.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Assuming a hero's code always leads to the best choice; treating honor as simple pride; ignoring the cost to others.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the value behind a heroic choice and chooses evidence showing its consequence.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for value-and-consequence analysis; `multiple-choice` for best claim; `match-pairs` for values to textual details; `multi-blank-cloze` for a choice-consequence frame.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "A heroic code tells a hero what seems honorable or loyal. The code can help the hero act bravely, but it can also make a choice costly."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep value terms defined in context; use one central choice per passage.

### Lesson 3: Epic Signals: Invocation, In Medias Res, And Epithets

- **Teaching goal:** Introduce common epic signals and connect them to reader expectations.
- **Student outcome:** The student can recognize invocation exposure, in medias res exposure, repeated epithets, and catalog/list structures in short excerpts.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Epic signals tell readers they are entering a large, public story shaped by memory, tradition, and elevated stakes.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Genre, heroic journey, narrator/speaker, and passage evidence.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating epic features as decorative old-fashioned language; assuming any list is epic; expecting full formal taxonomy.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies a feature and explains what it does for the passage.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for epic terms to examples; `passage-question` for feature purpose; `multiple-choice` for genre signal; `fill-blank` for completing definitions.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Epic stories often announce their importance. They may begin by calling for help to tell the story, start in the middle of action, repeat a hero's title, or list warriors, places, or gifts."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use brief invented epic-like lines; define terms before asking for recognition; avoid Greek or Latin technical overload.

### Lesson 4: Hospitality, Homecoming, Fate, And Choice

- **Teaching goal:** Teach recurring heroic motifs as interpretive tools.
- **Student outcome:** The student can interpret hospitality, homecoming, fate, or exile in context and connect it to theme.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Motifs repeat across classical stories, but their meaning depends on how a passage uses them.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Heroic code, journey episodes, context, and evidence explanation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating a motif as having the same meaning every time; thinking fate cancels choice; assuming homecoming is always happy.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies a motif and chooses the interpretation best supported by the passage.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for motif interpretation; `multiple-choice` for fate/free choice distinction; `match-pairs` for motifs to examples; `error-correction` for overbroad motif claims.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "A motif is a pattern that returns in many stories. Hospitality, homecoming, fate, and exile matter because each passage uses the pattern in its own way."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one or two motifs per lesson item; avoid assuming students know full source plots.

## Unit 3: Tragic Conflict And Comic Correction

### Lesson 1: Tragic Conflict And Hubris

- **Teaching goal:** Introduce tragic conflict and hubris as pressure points in a character's choice.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a conflict and explain how overconfidence, pride, or blindness worsens it.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Tragic conflict involves a serious pressure that reveals a flaw, limit, or dangerous choice.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Heroic code, choice and consequence, theme, evidence.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Any sad event is tragic; hubris means ordinary confidence; the character has no choice.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects the choice or attitude that creates tragic risk and supports it with a textual clue.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for conflict analysis; `multiple-choice` for hubris versus courage; `match-pairs` for conflict elements; `multi-blank-cloze` for a tragic-risk frame.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "A tragic conflict is not only a bad event. It is a serious pressure where a character's choice, flaw, or blindness can bring consequences."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use school-appropriate stakes; avoid adult material; define hubris in plain language.

### Lesson 2: Reversal, Recognition, And Consequence

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to track how a scene turns and what a character comes to understand.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify reversal, recognition, and consequence in a short tragic-ready scene.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Reversal changes the situation; recognition changes understanding; consequence shows the cost.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Tragic conflict, event sequence, character knowledge, and evidence explanation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Reversal means any surprise; recognition is the same as apology; consequence is only punishment.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student orders a scene's turn, recognition, and consequence, then chooses the best explanation of the change.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for sequence; `passage-question` for recognition; `multiple-choice` for consequence; `match-pairs` for terms to scene moments.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "In tragedy-ready scenes, the story may turn. A character may suddenly understand something, but that recognition does not erase the consequence."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep scene stages clear; do not ask students to infer too many hidden motives at once.

### Lesson 3: Comic Misunderstanding And Exaggeration

- **Teaching goal:** Show how comedy can use misunderstanding and exaggeration to reveal a social problem.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify the confusion or exaggeration in a comic-ready passage and explain what it exposes.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Classical comedy often corrects or exposes foolishness through confusion, disguise, status conflict, or exaggeration.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Conflict, speaker, point of view, evidence, and genre signals.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Comedy is only "something funny"; misunderstanding has no meaning; exaggeration means the passage cannot be serious.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the comic device and the social behavior it criticizes or corrects.
- **Recommended question types:** `dialogue-builder` for choosing a line that continues a misunderstanding; `passage-question` for social correction; `multiple-choice` for comic device; `match-pairs` for device to example.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Comedy can be funny and serious at the same time. A mistake, disguise, or exaggeration may reveal pride, greed, unfairness, or foolish social rules."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use mild, age-appropriate comic conflicts; keep speaker labels clear.

### Lesson 4: Genre Contrast: Tragic And Comic Conflict

- **Teaching goal:** Help students choose a genre frame based on textual evidence.
- **Student outcome:** The student can compare tragic and comic conflict signals and explain which frame best fits a passage.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Tragic and comic passages may both show conflict, but they handle pressure, consequence, misunderstanding, and correction differently.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Tragic conflict, reversal, recognition, comic misunderstanding, social correction, evidence.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Genre can be chosen by ending alone; comedy cannot have serious themes; tragedy cannot include irony or misunderstanding.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies genre signals and rejects a plausible but unsupported genre claim.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for genre claim; `passage-question` for evidence-based contrast; `match-pairs` for tragic/comic signals; `error-correction` for over-simple genre statements.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "A genre frame guides what you notice. Tragedy often stresses serious consequence and recognition. Comedy often uses confusion or exaggeration to correct social behavior."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Contrast one tragic-ready and one comic-ready feature at a time before mixing.

## Unit 4: Versions, Translations, And Point Of View

### Lesson 1: Compare Versions By One Criterion

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to focus comparison on one meaningful feature.
- **Student outcome:** The student can compare two short versions by wording, omitted detail, emphasis, tone, or point of view.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A useful comparison uses a criterion, then explains why the difference matters.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Evidence, theme, genre signals, and basic comparison.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Listing many differences without explaining any; saying one version is automatically wrong; comparing by vague preference.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects a criterion and chooses the comparison claim best supported by both passages.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for paired comparison; `match-pairs` for criteria to examples; `multiple-choice` for best comparison claim; `order-items` for comparison process.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "When you compare versions, choose one focus. You might compare wording, tone, what is included, who tells the scene, or what each version emphasizes."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use two very short passages; make the comparison focus explicit before asking for interpretation.

### Lesson 2: Translation Wording, Tone, And Emphasis

- **Teaching goal:** Show how word choice can shift tone and emphasis in translation-like passages.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a wording difference and explain how it changes the reader's impression.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Translation choices can make a speaker seem calmer, harsher, nobler, more doubtful, or more urgent.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Comparison criterion, tone, evidence from both passages, and speaker awareness.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Tone is a private feeling rather than evidence-based; different wording means incorrect translation; emphasis does not affect interpretation.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches word choices to tone shifts and chooses a supported explanation of emphasis.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for paired wording; `multiple-choice` for tone; `match-pairs` for words to tone effects; `fill-blank` for completing a comparison sentence.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "A translator chooses words. 'Commanded,' 'asked,' and 'pleaded' can describe similar speech but create different tones."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar tone words and short paired lines; avoid asking students to judge translation accuracy.

### Lesson 3: Point Of View And Adaptation Choices

- **Teaching goal:** Teach how narrator, speaker, omission, or addition can change interpretation.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain how point of view or adaptation choices affect what readers notice.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** The same event can feel different when told by a hero, a witness, an opponent, or a later storyteller.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Point of view, comparison, theme, evidence, tone.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Point of view is only first person versus third person; omitted details do not matter; adding a detail is always wrong.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies who controls the telling and explains how a specific choice shifts emphasis.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for point-of-view comparison; `multiple-choice` for adaptation effect; `match-pairs` for choices to effects; `error-correction` for unsupported version claims.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Adaptations make choices about who speaks, what gets included, and what gets left out. Those choices guide what the reader notices."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep pairs parallel; avoid more than one major adaptation choice per item.

## Unit 5: Imagery, Allusion, Wisdom, And Dialogue

### Lesson 1: Imagery And Metaphor In Short Passages

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to connect imagery and metaphor to meaning.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain how an image or metaphor shapes a passage's idea.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Imagery creates a sensory picture; metaphor describes one thing as another to suggest a connection.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Theme, evidence, wording, tone, and basic figurative language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Figurative language is decoration; every image has a fixed meaning; metaphor must be literal to matter.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies an image or metaphor and chooses the supported interpretation.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for image interpretation; `multiple-choice` for metaphor meaning; `match-pairs` for images to ideas; `multi-blank-cloze` for image-evidence frames.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Images help you picture a passage. Metaphors ask you to compare ideas. Ask: what does this picture make me notice?"
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one central image at a time; avoid obscure symbolic systems.

### Lesson 2: Allusion And Symbol Readiness

- **Teaching goal:** Introduce allusion and symbol readiness as context-based meaning tools.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use passage clues to interpret a reference or repeated object without overclaiming.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** An allusion points to another story, figure, or event; a symbol-like object gains meaning through repetition and context.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Motifs, context, imagery, evidence, and theme.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Allusion is just a name; symbols have one secret answer; any object in a passage is automatically a symbol.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the clue that gives an allusion or object meaning and rejects overbroad interpretations.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for allusion clues; `multiple-choice` for best symbolic interpretation; `match-pairs` for clue to meaning; `error-correction` for symbol overreach.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "An allusion is a meaningful reference. A symbol-like object becomes meaningful because of how the passage uses it, not because it has one secret answer."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Provide necessary background inside the passage or intro; avoid requiring outside classical knowledge.

### Lesson 3: Lyric Speaker, Repetition, And Structure

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to notice speaker, repeated language, and structure in lyric-like passages.
- **Student outcome:** The student can distinguish speaker from author and explain how repetition or line order shapes meaning.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** In compact literary forms, who speaks and what repeats can guide the reader toward the central concern.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Speaker/narrator, point of view, imagery, theme, evidence.
- **Likely misconceptions:** The speaker is always the author; repetition is accidental; line order does not affect meaning.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the speaker's concern and explains the effect of a repeated phrase or structural shift.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for speaker and repetition; `order-items` for line or idea sequence; `multiple-choice` for effect of repetition; `fill-blank` for speaker/author distinction.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "A poem-like passage has a speaker, even when the author's name is not given. Repeated words and line order can show what the speaker keeps returning to."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use very short lyric-like passages; label line breaks clearly.

### Lesson 4: Wisdom Sayings, Speeches, And Dialogues

- **Teaching goal:** Read compact forms as arguments about ethical questions.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify the question or value at stake in a wisdom saying, speech, or dialogue.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Wisdom literature and dialogues often compress a debate about justice, courage, loyalty, friendship, rule, or piety into a short exchange.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Theme, speaker, evidence, context, and motif vocabulary.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Short sayings are simple; dialogue is only conversation, not reasoning; one speaker automatically has the whole answer.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the ethical question and chooses the line that best supports the interpretation.
- **Recommended question types:** `dialogue-builder` for choosing a fitting response; `passage-question` for ethical question; `multiple-choice` for best interpretation; `match-pairs` for speaker positions.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Some classical texts ask big questions in small forms. A saying, speech, or dialogue may ask what justice, courage, friendship, or duty requires."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep exchanges brief and speaker labels clear; avoid abstract philosophy vocabulary unless defined.

## Unit 6: Transfer Across Classical Texts

### Lesson 1: Choose The Reading Move

- **Teaching goal:** Help students decide which analytical routine fits a new passage.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose whether to summarize, identify genre, analyze language, compare versions, use context, or select evidence first.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Strong readers diagnose the task before answering; different passages invite different first moves.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** All Level 3 routines from Units 1-5.
- **Likely misconceptions:** The most recent routine is always the right one; recognizing a term is enough; skipping summary saves time.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses an appropriate reading move and explains why it fits the passage or question.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for best reading move; `passage-question` for task diagnosis; `match-pairs` for passage features to routines; `order-items` for reading process.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Before answering, ask what the passage needs. Do you need the gist, a genre signal, evidence, a wording comparison, a context clue, or a language clue?"
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short mixed passages; assess the choice of move before full interpretation.

### Lesson 2: Build A Supported Interpretation

- **Teaching goal:** Combine summary, claim, evidence, and explanation on a new passage.
- **Student outcome:** The student can build a concise supported interpretation without relying on plot retell or unsupported opinion.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A transfer interpretation should still move through claim, evidence, and explanation.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Objective summary, theme, evidence, genre, language, context.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A broad theme word is enough; evidence can be loosely related; explanation should repeat the claim.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student completes a supported interpretation frame and identifies the weakest part of a sample response.
- **Recommended question types:** `multi-blank-cloze` for building a frame; `passage-question` for claim/evidence; `multiple-choice` for strongest explanation; `error-correction` for finite weak-response repair.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "A supported interpretation needs all three parts: a claim, evidence from the passage, and an explanation that connects the evidence to the claim."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep the final response constrained; use short evidence snippets; avoid open essay scoring.

### Lesson 3: Compare, Correct, And Transfer

- **Teaching goal:** Require students to compare passages or interpretations and repair common analytical mistakes.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a weak interpretation, explain the problem, and choose a stronger supported response.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Transfer includes noticing when an answer overreaches, ignores evidence, misuses context, or confuses plot with theme.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Full Level 3 toolkit, including comparison and close-reading routines.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Any answer with a literary term is strong; context can prove a claim alone; a symbol interpretation can ignore passage clues; comparison is preference.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student corrects plot-only, unsupported, symbol-overreach, context-overreach, or version-confusion responses in mixed tasks.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for paired transfer; `multiple-choice` for best correction; `error-correction` for finite repair; `match-pairs` for mistake type to example.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "A final reading task may mix skills. Watch for common mistakes: retelling instead of analyzing, choosing weak evidence, overreading a symbol, or using context without the passage."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use compact passages and responses; focus each question on one error type even when the lesson mixes skills.
