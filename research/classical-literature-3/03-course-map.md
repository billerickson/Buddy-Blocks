# Classical Literature 3 Course Map

Track: Classical Literature
Level: Level 3
Sources: Accepted research brief at `research/classical-literature-3/01-research-brief.md` and level blueprint at `research/classical-literature-3/02-level-blueprint.md`

## Course Design Notes

Classical Literature 3 is organized as a reading-analysis progression rather than a chronological history survey. The course begins with shared routines for summary, evidence, theme, and context; moves into heroic journey and epic features; broadens into tragedy and comedy readiness; teaches students to compare versions and translations; deepens close reading of language, imagery, allusion, wisdom sayings, and dialogue; and ends with cumulative transfer. Lesson and question design should use adapted episodes and brief accessible translated excerpts. Longer discussion, performance, and essay writing can enrich the course outside the app, but promoted Buddy Blocks items should remain compact and app-scorable.

## Unit 1: Reading Like A Literary Analyst

### Unit Purpose

Establish the core routines students need for every later unit: summarize before interpreting, distinguish plot from theme, select evidence, explain evidence, and use context as a reading aid. This unit turns prior story knowledge into disciplined literary response.

### Student Outcomes

- Summarize a passage objectively without adding unsupported interpretation.
- Distinguish a plot event, a character motive, and a theme statement.
- Select evidence that supports a claim and explain the evidence in a sentence frame.
- Identify when context helps interpret a passage and when it distracts from the text.
- Recognize common weak responses, such as plot retell posing as analysis.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Summary, major event, supporting detail, claim, evidence, quotation, paraphrase, explanation, theme, motive, consequence, narrator, speaker, context, interpretation.

### Prerequisites

Students should be able to retell longer adapted stories, identify basic character motives and themes, compare simple versions, and use short evidence from a text with support.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit draws on Level 2 story comprehension and prepares every later unit. Heroic, tragic, comic, translation, figurative language, and transfer lessons will all reuse the claim-evidence-explanation routine and the habit of summarizing before interpreting.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Analysis is the same as retelling the plot.
- A quotation proves a claim without explanation.
- A theme is any topic word, such as "honor" or "journey."
- Context means telling history facts instead of reading the passage.
- The best answer is always the longest answer.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Evidence and theme routines should reappear in every unit. Later review should ask students to choose the best evidence, reject unsupported claims, and revise plot-only interpretations.

## Unit 2: Heroes, Journeys, And Epic Signals

### Unit Purpose

Use heroic and journey literature to teach how repeated story structures, genre signals, and character choices create meaning. Students encounter epic features through accessible examples while keeping formal epic terminology light.

### Student Outcomes

- Track a journey episode through departure, trial, temptation, help, cost, and return or delay.
- Explain how a heroic code shapes a character's choice and consequence.
- Recognize epic signals such as invocation exposure, in medias res exposure, repeated epithets, catalogs or lists, and elevated stakes.
- Interpret motifs such as hospitality, homecoming, exile, honor, loyalty, fate, and free choice.
- Use evidence to explain how a trial reveals character or theme.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Hero, heroic code, epic, invocation, in medias res, journey, trial, temptation, helper, monster, hospitality, exile, homecoming, honor, loyalty, fate, free choice, epithet, catalog.

### Prerequisites

Students need objective summary, character motive, consequence, basic theme, genre awareness, and the evidence routine from Unit 1.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit extends Unit 1 analysis into classical heroic patterns. It prepares Unit 3 by making choice, reversal, consequence, and hubris visible; prepares Unit 4 by giving familiar episodes for version comparison; and prepares Unit 6 by supplying recurring motifs for transfer.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A hero is simply a perfect person.
- Epic means any old story with fighting.
- Fate removes all character choice.
- A repeated epithet or list is decorative only.
- Homecoming is automatically simple or happy.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Review Unit 1 by asking for summaries, evidence, and theme explanations in heroic contexts. Revisit heroic motifs later during tragedy, translation comparison, and transfer.

## Unit 3: Tragic Conflict And Comic Correction

### Unit Purpose

Introduce tragedy and comedy readiness as distinct ways stories organize conflict, consequence, recognition, misunderstanding, exaggeration, and social repair. Students learn genre features by reading short adapted scenes and recognizing how structure shapes meaning.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify tragic conflict, reversal, recognition, hubris, and consequence in a supported passage.
- Explain why a tragic mistake is more than "a good person dies."
- Identify comic misunderstanding, exaggeration, disguise, status conflict, or social correction.
- Compare tragic and comic treatments of conflict without reducing comedy to "anything funny."
- Support genre claims with textual details.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Tragedy readiness, tragic conflict, hubris, reversal, recognition, consequence, comedy readiness, misunderstanding, exaggeration, disguise, satire readiness, social correction, status, conflict.

### Prerequisites

Students need plot tracking, conflict, character motive, theme, heroic choice and consequence, and evidence explanation.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit depends on Unit 2's focus on choice and consequence, then broadens genre understanding before Unit 4's version comparisons. Tragic and comic signals also support Unit 6 transfer tasks that require students to choose among genre frames.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A tragic hero is simply a good person who dies.
- A bad ending automatically means tragedy.
- Comedy means any text with jokes.
- A misunderstanding has no serious meaning.
- Genre labels can be assigned without evidence.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Interleave heroic-code review with tragic choice and consequence. Bring back theme and evidence frames, and contrast tragic and comic signals after each is introduced.

## Unit 4: Versions, Translations, And Point Of View

### Unit Purpose

Teach students that adaptations and translations involve choices. Students compare short paired passages to notice how wording, omitted details, emphasis, tone, and point of view can shift interpretation while preserving a shared story base.

### Student Outcomes

- Compare two short versions or translations by a specific criterion.
- Explain how wording changes tone or emphasis.
- Identify how point of view or speaker affects interpretation.
- Distinguish "different" from "wrong" when comparing translations or adaptations.
- Use evidence from both passages in a comparison claim.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Version, adaptation, translation, wording, tone, emphasis, point of view, speaker, narrator, omission, addition, interpretation, comparison, criterion.

### Prerequisites

Students need theme, evidence, genre signals, and enough familiarity with heroic, tragic, and comic contexts to compare how different tellings shape meaning.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit uses episodes and motifs from Units 1-3 as comparison material. It prepares Unit 5 by making students attend closely to individual words, phrases, tone, and speaker choices.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A translation is the original text with no interpretive choices.
- One version must be correct and the other wrong.
- Comparison means listing every difference.
- Tone can be identified without attending to wording.
- Point of view does not affect meaning.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

3-4 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Return to evidence explanation by requiring support from both passages. Review genre signals through version comparisons, and keep comparison focused with one criterion at a time.

## Unit 5: Imagery, Allusion, Wisdom, And Dialogue

### Unit Purpose

Deepen close reading by focusing on how compact forms and language choices create meaning. Students study imagery, metaphor, symbol readiness, allusion, repetition, lyric speaker, wisdom sayings, speeches, and dialogues in short, rereadable passages.

### Student Outcomes

- Explain how imagery or metaphor shapes the meaning of a short passage.
- Recognize symbol readiness and allusion without treating them as one-answer puzzles.
- Identify speaker, audience, repetition, and structure in lyric or speech-like passages.
- Interpret a wisdom saying or dialogue as a literary form that raises an ethical question.
- Connect language choices to theme with evidence.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Image, imagery, metaphor, symbol readiness, allusion, repetition, structure, line, stanza, speaker, audience, speech, dialogue, wisdom literature, proverb, ethical question, justice, courage, loyalty, piety, friendship.

### Prerequisites

Students need summary, evidence, theme, version awareness, point of view, and basic figurative language knowledge from earlier levels.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit builds on Unit 4 attention to wording. It prepares Unit 6 by giving students compact language-based passages for final transfer and by revisiting enduring classical questions in several forms.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A symbol has one secret answer outside the passage.
- Allusion is name-dropping rather than meaning-making.
- Figurative language is decoration only.
- A speaker is always the author.
- A wisdom saying is automatically simple because it is short.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Review earlier motifs through imagery and allusion. Space evidence practice by asking students to connect one word, image, or repeated phrase to a claim.

## Unit 6: Transfer Across Classical Texts

### Unit Purpose

Bring the level together through mixed reading tasks. Students read new short passages, choose relevant analytical routines, identify genre or form signals, support theme claims with evidence, compare interpretive choices, and avoid common misconceptions.

### Student Outcomes

- Choose a useful reading move for a new passage: summarize, identify genre, compare wording, analyze language, or use context.
- Support a theme or genre claim with evidence and explanation.
- Compare two brief passages or interpretations using a clear criterion.
- Identify and correct weak interpretations such as plot-only claims, unsupported symbolism, or context overreach.
- Demonstrate retained understanding of major motifs, genres, and evidence routines from the level.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Transfer, reading move, genre signal, form, motif, claim, evidence, explanation, theme, context, comparison, interpretation, misconception, enduring question.

### Prerequisites

Students need the full body of Level 3 routines: summary, evidence, theme, heroic and epic signals, tragic and comic readiness, version comparison, close reading of language, and careful context use.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit integrates Units 1-5 and prepares students for later levels that may use longer excerpts, more independent close reading, fuller tragedy and comedy study, and more extended literary argument.

### Misconceptions To Address

- The most recent reading strategy works for every passage.
- A broad theme word is enough.
- Evidence can be chosen because it sounds important, even if it does not support the claim.
- Context either explains everything or should be ignored.
- Fast recognition of a term is the same as interpretation.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

3-4 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use deliberately mixed review. Include delayed retrieval of genre terms, motifs, evidence routines, version comparison, figurative language, and context boundaries so mastery reflects transfer rather than same-day pattern matching.
