# Classical Literature 3 Level Blueprint

Track: Classical Literature
Level: Level 3
Source: Accepted research brief at `research/classical-literature-3/01-research-brief.md`

## 1. Level Mission

Classical Literature 3 helps students move from knowing classical stories to analyzing how classical and traditional texts make meaning. Students read adapted episodes and short accessible translated excerpts with guidance, then learn to summarize objectively, notice genre signals, explain how choices and consequences develop theme, compare versions or translations, and support literary claims with precise evidence. The level treats classical literature as an ongoing conversation about courage, justice, honor, wisdom, loyalty, home, exile, and transformation while keeping historical context useful, brief, and in service of reading.

## 2. Durable Student Outcomes

By the end of Classical Literature 3, students should be able to:

1. Summarize an adapted episode or short excerpt objectively, separating major events from supporting details.
2. Support a literary claim with a short quotation, a precise paraphrase, and an explanation of how the evidence fits.
3. Analyze how character choices, conflict, reversal, and consequence help develop a theme.
4. Identify and apply major genre features of epic, myth, legend, tragedy readiness, comedy readiness, lyric poetry, wisdom literature, and dialogue.
5. Recognize recurring classical ideas such as heroic code, hospitality, fate and free choice, justice, honor, hubris, exile, homecoming, transformation, allusion, and symbol readiness.
6. Explain how figurative language, imagery, repetition, speaker, point of view, and structure shape meaning in a short passage.
7. Compare two versions, adaptations, or translations by naming a specific difference in wording, emphasis, tone, or point of view.
8. Use just-in-time historical or cultural context to clarify a passage without replacing literary interpretation with a history report.
9. Transfer the level's reading routines to a new short classical or traditional passage.

## 3. Concept And Skill Progression

The level begins with an analytical launch: objective summary, evidence, theme language, and context as support rather than substitute. Students then move into heroic and journey literature, where plot tracking remains important but the instructional focus shifts to choices, trials, cost, hospitality, homecoming, and heroic code. Epic features are introduced lightly through accessible signals such as invocation exposure, in medias res exposure, repeated epithets, catalog or list structures, and journey episodes.

The middle of the level broadens genre understanding. Students examine tragedy readiness through conflict, reversal, recognition, hubris, fate, and consequence, then comedy readiness through misunderstanding, exaggeration, disguise, status conflict, and social correction. These units do not expect formal advanced genre taxonomy; they teach students to connect visible form and story movement to meaning.

After students have practiced genre analysis, the sequence turns to version and translation awareness. Students compare short paired passages or adaptation choices, noticing how diction, emphasis, point of view, omitted details, and tone can change interpretation. This prepares them for closer attention to figurative and symbolic language: imagery, metaphor, allusion, repetition, speaker, and structural choices in brief lyric, wisdom, speech, or dialogue passages.

The level ends with transfer. Students combine summary, genre recognition, evidence use, theme analysis, context, and version awareness on new passages. The final evidence should show that students can choose the right reading move instead of merely repeating the most recent lesson pattern.

## 4. Vocabulary, Representation, Model, Text, And Form Progression

Early vocabulary should stabilize literary analysis language: summary, major event, detail, claim, evidence, quotation, paraphrase, explanation, theme, motive, consequence, conflict, narrator, speaker, point of view, context, and genre.

Heroic and journey vocabulary should include hero, heroic code, epic, invocation, in medias res, journey, trial, temptation, hospitality, exile, homecoming, fate, free choice, honor, loyalty, and cost. Representations should include episode chains, choice-and-consequence charts, motif trackers, and claim-evidence-explanation frames.

Tragedy and comedy readiness vocabulary should include tragic conflict, reversal, recognition, hubris, consequence, comedy, misunderstanding, exaggeration, disguise, satire readiness, and social correction. Students should use simple arc diagrams, before-and-after choice charts, and genre-signal sorts rather than memorize advanced formal labels.

Version and translation language should include adaptation, translation, version, wording, tone, emphasis, point of view, omission, addition, and interpretation. Text forms should progress from single adapted passages to short paired passages with a clear comparison focus.

Close-reading vocabulary should include image, imagery, metaphor, symbol readiness, allusion, repetition, structure, line, stanza, speech, dialogue, wisdom saying, and ethical question. Text work should use short, rereadable excerpts so students can attend to language without heavy memory demands.

## 5. Fluency Expectations

Fluency in this level is literary reading fluency rather than speed. Students should become more automatic with a small set of routines: summarize before interpreting, identify speaker or narrator, name the relevant genre signal, choose evidence, explain how evidence supports a claim, and compare two passages by a specific criterion. Students should also retain core terms and motifs through spaced practice, including theme, evidence, epic, hero, fate, hospitality, hubris, reversal, comedy, translation, imagery, allusion, and symbol.

Students are not expected to read full unabridged classical primary texts independently. They should, however, gain fluency with short adapted passages and accessible translated excerpts, including unfamiliar names, formal language, and brief context notes.

## 6. Conceptual Understanding Expectations

Students should understand that analysis is not the same as retelling. A summary names what happens; analysis explains how choices, language, structure, and genre create meaning. Evidence does not prove a claim by sitting next to it; the reader must explain the connection.

Students should understand genre as a set of expectations that shapes interpretation. Epic, myth, legend, tragedy readiness, comedy readiness, lyric poetry, wisdom literature, and dialogue each guide attention differently. A journey episode, a comic misunderstanding, and a wisdom saying may all ask enduring questions, but they do so through different forms.

Students should understand that versions and translations involve choices. A translation or adaptation is not simply "the same thing in different words." Wording, emphasis, omitted details, and point of view can shift tone and interpretation while preserving a shared story base.

Students should understand that classical literature belongs to historical cultures and also speaks into later traditions. Context helps readers interpret what is happening, but it should not flatten a text into a history fact, excuse everything, or erase ethical questions.

## 7. Application And Transfer Expectations

Students should apply the level's routines to unfamiliar passages: read for gist, identify the most relevant genre or form signal, notice a character choice or language pattern, select evidence, and answer with a concise claim and explanation. Transfer should include comparing two versions of a scene, choosing the best evidence for a theme, identifying a diagnostic misconception in a sample interpretation, and deciding when context helps.

Because Buddy Blocks lessons must be compact and app-scorable, most application will be constrained: selecting the best claim, matching evidence to interpretations, completing a claim-evidence-explanation frame, ordering interpretive steps, correcting a finite mistaken claim, or answering passage questions. Longer literary discussion and written essays remain appropriate outside the app, not as automatically scored lesson items.

## 8. Review And Spacing Plan

Review should spiral through short, diagnostic returns rather than isolated term drills. Early evidence routines should reappear in every unit. Genre terms should recur with new texts after delays: epic and heroic journey terms should return during tragedy, comedy, version comparison, and final transfer; tragedy and comedy signals should return when students compare genres; translation awareness should return during close reading of wording and tone.

Motifs should be spaced across units: hospitality, homecoming, fate/free choice, justice, honor, hubris, exile, and transformation should appear in varied contexts so students learn them as interpretive tools rather than memorized vocabulary. Context review should stay just-in-time and passage-based. Feedback should name the reading move: summarize objectively, use evidence, explain the quote, check the speaker, notice the genre signal, compare wording, or avoid symbol overreach.

## 9. Mastery Evidence For The End Of The Level

End-of-level mastery should be evidenced by a balanced body of constrained reading tasks. A student who has mastered Classical Literature 3 can read a new short adapted classical passage or accessible translated excerpt, summarize it objectively, identify major genre or form signals, explain a likely theme with evidence, and avoid substituting plot retell for analysis.

The student can compare two short versions or translations by naming a specific difference in wording, emphasis, tone, or point of view. The student can interpret imagery, repetition, allusion, or symbol readiness without treating figurative language as a secret code with one context-free answer. The strongest mastery evidence combines transfer and judgment: the student chooses the best evidence, rejects an unsupported claim, uses context carefully, and explains how a textual detail contributes to meaning.

## 10. Source And Product Constraint Note

This blueprint is derived from the accepted Classical Literature 3 research brief. The cited standards and learning-science sources in that brief shape the focus on text complexity, close analytic reading, vocabulary, evidence, discussion-ready interpretation, and spaced retrieval. Buddy Blocks product constraints shape the authoring stance: lessons should be compact, student-facing intros should expose new ideas before scored recall, questions should be app-scorable and diagnostic, and research artifacts should remain separate from final curriculum implementation under `src/content/curriculum/`.
