# Unit 3: Tragic Conflict And Comic Correction

Track: Classical Literature
Level: Level 3
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/classical-literature-3/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students develop readiness for tragedy and comedy by analyzing how conflict, reversal, recognition, misunderstanding, exaggeration, and social correction shape meaning. The unit teaches genre reasoning through short adapted scenes rather than full formal study of drama.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

After heroic literature, students are ready to look at choices and consequences through different genre frames. Tragic and comic forms complicate the idea of a hero by showing flawed judgment, recognition, public conflict, and social repair or exposure.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: tragic conflict, hubris, reversal, recognition, consequence, comic misunderstanding, exaggeration, social correction.
- Vocabulary: tragedy readiness, tragic conflict, hubris, reversal, recognition, consequence, comedy readiness, misunderstanding, exaggeration, disguise, satire readiness, social correction, status.
- Representations and models: conflict arcs, reversal charts, recognition before/after charts, comic confusion maps, tragic/comic signal sorts.
- Texts and forms: adapted scenes or excerpt-like passages from tragedy-like and comedy-like classical situations.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need Unit 1 evidence routines and Unit 2 work with choice, consequence, honor, fate, and motifs. They should be able to track who knows what in a scene and identify a character's motive.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Tragedy means any sad ending.
- A tragic hero is just a good person who dies.
- Comedy means any text with jokes.
- Misunderstanding is unimportant if it is funny.
- Genre claims do not need evidence.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with tragic conflict and hubris because students have already considered heroic choice and cost. Then teach reversal and recognition as structural movements. After tragedy readiness, pivot to comedy readiness through misunderstanding and exaggeration. End by contrasting tragic and comic conflict so students can use genre signals deliberately.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Review heroic code and fate/free choice as students analyze tragic conflict. Keep Unit 1 evidence frames active. Revisit motifs such as honor, justice, family duty, and public reputation in both tragic and comic scenes.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares Unit 4 because tragic and comic scenes often vary sharply across adaptations and translations. It prepares Unit 5 by making students attentive to tone, speaker, structure, and repeated language. It prepares Unit 6 by adding genre contrast to the final transfer toolkit.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify a tragic conflict and the choice under pressure.
- Explain hubris, reversal, recognition, or consequence in a supported passage.
- Identify comic misunderstanding, exaggeration, disguise, or social correction.
- Compare tragic and comic treatments of conflict.
- Reject oversimplified genre claims.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Tragic Conflict And Hubris**: Identify conflict under pressure and the overreach or blindness that worsens it.
2. **Reversal, Recognition, And Consequence**: Track how a scene turns and what a character comes to understand.
3. **Comic Misunderstanding And Exaggeration**: Analyze how confusion and exaggeration expose a social problem.
4. **Genre Contrast: Tragic And Comic Conflict**: Decide which genre frame best fits a passage and explain why.
