# Unit 4: Versions, Translations, And Point Of View

Track: Classical Literature
Level: Level 3
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/classical-literature-3/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn that versions, adaptations, and translations make interpretive choices. By the end of the unit, they should compare two short passages by a clear criterion and explain how wording, emphasis, tone, point of view, omission, or addition affects interpretation.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

Students now have enough genre and motif knowledge to compare tellings without becoming lost in plot. This unit shifts attention from what happened to how a telling shapes the reader's understanding.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: translation choice, adaptation choice, comparison criterion, tone, emphasis, point of view.
- Vocabulary: version, adaptation, translation, wording, tone, emphasis, point of view, narrator, speaker, omission, addition, interpretation, criterion.
- Representations and models: two-column comparison charts, wording-to-tone maps, point-of-view diagrams, evidence-from-both-passages frames.
- Texts and forms: paired adapted passages or brief accessible translated excerpts with controlled differences.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need Unit 1 evidence routines, Unit 2 heroic motifs, Unit 3 genre signals, and the ability to compare two passages by one feature.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Different wording means one version must be wrong.
- Comparison means listing all differences, not explaining one meaningful difference.
- Tone is a feeling guessed by the reader rather than inferred from words.
- Point of view is the same as plot.
- Omitted details do not affect meaning.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Start with comparison by one criterion so students avoid sprawling lists. Then teach wording, tone, and emphasis as close, visible comparison targets. End with point of view and adaptation choices because those require students to combine comparison with perspective and interpretive judgment.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Use familiar heroic, tragic, or comic material so comparison does not overload students with new story facts. Require evidence from both passages and bring back theme language when students explain why a version difference matters.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares Unit 5 by sharpening attention to language, speaker, tone, and structure. It also prepares Unit 6 by giving students a transfer routine for paired passages.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify a useful comparison criterion.
- Match a wording difference to a shift in tone or emphasis.
- Explain how point of view affects interpretation.
- Use evidence from both passages.
- Avoid "right translation/wrong translation" thinking.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Compare Versions By One Criterion**: Focus a comparison on wording, emphasis, plot detail, tone, or point of view.
2. **Translation Wording, Tone, And Emphasis**: Notice how word choices shift the reader's impression.
3. **Point Of View And Adaptation Choices**: Explain how narrator, speaker, omission, or addition changes interpretation.
