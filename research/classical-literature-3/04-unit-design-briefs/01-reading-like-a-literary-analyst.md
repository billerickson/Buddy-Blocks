# Unit 1: Reading Like A Literary Analyst

Track: Classical Literature
Level: Level 3
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/classical-literature-3/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students build the reading routines needed for the rest of the level: summarize objectively, distinguish plot from theme, make claims, select evidence, explain evidence, and use context carefully. By the end of the unit, students should treat analysis as a disciplined response to textual details rather than a longer retell of the story.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit comes first because later genre, comparison, and close-reading work all depend on basic analytical habits. Students entering Level 3 may know many classical stories, but they need stable routines for moving from story knowledge to interpretation.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: objective summary, interpretation, theme, evidence, context, claim support.
- Vocabulary: summary, major event, supporting detail, claim, evidence, quotation, paraphrase, explanation, theme, motive, consequence, narrator, speaker, context.
- Representations and models: summary ladders, plot/theme sorts, claim-evidence-explanation frames, context-use decision charts.
- Texts and forms: short adapted classical episodes or excerpt-like passages with minimal context notes.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should be able to retell longer stories, identify character motive and consequence, recognize basic themes, and use a short quotation or paraphrase with support. They should have prior exposure to narrator, dialogue, speaker, and basic figurative language.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Analysis means retelling more of the plot.
- A topic word such as "honor" is a full theme.
- Any quoted line supports any claim if it comes from the same passage.
- Evidence explains itself.
- Context is a replacement for textual evidence.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with objective summary so students separate what happens from what it means. Then introduce theme as a claim about meaning, not a topic label. After that, teach the claim-evidence-explanation routine explicitly. End with context because students need the routine first before deciding when historical or cultural information actually helps.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

This is the opening unit, so review draws from prior-level story comprehension: retelling episodes, identifying motive, naming conflict, recognizing theme, and comparing simple versions. It should make those skills more precise rather than reteaching them from scratch.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares Units 2-6 by establishing the routines that later lessons will reuse: summarize, name the reading focus, choose evidence, explain evidence, and avoid overclaiming. Later lessons will apply these habits to epic, tragedy, comedy, translation, imagery, dialogue, and transfer passages.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Sort summary details from interpretation.
- Distinguish plot events, topics, and theme statements.
- Match claims with relevant evidence.
- Complete a claim-evidence-explanation frame.
- Decide when a context note helps interpret a specific passage.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Objective Summary Before Interpretation**: Separate what the passage says from what the reader infers.
2. **From Plot To Theme**: Turn events and motives into a supported theme statement.
3. **Claim, Evidence, Explanation**: Build concise literary responses that connect evidence to a claim.
4. **Context As A Reading Tool**: Use brief context to clarify the passage without replacing interpretation.
