# Classical Literature 4 Course Map

Track: Classical Literature  
Level: Level 4  
Sources: Accepted research brief at `research/classical-literature-4/01-research-brief.md` and level blueprint at `research/classical-literature-4/02-level-blueprint.md`

## Course Design Notes

This course map moves from durable reading routines into major classical forms, then ends with transformation and transfer. The map does not try to survey all classical literature. Instead, it builds a sequence of compact mastery units that help students read older texts with genre awareness, evidence, and restraint. Each unit should use short translated or classical-style passages selected during implementation or QA; these planning artifacts define the instructional job before any final curriculum files are written.

## Unit 1: Close Reading Groundwork For Older Texts

### Unit Purpose

Establish the habits students need before substantial classical reading: orienting to speaker, situation, genre, and structure; separating summary from interpretation; using context carefully; and building concise claim/evidence/explanation responses.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify speaker, narrator, character, author, setting, and situation in a short passage.
- Distinguish summary, inference, interpretation, and unsupported opinion.
- Use rereading and paraphrase to make difficult translated language clearer.
- Select evidence that actually supports a claim.
- Explain how background context can illuminate a text without replacing close reading.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Close reading, rereading, summary, inference, interpretation, unsupported opinion, claim, evidence, explanation, genre, speaker, narrator, character, author, situation, context, paraphrase, structure.

### Prerequisites

Students should already be able to read accessible translated excerpts with support, summarize objectively, identify basic theme and genre, and use a simple claim/evidence/explanation routine.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This opening unit retrieves Level 3 routines and gives every later unit a shared method. Epic, drama, comedy, dialogue, lyric, and transformation lessons will all ask students to orient, summarize, choose evidence, and avoid overclaiming.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A difficult text must have one hidden answer.
- Summary and interpretation are the same task.
- Any quoted phrase can support any claim if it sounds important.
- Context is a substitute for reading the passage closely.
- The speaker, author, narrator, and character are automatically the same person.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Return to summary versus interpretation, speaker distinctions, and claim/evidence/explanation in every later unit. Include short delayed review items that ask students to repair overclaims or choose stronger evidence.

## Unit 2: Epic Scale, Heroic Values, And Motif

### Unit Purpose

Introduce epic and heroic narrative as forms that use scale, memory, repetition, public values, and journey or conflict patterns to explore human action. Students learn to read epic scenes for motif, value conflict, and the costs of heroic choices.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify epic genre signals such as elevated action, public stakes, journey, battle, council, invocation, epithet, repetition, and simile.
- Track how a motif such as honor, homecoming, exile, hospitality, justice, or suffering develops across an excerpt.
- Explain how a heroic choice reveals both value and cost.
- Distinguish background myth or setting information from evidence inside the passage.
- Support a theme claim about heroic action with specific textual details.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Epic, hero, invocation, epithet, simile, journey, exile, homecoming, honor, hospitality, council, public stakes, motif, value conflict, fate, divine or providential framing, theme development.

### Prerequisites

Students need Unit 1 orientation routines, basic genre awareness, objective summary, evidence selection, and a willingness to reread passages with names or cultural context.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit extends close reading into a major genre and prepares students for tragedy by showing how public values and individual choices can collide. Its motifs recur in comedy, dialogue, wisdom, and transformation units.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Epic is only a long adventure story.
- A hero must be morally perfect.
- Repetition is accidental or merely decorative.
- Background myth explains the passage without textual evidence.
- A theme is the same as a one-word topic such as "honor."

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Review Unit 1 evidence routines through epic claims. Revisit summary versus interpretation by asking students to separate "what the hero does" from "what the scene suggests about honor, duty, or homecoming."

## Unit 3: Tragedy, Choice, And Dramatic Irony

### Unit Purpose

Teach students to read tragedy as staged conflict shaped by character motive, limited knowledge, reversal, recognition, and the gap between what characters believe and what the audience may understand.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify the dramatic situation of a scene: who speaks, what each character wants, and what conflict drives the exchange.
- Distinguish dramatic irony from sarcasm by identifying a knowledge gap.
- Track how reversal or recognition changes the meaning of earlier action.
- Analyze how fate, choice, law, family, piety, justice, or pride may create a tragic conflict.
- Select evidence for a claim about character motive or theme without reducing tragedy to a simple lesson.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Tragedy, scene, dialogue, dramatic situation, conflict, motive, audience knowledge, dramatic irony, appearance and reality, reversal, recognition, fate, choice, law, piety, justice, pride, suffering.

### Prerequisites

Students need close reading routines, speaker and character distinctions, evidence selection, and experience with epic value conflict.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit builds on epic conflicts but shifts attention to staged speech, limited knowledge, and dramatic structure. It prepares comedy and satire readiness by teaching irony carefully before students meet comic inversion and social criticism.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Irony only means sarcasm.
- A tragic character is simply bad or foolish.
- Fate and choice cancel each other instead of creating tension.
- The most dramatic sentence is automatically the best evidence.
- A tragedy has one simple moral that solves the conflict.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Continue to retrieve epic motifs such as honor, law, suffering, and public duty. Review speaker/character distinctions and claim/evidence/explanation with scene-based questions.

## Unit 4: Comedy, Inversion, And Satire Readiness

### Unit Purpose

Introduce age-appropriate comedy and satire readiness through exaggeration, inversion, mistaken expectation, social criticism, and audience effect. Students learn that comic form can expose folly or disorder while still requiring careful textual evidence.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify comic genre signals such as exaggeration, reversal, disguise or mistaken expectation, verbal play, and social inversion.
- Explain how comedy produces an audience effect through contrast between appearance and reality.
- Distinguish playful exaggeration, social criticism, and mere cruelty.
- Recognize early satire readiness without requiring adult satire content.
- Support a claim about what a comic scene criticizes or reveals.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Comedy, comic reversal, inversion, exaggeration, mistaken expectation, audience effect, folly, social criticism, satire readiness, appearance and reality, tone, target, ridicule, restraint.

### Prerequisites

Students need dramatic irony from Unit 3, speaker and situation awareness, genre vocabulary, and evidence-based interpretation habits.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

Comedy reuses tragedy's attention to audience knowledge but shifts the effect from suffering to exposure, release, or correction. It prepares later dialogue, wisdom, and transformation lessons by asking students to infer the idea behind a surface action or joke.

### Misconceptions To Address

- If a passage is funny, it cannot be serious.
- Satire is just being mean.
- Exaggeration means the text has no real point.
- The joke's target is always the character who speaks.
- Audience effect can be guessed without evidence from structure or wording.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

3-4 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Interleave dramatic irony review with comic appearance/reality contrasts. Continue evidence selection practice by testing whether a claim identifies the actual target of criticism.

## Unit 5: Dialogue, Wisdom, Lyric, And Public Ideas

### Unit Purpose

Help students read shorter idea-bearing forms that are not primarily plot-driven. Students learn to track question and answer, speaker and addressee, aphorism and image, historical example and public value, and the difference between reasoning from a text and turning it into an opinion prompt.

### Student Outcomes

- Follow a dialogue or wisdom passage by identifying question, answer, claim, reason, and example.
- Distinguish speaker, addressee, author, and historical figure when reading lyric, dialogue, or biography/history.
- Interpret an aphorism, proverb, image, or short reflective passage in context.
- Explain how a text raises a moral, civic, theological, or philosophical question without flattening it.
- Use compact evidence to support an idea claim.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Dialogue, question, answer, claim, reason, example, wisdom literature, proverb, aphorism, lyric, speaker, addressee, image, lament, praise, history, biography, civic memory, public virtue, textual question.

### Prerequisites

Students need Unit 1 evidence routines, Unit 3 speaker distinctions, and experience with theme and motif from epic and drama.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit broadens the course beyond narrative and drama. It prepares the final transformation unit by teaching students to track ideas and voices precisely before comparing a source with a later echo.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A dialogue is just a conversation, not a structured inquiry.
- A proverb is always simple or obvious.
- The speaker of a lyric is automatically the author.
- Literature with ideas should be answered mainly with personal opinion.
- Historical or biographical examples remove the need for interpretation.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Review genre signals from epic, tragedy, and comedy while contrasting plot-driven and idea-driven forms. Space claim/evidence/explanation practice through short idea claims and evidence matches.

## Unit 6: Allusion, Transformation, Translation, And Transfer

### Unit Purpose

Bring the level together by teaching students to compare source and echo, notice translation or adaptation choices, interpret allusion, and transfer the full reading routine to an unfamiliar classical-style passage.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify an allusion or echo and explain what meaning it carries from a source pattern into a new passage.
- Compare how a transformation changes setting, speaker, emphasis, conflict, or theme.
- Notice how translation choices can affect tone, imagery, or interpretation.
- Use context carefully when comparing ancient, biblical, medieval, or Renaissance echoes.
- Complete an unfamiliar transfer passage by orienting, summarizing, identifying genre, analyzing theme, and supporting a concise claim.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Allusion, source, echo, adaptation, transformation, translation choice, connotation, emphasis, parallel, contrast, context, medieval echo, Renaissance drama, transfer, interpretive restraint, synthesis.

### Prerequisites

Students need all prior course routines: genre identification, speaker distinction, motif tracking, dramatic irony, comic inversion, idea-tracking, evidence selection, and concise explanation.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This final unit revisits the full level and prepares Level 5 by asking students to meet more difficult primary texts with stamina, humility, and precision. It does not introduce a new survey body; it consolidates and transfers the reading practices built across the level.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Allusion is only name-dropping.
- An adaptation has the same meaning as its source because it borrows a pattern.
- Translation choices are neutral and never affect interpretation.
- Context should settle every question before reading.
- Transfer means guessing from genre labels instead of reading the passage.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use cumulative review deliberately: every transfer passage should retrieve at least two earlier routines, such as speaker distinction plus genre, motif plus evidence, irony plus transformation, or context restraint plus claim/evidence/explanation.
