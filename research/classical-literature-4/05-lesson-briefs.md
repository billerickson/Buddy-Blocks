# Classical Literature 4 Lesson Briefs

Sources: accepted level blueprint, course map, and unit design briefs in `research/classical-literature-4/`.

## Unit 1: Close Reading Groundwork For Older Texts

### Lesson 1: Orient Before You Interpret

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to enter a difficult older passage by identifying speaker, situation, genre clues, and literal action before making an interpretation.
- **Student outcome:** The student can complete a basic orientation for a short passage and reject an interpretation that ignores the literal situation.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Orientation chart: Who is speaking or narrating? What is happening? What genre clues appear? What words or structure seem important?
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic passage reading, narrator and character language, and willingness to reread.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Interpreting from the first striking word; treating narrator, speaker, author, and character as identical; guessing genre from topic alone.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies speaker/situation/genre clues and chooses a claim that fits those observations.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for orientation from a shared excerpt; `multiple-choice` for speaker and situation checks; `match-pairs` for role vocabulary; `order-items` for reading routine sequence.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "Before you interpret an older text, slow down. Ask: Who is speaking? What is happening? What kind of text might this be?" Card 2: "A strong interpretation grows out of orientation. First name the situation, then make a claim."
- **Teacher-facing teaching note:** Use compact excerpts with visible clues; do not ask for theme before students have oriented to the passage.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep passages under about 120 words, limit unfamiliar names, and bold no more than one key term in prompts if formatting is later used.

### Lesson 2: Summary, Inference, Interpretation

- **Teaching goal:** Help students distinguish literal summary, reasonable inference, interpretation, and unsupported opinion.
- **Student outcome:** The student can classify reading statements and improve a weak interpretation by tying it to evidence.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Four-level ladder: summary says what happens; inference reads between lines; interpretation explains meaning; unsupported opinion lacks textual support.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Objective summary, basic evidence selection, and familiar claim language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating any personal reaction as interpretation; thinking summary is less important; calling every guess an inference.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student sorts statements accurately and chooses a better evidence-based interpretation.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for classification; `match-pairs` for statement type examples; `error-correction` for repairing an overclaim with finite accepted wording; `passage-question` for applying the distinction to a short passage.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "Readers do different jobs. Summary tells what the passage says. Inference notices what is implied. Interpretation explains what it means." Card 2: "A personal reaction can be interesting, but it is not a literary interpretation unless the text supports it."
- **Teacher-facing teaching note:** Keep unsupported opinion examples plausible rather than silly so students learn the real boundary.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one passage for several items and keep labels consistent.

### Lesson 3: Context Helps, Evidence Leads

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to use background context as a helper that must return to the passage's words, structure, or situation.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose context that illuminates a passage and reject context-only interpretations.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Context-use checklist: Does the note clarify a word, custom, genre, or situation? Does the interpretation still point back to textual evidence?
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Summary, evidence, genre, and basic historical-context awareness.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Context proves meaning without textual evidence; old texts are mainly history lessons; background notes should be memorized instead of applied.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student connects a context note to a specific detail and identifies a claim that overuses context.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for context-plus-passage reasoning; `multiple-choice` for best use of context; `match-pairs` for context notes to textual details; `fill-blank` for completing context/evidence sentences.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "Context can help you read an older text. It may explain a custom, place, genre, or difficult word." Card 2: "Context does not replace the passage. A strong answer says how the note helps you understand specific words or actions."
- **Teacher-facing teaching note:** Provide only the context needed for the item; avoid turning lessons into history lectures.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Limit context notes to one or two sentences and avoid requiring outside recall.

### Lesson 4: Build A Claim With Evidence

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to assemble a concise claim, evidence, and explanation chain for literary analysis.
- **Student outcome:** The student can select a text-grounded claim, match relevant evidence, and complete an explanation of how the evidence supports the claim.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Claim/evidence/explanation chain: claim answers the interpretive question; evidence points to the text; explanation shows the connection.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Summary versus interpretation, context restraint, and basic evidence quoting or paraphrasing.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Evidence is any line from the passage; explanation repeats the claim; longer claims are stronger; a claim should be vague enough to fit anything.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses the best supported claim and orders the reasoning chain correctly.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for reasoning sequence; `match-pairs` for claims to evidence; `multiple-choice` for best explanation; `multi-blank-cloze` for completing a concise analytic sentence.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "A strong literary answer has three parts: a claim, evidence, and explanation." Card 2: "The explanation is the bridge. It tells how the evidence proves or supports the claim."
- **Teacher-facing teaching note:** Use short evidence snippets so the lesson assesses reasoning rather than copying.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid requiring long written production; keep answer choices close enough to require evidence fit.

## Unit 2: Epic Scale, Heroic Values, And Motif

### Lesson 1: What Makes A Passage Epic?

- **Teaching goal:** Introduce epic genre signals and help students see epic as a form with public stakes, elevated action, and repeated patterns.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify epic signals in a passage and explain why they matter for reading.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Epic genre-signal chart: large public stakes, heroic action, journey or conflict, formal speech, invocation, epithet, simile, repetition, divine or providential frame.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Unit 1 orientation and basic genre vocabulary.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Epic means any exciting or long story; a list of events is enough to identify genre; genre labels are decorative.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies several epic signals and matches a genre signal to its effect.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for genre recognition; `match-pairs` for signal to meaning; `multiple-choice` for public-stakes checks; `fill-blank` for vocabulary.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "Epic is a heroic form. It often uses public stakes, journeys, formal speech, repeated phrases, and large conflicts." Card 2: "Genre is not just a label. Epic signals tell you what kind of meaning the passage is trying to make."
- **Teacher-facing teaching note:** Use excerpts that show form without requiring a full epic plot summary.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Preteach only essential genre terms; keep names and setting notes minimal.

### Lesson 2: Motifs Of Honor And Homecoming

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to track repeated motifs and explain how repetition develops meaning.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a motif such as honor or homecoming and explain how repeated details build a theme.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Motif tracker: repeated word, image, action, or value; where it appears; how it changes or gains weight.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Theme versus topic, evidence selection, and epic genre signals.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A motif is any important word once; repetition is accidental; naming "honor" is the same as explaining a theme.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches repeated details to a motif and selects a theme statement supported by the pattern.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for detail-to-motif; `passage-question` for motif development; `multiple-choice` for theme statement; `order-items` for tracing motif sequence.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "A motif is a repeated detail, image, action, or idea. In epic, motifs such as honor, homecoming, exile, and hospitality often carry meaning." Card 2: "Do not stop at naming the motif. Ask what the repetition suggests."
- **Teacher-facing teaching note:** Reuse one motif across several items to model tracking rather than isolated spotting.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep motif names concrete and avoid multiple competing motifs in the first scored item.

### Lesson 3: Heroic Choice And Cost

- **Teaching goal:** Help students analyze heroic choices as moments that reveal both values and consequences.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain what a heroic choice values and what cost or risk it brings.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Choice/cost table: action, value shown, cost or risk, evidence.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Epic genre, motif tracking, and claim/evidence/explanation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A hero's choice is automatically right; cost means the choice was wrong; action alone proves theme without motive or consequence.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies value and cost in a heroic action and supports a restrained interpretation.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for action analysis; `match-pairs` for action to value/cost; `multiple-choice` for best claim; `multi-blank-cloze` for completing a choice/cost explanation.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "Heroic literature often honors courage, loyalty, duty, or endurance. But a heroic choice can also carry a cost." Card 2: "To analyze a choice, ask what the action values and what consequence follows."
- **Teacher-facing teaching note:** Avoid framing heroes as flawless or useless; keep the tension visible.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one clear action per question and avoid requiring broad myth knowledge.

### Lesson 4: Epic Simile And Repeated Language

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to interpret epic simile and repeated language as meaning-making style.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain how a simile, epithet, or repeated phrase emphasizes a character, action, or theme.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Style-to-meaning map: repeated phrase or comparison, literal image, emphasized quality, interpretive effect.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Figurative language, motif, and epic genre signals.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Figurative language is decoration; an epithet is just a nickname; repeated language means the author ran out of words.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the emphasized quality in a simile or repetition and chooses its best interpretive effect.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for simile interpretation; `multiple-choice` for effect; `match-pairs` for style feature to function; `fill-blank` for terminology.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "Epic style often repeats phrases and uses large comparisons. These choices slow us down and focus attention." Card 2: "Ask: What quality does this comparison or repeated phrase emphasize?"
- **Teacher-facing teaching note:** Choose comparisons with clear images and avoid requiring technical scansion or original-language knowledge.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Provide short comparisons and define unfamiliar objects within the prompt when needed.

## Unit 3: Tragedy, Choice, And Dramatic Irony

### Lesson 1: Read The Dramatic Situation

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to orient to a dramatic scene by identifying speakers, immediate conflict, and scene pressure.
- **Student outcome:** The student can describe who is speaking, what is at stake, and what conflict drives a short scene.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Dramatic situation chart: speaker, addressee, what each knows, what each wants, what pressure shapes the exchange.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Speaker/character distinction and close-reading orientation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Dialogue is easy because characters speak directly; the loudest character controls the meaning; stage conflict is the same as physical fighting.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the dramatic situation and chooses an accurate description of the conflict.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for scene orientation; `match-pairs` for character to motive; `multiple-choice` for conflict; `order-items` for scene turn sequence.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "In drama, meaning happens through a scene. First ask: Who is speaking? To whom? What does each person want?" Card 2: "A dramatic conflict can be a clash of duties, fears, promises, or beliefs, not only a fight."
- **Teacher-facing teaching note:** Use short scenes with clear turns; avoid requiring knowledge of the full play.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep speaker labels simple and limit the number of characters to two or three.

### Lesson 2: Motive, Conflict, And Choice

- **Teaching goal:** Help students infer character motive from speech and action, then connect motive to tragic conflict.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify what a character wants and how a choice reveals a value or pressure.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Motive/conflict map: stated reason, implied motive, opposing pressure, choice.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Dramatic situation, inference, evidence selection, and epic choice/cost analysis.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Motive must be stated directly; one character is wholly right and the other wholly wrong; conflict can be solved by choosing the most emotional line.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches a character to a motive and chooses evidence that supports a claim about choice.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for character to motive; `passage-question` for evidence-based inference; `multiple-choice` for value conflict; `multi-blank-cloze` for motive/explanation.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "A motive is what drives a character to act. Sometimes a character states it; sometimes you infer it from words and choices." Card 2: "In tragedy, motives often collide with duties, laws, family, pride, or fear."
- **Teacher-facing teaching note:** Keep motives text-grounded and avoid psychoanalytic overreach.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use clear, repeated character names and avoid more than two competing motives in one item.

### Lesson 3: Dramatic Irony Is A Knowledge Gap

- **Teaching goal:** Define dramatic irony as a gap between what the audience knows and what a character knows or believes.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a dramatic irony situation and explain how the gap changes audience understanding.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Who-knows-what chart: audience knowledge, character belief, gap, effect.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Dramatic situation, speaker distinction, and inference.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Irony means sarcasm; any surprise is irony; dramatic irony means the character is lying.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student distinguishes dramatic irony from sarcasm or coincidence and names the knowledge gap.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for definition and nonexamples; `passage-question` for identifying the gap; `match-pairs` for audience/character knowledge; `fill-blank` for completing a knowledge-gap statement.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "Dramatic irony happens when the audience knows something a character does not know or misunderstands." Card 2: "The power comes from the gap: we hear a line differently because we know more than the character."
- **Teacher-facing teaching note:** Use explicit audience knowledge in the prompt so the app can score the concept cleanly.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Do not mix dramatic irony with verbal irony in the same first practice set.

### Lesson 4: Reversal, Recognition, And Tragic Meaning

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students how reversal and recognition reshape a tragic scene or passage.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a turning point and explain how new knowledge changes the meaning of earlier action.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Scene-turn sequence: expectation, event or discovery, reversal, recognition, changed meaning.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Dramatic conflict, motive, and dramatic irony.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Reversal is any event; recognition is just feeling sad; the final event alone contains the theme.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student sequences a scene turn and chooses an explanation of how recognition changes interpretation.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for scene turn sequence; `passage-question` for recognition effect; `multiple-choice` for turning point; `match-pairs` for terms to examples.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "A reversal changes the direction of a scene. Recognition is a moment of new understanding." Card 2: "In tragedy, a discovery can make earlier words or choices feel different."
- **Teacher-facing teaching note:** Use compact invented or adapted scenes with a clear before/after structure.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep the sequence to four or five steps and make time order explicit.

## Unit 4: Comedy, Inversion, And Satire Readiness

### Lesson 1: Comic Inversion And Exaggeration

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to recognize comic inversion and exaggeration as deliberate forms that can reveal folly.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify what has been reversed or exaggerated and infer why the contrast matters.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Comic contrast chart: expected order, inverted or exaggerated version, effect, possible criticism.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Dramatic situation and appearance/reality contrast.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Exaggeration means nonsense; comedy has no serious meaning; any reversal is equally important.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the comic device and chooses a supported explanation of its purpose.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for comic device; `match-pairs` for device to example; `multiple-choice` for purpose; `fill-blank` for key terms.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "Comedy often turns expectations upside down. A servant may sound wiser than a master, or a proud person may look foolish." Card 2: "Exaggeration can be funny, but it can also reveal what the passage criticizes."
- **Teacher-facing teaching note:** Use gentle, age-appropriate examples of folly; do not rely on adult satire.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep humor textual and visible; avoid sarcasm that depends on tone of voice alone.

### Lesson 2: Appearance, Reality, And Audience Effect

- **Teaching goal:** Help students explain how a gap between appearance and reality creates comic audience effect.
- **Student outcome:** The student can name the apparent situation, the real situation, and the effect on the audience.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Appearance/reality/effect triangle: what seems true, what is true, how the audience responds.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Dramatic irony, comic inversion, and evidence-based explanation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Audience effect is just "it is funny"; appearance/reality gaps always create tragedy; the audience's knowledge does not matter.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the gap and matches it to surprise, release, correction, or criticism.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for appearance to reality; `passage-question` for effect; `multiple-choice` for best explanation; `order-items` for setup and payoff.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "Comedy often lets the audience see a gap between what seems true and what is true." Card 2: "That gap can create surprise, release, or criticism. Explain the effect by pointing to the passage."
- **Teacher-facing teaching note:** Review dramatic irony briefly but emphasize the different comic effect.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one main gap per passage and keep answer choices effect-focused.

### Lesson 3: Satire Readiness: Finding The Target

- **Teaching goal:** Prepare students for satire by teaching them to identify the target of comic criticism and distinguish criticism from mere cruelty.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain what behavior, belief, or social pattern a comic passage criticizes.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Target chart: who or what is being exposed, what evidence shows it, what criticism the passage suggests.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Comic inversion, audience effect, and claim/evidence/explanation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** The target is whoever is mocked most visibly; satire means insulting a person; a funny passage cannot criticize a serious problem.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies a target and supports it with evidence from exaggeration, contrast, or consequence.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for target identification; `multiple-choice` for best criticism statement; `match-pairs` for evidence to target; `error-correction` for repairing an overbroad satire claim.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "Satire uses humor or exaggeration to criticize something. The target may be a behavior, habit, belief, or social pattern." Card 2: "Do not assume the target too quickly. Look for what the passage exposes through contrast and consequence."
- **Teacher-facing teaching note:** Keep targets age-appropriate and avoid treating ridicule as automatically virtuous.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use clear behavior-based targets and finite corrected claims for error-correction items.

## Unit 5: Dialogue, Wisdom, Lyric, And Public Ideas

### Lesson 1: Dialogue As Structured Inquiry

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to read dialogue as an inquiry organized by question, answer, claim, reason, and example.
- **Student outcome:** The student can map a short dialogue and identify how a reason or example supports a claim.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Inquiry map: question, first answer, challenge, reason, example, refined claim.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Speaker distinction, motive, and evidence selection.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Dialogue is random conversation; the last speaker is automatically correct; disagreement means one speaker is foolish.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the central question and matches claims with supporting reasons or examples.
- **Recommended question types:** `dialogue-builder` for choosing a logically fitting next line; `order-items` for inquiry sequence; `match-pairs` for claim to reason; `multiple-choice` for central question.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "A dialogue can be a structured search for understanding. Watch how questions, answers, reasons, and examples move the thinking." Card 2: "Do not only ask who wins. Ask how the conversation changes the idea."
- **Teacher-facing teaching note:** Use short artificial or adapted dialogues with clear logical moves.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep each turn brief and avoid abstract vocabulary without support.

### Lesson 2: Wisdom Sayings In Context

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to interpret aphorisms or proverbs in relation to surrounding situation and evidence.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain a wisdom saying's meaning in context and avoid treating it as a free-floating slogan.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Saying-in-context chart: exact saying, situation, key image or contrast, meaning for this passage.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Inference, theme, and context restraint.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A proverb means the same thing in every situation; short sayings are easy and need no evidence; any wise-sounding phrase is the theme.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches a saying to its contextual meaning and chooses evidence that clarifies it.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for contextual meaning; `multiple-choice` for best paraphrase; `match-pairs` for saying to situation; `fill-blank` for completing a context-based explanation.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "Wisdom literature often speaks in short sayings. A short saying still needs context." Card 2: "Ask what problem, choice, or image around the saying helps explain its meaning."
- **Teacher-facing teaching note:** Avoid treating wisdom texts as doctrine; keep the focus on interpretation of the passage.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one saying at a time and paraphrase archaic wording when needed in the prompt.

### Lesson 3: Lyric Speaker And Image

- **Teaching goal:** Help students distinguish lyric speaker from author and interpret imagery as a path to idea or emotion.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify speaker/addressee and explain how an image shapes meaning.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Speaker/image map: who speaks, to whom, image, feeling or idea, evidence.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Speaker distinction, figurative language, and evidence-based interpretation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** The speaker is automatically the poet; images are decorative; emotion alone is the interpretation.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies speaker and addressee, then chooses a supported explanation of an image.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for speaker and image; `multiple-choice` for author/speaker distinction; `match-pairs` for image to effect; `multi-blank-cloze` for completing interpretation.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "A lyric often gives us a speaker's voice, not a full plot. The speaker is not automatically the author." Card 2: "Images help carry feeling and idea. Ask what the image makes you notice."
- **Teacher-facing teaching note:** Use short, age-appropriate lyric-style excerpts and make addressee clues visible.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep imagery concrete and avoid dense symbolic layering in early items.

### Lesson 4: History, Biography, And Public Virtue

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to read historical or biographical examples as shaped portraits that communicate public values.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify what virtue, warning, or civic idea a portrait emphasizes and how evidence creates that emphasis.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Portrait-to-value chart: action, description, public value, evidence, caution about overclaiming.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Claim/evidence/explanation, motif, and context restraint.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Historical writing is only fact storage; biography automatically praises its subject; a public value is the same as a personal opinion.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches details to a public value and chooses a restrained interpretation of a historical portrait.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for portrait interpretation; `match-pairs` for action to value; `multiple-choice` for best claim; `order-items` for evidence-to-conclusion reasoning.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "History and biography can do literary work. A portrait of a leader, judge, citizen, or exile may emphasize a public value." Card 2: "Ask which details the passage selects and what those details make the reader admire, question, or remember."
- **Teacher-facing teaching note:** Use portraits that invite interpretation without requiring students to judge the whole historical figure.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep context brief and avoid political abstractions unsupported by the passage.

## Unit 6: Allusion, Transformation, Translation, And Transfer

### Lesson 1: Allusion Carries Meaning

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students that allusion carries meaning from a source pattern into a new passage.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a source echo and explain what idea or association it brings.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Source/echo chart: source detail, echo detail, shared idea, changed context, interpretive effect.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Motif, genre, context restraint, and evidence selection.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Allusion is only name-dropping; recognizing the source is enough; any shared object proves an allusion.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches source and echo and chooses a supported explanation of carried meaning.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for source to echo; `passage-question` for allusion effect; `multiple-choice` for meaning carried; `fill-blank` for vocabulary.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "An allusion is an echo of another text, story, or tradition. It can carry meaning from the source into the new passage." Card 2: "Recognition is only the first step. Ask what the echo adds or changes."
- **Teacher-facing teaching note:** Provide source information inside the lesson so students are not tested on outside recall.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short paired passages and make the shared detail explicit enough to assess interpretation.

### Lesson 2: Adaptation Changes Emphasis

- **Teaching goal:** Help students compare a source pattern with a transformation and identify changed emphasis.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain how a changed speaker, setting, conflict, or ending changes meaning.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Adaptation comparison table: same pattern, changed element, new emphasis, evidence.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Allusion, genre, speaker distinction, motif, and theme.
- **Likely misconceptions:** An adaptation has the same meaning as its source; any difference is a mistake; comparison is only listing similarities.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies a meaningful change and chooses the best explanation of its effect.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for paired comparison; `match-pairs` for source/adaptation elements; `multiple-choice` for changed emphasis; `order-items` for comparison reasoning steps.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "An adaptation borrows from a source but changes something: speaker, setting, conflict, tone, ending, or emphasis." Card 2: "A good comparison asks both what stays the same and what changes."
- **Teacher-facing teaching note:** Use paired passages brief enough that students can compare details without losing the main contrast.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Limit comparisons to one or two changed elements per item.

### Lesson 3: Translation Choices And Tone

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to notice how translation or wording choices shift tone, image, or interpretive emphasis.
- **Student outcome:** The student can compare two wording choices and explain which tone or emphasis each creates.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Translation-choice lens: word choice, connotation, image, tone, interpretive effect.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Figurative language, tone, evidence, and context restraint.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Translations are interchangeable; only plot matters; a single word choice can be interpreted without context.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses the stronger tone description for a wording choice and matches wording to effect.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for word choice to tone; `passage-question` for paired wording interpretation; `multiple-choice` for best emphasis; `fill-blank` for connotation language.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "When we read classical literature in translation, wording choices matter. A translator may choose a word that sounds solemn, gentle, harsh, or swift." Card 2: "Do not claim too much from one word. Ask how the choice fits the sentence and passage."
- **Teacher-facing teaching note:** Use invented translation-choice pairs or public-domain-safe excerpts during implementation; focus on effect, not original-language grammar.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Provide two clear wording options and keep tone choices accessible.

### Lesson 4: Unfamiliar Passage Transfer

- **Teaching goal:** Assess whether students can transfer the level's reading routines to an unfamiliar classical-style passage.
- **Student outcome:** The student can orient, summarize, identify genre, analyze a theme or idea, and support a concise claim from a new passage.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Transfer checklist: orient, summarize, identify genre clues, notice structure or motif, choose evidence, make restrained claim.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** All previous level routines and vocabulary.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Transfer means guessing from a genre label; the first interesting detail proves the claim; unfamiliar passages require outside knowledge first.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student applies multiple routines accurately to a new passage and chooses a supported final interpretation.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for cumulative reading; `order-items` for routine sequence; `match-pairs` for evidence to claims; `multiple-choice` for best synthesis claim.
- **Student-facing intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "In this final lesson, you will read a new passage using the routines from the level." Card 2: "Slow down: orient, summarize, notice genre and structure, then choose evidence for a careful claim."
- **Teacher-facing teaching note:** Use an unfamiliar but level-appropriate passage that does not require outside knowledge; assess integrated habits rather than new terminology.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep the passage substantial but manageable, about 150-220 words, with clear paragraphing and no more than three unfamiliar names.
