# Unit 1: Close Reading Groundwork For Older Texts

Track: Classical Literature  
Level: Level 4  
Stage: Unit Design Brief  
Source: `research/classical-literature-4/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students establish the reading routines needed for substantial classical literature: orienting to speaker and situation, separating summary from interpretation, using context with restraint, and building concise evidence-based claims.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit comes first because later genre work will fail if students treat difficult language as a guessing game or treat background knowledge as a replacement for the passage. It gives the level a shared method before epic, drama, comedy, dialogue, lyric, and transformation increase the interpretive load.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: orientation before interpretation, rereading, summary versus interpretation, evidence fit, context restraint.
- Vocabulary: speaker, narrator, character, author, situation, genre, summary, inference, interpretation, unsupported opinion, claim, evidence, explanation, context, paraphrase.
- Representations and models: passage orientation chart, summary/interpretation sort, context-use checklist, claim/evidence/explanation chain, overclaim repair.
- Texts and forms: short translated or classical-style excerpts, compact context notes, selected claims, evidence matches, sentence-level paraphrases.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should be able to read guided excerpts, summarize a short passage, identify basic genre and theme, and write or select a simple claim supported by evidence.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- The first impression of a difficult passage is enough.
- Summary and interpretation are interchangeable.
- Background information can prove an interpretation by itself.
- A claim is strong if it sounds deep, even when evidence is weak.
- The speaker, author, narrator, and character are the same by default.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with orientation so students know how to enter a passage before making claims. Then separate summary, inference, and interpretation so students can name the kind of thinking they are doing. Next, teach context as a helper that must return to textual evidence. End with claim/evidence/explanation so students can use the unit's routines in a compact analytic form.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

This opening unit reviews Level 3 habits: objective summary, basic genre signals, textual evidence, version awareness, and concise explanation. It should reveal fragile understandings rather than reteach all prior literature content.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

The unit prepares every later unit by making orientation, evidence, and restraint habitual. Epic motifs, dramatic irony, comic inversion, lyric speaker, and allusion will all depend on these routines.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify speaker, situation, genre clue, and immediate conflict or question.
- Classify a statement as summary, inference, interpretation, or unsupported opinion.
- Choose context that helps interpret the passage without replacing it.
- Match evidence to a claim and reject weak evidence.
- Complete a concise claim/evidence/explanation chain.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Orient Before You Interpret**: Identify speaker, situation, genre clues, and literal action before making a claim.
2. **Summary, Inference, Interpretation**: Sort kinds of reading statements and recognize unsupported opinion.
3. **Context Helps, Evidence Leads**: Use background knowledge only when it returns to words and structure in the passage.
4. **Build A Claim With Evidence**: Select evidence and explanation that support a concise literary claim.
