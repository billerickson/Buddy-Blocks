# Unit 5: Dialogue, Wisdom, Lyric, And Public Ideas

Track: Classical Literature  
Level: Level 4  
Stage: Unit Design Brief  
Source: `research/classical-literature-4/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students read shorter idea-bearing forms by tracking voice, question, reason, example, image, and public value. They learn to discuss moral, civic, theological, or philosophical questions as textual questions before turning to personal opinion.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

After narrative, tragedy, and comedy, students are ready for forms that carry ideas through inquiry, aphorism, image, and example rather than plot alone. This unit broadens genre awareness and prepares source/echo comparison in the final unit.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: inquiry, compact wisdom, lyric voice, image-to-idea movement, historical example, public virtue.
- Vocabulary: dialogue, question, answer, claim, reason, example, wisdom literature, proverb, aphorism, lyric, speaker, addressee, image, lament, praise, history, biography, civic memory.
- Representations and models: question/answer map, claim/reason/example chain, aphorism-in-context chart, speaker/addressee chart, image-to-idea map.
- Texts and forms: brief dialogues, wisdom sayings, lyric stanzas or classical-style reflective passages, historical or biographical portraits, short civic examples.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need orientation routines, speaker distinctions, evidence selection, and experience interpreting theme through action and conflict.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- A dialogue is unstructured conversation.
- A proverb's meaning is always obvious outside context.
- The lyric speaker is automatically the author.
- Idea-bearing literature should be answered mainly by opinion.
- History or biography provides facts but no literary form.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with dialogue because it makes reasoning visible through question and answer. Move to wisdom sayings to practice compact interpretation in context. Then study lyric speaker and image, where students must avoid author/speaker confusion. End with history and biography so students can see how public examples carry civic or moral ideas.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Review speaker distinctions from Units 1 and 3, motif and theme from Unit 2, and target/evidence reasoning from Unit 4. Continue compact claim/evidence/explanation practice.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares the final unit by making students precise with voice, idea, image, and evidence before comparing sources, echoes, translation choices, and transformations.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify question, answer, claim, reason, and example in dialogue.
- Interpret a wisdom saying in context.
- Distinguish lyric speaker from author and addressee.
- Connect image to idea without overclaiming.
- Explain how a historical or biographical example carries a public value.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Dialogue As Structured Inquiry**: Follow question, answer, claim, reason, and example.
2. **Wisdom Sayings In Context**: Interpret aphorism or proverb through surrounding situation.
3. **Lyric Speaker And Image**: Distinguish speaker from author and connect imagery to idea.
4. **History, Biography, And Public Virtue**: Read a public example as literary and moral argument.
