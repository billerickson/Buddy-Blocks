# Unit 3: Tragedy, Choice, And Dramatic Irony

Track: Classical Literature  
Level: Level 4  
Stage: Unit Design Brief  
Source: `research/classical-literature-4/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students read tragic scenes as staged conflicts where character motive, limited knowledge, fate, choice, reversal, and recognition shape meaning. They learn dramatic irony as a knowledge gap, not a synonym for sarcasm.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

Tragedy belongs after epic because students already know how public values and personal choices can collide. Drama increases the challenge by requiring students to track speech, motive, audience knowledge, and scene structure.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: staged conflict, motive, limited knowledge, fate and choice, reversal, recognition, tragic meaning.
- Vocabulary: tragedy, scene, dialogue, dramatic situation, conflict, motive, audience knowledge, dramatic irony, appearance, reality, reversal, recognition, law, piety, pride, suffering.
- Representations and models: who-wants-what chart, who-knows-what chart, conflict map, scene-turn tracker, reversal/recognition sequence.
- Texts and forms: age-appropriate tragic scenes or classical-style dramatic excerpts, short speeches, paired character claims, stage-situation notes.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need speaker/character distinctions, evidence selection, epic value conflict, and the ability to summarize a scene's literal action.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Irony always means sarcasm.
- A tragic character is only foolish or bad.
- Fate and choice cannot both matter.
- The loudest or saddest line is automatically the best evidence.
- Tragedy should produce one simple lesson that resolves the conflict.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with dramatic situation so students can orient to who speaks, what each character wants, and what conflict drives the scene. Then study motive and choice. Once students can track competing knowledge and motives, introduce dramatic irony. End with reversal and recognition so students see how tragic structure changes the meaning of earlier action.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Review Unit 1 speaker distinctions and Unit 2 value conflict. Continue claim/evidence/explanation practice through short scene-based interpretation.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares comedy by defining audience knowledge and appearance/reality gaps. It prepares dialogue and transformation by strengthening attention to voice, conflict, and changed understanding.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify dramatic situation, speaker, motive, and conflict.
- Distinguish dramatic irony from sarcasm or coincidence.
- Explain how a knowledge gap affects audience understanding.
- Sequence reversal and recognition.
- Support a character or theme claim with scene evidence.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Read The Dramatic Situation**: Identify speakers, conflict, and the scene's immediate pressure.
2. **Motive, Conflict, And Choice**: Track what characters want and how choices reveal values.
3. **Dramatic Irony Is A Knowledge Gap**: Recognize the gap between audience knowledge and character knowledge.
4. **Reversal, Recognition, And Tragic Meaning**: Explain how changed understanding reshapes the scene.
