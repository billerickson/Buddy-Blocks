# Unit 4: Comedy, Inversion, And Satire Readiness

Track: Classical Literature  
Level: Level 4  
Stage: Unit Design Brief  
Source: `research/classical-literature-4/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn to read comedy and satire readiness through exaggeration, inversion, mistaken expectation, audience effect, and social criticism. They practice finding the target of a comic passage without assuming that all humor is shallow or all ridicule is justified.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

Comedy follows tragedy because both depend on staged gaps between appearance and reality, but they create different effects. The unit also gives students a responsible bridge into satire without requiring mature satirical content.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: comic effect, inversion, exaggeration, mistaken expectation, social criticism, target of ridicule, restraint.
- Vocabulary: comedy, comic reversal, inversion, exaggeration, folly, audience effect, appearance, reality, social criticism, satire readiness, tone, target, ridicule.
- Representations and models: setup/payoff chart, appearance/reality contrast, target evidence table, exaggeration-to-criticism map.
- Texts and forms: age-appropriate comic scenes, short social reversals, fable-like comic examples, controlled satire-readiness passages.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need dramatic situation and irony from Unit 3, plus Unit 1 evidence habits and speaker distinctions.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Funny passages cannot have serious meaning.
- Satire means random insult.
- Exaggeration cancels truth.
- The target of the joke is always the person being laughed at most loudly.
- Audience effect can be named without evidence.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Start with comic inversion and exaggeration because these are visible and age-appropriate. Then connect appearance/reality gaps to audience effect. End with satire readiness by asking students to identify what behavior, belief, or social pattern the passage criticizes.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Review dramatic irony as a knowledge gap before comparing it with comic surprise. Review claim/evidence/explanation by asking students to support claims about the target and effect of humor.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

The unit prepares dialogue, wisdom, and transformation work by sharpening students' ability to infer an idea from surface action, tone, or speaker behavior.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify exaggeration, inversion, and mistaken expectation.
- Explain how a comic contrast creates audience effect.
- Distinguish satire readiness from mere cruelty.
- Identify the likely target of criticism.
- Support a claim about humor or social criticism with evidence.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Comic Inversion And Exaggeration**: Recognize reversals and overstatements that create comic meaning.
2. **Appearance, Reality, And Audience Effect**: Explain how a gap produces surprise, release, or criticism.
3. **Satire Readiness: Finding The Target**: Identify what a comic passage criticizes and what evidence supports that reading.
