# Unit 2: Epic Scale, Heroic Values, And Motif

Track: Classical Literature  
Level: Level 4  
Stage: Unit Design Brief  
Source: `research/classical-literature-4/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn to read epic and heroic narrative as literary form, not just adventure. They identify genre signals, track motifs, and explain how heroic choices reveal values, costs, and public stakes.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

Epic follows the groundwork unit because it is a natural first major genre for sustained classical reading. Its repeated structures, large stakes, and visible motifs give students a manageable way to practice theme development before drama and dialogue require more subtle speaker and audience work.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: epic scale, heroic value, public stakes, motif development, choice and cost, theme through repeated action.
- Vocabulary: epic, hero, invocation, epithet, simile, motif, honor, homecoming, exile, hospitality, council, battle, journey, fate, divine or providential framing.
- Representations and models: genre-signal chart, motif tracker, choice/cost table, simile-to-idea map, value-conflict evidence chain.
- Texts and forms: guided translated or classical-style epic excerpts, heroic speeches, journey episodes, council scenes, short comparisons of action and value.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need Unit 1 orientation, summary, evidence selection, context restraint, and basic knowledge that older heroic stories may combine human action with divine or providential framing.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Epic means any long story with action.
- A hero must be flawless.
- Motifs are repeated by accident.
- A one-word topic such as "honor" is already a theme.
- Knowing the myth makes close reading unnecessary.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Start with epic genre signals so students can identify the form. Then move to motif tracking, because repetition is one of the easiest ways to see meaning develop. Next, examine heroic choice and cost, which prepares students to analyze value conflict. End with epic simile and repeated language to show how form and style deepen theme.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Review Unit 1 orientation and evidence routines in every lesson. Students should repeatedly distinguish literal heroic action from interpretation of what the action suggests.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

Epic value conflicts prepare students for tragedy's conflicts of law, family, piety, pride, fate, and choice. Motif tracking prepares allusion and transformation work in the final unit.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify epic genre signals in a passage.
- Track a motif across repeated details.
- Explain how a heroic choice carries both value and cost.
- Interpret an epic simile or repeated phrase in context.
- Support a theme claim with focused evidence.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **What Makes A Passage Epic?**: Recognize epic genre signals and public stakes.
2. **Motifs Of Honor And Homecoming**: Track repeated ideas and images across an epic excerpt.
3. **Heroic Choice And Cost**: Analyze how a hero's action reveals values and consequences.
4. **Epic Simile And Repeated Language**: Interpret how style, comparison, and repetition deepen meaning.
