# Classical Literature 4 Level Blueprint

Track: Classical Literature  
Level: Level 4  
Source: Accepted research brief at `research/classical-literature-4/01-research-brief.md`

## 1. Level Mission

Classical Literature 4 builds students' readiness for substantial older texts by teaching them to read translated excerpts and selected shorter classical forms with humility, stamina, and textual precision. Students strengthen the habits of orienting to genre, speaker, situation, structure, and context before making claims. They learn that epic, drama, comedy, dialogue, lyric, wisdom literature, history, and later transformations each shape meaning differently. By the end of the level, students should be able to meet an unfamiliar classical-style passage, summarize it accurately, identify the genre work it is doing, analyze how a theme or idea develops, and support a concise interpretation with evidence from the text.

## 2. Durable Student Outcomes

By the end of Classical Literature 4, students should be able to:

1. Read sustained translated excerpts or selected complete shorter works with guided support, rereading when structure or language is difficult.
2. Distinguish summary, inference, interpretation, and unsupported opinion in literary discussion.
3. Identify genre signals in epic, tragedy, comedy, dialogue, lyric, history/biography, wisdom literature, and satire readiness, then explain how genre shapes meaning.
4. Track narrator, speaker, character, author, and historical figure distinctions, especially in drama, lyric, dialogue, and adapted sources.
5. Analyze how theme develops through conflict, character choice, structure, imagery, motif, allusion, and repeated language.
6. Interpret figurative language, symbol, dramatic irony, rhetorical patterning, and allusion in context without overclaiming.
7. Compare translation choices, adaptations, transformations, or later echoes of classical and biblical material.
8. Use background knowledge judiciously, letting context illuminate the text without replacing close reading.
9. Make concise literary claims supported by well-chosen textual evidence and explanation.
10. Transfer these habits to an unfamiliar classical-style passage with appropriate confidence and caution.

## 3. Concept And Skill Progression

The level begins by stabilizing reading habits that protect students from premature interpretation: orienting to speaker, setting, genre, situation, and literal action; separating summary from analysis; and using evidence before forming a claim. Early lessons should include compact excerpts and guided rereading so students experience difficulty as part of the work rather than as failure.

Students then study epic and heroic narrative as a form that uses scale, memory, conflict, simile, and public values to make meaning. They learn to track heroic choices, costs, hospitality, honor, homecoming, exile, divine or providential framing, and repeated motifs. The emphasis is not on memorizing mythological facts but on seeing how epic form organizes action and values.

The middle of the level turns to drama, first tragedy and then comedy or satire readiness. Students learn to read scenes as staged conflicts: what each character knows, wants, says, and fails to see. They distinguish dramatic irony from sarcasm, track reversal and recognition, and notice how comedy uses exaggeration, inversion, and social criticism to expose folly without turning every joke into a simple moral.

Next, students read dialogue, wisdom, lyric, and historical or biographical forms as different ways literature carries ideas. They follow question-and-answer reasoning, distinguish speaker from author, interpret aphorism and image, and use compact evidence to explain a moral, civic, theological, or philosophical question raised by a text.

The level ends with allusion, transformation, translation, and transfer. Students compare a source pattern with a later echo, notice how a new setting or speaker changes meaning, and practice reading an unfamiliar passage by naming genre, situation, theme development, and evidence. The final work should integrate close reading, context restraint, genre reasoning, and concise claim/evidence/explanation.

## 4. Vocabulary, Representation, Model, Text, And Form Progression

Early vocabulary should review and sharpen: summary, inference, interpretation, claim, evidence, explanation, genre, speaker, narrator, character, author, context, motif, theme, and structure. Students should use short passage maps that identify who speaks, what happens, what changes, and which words matter.

Epic vocabulary and forms should include invocation, hero, quest, exile, homecoming, honor, hospitality, council, battlefield, journey, simile, epithet, repetition, and motif. Representations should include event chains, motif trackers, value-conflict charts, and evidence tables.

Dramatic vocabulary should include scene, dialogue, stage situation, conflict, motive, reversal, recognition, tragic flaw as a cautious term, dramatic irony, audience knowledge, comedy, exaggeration, inversion, social criticism, and folly. Representations should include who-knows-what charts, character motive maps, scene-turn trackers, and appearance/reality contrasts.

Dialogue, lyric, wisdom, and historical forms should add speaker, addressee, argument, reason, question, answer, aphorism, proverb, image, lament, praise, example, portrait, public virtue, and civic memory. Students should use claim/reason/evidence chains, image-to-idea maps, and speaker-position charts.

Transformation and transfer vocabulary should include allusion, source, echo, adaptation, transformation, translation choice, connotation, emphasis, parallel, contrast, and interpretive restraint. Students should compare paired passages with source/echo charts, changed-emphasis notes, and concise claim/evidence/explanation frames.

## 5. Fluency Expectations

Fluency in this level is literary fluency rather than speed. Students should become more automatic with orientation routines: identify genre and speaker, paraphrase difficult sentences, summarize action, notice repeated language, select relevant evidence, and explain how evidence supports a claim. They should practice short, frequent retrieval of key terms and genre signals so that vocabulary does not crowd out interpretation.

Students should also gain fluency with concise analytic response forms. By the end of the level, a student should be able to produce or select a strong claim/evidence/explanation chain, name a common misconception in an interpretation, and choose a more text-grounded reading when distractors overreach.

## 6. Conceptual Understanding Expectations

Students should understand that older texts often require rereading, background support, and patience, but that the words and structure of the passage remain the central evidence. They should understand genre as a set of expectations and pressures that shapes what a text can do, not merely as a label placed after reading.

Students should understand that literary meaning often emerges through relationship: a character's stated motive and actual action, a speaker's image and implied idea, an audience's knowledge and a character's ignorance, a source pattern and a later transformation, or a public value and its cost. They should know that more than one interpretation may be defensible, but not every interpretation is equally supported.

Students should also understand that context has a disciplined role. Ancient custom, civic life, biblical language, medieval worldview, Renaissance drama, and translation context can illuminate a passage, but background knowledge should not replace close reading or become a lecture detached from the text.

## 7. Application And Transfer Expectations

Application should appear in passage-based tasks where students orient, interpret, and justify. Students should classify a passage by genre, match textual evidence to claims, identify a speaker's motive, explain how a motif develops, choose the best interpretation of an allusion, and compare how an adaptation changes emphasis.

Transfer should be visible when students meet an unfamiliar classical-style passage or paired passage and still use the course routines: read for situation, distinguish summary from interpretation, choose a genre lens, attend to structure and diction, use context carefully, and support a concise claim. Transfer tasks should avoid open-ended opinion prompts as the main evidence; they should require text-grounded reasoning the app can score through constrained choices, matching, ordering, cloze, and short exact responses.

## 8. Review And Spacing Plan

Review should be cumulative from the first unit. Summary versus interpretation, claim/evidence/explanation, genre signals, speaker distinctions, context restraint, motif tracking, and allusion should reappear in small doses across later units. Each new genre should retrieve earlier routines before adding new demands.

Epic motifs should reappear during tragedy, comedy, dialogue, and transformation work so students see how honor, homecoming, justice, fate, wisdom, rule, friendship, suffering, and human limitation change across forms. Speaker/narrator/author distinctions should be reviewed before lyric, dialogue, and satire readiness. Irony should be revisited after tragedy when students meet comedy and adaptation, with feedback naming the gap between appearance and reality, expectation and outcome, or audience and character knowledge.

Writing and discussion routines should be spaced through short app-scorable approximations: choose the best claim, match evidence to claim, order reasoning steps, correct an overclaim, and complete concise explanation frames. Final transfer should combine earlier skills rather than introduce new terminology.

## 9. Mastery Evidence For The End Of The Level

End-of-level mastery should be evidenced by a balanced body of passage-based work. A student who has mastered Classical Literature 4 can read a substantial guided excerpt or unfamiliar classical-style passage, identify genre and speaker, summarize the literal situation, explain a theme or idea through specific textual features, and choose or produce a concise evidence-based explanation.

The student can recognize how epic, tragedy, comedy, dialogue, lyric, wisdom, history/biography, and transformation tasks differ; track complex character or speaker positions; interpret allusion, symbol, imagery, and dramatic irony in context; and compare a source with an adaptation or translation choice without overstating the evidence. Strong mastery includes intellectual humility: the student notices what the passage supports, what context helps clarify, and what remains uncertain.
