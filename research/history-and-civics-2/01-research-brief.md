# History And Civics 2 Research Brief

Track: History And Civics  
Level: Level 2  
Intended student profile: students with basic timeline, map, source, community, and civic vocabulary, ready for an ancient and classical world survey grounded in inquiry and sources.

This brief defines outcomes and instructional priorities only. It does not define units, lessons, or questions.

## 1. End-Of-Level Outcomes

By the end of History And Civics 2, students should be able to use historical inquiry questions; distinguish primary and secondary sources; use timelines and maps for ancient civilizations; describe major features of Mesopotamia, Egypt, Israel, India, China, Greece, Rome, and early Christianity in historical context; explain basic ancient government, law, religion, trade, writing, technology, and civic ideas; and support simple claims with evidence from maps, artifacts, images, timelines, and short texts.

## 2. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should know basic timeline/map/source vocabulary, community/government/rules/rights/responsibilities, needs/wants/goods/services, and claim/evidence sentence frames. They should be able to read short informational passages or interpret images with support.

## 3. Major Concept Clusters

- **Historical Inquiry:** questions, sources, evidence, claim, and conclusion.
- **Ancient Geography:** rivers, seas, mountains, deserts, trade routes, settlement, and environment.
- **Civilization Features:** cities, writing, law, religion, social roles, technology, trade, and government.
- **Classical Civic Ideas:** citizenship, law, republic, empire, democracy exposure, duty, and public life.
- **Cultural Contributions:** literature, architecture, mathematics, science, art, and enduring ideas.

## 4. Recommended Progression

Begin with inquiry and source routines, then geography tools for ancient-world orientation. Move through river-valley and ancient civilizations with repeated comparison categories. Place Greece and Rome after students have map/timeline and civilization-feature tools. End with classical civic ideas and enduring contributions, using evidence from sources rather than trivia recall alone.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Ancient means primitive.
- A civilization is just a place on a map.
- Primary sources are automatically true.
- Geography determines everything.
- Democracy in Athens was the same as modern democracy.
- Rome was only an empire, not also a republic at an earlier stage.
- Religion, government, and culture can be cleanly separated in ancient worlds.

## 6. Developmental Constraints And Reading/Load Concerns

Use maps and visuals heavily. Avoid too many names, dates, rulers, and battles. Frame slavery, conquest, empire, and religious difference accurately but age-appropriately. Keep source excerpts short. Teach vocabulary before asking students to compare civilizations.

## 7. Assessment Implications

Use map questions, timeline ordering, source/image interpretation, civilization-feature matching, and short evidence claims. Distractors should reveal errors about sequence, location, source type, civic terms, and overgeneralized comparisons. Feedback should point to map evidence, timeline position, source detail, or concept definition.

## 8. Spiral Review Priorities

Timeline, map key, region, river, source, evidence, claim, civilization, law, citizen, monarchy, republic, democracy, empire, trade, contribution, cause/effect, change/continuity, compare/contrast.

## 9. Concepts That Should Not Be Taught Yet

Avoid dense dynasty lists, exhaustive wars, advanced theology, original-language source analysis, historiography, modern ideological judgment as the main frame, and document-based essays.
