# French 1 Course Map

Track: French  
Level: Level 1  
Sources: Accepted research brief at `research/french-1/01-research-brief.md` and level blueprint at `research/french-1/02-level-blueprint.md`

## Course Design Notes

French 1 is ordered from immediate classroom communication into personal identity, family and description, preferences, school and places, food and choices, and finally cumulative novice communication. The map avoids treating Level 1 as a grammar survey. Grammar, pronunciation, and culture appear when they help students understand or say something useful. Each unit includes interpretive exposure, supported interpersonal response, and modest presentational production, with spaced retrieval of earlier language.

## Unit 1: First French For Real Interaction

### Unit Purpose

Give students their first usable French: greetings, farewells, politeness, classroom survival phrases, and pronunciation habits. Students should quickly understand that French is for interaction and that listening comes before confident production.

### Student Outcomes

- Greet and say goodbye in familiar formal and informal situations.
- Use and understand merci, s'il vous plaît, pardon, excusez-moi, and basic classroom phrases.
- Ask and answer name and well-being questions with rehearsed language.
- Recognize early sound-spelling patterns, including final silent consonants, accents, and ç.
- Follow short classroom directions and use repair phrases when meaning is unclear.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Bonjour, salut, au revoir, à bientôt, merci, s'il vous plaît, pardon, excusez-moi, comment ça va, ça va bien/mal/comme ci comme ça, je m'appelle, écoutez, répétez, regardez, écrivez, je ne comprends pas, final silent consonants, accents, ç, liaison exposure, tu/vous as politeness awareness.

### Prerequisites

Students need no prior French. They should be able to follow short English directions, repeat unfamiliar sounds with support, and tolerate partial understanding.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit establishes greeting, repair, listening, and pronunciation routines that recur in every later unit. Name and well-being questions prepare for personal identity questions in Unit 3. Tu/vous awareness returns in classroom, school, and food exchanges.

### Misconceptions To Address

- French is English with swapped words.
- All final letters are pronounced.
- Accents and ç are decoration.
- Tu and vous mean the same thing in every situation.
- A learner should understand every word before responding.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Greet-and-respond routines should open later units. Classroom phrases and repair language should appear in prompts and feedback throughout the level. Pronunciation patterns introduced here should be revisited in short listening and speaking tasks, not isolated drills.

## Unit 2: Numbers, Calendar, Weather, And Colors

### Unit Purpose

Build high-reuse language that supports daily classroom routines, age, dates, simple descriptions, schedules, and weather talk. Students practice listening carefully because numbers, days, months, and weather phrases are easy to confuse when first heard.

### Student Outcomes

- Recognize, say, and type numbers 0-31 with support, and recognize tens to 100 for later use.
- Ask and answer age with j'ai ___ ans.
- Identify days, months, dates, and birthdays in simple phrases.
- Understand and produce common weather expressions.
- Use color adjectives in simple noun phrases while noticing agreement as exposure.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Numbers 0-100 with emphasis on 0-31; Quel âge as-tu? J'ai ___ ans; days; months; aujourd'hui, demain, hier; C'est lundi; Quelle est la date? Mon anniversaire est...; il fait beau/froid/chaud/mauvais; il pleut; il neige; colors; noun-color phrases such as le cahier bleu and la trousse rouge.

### Prerequisites

Students should know Unit 1 greetings, listening routines, and name/well-being exchanges. English concepts of number, date, calendar, and weather are assumed.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

Numbers support age, prices, classroom quantities, times, and food ordering. Calendar and weather language become a daily review source. Colors prepare students to notice adjective position and agreement before the family and description unit.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Translating number words by sound instead of listening for the exact word.
- Using être for age because English says "I am twelve."
- Treating weather phrases as word-for-word English.
- Assuming French adjectives always come before nouns.
- Ignoring agreement or article clues in noun-color phrases.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Numbers and calendar language should be spaced daily in later contexts: age, birthdays, schedules, prices, quantities, and weather cards. Colors should return with people, school objects, foods, and places as light agreement review.

## Unit 3: Introducing Myself And Asking Questions

### Unit Purpose

Help students use practiced language to give basic personal information and ask simple questions. The unit introduces the most important novice interpersonal pattern: question word, familiar verb chunk, short answer.

### Student Outcomes

- Introduce self with name, age, origin, residence, and student identity.
- Ask and answer memorized questions using comment, quel/quelle, où, d'où, qui, and est-ce que exposure.
- Use je suis, j'ai, j'habite, je viens de, and tu/vous question forms in context.
- Choose tu or vous when the relationship is clear.
- Interpret short self-profile texts with familiar language and cognates.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Je m'appelle; Comment tu t'appelles? Comment vous appelez-vous? J'ai ___ ans; Je suis élève; Je suis américain/américaine; J'habite à...; Je viens de...; Comment, quel/quelle, où, d'où, qui; oui, non; et, mais; tu/vous; simple francophone place names.

### Prerequisites

Students need greetings, politeness formulas, numbers for age, basic pronunciation routines, and comfort answering with rehearsed phrases.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit turns the opening name and age language into a broader identity toolkit. Question words recur in every later unit, while être and avoir chunks prepare for family descriptions, school, and routines.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Answering every question with a memorized self-introduction instead of listening to the question word.
- Using j'ai for identity or je suis for age.
- Treating tu and vous as random variants.
- Assuming cognates always have exactly the English meaning.
- Overproducing long sentences before short ones are accurate.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Start later units with one personal question from this unit. Interleave question words, age, origin, residence, and politeness forms in dialogue-builder and short profile tasks.

## Unit 4: Family, People, And Descriptions

### Unit Purpose

Expand personal communication from "who I am" to "who is in my world." Students learn family vocabulary, articles, il y a, basic possession chunks, and simple adjective agreement through people descriptions.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify immediate family members and familiar people with articles.
- Use il y a to say what or who is present.
- Use mon, ma, and mes as controlled possession chunks.
- Describe people with a small set of adjectives, noticing masculine/feminine and singular/plural agreement.
- Interpret short family or friend descriptions and produce two or three simple sentences.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

La famille, les parents, la mère, le père, le frère, la soeur, le grand-père, la grand-mère, l'ami, l'amie; un, une, le, la, l', les; mon, ma, mes; il y a; grand/grande, petit/petite, sympa, drôle, intelligent/intelligente, brun/brune, blond/blonde; très, aussi.

### Prerequisites

Students need personal identity language, être and avoir chunks, numbers for age, question words, and basic color/adjective exposure.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit deepens article and adjective awareness that began with colors. It prepares students for school objects, foods, places, and capstone descriptions where gender, number, and il y a return.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Thinking grammatical gender means biological gender.
- Dropping articles because English sometimes does.
- Using one adjective form for every noun.
- Treating il y a as a literal word-for-word phrase.
- Confusing mon/ma with the owner's gender instead of the noun that follows.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Family and adjective language should recur in self-profile, school, and capstone tasks. Article and agreement checks should be brief but frequent, with feedback naming the noun clue.

## Unit 5: Likes, Activities, And Opinions

### Unit Purpose

Give students a flexible way to talk about preferences, activities, and simple reasons. This unit makes negation and infinitive activity chunks useful rather than abstract.

### Student Outcomes

- State likes and dislikes with j'aime, j'adore, je n'aime pas, and je déteste.
- Understand and use common activity chunks such as jouer, écouter, regarder, lire, chanter, danser, étudier, and parler français.
- Ask and answer Qu'est-ce que tu aimes? and Est-ce que tu aimes...?
- Use ne...pas as a negation frame around the verb in controlled contexts.
- Add simple connectors and reasons with et, mais, parce que, super, intéressant, difficile, and amusant.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

J'aime, j'adore, je n'aime pas, je déteste; jouer au foot, jouer aux jeux vidéo, écouter de la musique, regarder un film, lire, chanter, danser, étudier, parler français; Qu'est-ce que tu aimes? Est-ce que tu aimes...? Pourquoi? parce que; et, mais; ne...pas.

### Prerequisites

Students need identity questions, être/avoir chunks, basic sentence frames, question-word listening, and willingness to rehearse aloud before production.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

Preferences later support school subjects, foods, places, and routines. Negation and connectors return in school and capstone tasks. Activity chunks provide exposure to -er verbs without requiring a full conjugation chart.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Translating "I like to" word by word.
- Omitting ne or pas from the negation frame.
- Treating infinitive activity chunks as fully conjugated verbs.
- Giving a preference answer that does not match the question.
- Thinking parce que must introduce a long explanation.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Spiral preference language into every remaining unit. Use delayed retrieval of j'aime/je n'aime pas with school subjects, foods, weather, and routines.

## Unit 6: School, Objects, And Everyday Places

### Unit Purpose

Move novice communication into school and place contexts students can imagine and use. Students practice classroom objects, school subjects, location phrases, il y a review, and aller/fair chunks for places and school routines.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify common classroom objects and school subjects with articles.
- Describe what is in a classroom or bag using il y a.
- Say where something or someone is using basic prepositions and place phrases.
- Use je vais à... and je fais... chunks in familiar school and place contexts.
- Build short school or town mini-dialogues that combine earlier question words and politeness.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Le stylo, le crayon, le cahier, le livre, le sac, la trousse, la classe, l'école, les devoirs; le français, les maths, l'histoire, les sciences, l'art, la musique; dans, sur, sous, devant, derrière, à côté de; la bibliothèque, le café, le parc, le cinéma, le musée; je vais à..., je fais mes devoirs, je fais du sport.

### Prerequisites

Students need articles, adjective and color exposure, il y a, personal questions, preference chunks, and classroom language.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit reuses articles, colors, numbers, likes, and il y a in school settings. Places and aller prepare students for food and choice exchanges, routines, and the final capstone.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Ignoring le/la/les with school objects.
- Treating à as a direct English equivalent in every phrase.
- Confusing il y a with "he has."
- Overgeneralizing je vais without a destination phrase.
- Reading a sentence about location without checking the preposition.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Interleave school objects with colors and articles. Use short mixed prompts where students decide whether the sentence is about existence, location, liking, or going.

## Unit 7: Food, Choices, Routines, And Novice Capstone

### Unit Purpose

Bring French 1 language together in familiar choice, food, place, and routine contexts. Students use vouloir and polite ordering chunks, describe simple daily routines with faire/aller/aimer, notice francophone cultural contexts, and complete mixed novice communication tasks.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify common foods and drinks with articles and polite choice phrases.
- Use je voudrais and je veux in appropriate controlled contexts, with politeness emphasis.
- Interpret short food, place, or routine exchanges.
- Describe a simple daily routine with a few familiar chunks.
- Complete a cumulative novice task combining interpretive reading/listening, interpersonal reply, and short presentational output.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Le pain, le fromage, le croissant, le sandwich, la pomme, la salade, l'eau, le jus, le chocolat chaud; je voudrais, je veux, s'il vous plaît, c'est combien?; le matin, l'après-midi, le soir; je vais, je fais, j'aime, je mange, je bois as formulaic exposure; francophone places and everyday practices; respectful comparison language.

### Prerequisites

Students need greetings and politeness, numbers, articles, likes/dislikes, il y a, school/place vocabulary, aller/fair chunks, and question-word routines.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This final unit consolidates the level. It prepares French 2 by making students comfortable recombining familiar chunks, noticing routine language, and interpreting short contextualized texts without expecting full tense or verb-system mastery.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Using je veux in every request without politeness.
- Translating food articles and partitives as a full system before readiness.
- Assuming culture means memorizing trivia about one country.
- Producing disconnected word lists instead of short communicative sentences.
- Forgetting earlier question words, articles, or negation when contexts are mixed.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use cumulative review intentionally: greetings, numbers, articles, likes, il y a, question words, places, and politeness should all reappear. The capstone should be mixed but novice-sized, with short texts and supported output.
