# French 1 Lesson Briefs

Sources: accepted level blueprint, course map, and unit design briefs in `research/french-1/`.

## Unit 1: First French For Real Interaction

### Lesson 1: Bonjour, Salut, And Goodbye

- **Teaching goal:** Students learn common greetings and farewells as choices that fit social situations.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose and use bonjour, salut, au revoir, and à bientôt in short familiar contexts.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Greeting cards and two-turn exchanges show that register and moment matter.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** No French required; students need to distinguish formal and informal situations in English.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating bonjour and salut as identical; using goodbye phrases as greetings; assuming one phrase fits every person.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a short situation, the student chooses a fitting greeting or farewell and can build a two-line exchange.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for greeting choice; `match-pairs` for phrase meanings; `dialogue-builder` for next-line fit; `speaking-prompt` for low-stakes oral rehearsal.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Present two pictures: greeting a teacher and greeting a friend. Model why bonjour is safer with an adult and salut fits a friend.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep contexts one sentence long; do not introduce full formal grammar yet; use audio or pronunciation notes when possible.

### Lesson 2: Polite Words And Classroom Survival

- **Teaching goal:** Students learn politeness, classroom directions, and repair phrases that help them participate in later lessons.
- **Student outcome:** The student can match and use merci, s'il vous plaît, pardon, excusez-moi, écoutez, répétez, regardez, écrivez, and je ne comprends pas.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Classroom action cards connect French phrases to actions or social purposes.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic greetings from Lesson 1 and willingness to respond to short commands.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Seeing polite language as optional filler; confusing pardon and merci; treating classroom commands as vocabulary labels rather than actions.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can choose a phrase for a classroom need and identify an action after hearing or reading a command.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for phrase-to-meaning; `listening-question` for command recognition when audio exists; `multiple-choice` for social use; `speaking-prompt` for repair phrase rehearsal.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Model a learner moment: hearing a direction, not understanding, and using je ne comprends pas or répétez, s'il vous plaît.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep commands as imperatives only in fixed phrases; avoid explaining imperative conjugation.

### Lesson 3: Comment Ça Va? First Mini-Dialogue

- **Teaching goal:** Students build a short greeting and well-being exchange using memorized but meaningful lines.
- **Student outcome:** The student can understand, order, and produce a simple exchange with comment ça va and ça va bien/mal/comme ci comme ça.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A four-line mini-dialogue shows greeting, question, answer, and reciprocal prompt.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Greetings, farewells, merci, and basic listening habits.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Answering comment ça va with a name; omitting the return question et toi?; using a farewell in the middle of an exchange.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can choose the logical next line and complete a short dialogue in order.
- **Recommended question types:** `dialogue-builder` for pragmatic fit; `order-items` for conversation sequence; `fill-blank` for fixed phrase completion; `speaking-prompt` for oral fluency.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Act out the exchange with one happy, one neutral, and one unhappy response so students connect language to meaning.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Limit to four or five lines; do not require spelling of ça before students have seen it several times.

### Lesson 4: French Sounds You Notice First

- **Teaching goal:** Students notice several high-frequency French sound-spelling patterns in words they already know.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify that some final consonants are silent, accents affect spelling and sound, ç sounds like /s/, and liaison can link words.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Familiar phrase cards pair written French with sound clues, such as salut, français, ça va, and vous avez.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Unit 1 phrases and ability to compare spoken and written forms.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Pronouncing every final letter; ignoring accents; reading ç like English c in cat; assuming liaison means every final letter is always pronounced.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can choose the word or phrase that matches a sound clue and explain the clue in simple language.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for sound-pattern recognition; `listening-question` for heard-word discrimination; `error-correction` for adding ç or accent marks in familiar words; `speaking-prompt` for rehearsing phrases.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use known phrases only. Ask, "What do you see? What do you hear? What letter is quiet or changed?"
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep explanation language nontechnical; avoid IPA and broad phonology rules.

## Unit 2: Numbers, Calendar, Weather, And Colors

### Lesson 1: Numbers For Age And Classroom Counts

- **Teaching goal:** Students recognize and use numbers for age and small classroom quantities.
- **Student outcome:** The student can match number words to numerals, answer Quel âge as-tu? with j'ai ___ ans, and understand controlled counts.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Number cards and age profile cards connect numeral, spoken/written word, and phrase use.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Greetings and basic question-response routines.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Using je suis for age; confusing close-sounding numbers; typing a numeral when the prompt asks for a French word or vice versa.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can identify numbers 0-31 and use j'ai ___ ans in a short age answer.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for number-word matching; `text-input` for controlled numeric answers; `fill-blank` for j'ai ___ ans; `listening-question` for number discrimination.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Teach age as a whole phrase first: In French, you "have" years. Use j'ai douze ans, not je suis douze.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Emphasize 0-31; include tens to 100 as recognition only when useful; avoid long number strings.

### Lesson 2: Dates, Days, And Birthdays

- **Teaching goal:** Students use days, months, dates, and birthdays in short routine statements.
- **Student outcome:** The student can interpret and complete phrases such as aujourd'hui c'est lundi, la date est le 5 mai, and mon anniversaire est en juin.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Calendar cards show day, date, month, and birthday phrases.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Numbers 1-31, greetings, and basic classroom routine phrases.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Translating English date order directly; using a month without en when the phrase requires it; confusing day and month words.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can match dates to written phrases and answer a birthday/date prompt with supported language.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for days/months; `fill-blank` for date phrases; `order-items` for phrase order; `passage-question` for a small calendar note.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Start from today's date card and point to each part: day, number, month.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep dates simple; avoid teaching ordinal systems beyond le premier as exposure if needed.

### Lesson 3: Weather Windows

- **Teaching goal:** Students understand and produce common weather expressions as fixed novice phrases.
- **Student outcome:** The student can match weather expressions to meanings and complete short weather statements.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Weather-window cards pair an icon, a phrase, and a short response such as il fait froid.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Classroom phrase routines, basic calendar language, and exposure to il as a word in fixed phrases.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Translating "it is cold" as il est froid for weather; confusing il pleut and il neige; treating fait as a stand-alone weather word.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a weather icon or short forecast, the student chooses or completes the correct French phrase.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for icon-to-phrase; `fill-blank` for il fait phrases; `listening-question` for weather recognition; `constructed-response` for a one-sentence weather report.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Present weather as chunks: il fait beau, il fait froid, il pleut. Do not unpack full verb grammar.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one weather phrase per item at first; keep production to a single sentence.

### Lesson 4: Colors With Nouns

- **Teaching goal:** Students use color words with familiar nouns while noticing French adjective position and simple agreement.
- **Student outcome:** The student can understand and build phrases such as le cahier bleu, la trousse rouge, and les stylos verts.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Object-color labels show that many French color adjectives follow the noun and may change form.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic classroom objects as exposure, articles le/la/les as early clues, and color meanings.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Placing every color before the noun; ignoring the article; using one unchanged color spelling for plural nouns.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can match color-noun phrases, order words correctly, and select an agreement form in highly controlled cases.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for color meaning; `order-items` for noun-color order; `fill-blank` for controlled adjective forms; `error-correction` for English word order.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show "blue notebook" and le cahier bleu side by side. Ask what moved compared with English.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use only common regular colors and familiar objects; avoid exceptions and full adjective agreement rules.

## Unit 3: Introducing Myself And Asking Questions

### Lesson 1: My Name, Age, And Student Identity

- **Teaching goal:** Students combine name, age, and student identity into a short profile.
- **Student outcome:** The student can say or write je m'appelle, j'ai ___ ans, and je suis élève in a supported self-introduction.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Profile cards model a three-sentence novice introduction.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Greetings, numbers for age, and je m'appelle from Unit 1.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Using je suis for age; leaving out ans; using only a name when the prompt asks for identity too.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can complete a profile and choose the correct phrase for name, age, or identity.
- **Recommended question types:** `multi-blank-cloze` for profile completion; `multiple-choice` for j'ai vs je suis; `constructed-response` for a short self-profile; `speaking-prompt` for oral rehearsal.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Model a tiny profile and mark what each sentence tells: name, age, role.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep profile output to three sentences; provide sentence frames for production.

### Lesson 2: Where I Am From And Where I Live

- **Teaching goal:** Students distinguish origin and residence phrases in simple personal information.
- **Student outcome:** The student can understand and use je viens de... and j'habite à... with familiar place names.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Map cards show origin and current residence as related but different ideas.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Self-introduction phrases, question words as exposure, and place names or map-reading basics.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating je viens de and j'habite à as identical; translating place prepositions word by word; answering d'où with a current location when the prompt asks origin.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can match a question to an origin or residence answer and complete a simple profile.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for question-to-answer function; `fill-blank` for phrase completion; `passage-question` for a short profile; `text-input` for controlled place phrase production.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use one fictional student who is from Montréal but lives in Dallas. Keep the contrast visible.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar city/country names and do not teach country-gender preposition rules.

### Lesson 3: Question Words That Shape Answers

- **Teaching goal:** Students use question words to predict the kind of answer needed.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify whether comment, qui, où, d'où, quel/quelle, or pourquoi calls for name, person, place, origin, age/date, or reason.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A question-word chart links each word to an answer type and example.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic self-introduction, age, residence/origin phrases, and classroom response routines.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Answering by picking a familiar phrase without reading the question word; confusing où and d'où; treating pourquoi as a yes/no question.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a question, the student chooses an answer that matches the question word and can explain the clue.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for question word to answer type; `dialogue-builder` for fitting replies; `multiple-choice` for answer choice; `constructed-response` for a brief explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Model a "listen for the first clue" routine: question word first, then familiar words.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep questions short and familiar; avoid multiple unfamiliar words in the same prompt.

### Lesson 4: Tu Or Vous In Simple Exchanges

- **Teaching goal:** Students choose tu or vous forms based on relationship and politeness in controlled exchanges.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify when an informal or polite address fits and choose a matching question line.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Situation cards compare talking to a friend, a teacher, one adult, or a group.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Greetings, politeness phrases, personal questions, and basic classroom social roles.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating tu and vous as spelling variants; using tu with every person because it is shorter; thinking vous is only plural.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can choose the appropriate question or reply for a clear situation and explain "friend" or "polite/group" as the clue.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for tu/vous choice; `dialogue-builder` for next line; `match-pairs` for situation-to-form; `error-correction` for mismatched address in simple prompts.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Do not overteach conjugation. Keep the focus on social meaning and a few memorized question forms.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use clear relationship labels; avoid ambiguous contexts where either form could be acceptable.

## Unit 4: Family, People, And Descriptions

### Lesson 1: Family Words Need Articles

- **Teaching goal:** Students learn family nouns with articles as part of the word, not optional extras.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify close family words and choose le, la, l', un, une, or les in controlled contexts.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Family-tree labels pair each person noun with a common article.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Person identity phrases, age numbers, and awareness that French nouns often need articles.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Dropping articles; assuming gender is always about the real person's gender rather than a noun pattern; confusing soeur and frère.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can match family words to meanings and complete short phrases with a fitting article.
- **Recommended question types:** `flash-card` for family-word retrieval; `match-pairs` for noun meaning; `fill-blank` for articles; `passage-question` for a simple family tree note.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Teach words as chunks: le frère, la soeur, les parents. Say "the article travels with the noun."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Limit to immediate family and grandparents; keep accents visible but do not overtest spelling at first.

### Lesson 2: Il Y A In My Family And World

- **Teaching goal:** Students use il y a to say that someone or something exists in a family, room, or picture.
- **Student outcome:** The student can interpret and build sentences such as Il y a deux frères and Il y a une trousse.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Picture-inventory cards show "there is/there are" as a useful description frame.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Family words, articles, numbers, classroom objects as exposure, and basic sentence frames.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Translating il y a as "he has"; using a singular/plural English distinction that French il y a does not show; omitting the article after il y a.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a picture or inventory, the student completes an il y a sentence with number/article/noun support.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for il y a phrases; `multi-blank-cloze` for inventory sentences; `multiple-choice` for meaning; `constructed-response` for one descriptive sentence.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare "J'ai un frère" with "Il y a un frère dans la photo" only as meaning, not as a full grammar contrast.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use small quantities and familiar nouns; avoid abstract explanations of il.

### Lesson 3: Describing People With Matching Adjectives

- **Teaching goal:** Students describe people with simple adjectives while noticing adjective placement and agreement.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose or complete short descriptions such as ma soeur est grande and mon frère est brun.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** People cards display noun, article/possession, adjective form, and meaning.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Family nouns, mon/ma exposure, colors with nouns, and être chunks.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Using one adjective form for every noun; putting every adjective before the noun; matching adjective to the speaker instead of the described person/noun.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can match a person description to a picture and select a controlled adjective form.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for adjective choice; `fill-blank` for endings; `error-correction` for mismatched forms; `constructed-response` for a two-sentence description.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use pairs like grand/grande and brun/brune to show the written clue without turning it into a full adjective chart.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use a small adjective set; avoid irregular placement exceptions beyond common modeled phrases.

### Lesson 4: My, Your? Mon, Ma, Mes In Context

- **Teaching goal:** Students use possession chunks with familiar family and friend nouns.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose mon, ma, or mes based on the noun that follows and understand ton/ta/tes as exposure if included.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Possession cards show that the possessive form points to the noun, not the owner.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Family nouns with articles, singular/plural awareness, and simple descriptions.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Choosing mon/ma based on the speaker's gender; using mes with one person; omitting the noun after the possessive.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can complete short phrases and read a short family description with possession chunks.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for mon/ma/mes; `match-pairs` for possessive phrase meaning; `error-correction` for wrong possessive; `passage-question` for a family note.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Place le frère -> mon frère, la soeur -> ma soeur, les parents -> mes parents side by side.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep focus on mon/ma/mes; introduce your forms only in recognition if needed.

## Unit 5: Likes, Activities, And Opinions

### Lesson 1: J'aime And J'adore Activity Chunks

- **Teaching goal:** Students state positive preferences using j'aime and j'adore with common activity chunks.
- **Student outcome:** The student can understand and produce short positive preference statements about familiar activities.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Activity cards pair a phrase with an image and a preference scale.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Identity phrases, question-response routines, and basic pronunciation practice.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Translating "I like to" word by word; changing the activity verb after j'aime; confusing activity nouns with action chunks.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can match activity chunks, complete j'aime/j'adore sentences, and produce one short preference statement.
- **Recommended question types:** `flash-card` for activity vocabulary; `match-pairs` for activity meanings; `multi-blank-cloze` for preference statements; `speaking-prompt` for oral preference practice.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Treat j'aime jouer as a chunk meaning "I like to play." Avoid a formal infinitive lecture.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use 6-8 activities; avoid requiring long reasons in this first preference lesson.

### Lesson 2: Je N'aime Pas: Negation As A Frame

- **Teaching goal:** Students learn ne...pas as a frame around the verb in controlled preference statements.
- **Student outcome:** The student can understand, complete, and correct sentences such as je n'aime pas danser.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A visual frame shows ne / n' before aime and pas after aime.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Positive preference chunks and activity vocabulary.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Using pas alone in controlled writing; placing pas after the activity; forgetting n' before a vowel sound.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can complete a negative preference sentence and distinguish positive from negative meaning.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for ne/pas placement; `error-correction` for missing frame pieces; `multiple-choice` for meaning; `order-items` for sentence order.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show je n'aime pas as one frame before swapping activities in and out.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep all negative examples with aimer; do not generalize across many verbs yet.

### Lesson 3: Asking About Preferences

- **Teaching goal:** Students ask and answer simple questions about likes and dislikes.
- **Student outcome:** The student can respond appropriately to Qu'est-ce que tu aimes? and Est-ce que tu aimes...?
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Preference mini-dialogues contrast open "what do you like?" questions with yes/no est-ce que questions.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** J'aime, j'adore, je n'aime pas, activity chunks, tu as informal address exposure.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Answering a yes/no question with an unrelated favorite; treating qu'est-ce que and est-ce que as the same; ignoring the activity named in the question.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can choose a fitting reply and build a short preference exchange.
- **Recommended question types:** `dialogue-builder` for reply fit; `multiple-choice` for question type; `order-items` for dialogue order; `constructed-response` for one question-answer pair.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Put two questions side by side and ask what kind of answer each one expects.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep questions to familiar activities; avoid multiple preference clauses in one prompt.

### Lesson 4: Simple Reasons With Parce Que

- **Teaching goal:** Students add short reasons and connectors to preference statements.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use parce que, et, and mais to make a simple preference statement with a reason.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Sentence frames show preference + connector + short adjective reason.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Positive and negative preference statements, basic adjectives such as amusant, intéressant, difficile, and super.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Writing a long English-style explanation; using parce que without a reason; choosing an adjective that contradicts the preference.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can choose a logical reason for a preference and produce one supported sentence.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for logical reason; `multi-blank-cloze` for sentence frames; `passage-question` for a short preference note; `constructed-response` for a brief opinion.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Model compact reasons: J'aime la musique parce que c'est amusant. Je n'aime pas les maths parce que c'est difficile.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep reasons one adjective or very short phrase; do not require subordinate-clause mastery.

## Unit 6: School, Objects, And Everyday Places

### Lesson 1: Classroom Objects And School Subjects

- **Teaching goal:** Students identify school objects and subjects with articles and use preference language in a school context.
- **Student outcome:** The student can match common school nouns to meanings and say whether they like a subject.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Backpack and schedule cards show nouns with articles and school-subject phrases.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Articles, colors, j'aime/je n'aime pas, numbers and classroom directions.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Dropping articles; confusing object nouns with subject nouns; using the wrong article because English has one "the."
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can identify a school noun with its article and complete a simple school preference statement.
- **Recommended question types:** `flash-card` for school nouns; `match-pairs` for meaning; `fill-blank` for articles; `constructed-response` for one school preference.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Sort words into "things in a bag" and "classes on a schedule" before asking for preferences.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use a small set of high-frequency school words; avoid detailed schedule reading.

### Lesson 2: Il Y A And Where Things Are

- **Teaching goal:** Students distinguish existence with il y a from location with est plus a preposition.
- **Student outcome:** The student can describe what is in a classroom or bag and where a familiar object is located.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Classroom-map cards contrast Il y a un livre and Le livre est sur la table.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Il y a from Unit 4, articles, school objects, and simple preposition meanings.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Using il y a for every location; ignoring the preposition; treating sur, sous, and dans as interchangeable.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can choose whether a prompt asks what exists or where it is and complete a location phrase.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for existence vs location; `match-pairs` for preposition meanings; `fill-blank` for location sentence; `error-correction` for wrong preposition.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Ask two different questions: "What is there?" and "Where is it?" Keep the answers visibly different.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar objects and simple locations; avoid complex room descriptions.

### Lesson 3: Going Places With Je Vais

- **Teaching goal:** Students use je vais à... with familiar school and town destinations.
- **Student outcome:** The student can understand and produce controlled sentences about going to school, the library, the park, the café, or the cinema.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Place cards and route prompts connect a destination to je vais à...
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Place nouns as exposure, school context, question words, and basic politeness.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating je vais as complete without a destination; overgeneralizing à forms; confusing je vais and j'aime when both use familiar nouns.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can match a destination to a sentence and answer Où vas-tu? with a supported place phrase.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for place meaning; `fill-blank` for je vais à...; `dialogue-builder` for destination questions; `constructed-response` for one route sentence.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Keep aller as a chunk. Use "je vais à + place" and do not teach the full paradigm.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid full contractions and country preposition rules; use fixed modeled place phrases.

### Lesson 4: School Mini-Dialogues

- **Teaching goal:** Students combine school vocabulary, preferences, places, and polite phrases in short exchanges.
- **Student outcome:** The student can interpret and build a school mini-dialogue about an object, subject, place, or activity.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Three- to five-line dialogues show greeting, question, answer, and polite close.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** School nouns, preferences, il y a/location, je vais, greetings, tu/vous awareness.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Choosing a reply that matches one word but not the question; forgetting politeness in a request; mixing liking and going forms.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can choose the best next line and complete a short school exchange.
- **Recommended question types:** `dialogue-builder` for pragmatic fit; `order-items` for dialogue sequence; `passage-question` for a school note; `constructed-response` for a two-line exchange.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Have students underline the question word or target phrase before choosing the answer.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep dialogues short; use familiar words and one communication purpose per exchange.

## Unit 7: Food, Choices, Routines, And Novice Capstone

### Lesson 1: Foods, Drinks, And Polite Requests

- **Teaching goal:** Students identify common foods and drinks and request them politely.
- **Student outcome:** The student can understand food words with articles and use je voudrais ___, s'il vous plaît in a controlled request.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Café menu cards connect food nouns, articles, and request phrases.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Articles, politeness, numbers as exposure, likes, and basic dialogue-building.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Dropping articles with food nouns; using je veux in every service situation; translating "I would like" word by word.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can match foods to meanings and build a polite one-line request.
- **Recommended question types:** `flash-card` for food vocabulary; `match-pairs` for food meanings; `dialogue-builder` for ordering; `speaking-prompt` for polite request rehearsal.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Model a café exchange and contrast a polite request with a too-direct one.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use common foods and drinks only; do not teach partitive articles as a system.

### Lesson 2: Je Voudrais, Prices, And Choices

- **Teaching goal:** Students combine wanting, numbers, prices, and choices in short café or shop exchanges.
- **Student outcome:** The student can ask or answer c'est combien? and choose a food or drink with a price in a simple exchange.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Mini-menu cards show item, price, and polite request language.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Food vocabulary, je voudrais, numbers, politeness, and question words.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Ignoring the price number heard or read; using c'est combien? as an answer; treating je voudrais and je veux as the same in polite contexts.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can choose the correct price or request line and complete a short ordering dialogue.
- **Recommended question types:** `listening-question` for price recognition if audio exists; `dialogue-builder` for service exchange; `multiple-choice` for pragmatic choice; `text-input` for numeric price answers.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Keep price phrases short: C'est trois euros. Focus on recognizing the number and polite exchange flow.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use small prices and familiar items; avoid decimal comma and complex currency conventions.

### Lesson 3: Simple Routines With Familiar Chunks

- **Teaching goal:** Students describe a simple daily routine using known present-tense chunks and time-of-day phrases.
- **Student outcome:** The student can order and produce short routine statements with le matin, l'après-midi, le soir, je vais, je fais, j'aime, je mange, and je bois as formulaic exposure.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Routine strips organize time phrase + familiar action chunk + object/place.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** School places, food, likes, je vais, je fais, and basic connectors.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating the routine as a past-tense story; changing every verb form without a model; writing disconnected word lists instead of short statements.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can order a three-step routine and write or say two supported routine sentences.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for routine sequence; `multi-blank-cloze` for routine sentences; `passage-question` for a short routine note; `constructed-response` or `speaking-prompt` for supported production.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Present routines as "today/general habit" chunks, not a full tense lesson.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Limit to three routine moments; keep output to two or three sentences.

### Lesson 4: French 1 Capstone Exchange

- **Teaching goal:** Students recombine familiar French 1 language in a compact mixed novice task.
- **Student outcome:** The student can interpret a short familiar text, choose appropriate dialogue replies, and produce a brief self or everyday profile.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A capstone packet includes a profile, mini-dialogue, menu/place prompt, and supported presentational response.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** All prior units: greetings, numbers, dates/weather, identity, family, descriptions, likes, school, places, food, routines, question words, articles, and politeness.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Translating word by word instead of using known chunks; ignoring question words; forgetting earlier article or negation patterns when topics are mixed; trying to write beyond novice control.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student completes a mixed set with accurate interpretation, fitting replies, and a comprehensible short output using several familiar chunks.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for interpretive reading; `dialogue-builder` for interpersonal replies; `multi-blank-cloze` for cumulative form use; `constructed-response` and `speaking-prompt` for presentational output.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Remind students to hunt for familiar clues: greeting, question word, article, number, verb chunk, and context.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep the capstone mixed but short; score production for comprehensibility and required chunks, not extended grammar.
