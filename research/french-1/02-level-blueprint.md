# French 1 Level Blueprint

Track: French  
Level: Level 1  
Source: Accepted research brief at `research/french-1/01-research-brief.md`

## 1. Level Mission

French 1 helps beginning learners become confident novice communicators who can understand, choose, and produce short French expressions in highly familiar situations. Students build a small but reusable language toolkit: greetings, classroom survival phrases, numbers, calendar and weather language, personal information, family, descriptions, likes and dislikes, school language, foods, places, and simple routines. The level treats pronunciation, sound-spelling, culture, and grammar as supports for meaning rather than as isolated surveys. By the end of the level, students should be able to interpret short familiar spoken and written French, participate in brief rehearsed exchanges, and create simple presentational statements about themselves and everyday contexts while using feedback, cognates, visuals, and spaced retrieval to keep improving.

## 2. Durable Student Outcomes

By the end of French 1, students should be able to:

1. Understand and use common greetings, farewells, politeness formulas, classroom directions, and survival expressions.
2. Recognize and pronounce high-frequency French sound-spelling patterns, including final silent consonants, accents, ç, common vowel combinations, nasal vowels, and liaison exposure.
3. Ask and answer memorized or recombined questions about name, age, origin, residence, grade, family, likes, school, food, places, and simple routines.
4. Interpret short spoken and written French texts made of familiar words, cognates, predictable sentence patterns, and visual context.
5. Produce brief presentational statements about self, family, school, weather, interests, food preferences, places, and daily routines.
6. Use high-frequency vocabulary with articles, singular/plural forms, basic adjective agreement, common prepositions, question words, connectors, and negation frames.
7. Use a limited set of present-tense verbs and formulas in context, including être, avoir, il y a, aimer/adorer/détester, aller, faire, vouloir, and common -er verb chunks.
8. Notice everyday cultural products, practices, and perspectives from French-speaking communities and make simple respectful comparisons.
9. Use novice language-learning habits: listen before producing, infer from cognates and context, rehearse aloud, retrieve cumulatively, and revise after feedback.

## 3. Concept And Skill Progression

The level begins with high-comfort communication: greetings, names, politeness, classroom language, and listening/responding routines. This opening work establishes that French is used for real interaction, while also giving students early success with short phrases they can reuse.

Pronunciation and sound-spelling are introduced early through words students already know or can infer. Students notice that French spelling is not a simple letter-for-letter version of English: final consonants may be silent, accents matter, ç changes sound, nasal vowels need special listening attention, and liaison can make a normally silent consonant heard before a vowel. This work should stay light and frequent rather than becoming a phonetics unit.

Students then build high-reuse language for numbers, age, dates, weather, colors, and classroom objects. These domains supply repeated practice with listening discrimination, spelling, article awareness, and simple question-answer patterns. Calendar and weather also create a natural daily review loop.

The course moves into personal identity: je m'appelle, j'ai, je suis, j'habite, common question words, tu/vous choices, and basic interpersonal replies. Students learn être and avoir as useful chunks before seeing them as systems. They practice answering with short complete phrases rather than isolated words when the task calls for communication.

Family and description lessons add articles, noun gender, plural awareness, il y a, basic possession chunks, and adjective agreement. Grammar stays tied to meaning: articles help identify nouns, endings help descriptions match nouns, and il y a helps students say what exists in a family, classroom, or place.

Likes and activities give students a strong communicative reason to use verbs. Students learn j'aime, je n'aime pas, j'adore, je déteste, simple infinitive activity chunks, common -er verb exposure, connectors, and short preference explanations. They practice moving from recognition to supported production and then to short speaking or writing.

School, places, food, and routines broaden contexts without overloading grammar. Students interpret and build mini-dialogues about classes, objects, places in town, where someone is going, what someone does, what someone wants, and what someone eats or drinks. They meet aller, faire, and vouloir in controlled chunks, not full paradigms.

The level ends with mixed novice tasks. Students combine listening/reading interpretation, interpersonal choices, and short presentational output in familiar contexts. The final emphasis is transfer: when a prompt changes topic or format, the student still uses context, known chunks, question words, articles, and feedback to communicate.

## 4. Vocabulary, Representation, Model, Text, And Form Progression

Vocabulary begins with formulaic interaction: bonjour, salut, au revoir, merci, s'il vous plaît, comment ça va, je m'appelle, classroom commands, and simple repair phrases such as je ne comprends pas. Early representation should include audio, pictures, gesture-like descriptions, mini-dialogues, and flash-card retrieval after exposure.

Sound-spelling work should use familiar examples: français, garçon, école, élève, bon, bien, non, un, une, vous avez, les amis, and classroom words. Students should see spelling, hear audio, choose the word they heard, and explain simple pronunciation clues before being asked to type forms.

Numbers, calendar, weather, and colors develop from recognition to flexible use. Students first match words to meanings, then complete phrases such as j'ai douze ans, aujourd'hui c'est lundi, il fait froid, and le cahier bleu. Texts should stay short: calendar cards, weather reports, simple labels, schedules, and one- or two-turn exchanges.

Identity and family vocabulary uses question-answer models: Comment tu t'appelles? Quel âge as-tu? D'où viens-tu? Où habites-tu? Qui est-ce? Students learn subject pronouns and core verbs in chunks: je suis, tu es, il/elle est, j'ai, tu as, il/elle a. They should use articles with nouns: un frère, une soeur, le père, la mère, les parents.

Descriptions and possessions add adjectives and agreement in controlled forms: grand/grande, petit/petite, brun/brune, sympa, drôle, intelligent/intelligente, mon/ma/mes as chunks. Students should compare English and French word order carefully, noticing that many adjectives follow nouns while some common ones precede.

Likes, school, food, places, and routines use sentence frames and short texts: J'aime jouer au foot. Je n'aime pas chanter. Je vais à l'école. Je fais mes devoirs. Je voudrais un sandwich. Il y a un café. Students should read and build mini-dialogues, complete cloze passages, order sentence parts, and produce short oral or written responses.

By the end, text forms can include a short self-profile, a school schedule snippet, a simple map or place description, a food preference exchange, a routine list, and a compact capstone dialogue. These texts should remain novice-friendly: familiar vocabulary, predictable structures, visuals when helpful, and no extended narration across time frames.

## 5. Fluency Expectations

French 1 fluency means accurate, comprehensible use of familiar chunks with growing independence. Students should quickly recognize and respond to greetings, classroom directions, numbers 0-100 in controlled contexts, days and months, common weather phrases, high-frequency question words, articles, and familiar verbs in memorized frames.

Productive fluency should stay modest. Students should be able to answer practiced questions with short phrases or sentences, complete common sentence frames, build simple dialogues, and say or write a few connected statements about themselves or a familiar topic. Speed is not the main measure; the better evidence is retained, appropriate, understandable language after spaced review.

Pronunciation fluency should focus on intelligibility and noticing. Students should rehearse high-frequency phrases aloud, identify major sound-spelling clues, and improve after feedback, but they should not be judged against native-like speed, accent, or spontaneous extended speech.

## 6. Conceptual Understanding Expectations

Students should understand that French communication depends on context, relationship, and form. Tu and vous are not interchangeable; greetings and politeness phrases fit social situations; questions cue the type of answer expected; and short replies can still be meaningful communication.

Students should understand that French spelling gives sound clues but not always English-like ones. Silent final letters, accents, ç, nasal vowels, and liaison exposure should be treated as recurring patterns that help listening and pronunciation, not as exceptions to memorize one by one.

Students should understand that articles, gender, number, and adjective agreement carry meaning. Grammatical gender is a noun pattern, not biological gender; le, la, l', un, une, and les cannot simply be ignored; and adjective endings help descriptions match the noun.

Students should understand grammar as reusable patterns. Être, avoir, il y a, aimer, aller, faire, vouloir, ne...pas, and question words help them express identity, possession, existence, preferences, movement, activity, wants, and negation in familiar contexts. They do not need full abstract mastery of all verb systems in this level.

## 7. Application And Transfer Expectations

Students should apply French in short novice contexts: greeting someone politely, following a classroom direction, identifying the number or date they hear, reading a weather card, introducing themselves, describing a family member, stating a preference, choosing the right reply in a school or food exchange, and writing or saying a short profile.

Transfer is visible when students can move the same language across domains. For example, il y a can describe a family, a classroom, or a town; j'aime can introduce activities, school subjects, or foods; articles and adjective agreement should matter whether the noun is a person, object, or food; and question words should guide answers even when the topic changes.

Students should also transfer learning habits: listen for familiar sounds, use cognates cautiously, look for articles and endings, identify the question word, rely on visuals and context, and revise after feedback. Culture transfer should involve respectful noticing and comparison rather than stereotypes or trivia recall.

## 8. Review And Spacing Plan

Review should be cumulative from the first unit. Greetings, politeness, classroom language, question words, numbers, dates, weather, articles, core verbs, and pronunciation patterns should reappear in later contexts rather than being left behind.

Vocabulary should be spaced through varied retrieval: recognition first, then typed or spoken production after students have seen and heard the language. Short flash-card and matching tasks can support retention, but later review should ask students to use words in phrases, mini-dialogues, and short texts.

Grammar should be revisited in new communicative settings. Articles and adjective agreement first appear with people and objects, then return with school and food. Negation begins with preferences and returns with routines. Il y a appears with family, classrooms, places, and capstone descriptions. Question words should be interleaved across units so students answer the question being asked rather than translating word by word.

Pronunciation review should be brief and frequent. Final silent consonants, accents, ç, nasal vowels, and liaison exposure should be checked with listening, speaking rehearsal, and spelling-to-sound prompts across the level.

Feedback should name the clue students can use next time: the article, ending, question word, sound pattern, context, politeness relationship, or negation frame. Distractors in later questions should remain diagnostic: wrong article, false cognate, English word order, tu/vous mismatch, wrong question word, omitted ne...pas, or overpronounced final consonants.

## 9. Mastery Evidence For The End Of The Level

End-of-level mastery should be evidenced by a balanced body of interpretive, interpersonal, and presentational tasks. A student who has mastered French 1 can understand short familiar audio or written prompts, choose appropriate replies in rehearsed exchanges, ask and answer basic questions, and create brief statements about self, family, school, likes, food, places, weather, and routines.

The student can use high-frequency forms with reasonable accuracy: articles with familiar nouns, simple plural awareness, basic adjective agreement, être and avoir chunks, il y a, j'aime/je n'aime pas, je vais, je fais, je voudrais, common question words, and short connectors such as et, mais, and parce que. The student can recognize major pronunciation and spelling clues and improve after a hint or model.

The strongest mastery evidence includes transfer: the student handles familiar language in a changed context, uses a question word to shape the response, distinguishes tu/vous when context is clear, interprets a short text with cognates and visuals, and produces a compact self-profile or dialogue that is comprehensible without relying on word-for-word English translation.

## Source And Product Constraint Note

This blueprint is derived from the accepted French 1 research brief. The plan follows the ACTFL/NCSSFL novice emphasis described there: familiar contexts, short formulaic language, interpretive exposure before production, and communication across interpretive, interpersonal, and presentational modes. Buddy Blocks V3 product constraints shape the artifact level and authoring stance: build top-down from research, keep lessons compact but serious, use exposure before unsupported recall, write diagnostic distractors, include useful feedback and second-attempt hints, and rely on spaced cumulative review. Final curriculum implementation under `src/content/curriculum` is intentionally out of scope for this stage.
