# Unit 1: First French For Real Interaction

Track: French  
Level: Level 1  
Stage: Unit Design Brief  
Source: `research/french-1/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students use their first French for immediate interaction: greeting, saying goodbye, being polite, following simple classroom language, asking and answering how someone is, and noticing early sound-spelling patterns. By the end of the unit, students should feel that short French phrases are usable, not just vocabulary to memorize.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit comes first because beginning learners need quick communicative wins and routines for handling partial understanding. It also sets the level's habits: listen first, use context, rehearse aloud, notice sound-spelling, and accept that a short accurate phrase can be real communication.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: formal and informal greetings, politeness, repair language, classroom commands, phrase-level listening, pronunciation noticing.
- Vocabulary and forms: bonjour, salut, au revoir, à bientôt, merci, s'il vous plaît, pardon, excusez-moi, comment ça va, ça va bien/mal/comme ci comme ça, je m'appelle, écoutez, répétez, regardez, écrivez, je ne comprends pas.
- Representations and texts: greeting cards, short two-turn dialogues, classroom direction cards, sound-spelling examples, audio-supported phrase recognition.
- Pronunciation targets: final silent consonants as a common pattern, accents as sound clues, ç as /s/, and first liaison exposure in familiar phrases.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need no French. They need to read short prompts in English, repeat brief oral phrases, compare sounds, and follow a simple app task after a model.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Every French letter should be pronounced.
- Salut and bonjour fit all situations equally.
- Polite words are optional filler instead of part of the exchange.
- A learner must understand every word before making a reasonable response.
- Accents and ç do not matter because they look like decorations.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

The unit starts with greeting and goodbye choices because those phrases are immediately useful. It then adds politeness and classroom survival so students can navigate later lessons. The third lesson builds the first mini-dialogue around comment ça va. The final lesson pauses to notice sound-spelling patterns in already familiar words, avoiding an abstract pronunciation lecture.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

This is the first unit, so review is internal: each lesson reuses the previous lesson's greetings, politeness, and listening habits. Classroom phrases from Lesson 2 should frame tasks in Lessons 3 and 4.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares personal introductions in Unit 3, tu/vous awareness across the level, classroom and school language in Unit 6, food-service politeness in Unit 7, and all later pronunciation and listening work.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Choose a greeting or farewell that fits a situation.
- Match politeness and classroom phrases to meanings or actions.
- Build a short well-being dialogue in a logical order.
- Recognize sound-spelling clues in familiar words.
- Use repair language appropriately when meaning is unclear.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Bonjour, Salut, And Goodbye**: Distinguish common greetings and farewells by situation.
2. **Polite Words And Classroom Survival**: Use politeness, directions, and repair phrases to participate in lessons.
3. **Comment Ça Va? First Mini-Dialogue**: Build a short greeting and well-being exchange.
4. **French Sounds You Notice First**: Notice final silent consonants, accents, ç, and liaison exposure in familiar phrases.
