# Unit 3: Introducing Myself And Asking Questions

Track: French  
Level: Level 1  
Stage: Unit Design Brief  
Source: `research/french-1/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students give basic personal information and respond to simple questions about name, age, identity, origin, and residence. They learn to treat question words and social context as clues for choosing an answer.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

After students can greet, count, and answer age, they are ready to build a fuller self-introduction. This is the first unit where learners recombine several earlier chunks into a compact profile and basic interpersonal exchange.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: self-introduction, question-answer alignment, être and avoir chunks, origin and residence, tu/vous choices.
- Vocabulary and forms: je m'appelle, j'ai ___ ans, je suis élève, je suis américain/américaine, j'habite à..., je viens de..., Comment tu t'appelles? Quel âge as-tu? Où habites-tu? D'où viens-tu?
- Representations and texts: profile cards, identity mini-dialogues, map labels, question-word charts, short self-profile passages.
- Forms: je suis vs j'ai, question words, oui/non, et, mais.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need Unit 1 greetings and name phrases, Unit 2 numbers for age, and basic listening/reading routines. They should be comfortable with short memorized answers.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Every personal question can be answered with the same memorized self-introduction.
- J'ai and je suis are interchangeable.
- Tu and vous are random style choices.
- A cognate always means exactly the English word.
- A good answer must be long to count as real French.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

The sequence begins by strengthening the most useful self-introduction chunks. It then adds origin and residence so students can use maps and place names. The third lesson explicitly teaches question words as answer clues. The final lesson returns to tu/vous in interpersonal exchanges because students now have enough questions and answers for the distinction to matter.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Greetings, name, age, numbers, and polite phrases are reviewed in every lesson. Calendar and weather can appear as light warm-ups but should not distract from identity questions.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares family introductions, people descriptions, preference exchanges, school mini-dialogues, food ordering, and capstone tasks. Question-word habits become a throughline for all later interpretation and dialogue building.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Give name, age, and student identity in short phrases.
- Distinguish j'ai from je suis in familiar contexts.
- Interpret and answer où, d'où, qui, comment, and quel/quelle questions.
- Choose tu or vous when the relationship is clear.
- Read a short profile and identify key personal information.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **My Name, Age, And Student Identity**: Combine name, age, and je suis/j'ai chunks in a basic profile.
2. **Where I Am From And Where I Live**: Use origin and residence phrases with maps and place names.
3. **Question Words That Shape Answers**: Use question words to decide what kind of answer is needed.
4. **Tu Or Vous In Simple Exchanges**: Choose familiar or polite address in short interpersonal contexts.
