# Logic 3 Level Blueprint

Track: Logic
Level: Level 3
Source: Accepted research brief at `research/logic-3/01-research-brief.md`

## 1. Level Mission

Logic 3 introduces students to the first formal tools of deductive reasoning while keeping the language concrete, visual, and age-appropriate. Students move from precise terms and categorical propositions into Venn diagrams, then use those diagrams and standard-form arguments to test syllogisms for validity. They learn to separate argument structure from factual truth, distinguish validity from soundness, reason about clear if-then statements, and translate short natural-language arguments without losing meaning. By the end of the level, students should be able to slow down, name the parts of an argument, choose a helpful representation, and decide whether a conclusion must follow from the stated premises.

## 2. Durable Student Outcomes

By the end of Logic 3, students should be able to:

1. Identify terms, categories, propositions, subjects, predicates, premises, and conclusions in short arguments.
2. Translate simple categorical statements using all, no, some, not all, some are not, and related everyday phrasing.
3. Use Venn diagrams to represent inclusion, exclusion, overlap, and partial overlap between categories.
4. Interpret Venn diagrams to decide which categorical claims are supported, contradicted, or not proven.
5. Put short two-premise arguments into standard form with premises before the conclusion.
6. Test simple categorical syllogisms for validity by checking whether the conclusion must follow if the premises are true.
7. Distinguish validity, truth, and soundness in accessible cases.
8. Interpret if-then statements and identify necessary and sufficient conditions in clear rule-based contexts.
9. Avoid common formal-reasoning errors, including treating "some" as "most," "not all" as "none," reversing conditionals, and judging validity by whether a conclusion sounds true.
10. Translate short natural-language arguments into precise standard form and evaluate them using the level's tools.

## 3. Concept And Skill Progression

The level begins with the smallest useful units of formal logic: terms, categories, and propositions. Students learn that a term names a class or category, while a proposition makes a claim that can be true or false. Early work emphasizes careful language: all, no, some, some are not, not all, subject term, predicate term, and conclusion. Students should repeatedly sort statements from non-statements and claims from questions, commands, examples, or definitions.

Students then represent categorical claims visually. Venn diagrams appear before formal syllogisms so that students can see inclusion, exclusion, and overlap before they are asked to reason with them in words alone. The progression should move from one-claim diagrams to two-claim diagrams, then from diagrams to supported or unsupported conclusions.

Once categorical language and Venn representations are stable, students build standard-form syllogisms. They identify two premises and a conclusion, find the middle term, and check whether the conclusion follows from the stated relationship among categories. The main skill is not memorizing syllogism names; it is asking, "If these premises were true, would the conclusion have to be true?"

After validity is usable, students separate structure from factual accuracy. They compare valid arguments with false premises, invalid arguments with true-sounding conclusions, and sound arguments whose premises are true and whose structure is valid. This unit should make the difference obvious with friendly, noncontroversial examples before asking students to classify more mixed cases.

The level then adds conditional reasoning. Students translate if-then statements, distinguish sufficient and necessary conditions in clear situations, and avoid converse or inverse errors. Conditional work should remain concrete: game rules, classroom rules, machine rules, and simple cause or permission statements are appropriate. Formal symbolic notation should remain light or absent.

The level ends with translation and transfer. Students meet short natural-language arguments, decide which sentences are premises and conclusions, rewrite fuzzy claims into standard categorical or conditional form, choose a Venn or if-then tool, and evaluate whether the reasoning works.

## 4. Vocabulary, Representation, Model, Text, And Form Progression

Early vocabulary should stabilize claim, proposition, statement, true, false, term, category, subject term, predicate term, quantifier, all, no, none, some, some are not, not all, premise, conclusion, and standard form. Students should first work with simple sentences about familiar categories, such as books, games, animals, classroom objects, team members, or invented clubs.

Venn vocabulary and models should include circle, category, overlap, outside, inside, empty region, shaded region, X mark, inclusion, exclusion, and support. Representations should progress from one-circle category membership to two-circle inclusion and overlap diagrams, then to diagrams that combine two premises and test a conclusion.

Syllogism vocabulary should include syllogism, two-premise argument, middle term, major term, minor term, valid, invalid, must follow, possible, impossible, and counterexample. Forms should progress from clearly labeled standard-form arguments to partially scrambled arguments and then to short paragraphs that students translate.

Truth and soundness vocabulary should include true premise, false premise, valid structure, invalid structure, sound, unsound, factual accuracy, and argument structure. Students should see the same structure filled with obviously true, obviously false, or invented content so they can judge form apart from real-world belief.

Conditional vocabulary should include if, then, condition, sufficient condition, necessary condition, required, enough, only if, converse, inverse, follows, and does not follow. Models may include simple rule cards, flow arrows, two-column condition/result tables, and ordered steps for testing a conditional.

Text complexity should grow gradually. Early prompts should be one sentence. Mid-level prompts can use short standard-form arguments. End-level prompts can use compact paragraphs of three to five sentences, but the reasoning load should come from logic, not from dense reading or controversial content.

## 5. Fluency Expectations

Logic 3 fluency is not speed alone. Students should become fluent enough to recognize common logical forms, translate recurring quantifier language, and use the same test questions consistently: What are the terms? What is being claimed? What are the premises? What is the conclusion? Does the conclusion have to follow?

Students should practice quick retrieval of core distinctions: term versus proposition, premise versus conclusion, all versus some, not all versus none, valid versus true, and sufficient versus necessary. Short cumulative review is appropriate, but timed pressure should not be the main evidence of mastery because formal logic requires careful reading.

## 6. Conceptual Understanding Expectations

Students should understand that logic studies relationships among claims, not only whether a claim sounds believable. A proposition can be true or false; an argument can be valid or invalid; and those judgments answer different questions. They should understand that a valid argument preserves truth from premises to conclusion, while a sound argument is valid and has true premises.

Students should understand categorical reasoning as reasoning about relationships among classes. "All A are B" puts A inside B; "No A are B" separates the categories; "Some A are B" proves at least one overlap; and "Not all A are B" proves at least one A outside B. They should know that a diagram is a reasoning tool, not decoration.

Students should also understand that a conditional gives a one-way rule. If P is enough for Q, Q does not automatically prove P. If Q is required for P, lacking Q blocks P. These ideas should remain tied to clear examples rather than symbolic abstraction.

## 7. Application And Transfer Expectations

Students should apply Logic 3 tools to short school, story, game, and everyday rule contexts. They should translate from ordinary language to standard form, use a diagram or rule test when useful, and decide whether a proposed conclusion follows. Transfer should be visible when a student can handle a new context with the same logical structure, reject a tempting but unsupported conclusion, and explain the reason in a short selected or typed response.

Students should also transfer across representations. A categorical sentence should connect to a Venn diagram; a Venn diagram should connect to a supported or unsupported conclusion; a paragraph should connect to standard form; and a conditional rule should connect to necessary and sufficient language. Later units should continue to retrieve earlier vocabulary and diagrams so the student sees formal logic as one connected toolkit.

## 8. Review And Spacing Plan

Review should begin immediately and remain cumulative. Unit 1 vocabulary should reappear in every later unit: term, proposition, premise, conclusion, all, no, some, and not all. Venn interpretation should be reviewed during syllogism work, truth/validity work, and final translation tasks. Premise and conclusion identification should be spaced across all units so students do not treat standard form as a one-unit skill.

Misconceptions should be revisited with delayed, diagnostic prompts. Students should repeatedly contrast "some" with "all," "not all" with "none," valid with true, and if-then with its converse. Each later unit should include a small amount of earlier material in a new setting, such as using a Venn diagram to test a syllogism or using standard form to compare validity and soundness.

Feedback should name the logical feature behind the answer. Instead of only saying "correct," explanations should mention the quantifier, category relationship, middle term, conclusion test, truth/validity distinction, or one-way conditional rule.

## 9. Mastery Evidence For The End Of The Level

End-of-level mastery should be shown by a balanced body of work across translation, representation, and evaluation. A student who has mastered Logic 3 can identify terms and propositions, translate categorical statements, use Venn diagrams to model category relationships, place arguments into standard form, and test simple syllogisms for validity.

The student can distinguish valid from true and sound from merely persuasive. They can evaluate obvious valid-but-false, invalid-but-true-sounding, sound, and unsound examples. They can interpret if-then statements, identify clear necessary and sufficient conditions, and avoid reversing the rule. Most importantly, they can meet a short natural-language argument, preserve its meaning in standard form, and decide whether the conclusion must follow from the premises.
