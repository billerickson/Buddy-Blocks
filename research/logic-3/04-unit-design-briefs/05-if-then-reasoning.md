# Unit 5: If-Then Reasoning

Track: Logic
Level: Level 3
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/logic-3/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students reason with clear conditional rules. They translate if-then statements, identify sufficient and necessary conditions, and avoid assuming that a one-way rule automatically works backward.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

Conditionals come after validity and soundness because students already have the central question: what must follow from what is given? This unit extends that question beyond category arguments while keeping the examples concrete.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: one-way rule, sufficient condition, necessary condition, following and not following, converse and inverse errors.
- Vocabulary: conditional, if, then, condition, sufficient, necessary, enough, required, only if, converse, inverse, follows, does not follow, rule.
- Representations and models: rule cards, if-then arrows, condition/result tables, examples and non-examples, direction checks.
- Texts and forms: game rules, classroom rules, club rules, simple machine rules, permission rules, short conditional arguments.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need premise/conclusion reasoning, validity as "must follow," and comfort with short rule statements.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- If P then Q also means if Q then P.
- Sufficient and necessary are the same idea.
- "Only if" can be read in the same direction as ordinary "if" without checking.
- A rule proves more cases than it states.
- If the result is absent or present, the student can always infer the condition without checking direction.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Students first translate ordinary rules into if-then form and test direct cases. Then they learn sufficient and necessary language with "enough" and "required" frames. Finally, they confront converse and inverse traps, using the rule direction to decide what follows and what does not.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Review validity language from Unit 4: must follow, does not follow, structure, and possible counterexample. Continue short premise/conclusion checks so conditional arguments remain arguments, not isolated grammar exercises.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

Unit 6 combines categorical and conditional reasoning in natural-language arguments. Later formal logic study can build symbolic conditional notation on this concrete foundation.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Translate rules into if-then form.
- Identify the sufficient condition and necessary condition in clear cases.
- Decide whether a conclusion follows from a rule and a fact.
- Identify converse and inverse errors.
- Correct an overclaim by naming what the rule actually proves.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **If-Then Rules**: Translate and test direct conditional statements.
2. **Necessary And Sufficient Conditions**: Use "enough" and "required" to distinguish condition roles.
3. **Converse And Inverse Traps**: Avoid reversing or negating a conditional rule incorrectly.
