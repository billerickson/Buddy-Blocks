# Unit 1: Terms, Propositions, And Categorical Language

Track: Logic
Level: Level 3
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/logic-3/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn the language pieces that formal logic depends on: terms, categories, propositions, subject terms, predicate terms, and categorical quantifiers. By the end of the unit, students can translate short everyday claims into precise categorical statements without changing their meaning.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit comes first because later diagrams, syllogisms, and validity tests all depend on careful wording. If students overread "some," confuse a term with a proposition, or miss the subject and predicate terms, later argument tests become guesswork.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: categories as classes of things, propositions as true-or-false claims, categorical statements as claims about category relationships, quantifier strength.
- Vocabulary: term, category, proposition, statement, true, false, subject term, predicate term, quantifier, all, no, none, some, some are not, not all.
- Representations and forms: term cards, claim/non-claim sorting, subject-predicate frames, categorical sentence frames, everyday sentence to standard-form translation.
- Texts: short, familiar sentences about clubs, books, games, animals, classroom objects, and invented groups.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should understand claims, examples, definitions, all/some/none language, and basic premise/conclusion vocabulary from earlier logic work.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- A term or category word is already a complete proposition.
- Every sentence has a truth value, including questions and commands.
- "Some" means most.
- "Not all" means none.
- Translating a claim can add stronger meaning than the original sentence allowed.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

The sequence begins with terms and categories before asking students to judge full propositions. It then introduces propositions and categorical form, including subject and predicate terms. The unit closes with quantifier translation because students need both term awareness and proposition awareness before they can preserve the exact strength of all, no, some, and not all claims.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

This is the opening Logic 3 unit, so review comes from earlier informal logic: claims, reasons, examples, counterexamples, definitions, and all/some/none language. The unit should make prior informal knowledge more precise without rushing into formal notation.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

Unit 2 needs precise categorical claims to diagram. Unit 3 needs subject, predicate, premise, and conclusion language for syllogisms. Units 4-6 need students to preserve exact wording while judging validity, soundness, conditionals, and translated arguments.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Sort terms, propositions, questions, commands, and definitions.
- Identify subject and predicate terms in categorical statements.
- Match quantifier words to their logical meaning.
- Translate everyday wording into all/no/some/not all categorical form.
- Avoid strengthening or weakening a claim during translation.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Terms And Categories**: Distinguish category names from complete claims and identify examples and non-examples.
2. **Propositions And Categorical Form**: Recognize statements that can be true or false and identify subject and predicate terms.
3. **All, No, Some, And Not All**: Interpret and translate categorical quantifiers without overclaiming.
