# Unit 3: Standard-Form Syllogisms

Track: Logic
Level: Level 3
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/logic-3/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn to work with two-premise categorical arguments in standard form. They identify premises, conclusion, and middle term, then test whether the conclusion must follow from the premises.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

Syllogisms belong after categorical language and Venn diagrams because they combine both. Students now have enough vocabulary and visual reasoning to evaluate a formal argument without relying on whether the conclusion merely sounds reasonable.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: standard form, two-premise argument, middle-term connection, endpoint terms, validity as must-follow structure.
- Vocabulary: syllogism, standard form, premise, conclusion, middle term, endpoint term, major term, minor term, valid, invalid, must follow, possible, counterexample.
- Representations and models: standard-form templates, term highlighting, argument cards, Venn-based syllogism checks, possible counterexample scenarios.
- Texts and forms: short standard-form arguments, scrambled premises and conclusions, argument-to-diagram prompts, valid/invalid classification.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need all Unit 1 categorical language, all Unit 2 diagram interpretation, and prior informal understanding of premise and conclusion.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- A conclusion is valid if it sounds true.
- Any two premises that share a term prove the conclusion.
- The middle term can be ignored after the premises are read.
- "Some" premises can prove an all conclusion.
- A counterexample attacks truth rather than showing that the structure allows another possibility.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Students first arrange arguments into standard form so the parts are visible. They then focus on the middle term, because many invalid syllogisms fail there. After that, they test clearly valid syllogisms with Venn and term-tracking support. The final lesson contrasts invalid patterns and possible counterexamples so students learn not to assume every formal-looking argument works.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Each lesson should retrieve categorical translation and Venn interpretation. The first scored work should not introduce a new formal pattern without an intro or guided exposure.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

Unit 4 depends on students already understanding validity as structure. Unit 6 depends on standard-form habits when students translate paragraphs into evaluable arguments.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify premises and conclusion in a two-premise argument.
- Put an argument into standard-form order.
- Locate the middle term and endpoint terms.
- Decide whether a categorical conclusion must follow.
- Diagnose invalid syllogisms caused by weak quantifiers, missing middle-term connection, or overclaiming.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Premises, Conclusions, And Standard Form**: Arrange two-premise arguments so their structure is visible.
2. **Find The Middle Term**: Identify the connecting term and explain its role.
3. **Test Valid Syllogisms**: Use term tracking and Venn support to see why a conclusion must follow.
4. **Spot Invalid Syllogisms**: Recognize unsupported conclusions and possible counterexamples.
