# Unit 6: Translation And Argument Testing

Track: Logic
Level: Level 3
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/logic-3/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students bring the full level together by translating short natural-language arguments into standard form, choosing the right reasoning tool, and evaluating whether conclusions follow from the stated premises.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This final unit belongs after students have learned the separate tools. Transfer requires students to choose among categorical translation, Venn diagrams, syllogism tests, truth/soundness checks, and conditional rule tests instead of being told which tool applies.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: preserving meaning during translation, standard-form argument testing, tool selection, supported versus unsupported conclusions, overclaim diagnosis.
- Vocabulary: translate, standard form, premise, conclusion, categorical statement, conditional statement, validity, soundness, Venn test, rule test, supported, unsupported, overclaim.
- Representations and models: paragraph-to-standard-form charts, tool-choice menus, combined Venn and conditional examples, final argument test routines.
- Texts and forms: compact paragraphs, short invented arguments, school or game scenarios, mixed categorical and conditional prompts.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need all prior units: categorical language, Venn diagrams, standard-form syllogisms, validity, truth, soundness, and conditionals.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Every sentence in a paragraph is a premise.
- Translation is copying words rather than preserving logical meaning.
- The newest tool is the right tool for every argument.
- If a conclusion seems true, the argument has passed.
- A premise can be made stronger during translation to make the argument work.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

The unit begins with translation from paragraph to standard form because students need clean arguments before testing. It then asks students to choose a tool based on the structure of the claims. The final lesson mixes the level's reasoning patterns so students demonstrate flexible mastery.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

This unit is cumulative by design. Each lesson should retrieve at least two earlier strands, such as categorical quantifiers and validity, Venn diagrams and soundness, or conditionals and standard form.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

The unit prepares students for later formal logic, debate, philosophy, and source-based reasoning by strengthening translation, structure-first evaluation, and careful use of evidence.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify premises and conclusion in compact natural-language arguments.
- Rewrite arguments in standard form without changing quantifier or conditional strength.
- Choose an appropriate diagram, syllogism, soundness, or conditional test.
- Decide whether a conclusion is supported, unsupported, valid, invalid, sound, or unsound as appropriate.
- Diagnose the specific reasoning mistake behind an overclaim.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Translate Short Arguments**: Move from compact paragraphs to standard-form premises and conclusions.
2. **Choose The Right Logic Tool**: Select Venn, syllogism, soundness, or conditional tests based on argument structure.
3. **Final Argument Tests**: Evaluate mixed arguments and diagnose the main reasoning mistake.
