# Unit 2: Venn Diagrams And Category Relationships

Track: Logic
Level: Level 3
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/logic-3/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students use Venn diagrams to represent and interpret categorical claims. They learn that each mark, shaded region, overlap, or empty space has a specific logical meaning, and that a diagram can prove some claims while leaving others unknown.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

Venn diagrams come before syllogisms because students need a visual way to reason about all, no, some, and not all before handling multi-premise arguments. The diagrams reduce cognitive load and make overclaims visible.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: category inclusion, exclusion, overlap, partial overlap, proven information, unknown information.
- Vocabulary: Venn diagram, circle, category, region, overlap, outside, inside, empty region, shaded region, X mark, inclusion, exclusion, support, contradict, not proven.
- Representations and models: one-circle and two-circle Venn diagrams described in words, marked X examples, shaded empty regions, paired categorical sentence and diagram prompts.
- Texts and forms: one-claim diagram prompts, two-premise diagram prompts, diagram-to-claim choices, supported/unsupported conclusion checks.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need Unit 1 categorical language, especially subject and predicate terms and the quantifiers all, no, some, some are not, and not all.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- A blank region automatically means no members are there.
- "Some A are B" proves most or all A are B.
- "Not all A are B" proves no A are B.
- A diagram that looks plausible is enough even if it does not represent the wording.
- The visual model is separate from the claim instead of a way to reason about it.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

The unit opens with the basic map of a two-circle diagram. It then separates universal claims from negative claims, because all and no require different diagrams. Next, it teaches particular claims such as some and not all, where X marks and partial information are central. The final lesson asks students to read a completed diagram and decide what is proven, contradicted, or not proven.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Every lesson retrieves Unit 1 quantifiers and subject/predicate terms. Students should translate a claim before diagramming it so visual work remains tied to precise language.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

Unit 3 uses Venn diagrams to test syllogisms. Unit 4 uses Venn-supported structure to separate validity from truth. Unit 6 asks students to choose diagrams when a translated argument is categorical.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Match categorical claims to accurate Venn descriptions.
- Interpret X marks, shaded regions, inclusion, exclusion, and overlap.
- Decide whether a Venn diagram supports a proposed conclusion.
- Identify diagram errors caused by quantifier confusion.
- Combine two premises in a single diagram when the information is simple.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Venn Basics: Inside, Outside, Overlap**: Learn the regions of a two-circle diagram and what category membership means.
2. **Diagram All And No Claims**: Represent inclusion and exclusion accurately.
3. **Diagram Some And Not All Claims**: Use X marks and outside regions to represent partial information.
4. **Read What A Venn Diagram Proves**: Decide whether a completed diagram supports, contradicts, or leaves a claim unknown.
