# Logic - Level 3 Lesson Briefs

Sources: accepted level blueprint, course map, and unit design briefs in `research/logic-3/`.

## Unit 1: Terms, Propositions, And Categorical Language

### Lesson 1: Terms And Categories

- **Teaching goal:** Help students identify terms as category names rather than complete claims.
- **Student outcome:** The student can distinguish a term or category from a proposition and can give examples and non-examples of a category.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A term names a class, such as puzzle games, library books, or mammals. A term by itself is not true or false.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Examples, non-examples, definitions, and informal claim language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating any meaningful word group as a claim; thinking a term has to name only one object; confusing a category with an example of that category.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given mixed phrases, the student can sort terms from complete propositions and match categories to fitting examples.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for term/proposition recognition; `match-pairs` for categories and examples; `fill-blank` for term/category sentence frames.
- **Suggested intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "A term names a category. 'Board games' is a term because it names a group. It is not true or false by itself." Card 2: "A proposition makes a claim, such as 'All chess sets are games.' In this lesson, first notice whether you have only a category name or a complete claim."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar categories and short phrases. Avoid abstract categories like justice or beauty in this opening lesson.

### Lesson 2: Propositions And Categorical Form

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to recognize propositions and identify subject and predicate terms in categorical statements.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify whether a sentence can be true or false and can name the subject term and predicate term in simple categorical form.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A proposition is a statement that can be true or false. A categorical proposition links a subject term to a predicate term.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Terms and categories from Lesson 1, complete sentences, and basic true/false language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating questions, commands, or definitions as propositions; thinking the predicate term is always the last word; ignoring multi-word terms.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can sort statements from non-statements and label subject and predicate terms in claims such as "Some library books are mysteries."
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for proposition recognition; `match-pairs` for subject/predicate identification; `multi-blank-cloze` for categorical frames.
- **Suggested intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "A proposition is a statement that could be true or false. 'Some games are puzzles' is a proposition. 'Are some games puzzles?' is a question, not a proposition." Card 2: "In a categorical proposition, the subject term is the category being discussed. The predicate term is the category it is being connected to."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep sentences one clause long. Use explicit labels before asking students to infer subject and predicate terms.

### Lesson 3: All, No, Some, And Not All

- **Teaching goal:** Build precise understanding of categorical quantifiers and everyday translations.
- **Student outcome:** The student can interpret and translate all, no, some, some are not, and not all without making the claim stronger or weaker.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Quantifiers tell how much of one category is being connected to another. "Some" means at least one, while "not all" means at least one exception.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Terms, propositions, subject terms, and predicate terms.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating some as most; treating not all as none; translating "every" and "none" inconsistently; changing "some are not" into "no."
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can choose the correct standard-form version of everyday wording and identify what a quantifier does and does not prove.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for quantifier meaning; `match-pairs` for everyday wording to standard form; `fill-blank` and `error-correction` for exact translations.
- **Suggested intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "Quantifiers are words like all, no, some, and not all. They control the strength of a claim." Card 2: "Some means at least one. Not all means at least one exception. Do not turn a weaker claim into a stronger one."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use direct sentences and avoid double negatives except when teaching "some are not" and "not all" deliberately.

## Unit 2: Venn Diagrams And Category Relationships

### Lesson 1: Venn Basics: Inside, Outside, Overlap

- **Teaching goal:** Introduce the regions of a two-circle Venn diagram as category-membership information.
- **Student outcome:** The student can describe what inside, outside, and overlap regions mean in a two-category Venn diagram.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Each circle represents a category. The overlap is for things in both categories. Outside a circle means not in that category.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Category, term, subject term, predicate term, and examples/non-examples.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Thinking the circles are just labels; assuming every region must contain something; confusing outside one circle with outside both circles.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a described item location, the student can state which categories the item belongs to and which it does not.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for region interpretation; `match-pairs` for locations and meanings; `fill-blank` for category membership statements.
- **Suggested intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "A Venn diagram uses circles for categories. If an item is inside a circle, it belongs to that category." Card 2: "The overlap means both categories. The space outside a circle means not in that category."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use text descriptions of diagrams rather than requiring visual precision. Keep to two categories.

### Lesson 2: Diagram All And No Claims

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to represent universal inclusion and exclusion claims.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose or describe a Venn diagram for "All A are B" and "No A are B" statements.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** "All A are B" places every A inside B. "No A are B" leaves no overlap between A and B.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Venn regions, all/no quantifiers, subject and predicate terms.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Reversing all claims into "All B are A"; treating no as not all; assuming no overlap from an unmarked diagram.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can match universal claims to accurate diagram descriptions and reject common reversed or weakened versions.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for diagram choice; `match-pairs` for claim to diagram meaning; `order-items` for steps in translating then diagramming.
- **Suggested intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "For 'All A are B,' the A circle sits inside the B circle or the A-only region is empty." Card 2: "For 'No A are B,' there is no overlap between A and B."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep category names short. Avoid mixing all, no, and some in the same early prompt.

### Lesson 3: Diagram Some And Not All Claims

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to represent particular and exception claims without overclaiming.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use X-mark descriptions for "Some A are B," "Some A are not B," and "Not all A are B."
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** "Some" proves at least one member. "Some A are not B" and "Not all A are B" prove at least one A outside B.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Venn basics, all/no diagrams, and quantifier meanings.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating some as all; treating not all as none; placing an X in the wrong region; assuming a particular claim proves a whole category relationship.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can identify where the X belongs for some and not all claims and explain what remains unknown.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for X placement; `fill-blank` for claim descriptions; `match-pairs` for quantifier to diagram action.
- **Suggested intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "An X means at least one example exists in that region." Card 2: "'Some A are B' puts an X in the overlap. 'Not all A are B' puts an X in the part of A outside B."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one claim per question at first. Do not require drawing; describe region choices in words.

### Lesson 4: Read What A Venn Diagram Proves

- **Teaching goal:** Help students infer only what a completed Venn diagram supports.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether a Venn diagram supports, contradicts, or does not prove a proposed claim.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A diagram proves what its marks and shaded or empty regions show. It does not prove extra claims just because they might be true.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** All Unit 2 diagram conventions and Unit 1 quantifier meanings.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating unknown as false; assuming a blank-looking region is empty; accepting a claim because it sounds plausible; overreading some as all.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a described diagram, the student can choose a supported conclusion and reject an unsupported overclaim.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for shared diagram descriptions; `multiple-choice` for supported conclusions; `match-pairs` for claim status.
- **Suggested intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "When you read a Venn diagram, ask: What is marked? What is empty or shaded? What is not shown?" Card 2: "Not proven does not mean false. It means the diagram has not given enough information."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep diagram descriptions consistent and brief. Use supported/contradicted/not proven language repeatedly.

## Unit 3: Standard-Form Syllogisms

### Lesson 1: Premises, Conclusions, And Standard Form

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to arrange two-premise arguments in standard form.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify two premises and a conclusion and place the conclusion after the premises.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Standard form lists the reasons first and the conclusion last so the argument's structure can be tested.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Proposition, premise, conclusion, categorical statement, and quantifier translation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating every sentence as a premise; putting the most interesting sentence first; confusing explanation order with argument order.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can reorder a short argument into Premise 1, Premise 2, Therefore conclusion.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for standard-form ordering; `multiple-choice` for premise/conclusion identification; `multi-blank-cloze` for labeled argument forms.
- **Suggested intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "A syllogism is a two-premise argument. In standard form, put the premises first and the conclusion last." Card 2: "The word 'therefore' marks the conclusion you are supposed to test."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use three-sentence arguments. Avoid hidden premises in this first syllogism lesson.

### Lesson 2: Find The Middle Term

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to find the term that connects the two premises in a syllogism.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify the middle term and distinguish it from the two terms in the conclusion.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** The middle term appears in the premises and connects the conclusion's subject and predicate terms. It usually does not appear in the conclusion.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Standard form, subject term, predicate term, premise, conclusion.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Picking the term that appears in the conclusion; assuming the longest phrase is the middle term; missing repeated multi-word terms.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a standard-form syllogism, the student can name the middle term and explain its connecting role.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for middle-term selection; `match-pairs` for terms and roles; `fill-blank` for repeated-term identification.
- **Suggested intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "The middle term is the connector. It appears in the premises to link the two conclusion terms." Card 2: "In 'All robins are birds; all birds are animals; therefore all robins are animals,' birds is the middle term."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Bold labels are not available in scored prompts, so keep term names simple and repeated exactly.

### Lesson 3: Test Valid Syllogisms

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to test whether a syllogism's conclusion must follow from its premises.
- **Student outcome:** The student can recognize simple valid categorical syllogisms using term tracking or Venn support.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A syllogism is valid when the conclusion has to be true if the premises are true.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Standard form, middle term, Venn all/no/some claims, and premise/conclusion language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Judging validity by factual truth; ignoring quantifiers; thinking the conclusion follows because it uses familiar words.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can choose the valid conclusion for a pair of premises and identify why the middle term makes it follow.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for valid conclusions; `order-items` for testing steps; `passage-question` for standard-form argument checks.
- **Suggested intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "To test validity, pretend the premises are true. Then ask: Must the conclusion be true?" Card 2: "Do not ask whether the facts are real yet. First test the structure."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use clear universal and negative examples before mixing some claims. Avoid formal syllogism names.

### Lesson 4: Spot Invalid Syllogisms

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to identify unsupported conclusions in formal-looking arguments.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide when a syllogism is invalid and name the main reason, such as overclaiming or a weak middle-term connection.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** An invalid syllogism leaves open at least one possible case where the premises are true and the conclusion is false.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Valid syllogism testing, Venn interpretation, middle term.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Believing formal wording guarantees validity; treating shared terms as enough; turning some into all; overlooking possible counterexamples.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can reject an invalid conclusion and choose a concise diagnosis of the reasoning mistake.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for validity classification and diagnosis; `match-pairs` for invalid pattern to error; `passage-question` for compact argument evaluation.
- **Suggested intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "Some arguments look formal but still do not prove their conclusions." Card 2: "If the premises could be true while the conclusion is false, the argument is invalid."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep invalid examples short and diagnostic. Use invented categories when real-world knowledge might distract.

## Unit 4: Validity, Truth, And Soundness

### Lesson 1: Validity Is About Structure

- **Teaching goal:** Stabilize validity as a structure test, not a factual truth test.
- **Student outcome:** The student can classify arguments as valid or invalid while setting aside whether the content is actually true.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Validity asks whether the conclusion follows if the premises are true. The facts can be silly, invented, true, or false.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Syllogism testing and standard form.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Valid means true; false content always makes an argument invalid; a true conclusion proves the reasoning was valid.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can identify valid structure in an argument with false or invented premises.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for structure classification; `match-pairs` for terms to definitions; `passage-question` for argument checks.
- **Suggested intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "Validity is about structure. Ask whether the conclusion must follow if the premises were true." Card 2: "An argument can be valid even when its premises are not true in real life."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use obviously invented examples so students are not tempted into factual debate.

### Lesson 2: Truth Of Premises And Conclusions

- **Teaching goal:** Separate truth values of individual propositions from validity of arguments.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify whether a claim is true or false in simple cases and explain that this is different from argument validity.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Truth belongs to propositions. Validity belongs to arguments.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Proposition, premise, conclusion, valid/invalid.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Calling a single statement valid; using truth of one sentence to judge an entire argument's structure; assuming a false conclusion always means invalid reasoning.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can sort "truth question" prompts from "validity question" prompts and classify simple propositions.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for truth versus validity questions; `multiple-choice` for claim classification; `fill-blank` for definitions.
- **Suggested intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "A proposition can be true or false. An argument can be valid or invalid." Card 2: "Do not call one sentence valid. Validity needs at least a premise-conclusion relationship."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use simple, widely known factual claims and invented claims clearly marked as pretend when needed.

### Lesson 3: Sound Or Unsound?

- **Teaching goal:** Teach soundness as valid structure plus true premises.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether a simple argument is sound, unsound because invalid, or unsound because at least one premise is false.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A sound argument passes two checks: the argument is valid, and its premises are true.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Validity, proposition truth, premise and conclusion identification.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Sound means persuasive; soundness depends only on the conclusion; false premises make an argument invalid instead of unsound.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can apply a two-step soundness test and choose the correct classification.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for soundness steps; `multiple-choice` for classification; `passage-question` for argument evaluation.
- **Suggested intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "Soundness is a stronger test than validity. First check whether the structure is valid. Then check whether the premises are true." Card 2: "If either check fails, the argument is unsound."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep factual premises obvious and noncontroversial. State when a premise should be treated as given.

## Unit 5: If-Then Reasoning

### Lesson 1: If-Then Rules

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to translate and test direct conditional statements.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify the if-part and then-part of a rule and decide what follows from the if-part being met.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** In "If P, then Q," P is enough to trigger Q. The rule has a direction.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Premise/conclusion reasoning and validity as must-follow.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Reversing the rule; thinking the then-part causes the if-part; treating every rule as a biconditional.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can translate ordinary rule wording into if-then form and choose a conclusion that follows directly.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for if/then identification; `fill-blank` for rule translation; `order-items` for conditional test steps.
- **Suggested intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "A conditional is an if-then rule. The if-part gives a condition. The then-part says what follows." Card 2: "The direction matters: if P then Q does not automatically mean if Q then P."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use concrete rules about clubs, games, machines, or classroom routines. Avoid causal controversies.

### Lesson 2: Necessary And Sufficient Conditions

- **Teaching goal:** Help students distinguish "enough for" from "required for" in clear conditional rules.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify sufficient and necessary conditions using enough and required language.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A sufficient condition is enough to guarantee the result. A necessary condition is required for the result.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** If-then rule direction and direct conditional testing.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating sufficient and necessary as synonyms; identifying the result as always sufficient; misreading "only if."
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can choose which condition is enough and which condition is required in a simple rule.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for condition roles; `multiple-choice` for necessary/sufficient classification; `multi-blank-cloze` for enough/required frames.
- **Suggested intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "Sufficient means enough. If the sufficient condition happens, the result follows." Card 2: "Necessary means required. If the necessary condition is missing, the result cannot happen."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one rule at a time. Introduce "only if" with a clear teaching example before scoring.

### Lesson 3: Converse And Inverse Traps

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to recognize and avoid invalid reversals and negations of conditional rules.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify whether a proposed conclusion is the original rule, the converse, the inverse, or a valid direct inference.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A converse reverses if and then. An inverse negates both parts. Neither automatically follows from the original rule.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** If-then rules and necessary/sufficient conditions.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Assuming the converse is always true; assuming the inverse is always true; rejecting a valid direct inference because it sounds too simple.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can spot a reversed or negated overclaim and choose what the rule actually proves.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for trap identification; `match-pairs` for original/converse/inverse forms; `passage-question` for short rule scenarios.
- **Suggested intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "If P then Q has a direction. The converse says if Q then P. The inverse says if not P then not Q." Card 2: "The converse and inverse may sound tempting, but they do not automatically follow."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep P and Q as simple everyday clauses. Do not introduce contrapositive as a required concept in this level.

## Unit 6: Translation And Argument Testing

### Lesson 1: Translate Short Arguments

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to turn compact natural-language arguments into standard form while preserving meaning.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify premises and conclusion in a short paragraph and rewrite them as a standard-form argument.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Translation should keep the logical strength of the original words. Do not add "all" when the paragraph only says "some."
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Premises, conclusions, categorical quantifiers, conditionals, and standard form.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Treating every sentence as a premise; strengthening the argument while translating; dropping important quantifiers; mistaking background information for a reason.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can choose the correct standard-form version of a short argument and identify the conclusion.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for paragraph interpretation; `order-items` for standard-form ordering; `error-correction` for exact wording fixes.
- **Suggested intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "Natural language arguments often mix reasons and conclusions in ordinary order. Standard form puts the premises first and the conclusion last." Card 2: "A good translation keeps the same strength: all stays all, some stays some, and if-then rules keep their direction."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use three- to four-sentence paragraphs. Keep contexts familiar and avoid irrelevant details until after mastery is visible.

### Lesson 2: Choose The Right Logic Tool

- **Teaching goal:** Help students choose an evaluation tool based on the structure of an argument.
- **Student outcome:** The student can decide whether a prompt calls for categorical translation, Venn reasoning, syllogism testing, soundness checking, or conditional rule testing.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** The right tool depends on the argument's structure, not on which topic was most recent.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** All prior Logic 3 tools and their purposes.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Using a Venn diagram for every argument; using truth checks when validity is being asked; using conditional tests for categorical claims.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can match a short prompt to the best logic tool and justify the choice with the relevant feature.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for tool and structure; `multiple-choice` for best tool; `passage-question` for short scenario tool selection.
- **Suggested intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "Logic tools have jobs. Venn diagrams help with categories. Conditional tests help with if-then rules. Soundness checks combine validity and true premises." Card 2: "Before solving, ask: What kind of structure do I see?"
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep each prompt focused on one main structure. Use familiar tool names repeatedly.

### Lesson 3: Final Argument Tests

- **Teaching goal:** Assess flexible Logic 3 transfer across categorical, syllogistic, soundness, and conditional reasoning.
- **Student outcome:** The student can evaluate mixed short arguments and diagnose the main reasoning mistake or support.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A careful reasoner translates first, chooses a tool, tests what follows, and avoids overclaiming.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Complete Logic 3 sequence.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Jumping to an answer before translating; calling true-sounding conclusions valid; reversing conditionals; overreading Venn information; strengthening some into all.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can classify several mixed arguments and select the best explanation for why each conclusion is supported or unsupported.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for compact arguments; `multiple-choice` for diagnosis; `order-items` for evaluation routine; `match-pairs` for argument and error type.
- **Suggested intro content for `config.intro`:** Card 1: "Final argument tests use the whole toolkit. Read carefully, translate the structure, choose the tool, then decide what follows." Card 2: "A strong answer names the reason: quantifier mistake, unsupported middle term, truth/validity confusion, or reversed conditional."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short paragraphs and one main reasoning issue per question. Avoid controversial topics and dense symbolic notation.
