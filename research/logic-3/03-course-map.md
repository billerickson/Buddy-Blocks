# Logic 3 Course Map

Track: Logic
Level: Level 3
Sources: Accepted research brief at `research/logic-3/01-research-brief.md` and level blueprint at `research/logic-3/02-level-blueprint.md`

## Course Design Notes

Logic 3 moves from language precision to visual category reasoning, then into standard-form arguments, validity, soundness, conditionals, and transfer. The sequence is intentionally cumulative: students first learn to say exactly what a claim means, then draw what it means, then test whether a conclusion follows. The course avoids advanced symbolic logic and formal proof systems. It uses short, familiar, noncontroversial contexts so the logical structure stays visible.

## Unit 1: Terms, Propositions, And Categorical Language

### Unit Purpose

Build the language foundation for formal logic. Students distinguish terms from propositions, identify subject and predicate terms, and translate everyday category claims into precise categorical language using all, no, some, some are not, and not all.

### Student Outcomes

- Distinguish a term or category from a proposition that can be true or false.
- Identify subject terms and predicate terms in simple categorical statements.
- Interpret all, no, some, some are not, and not all without overclaiming.
- Translate short everyday sentences into precise categorical form.
- Recognize common quantifier mistakes such as treating some as most or not all as none.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Term, category, proposition, statement, true, false, subject term, predicate term, quantifier, all, no, none, some, some are not, not all, categorical statement, translate.

### Prerequisites

Students should already understand claims, definitions, examples, counterexamples, all/some/none language, and the difference between a reason and a conclusion from earlier logic work.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit formalizes informal claim language from earlier levels. It prepares Unit 2 by giving every Venn diagram a precise sentence to represent. It prepares Units 3 and 6 by making standard-form argument language possible.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Treating a category word as a complete claim.
- Thinking every sentence is a proposition.
- Reading "some" as "most" or "only a few."
- Reading "not all" as "none."
- Changing the meaning of a sentence when translating it into formal wording.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

3-4 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use quick retrieval of claim, premise, conclusion, definition, and counterexample from earlier logic. Later units should keep asking students to name the terms and quantifier before they reason with a statement.

## Unit 2: Venn Diagrams And Category Relationships

### Unit Purpose

Teach students to use Venn diagrams as reasoning tools for categorical statements. Students represent inclusion, exclusion, overlap, and partial exclusion, then interpret what a diagram proves and what it leaves unknown.

### Student Outcomes

- Represent all, no, some, some are not, and not all statements with Venn diagrams.
- Interpret inside, outside, overlap, exclusion, empty regions, and X marks as logical information.
- Decide whether a diagram supports, contradicts, or fails to prove a categorical claim.
- Combine two simple categorical premises in one diagram.
- Avoid treating a diagram as decoration rather than evidence.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Venn diagram, circle, category, region, overlap, outside, inside, empty region, shaded region, X mark, inclusion, exclusion, support, contradict, not proven.

### Prerequisites

Students need Unit 1 categorical vocabulary and comfort reading simple visual models. They should understand that all, no, some, and not all make different strengths of claims.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit turns Unit 1 language into a visual model. It prepares Unit 3 by giving students a way to test categorical syllogisms. It also supports Unit 4 because Venn diagrams help separate validity from factual truth.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Thinking Venn diagrams only show examples, not claims.
- Marking "some" as if it proved all.
- Treating "not all" as a no-overlap statement.
- Assuming empty-looking space proves no members exist unless the diagram marks that information.
- Choosing a conclusion because it seems reasonable rather than because the diagram proves it.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Interleave Unit 1 translation with diagram building. Later syllogism lessons should periodically return to Venn reasoning instead of making validity feel purely verbal.

## Unit 3: Standard-Form Syllogisms

### Unit Purpose

Introduce syllogisms as two-premise category arguments. Students identify premises, conclusion, middle term, and endpoint terms; arrange arguments in standard form; and test whether the conclusion must follow from the premises.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify premises and conclusions in short two-premise arguments.
- Put a syllogism into standard form.
- Find the middle term and explain its job connecting the other terms.
- Use a Venn diagram or term-tracking test to decide whether a conclusion follows.
- Identify common invalid syllogism patterns in accessible cases.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Syllogism, standard form, premise, conclusion, middle term, endpoint term, major term, minor term, valid, invalid, must follow, possible, counterexample.

### Prerequisites

Students need Units 1-2 categorical language and Venn diagram interpretation, plus prior informal work with premises, conclusions, examples, and counterexamples.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit applies categorical language and Venn diagrams to full arguments. It prepares Unit 4 by making validity stable before truth and soundness are introduced. It prepares Unit 6 by giving students a standard-form template for translated arguments.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Judging an argument by whether the conclusion sounds believable.
- Losing track of the middle term.
- Assuming a conclusion follows just because two premises share a term.
- Treating "some" information as if it guarantees an all-statement.
- Ignoring the exact wording of quantifiers in the premises.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Begin each lesson with a short categorical translation or Venn interpretation. Use mixed valid and invalid examples so students do not learn a single answer pattern.

## Unit 4: Validity, Truth, And Soundness

### Unit Purpose

Separate the structure of reasoning from the factual accuracy of claims. Students learn that validity asks whether the conclusion must follow from the premises, truth asks whether a claim matches reality, and soundness requires both valid structure and true premises.

### Student Outcomes

- Distinguish a true or false proposition from a valid or invalid argument.
- Classify arguments as valid, invalid, sound, or unsound in clear cases.
- Recognize valid arguments with false premises and invalid arguments with true-sounding conclusions.
- Explain why "valid means true" is a mistake.
- Use factual truth only after the structure test has been applied.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Truth, falsehood, validity, invalidity, soundness, unsound, true premise, false premise, conclusion, structure, factual accuracy, persuasive, follows.

### Prerequisites

Students need stable premise and conclusion identification, basic categorical syllogism testing, and comfort with obviously true, false, and invented statements.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit deepens Unit 3's validity work and prepares students to evaluate conditionals and translated arguments with clearer criteria. Unit 6 will require students to decide whether problems are about truth, validity, or soundness.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Valid means true.
- Sound means persuasive or popular.
- A true conclusion makes an argument valid.
- A false premise always makes the argument invalid.
- An argument should be rejected before its structure is understood.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

3-4 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Review syllogism structure with examples whose content is obviously silly, false, or invented. Continue using Venn diagrams when structure is hard to see.

## Unit 5: If-Then Reasoning

### Unit Purpose

Introduce conditional reasoning with clear, concrete if-then rules. Students identify sufficient and necessary conditions, test what follows and what does not follow, and avoid converse and inverse errors.

### Student Outcomes

- Translate clear rule statements into if-then form.
- Identify sufficient and necessary conditions in familiar cases.
- Decide what follows when the if-part is met or when the required condition is missing.
- Recognize that a conditional rule does not automatically work backward.
- Identify converse and inverse mistakes in accessible examples.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Conditional, if, then, condition, sufficient, necessary, enough, required, only if, converse, inverse, follows, does not follow, rule.

### Prerequisites

Students need experience with claims, premise/conclusion reasoning, and validity as "must follow." They should also be comfortable reading short rules and examples.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit broadens formal reasoning beyond categorical statements while reinforcing validity as a must-follow relationship. It prepares Unit 6 because translated arguments may include both categorical and conditional claims.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Believing every if-then statement works backward.
- Confusing sufficient with necessary.
- Treating "only if" as the same as "if" without checking the direction.
- Assuming a rule proves more than it states.
- Thinking a failed result always proves the if-part did not happen without considering the rule's direction.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

3-4 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Interleave short validity questions from Units 3-4. Use rule cards, flow arrows, and examples/non-examples so students can compare conditional reasoning with categorical reasoning.

## Unit 6: Translation And Argument Testing

### Unit Purpose

Bring the level together through short natural-language arguments. Students translate ordinary wording into standard form, choose a Venn or conditional test, and evaluate whether the conclusion follows without being distracted by surface plausibility.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify premises, conclusions, and hidden categorical or conditional structure in short paragraphs.
- Rewrite short arguments in standard form while preserving meaning.
- Choose an appropriate logic tool: categorical translation, Venn diagram, syllogism test, truth/validity/soundness distinction, or conditional rule test.
- Evaluate whether a conclusion must follow from the stated premises.
- Diagnose a likely reasoning mistake in a short argument.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Translate, standard form, premise, conclusion, categorical statement, conditional statement, validity, soundness, Venn test, rule test, supported, unsupported, overclaim.

### Prerequisites

Students need all prior Logic 3 units: categorical language, Venn diagrams, syllogisms, validity versus truth, soundness, and conditional reasoning.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This final unit reviews and combines the whole level. It prepares later formal logic study by making students flexible with translation, representation choice, and argument evaluation before they meet heavier notation or proof systems.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Copying natural language into standard form without preserving meaning.
- Treating every paragraph sentence as a premise.
- Choosing a diagram or rule test because it is recent rather than because it fits.
- Overclaiming beyond the premises.
- Confusing truth, validity, and soundness in mixed arguments.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

3-4 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Every lesson in this unit should be cumulative. Question sets should mix categorical, Venn, syllogism, truth/soundness, and conditional review while keeping each individual prompt compact.
