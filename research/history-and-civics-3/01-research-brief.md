# History And Civics 3 Research Brief

Track: History And Civics  
Level: Level 3  
Intended student profile: students with ancient-world orientation and basic inquiry skills, ready for medieval and early modern worlds, cross-cultural comparison, and the development of law, representation, exchange, and pluralism.

This brief defines outcomes and instructional priorities only. It does not define units, lessons, or questions.

## 1. End-Of-Level Outcomes

By the end of History And Civics 3, students should be able to describe major features of Byzantine, Islamic, African, Asian, and European medieval worlds; explain feudalism, towns, trade, learning, religious/cultural pluralism, Renaissance, Reformation, exploration, encounters, and early nation-states in age-appropriate ways; use maps and timelines to trace exchange and change; and support claims with primary/secondary source evidence.

## 2. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should know ancient-world geography, civilization features, source/evidence routines, timeline/map skills, and basic civic terms such as law, citizen, republic, empire, and government.

## 3. Major Concept Clusters

- **Medieval Worlds:** Byzantine, Islamic, African, Asian, and European societies as connected but distinct.
- **Power And Institutions:** empire, kingdom, feudal relationships, church/religious institutions, towns, guilds, law, and representation.
- **Exchange And Encounter:** trade routes, pilgrimage, conquest, migration, exploration, disease, technology, and ideas.
- **Renaissance And Reformation:** humanism exposure, printing, reform, religious conflict, and changing authority.
- **Source Perspective:** accounts, maps, art, laws, travelers, and chronicles as evidence with perspective.

## 4. Recommended Progression

Start with map/timeline review after Rome. Build regional medieval worlds without treating Europe as the whole story. Use trade and exchange as a connective thread. Introduce law and representation through concrete examples. Move into Renaissance/Reformation and exploration as transformations with mixed consequences. End with source-based comparisons and change-over-time claims.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- The Middle Ages happened only in Europe.
- Medieval means nothing changed.
- Religious identity explains every action.
- Exploration was only heroic discovery.
- Trade means goods only, not ideas, technology, and disease.
- A source from the past is neutral because it is old.
- Representation developed all at once.

## 6. Developmental Constraints And Reading/Load Concerns

Avoid huge name/date coverage. Use maps for exchange routes and regions. Handle religion respectfully and historically. Frame conquest, colonization, disease, and conflict accurately but age-appropriately. Keep source excerpts short and contextualized.

## 7. Assessment Implications

Use map/timeline tasks, source perspective questions, comparison charts, and evidence-supported claims. Distractors should include Eurocentric overgeneralization, sequence errors, trade-route confusion, source perspective neglect, and one-cause explanations.

## 8. Spiral Review Priorities

Source, evidence, perspective, timeline, map, empire, kingdom, feudalism, town, trade, pilgrimage, Renaissance, Reformation, exploration, encounter, representation, law, change/continuity.

## 9. Concepts That Should Not Be Taught Yet

Avoid exhaustive dynastic chronology, sectarian theology debates, graphic violence, graduate-level world-systems theory, complex constitutional history, and long research essays.

## 10. Source Notes

C3 shaped inquiry and evidence practices. NCSS shaped global connections, culture, institutions, and time/change. TEKS and Core Knowledge shaped medieval/early modern scope. Common Core shaped source reading and explanation. Buddy Blocks docs shaped compact source tasks and feedback.
