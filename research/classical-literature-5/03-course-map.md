# Classical Literature 5 Course Map

Track: Classical Literature
Level: Level 5
Sources: Accepted research brief at `research/classical-literature-5/01-research-brief.md` and level blueprint at `research/classical-literature-5/02-level-blueprint.md`

## Course Design Notes

This course map treats Level 5 as a compact advanced seminar sequence rather than an exhaustive canon survey. The units move from transferable reading habits into selected ancient, Christian and late antique, Renaissance and early modern, modern civic and economic, and final synthesis work. Lesson counts are intentionally uneven: some units need more interpretive scaffolding because they introduce several genres at once, while the final unit is shorter and more synthetic. Every unit should keep the core routine active: orient the text, distinguish speaker and author, summarize before evaluating, use precise evidence, and compare across texts without flattening historical and formal differences.

## Unit 1: Seminar Tools For Difficult Classics

### Unit Purpose

Establish the habits and vocabulary students need before entering a dense primary-text corpus. Students learn to orient a passage, distinguish summary from interpretation and evaluation, track speaker and genre, and use evidence in a compact seminar-style response.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify author, work or work-type, form, speaker, audience, occasion, and central question in a short passage description.
- Distinguish summary, interpretation, evaluation, and unsupported opinion.
- Use annotation and rereading strategies for difficult syntax, unfamiliar context, and important terms.
- Select evidence that actually supports a claim.
- Practice seminar norms: cite, question, listen, revise, and separate text from personal reaction.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Primary text, translation, edition, excerpt, annotation, close reading, seminar question, summary, interpretation, evaluation, claim, evidence, context, speaker, author, narrator, audience, occasion, genre, form, diction, structure, irony, allusion, objection, turn.

### Prerequisites

Students should be able to read substantial translated excerpts with support, summarize difficult passages, identify basic genre and speaker, cite evidence, and participate in discussion. They should also understand broad historical periods at an introductory level.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit turns prerequisite literature and humanities skills into a durable routine for every later unit. Ancient dialogue, biblical narrative, essay, drama, civic argument, economic prose, manifesto, modern drama, and scientific reflection all depend on the same orientation and evidence habits.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A hard passage means the reader has failed.
- Context alone gives the meaning.
- A vivid speaker must be the author's own view.
- Summary, interpretation, and evaluation are interchangeable.
- Any quote from the passage counts as evidence.
- Classics matter only because authorities say they do.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Every later unit should include short review of orientation, speaker awareness, evidence selection, and summary-before-judgment. Unit 1 vocabulary should reappear in all question sets, especially genre, speaker, claim, evidence, and context.

## Unit 2: Ancient Questions Of Justice, Virtue, And The City

### Unit Purpose

Introduce ancient forms and problems through selected dialogue, comedy, ethical and political prose, biography, and Stoic meditation. Students practice reading arguments and dramatic voices while tracing questions about justice, education, law, virtue, friendship, citizenship, and the examined life.

### Student Outcomes

- Recognize how dialogue, comedy, treatise, biography, and meditation shape interpretation.
- Track interlocutors, comic exaggeration, argument steps, examples, definitions, and objections.
- Explain ancient questions about justice, virtue, law, soul, city, education, friendship, and citizenship.
- Distinguish a character's position from the author's likely instructional design.
- Compare ancient literary, philosophical, and moral forms without treating them as one genre.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Dialogue, interlocutor, dialectic, comedy, satire, irony, treatise, virtue, vice, justice, law, city, soul, regime, citizen, rhetoric, education, biography, exemplar, moral contrast, meditation, Stoic, self-command, providence.

### Prerequisites

Students need Unit 1 orientation and evidence habits, basic ancient Greek and Roman context, and willingness to reread a passage whose speaker and conclusion may be indirect.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit applies Unit 1 tools to ancient texts and supplies recurring questions for the rest of the level. Later Christian, Renaissance, modern civic, economic, dramatic, and scientific texts will revise, reject, inherit, or transform ancient ideas of virtue, city, law, nature, education, and self-command.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Dialogue is just a transcript of the author's opinion.
- Comedy should be read only literally.
- Ancient moral examples are simple hero worship.
- The conclusion of an argument is all that matters.
- Stoic meditation is the same as detached indifference.
- Context replaces close attention to the words.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

5-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Review Unit 1 categories through ancient material. Keep dialogue/speaker distinction active when later reading confession, essay, drama, and manifesto. Revisit ancient justice, virtue, and city questions in Units 3, 5, and 6.

## Unit 3: Scripture, Confession, And The Inner Life

### Unit Purpose

Read selected biblical and Augustinian texts as literary, theological, historical, and philosophical works that reshape classical questions of self, city, time, memory, love, conversion, and community. The unit emphasizes respectful textual understanding and genre-sensitive interpretation.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify genre differences among gospel narrative, parable, acts, epistle, confession, and theological-philosophical reflection.
- Explain how narrative, image, memory, conversion, and inward conflict shape meaning.
- Distinguish literary-theological interpretation from devotional assertion or dismissal.
- Compare inner life, city, law, and love with ancient virtue and civic questions.
- Use compact evidence to support interpretations of figurative and reflective passages.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Gospel, parable, acts, epistle, confession, conversion, memory, will, love, time, creation, city, pilgrimage, literal sense, figurative language, theological claim, inward conflict, narrative movement, spiritual autobiography.

### Prerequisites

Students need Unit 1 reading routine, Unit 2 ancient questions, and basic familiarity with biblical genres and late antique context. They should be able to approach religious texts respectfully as texts without turning the course into devotional or anti-devotional instruction.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit inherits ancient questions of virtue, law, city, and soul, then prepares later work on conscience, inwardness, custom, self-examination, political order, and modern identity. Augustine's introspective form anticipates Montaigne, Shakespearean inward conflict, Ibsen, and modern reflection on selfhood.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Religious texts can only be read devotionally or dismissively.
- A parable is merely a simple moral slogan.
- Confession is only autobiography and not argument.
- Inner conflict is private feeling without philosophical stakes.
- Context or doctrine alone can replace close reading.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Review Unit 2 questions of virtue, law, city, and soul while introducing conversion, love, memory, and time. Space speaker and genre checks carefully because biblical and confessional forms invite quick misclassification.

## Unit 4: Renaissance Self, State, Satire, And Stage

### Unit Purpose

Explore Renaissance and early modern texts that complicate inherited moral and civic categories through statecraft, custom, skepticism, satire, essayistic self-examination, and drama. Students learn to read prudential argument, irony, grotesque exaggeration, soliloquy, character conflict, and tragic consequence.

### Student Outcomes

- Interpret political counsel without assuming it is either simple endorsement or simple villainy.
- Recognize essayistic self-examination, skeptical testing, satire, grotesque exaggeration, dramatic irony, and soliloquy.
- Explain how form changes the treatment of ambition, custom, education, conscience, and power.
- Compare ancient virtue and Christian inwardness with early modern selfhood and statecraft.
- Diagnose literal readings of irony, satire, and dramatic speech.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Prince, republic, fortune, necessity, prudence, counsel, custom, skepticism, essay, self-examination, satire, grotesque, dramatic irony, soliloquy, tragedy, comedy, character foil, motive, ambition, conscience, appearance and reality.

### Prerequisites

Students need stable genre and speaker awareness, ancient and Christian inner-life questions, and basic early modern context around monarchy, republics, humanism, religious tension, and theater.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit revisits ancient city and virtue questions under new political and literary pressure. It prepares modern civic and economic texts by sharpening attention to power, consent, public speech, private motive, custom, and social role. It also prepares Ibsen by showing how drama reveals ideas through action.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Machiavellian analysis is identical with moral approval.
- Satire says exactly what its surface voice says.
- Essays are random opinions rather than shaped experiments in judgment.
- A dramatic character's speech is the play's direct thesis.
- Historical strangeness makes a text irrelevant.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Interleave ancient virtue, Christian inwardness, and Unit 1 speaker checks. Later modern units should return to statecraft, custom, public opinion, social role, and appearance/reality as repeated questions.

## Unit 5: Liberty, Democracy, Economy, And Critique

### Unit Purpose

Study modern civic, political, economic, and critical prose through questions of rights, consent, constitutional order, equality, democracy, commerce, labor, revolution, and civil society. Students learn to read arguments by tracking definitions, assumptions, audience, evidence, objections, and consequences.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify claims, reasons, assumptions, examples, definitions, objections, and audience in modern argument.
- Compare rights, consent, law, constitution, equality, democracy, commerce, labor, and critique across authors.
- Distinguish founding argument, democratic analysis, economic explanation, and manifesto rhetoric.
- Evaluate arguments with textual evidence before applying them to present-day opinion.
- Explain how modern texts transform ancient and early modern questions of virtue, city, order, and freedom.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Natural rights, consent, social contract, constitution, faction, federalism, separation of powers, democracy, equality, mores, commerce, division of labor, self-interest, civil society, ideology, labor, alienation, class, manifesto, revolution, reform, audience.

### Prerequisites

Students need the Unit 1 argument and evidence routine, ancient and early modern city/state questions, and basic historical context for Enlightenment political thought, the American founding, democratic society, commercial life, and industrial critique.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit explicitly compares modern liberty with ancient virtue, constitutional design with regime questions, economic explanation with justice, and critique with earlier moral and political forms. It prepares the final capstone by giving students several high-stakes prose arguments to synthesize with literary and philosophical texts.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Political texts can be read only as current opinion.
- A conclusion is the whole argument.
- Rights, equality, democracy, and economy mean the same thing in every author.
- Economic explanation is automatically moral endorsement.
- Manifesto rhetoric should be treated as neutral analysis.
- Present-day agreement or disagreement can replace textual understanding.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

5-7 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Review speaker, audience, genre, claim/reason/objection, and ancient-to-modern comparisons. Interleave earlier questions of justice, law, virtue, city, custom, and human nature so modern texts are not isolated from the level's capstone map.

## Unit 6: Modern Drama, Science, And Capstone Synthesis

### Unit Purpose

Bring the level to synthesis through modern drama, scientific-humanistic reflection, and cross-era comparison. Students examine social pressure, selfhood, responsibility, scientific models of life, and the limits of disciplinary explanation before completing compact capstone transfer tasks.

### Student Outcomes

- Read modern drama as action, speech, conflict, social pressure, and staged consequence.
- Interpret scientific-humanistic reflection as a form that crosses disciplinary boundaries.
- Compare older questions of nature, life, duty, self-command, freedom, and the good with modern forms.
- Build a synthesis map connecting authors, forms, periods, questions, and evidence.
- Apply the reading routine to an unfamiliar excerpt and answer a focused comparative question.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Realism, social pressure, public respectability, private motive, responsibility, duty, selfhood, scientific model, life, reductionism, interdisciplinarity, analogy, evidence, limits of explanation, synthesis, transfer, comparison matrix, capstone claim.

### Prerequisites

Students need all prior units: seminar tools, ancient and Christian questions, early modern drama and statecraft, and modern civic/economic argument. They should be ready to compare without collapsing texts into a single answer.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This is the final unit, so it gathers the level's recurring questions rather than preparing a later unit. It connects modern drama with Shakespeare, scientific reflection with ancient and Christian nature questions, and capstone synthesis with every prior genre and period.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Modern means automatically more relevant or more correct.
- Drama is reducible to a lesson statement.
- Science and humanities ask unrelated questions.
- Synthesis means naming similarities while ignoring differences.
- A comparison claim can stand without precise evidence.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use cumulative retrieval of authors, forms, speaker roles, argument moves, and recurring questions. Final transfer should include unfamiliar or recombined passages so mastery is not just recall of earlier items.
