# Classical Literature 5 Lesson Briefs

Sources: accepted level blueprint, course map, and unit design briefs in `research/classical-literature-5/`.

## Unit 1: Seminar Tools For Difficult Classics

### Lesson 1: Orient The Text Before You Judge

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students a first-pass routine for difficult primary texts.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify form, speaker, audience, situation, and central question before judging a passage.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Orientation card: Who is speaking? What form is this? To whom? In what situation? What problem or question is active?
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic literary terms, prior experience with excerpts, and willingness to reread.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Difficulty means failure; context alone gives meaning; the fastest opinion is the best answer.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a short passage description, the student selects accurate orientation details and chooses a responsible first reading move.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `multiple-choice` for first-move decisions; `match-pairs` for orientation categories; `passage-question` for applying the routine to a compact excerpt.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Before you judge a difficult classic, orient yourself. Ask who is speaking, what form you are reading, who the audience is, what situation is present, and what question the passage is trying to face."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep passages short and use one orientation layer at a time before combining all categories.

### Lesson 2: Summary, Interpretation, Evaluation

- **Teaching goal:** Separate three response jobs students often collapse.
- **Student outcome:** The student can classify a sentence as summary, interpretation, evaluation, or unsupported opinion.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Response ladder: summary says what the text says; interpretation explains meaning; evaluation judges value or truth after understanding.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Ability to paraphrase a passage and cite a phrase.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Interpretation is personal reaction; evaluation can happen before understanding; summary includes judgment words.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies response types and revises an overreaching response into a better-supported one.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `multiple-choice` for classifying response jobs; `order-items` for sequencing reading moves; `error-correction` for finite revisions of unsupported claims.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "A strong reader does not do every job at once. First summarize what the passage says. Then interpret what it means. After that, evaluate or compare with reasons."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use brief responses, not full essays; avoid abstract terminology without examples.

### Lesson 3: Speaker, Genre, And Form

- **Teaching goal:** Build caution around author, speaker, narrator, character, and form.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain why genre and speaker affect interpretation.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Speaker-form map: author creates a form; a speaker, narrator, or character may not state the author's own view.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic genre awareness and Unit 1 orientation categories.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Every sentence in a text is the author's direct opinion; genre labels are trivia; dramatic speech is the play's thesis.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches forms to interpretive cautions and identifies a speaker-author confusion.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `match-pairs` for forms and cautions; `multiple-choice` for diagnosing speaker confusion; `dialogue-builder` for choosing a careful seminar reply.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "In classics, voice matters. A dialogue has interlocutors, a play has characters, a satire may exaggerate, and an essay may test an idea. Do not rush from 'someone says this' to 'the author believes this.'"
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar form names first; do not require detailed author biography.

### Lesson 4: Evidence That Carries A Claim

- **Teaching goal:** Practice choosing textual evidence that actually supports an interpretation.
- **Student outcome:** The student can select evidence, reject weak evidence, and connect evidence to a claim.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Evidence-fit chart: claim, quoted or paraphrased detail, explanation of how the detail supports the claim.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Summary, interpretation, speaker awareness, and basic citation habits.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Any quote counts; evidence is decoration; longer evidence is automatically stronger; context can replace passage words.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses the best evidence for a claim and identifies why another option does not support it.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `passage-question` for evidence selection; `multiple-choice` for best explanation; `order-items` for claim-evidence-reasoning sequence.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Evidence carries a claim when it points to the exact reason your interpretation makes sense. A strong answer connects claim, passage detail, and explanation."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep claims narrow and evidence options short; require one connection at a time.

## Unit 2: Ancient Questions Of Justice, Virtue, And The City

### Lesson 1: Socratic Dialogue And The Examined Question

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to track a dialogue as a sequence of questions, answers, definitions, and revisions.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a Socratic question, an interlocutor's claim, and the turn that changes the discussion.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Dialogue turn chart with speaker, claim, question, problem, and revision.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Unit 1 speaker and evidence tools; basic familiarity with Plato as dialogue.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Dialogue is a simple answer key; Socrates' question is the same as a final doctrine; every speaker has equal authority.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student can summarize a short dialogue movement and select the question that exposes a weak definition.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `dialogue-builder` for selecting the next careful question; `order-items` for dialogue movement; `passage-question` for close reading of a compact exchange.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "A Socratic dialogue often moves by testing definitions. Watch who speaks, what definition is offered, what question presses it, and how the answer changes."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short exchanges and paraphrased context; keep Greek terms out unless defined in the prompt.

### Lesson 2: Comedy, Satire, And The Educated City

- **Teaching goal:** Help students read comic exaggeration as a way of raising civic and educational questions.
- **Student outcome:** The student can distinguish literal surface from satiric target in an ancient comedy-style passage.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Satire chart: surface claim, exaggeration, target, deeper question.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Speaker-form caution, basic ancient city context, and comfort with indirect meaning.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Comedy is not serious; satire means the text literally endorses the ridiculous claim; laughter cancels interpretation.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies an exaggeration and names the civic or educational question it exposes.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `multiple-choice` for satire target; `match-pairs` for surface and deeper meaning; `passage-question` for comic detail.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Comedy can think by exaggerating. Ask what looks absurd on the surface and what serious question the absurdity points toward."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid unframed adult material; use clean, bounded comic situations.

### Lesson 3: Aristotle On Virtue, Habit, And Friendship

- **Teaching goal:** Introduce virtue as trained habit and practical judgment rather than simple rule following.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain how habit, choice, mean, and friendship function in an Aristotelian-style passage.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Virtue map: repeated action shapes character; judgment seeks the fitting action in a concrete situation.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Unit 2 justice and education questions; ability to track definition and example.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Virtue is only feeling nice; the mean is always the middle amount; friendship is only private preference.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects the best explanation of virtue or friendship from a short passage and rejects a mechanical reading.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `multiple-choice` for conceptual distinctions; `fill-blank` for key terms in a model sentence; `passage-question` for applying definition to example.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "For Aristotle, virtue grows through habit and judgment. The good action is not random and not mechanical; it fits the person, the situation, and the purpose."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Define specialized terms in the intro; use one concept per question before comparison.

### Lesson 4: Plutarch And The Moral Example

- **Teaching goal:** Teach biography as moral comparison and attention to character, not simple hero worship.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify how a biographical anecdote invites moral judgment through contrast and detail.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Exemplar table: action, motive, consequence, contrasting example, question for the reader.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Ancient virtue vocabulary and evidence-fit habits.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A famous life is automatically admirable; one anecdote proves everything; biography is only fact collection.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student explains what a biographical detail is meant to reveal and avoids overgeneralizing from it.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `passage-question` for detail interpretation; `match-pairs` for action and moral issue; `multiple-choice` for avoiding overclaim.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "A moral biography asks you to notice actions, motives, and consequences. The point is not only what happened, but what the example helps you examine."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short anecdotes and clearly name the contrast under study.

### Lesson 5: Stoic Meditation And Self-Command

- **Teaching goal:** Read Stoic meditation as a disciplined practice of judgment, attention, and desire.
- **Student outcome:** The student can distinguish Stoic self-command from apathy or lack of love.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Control distinction: what is up to us, what is not, and how judgment shapes response.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Ancient virtue and moral reflection; ability to summarize aphoristic prose.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Stoicism means not caring; a short maxim needs no interpretation; self-command is selfish isolation.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the judgment a Stoic-style meditation asks the reader to practice.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `fill-blank` for control distinction; `multiple-choice` for misconception diagnosis; `passage-question` for interpreting an aphorism.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "A Stoic meditation often asks: What is up to me? What is not? What judgment can I correct before I act?"
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep aphorisms short; avoid making students evaluate the whole philosophy at once.

## Unit 3: Scripture, Confession, And The Inner Life

### Lesson 1: Gospel Narrative, Parable, And Meaning

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to read narrative and parable as forms that invite interpretation.
- **Student outcome:** The student can distinguish story surface, image, conflict, and deeper question in a gospel or parable-style passage.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Parable reading frame: story situation, surprising turn, image, interpretive question.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Genre awareness, evidence selection, and respectful handling of religious texts.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A parable is a flat moral slogan; religious meaning removes the need for literary reading; images are decorative.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the turn in a parable and chooses an interpretation supported by story details.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `passage-question` for story evidence; `order-items` for narrative movement; `multiple-choice` for interpretation vs slogan.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "A parable is a short story that presses a question. Notice the situation, the surprising turn, the image, and what the story asks the listener to reconsider."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use brief, respectful passages and avoid doctrinal disputes beyond textual interpretation.

### Lesson 2: Acts, Epistles, And Communities In Argument

- **Teaching goal:** Show how early community texts reason through belief, practice, conflict, and counsel.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify audience, issue, claim, reason, and counsel in an acts or epistle-style passage.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Community argument map: audience, problem, claim, reason, counsel, hoped-for action.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Claim/evidence vocabulary and basic awareness of epistle as addressed writing.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Epistles are private notes without argument; counsel is merely command; audience does not matter.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects the claim and audience for a short passage and explains why the counsel fits the problem.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `match-pairs` for argument parts; `passage-question` for claim and audience; `multi-blank-cloze` for completing an argument map.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Community writings often answer a real problem. Ask who is addressed, what issue is being faced, what claim is made, and what action is urged."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep historical background minimal and relevant; define community terms in the prompt.

### Lesson 3: Augustine's Confession Of Memory And Will

- **Teaching goal:** Read confession as shaped inquiry into self, memory, desire, and will.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain how a confessional passage moves from personal memory to a larger question.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Confession movement: remembered event, inward conflict, address to God or reader, interpretive lesson.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Unit 2 self-command and Unit 3 genre sensitivity.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Confession is just autobiography; memory is neutral record; inward conflict is only emotion.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies a shift from event to reflection and chooses the best explanation of will or desire.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `order-items` for confessional movement; `passage-question` for inner conflict; `multiple-choice` for genre and purpose.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "In confession, a remembered event can become a question about the self. Watch how the writer moves from what happened to what it reveals about memory, desire, or will."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use compact passages and explain theological vocabulary when it appears.

### Lesson 4: Two Cities And Ordered Loves

- **Teaching goal:** Connect inner life, love, community, and historical imagination.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain the idea that communities are shaped by what they love and order most.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Ordered-loves map: object of love, order of priority, kind of community, resulting action.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Augustine's confessional inwardness, ancient city questions, and evidence selection.
- **Likely misconceptions:** The two cities are only geographic places; love is only emotion; private inward life has no civic consequence.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches loves, communities, and consequences, then compares the idea with ancient city questions.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `match-pairs` for loves and consequences; `multiple-choice` for misconception diagnosis; `passage-question` for applying the model.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "A community can be described by what it loves most. Ordered loves ask what comes first, what serves it, and what kind of city or life results."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep comparisons narrow; avoid asking for full theological history.

## Unit 4: Renaissance Self, State, Satire, And Stage

### Lesson 1: Machiavelli: Counsel, Fortune, And Necessity

- **Teaching goal:** Read political advice through problem, audience, fortune, necessity, and consequence.
- **Student outcome:** The student can distinguish analysis of power from simple moral endorsement or dismissal.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Statecraft problem chart: ruler's problem, unstable condition, counsel, risk, consequence.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Ancient city questions, speaker/audience awareness, and claim-reason mapping.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Machiavelli's advice is either pure villainy or plain common sense; audience and situation do not matter.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the problem a piece of counsel answers and avoids collapsing analysis into approval.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `passage-question` for counsel and audience; `multiple-choice` for endorsement vs analysis; `match-pairs` for terms and meanings.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Political counsel often answers a pressured problem. Before judging it, ask: Who is advised? What danger is present? What action is recommended? What consequence is expected?"
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use bounded excerpts and avoid treating the lesson as modern political debate.

### Lesson 2: Montaigne And The Essay As Trial

- **Teaching goal:** Teach the essay as a shaped experiment in judgment.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify how an essay tests a thought, shifts perspective, or qualifies a claim.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Essay trial map: starting question, example, turn, qualification, provisional judgment.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Summary vs interpretation, claim/evidence, and early modern self-examination context.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Essay means random opinion; uncertainty means weakness; a qualified claim has no point.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student tracks a turn in an essay-style passage and explains why the qualification matters.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `order-items` for essay movement; `passage-question` for interpretive turn; `multiple-choice` for qualified claim.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "An essay can try an idea rather than simply announce a conclusion. Watch for examples, turns, qualifications, and a judgment that remains thoughtful instead of final too soon."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep passages short and signal when the writer is testing rather than concluding.

### Lesson 3: Rabelais, Satire, And Education

- **Teaching goal:** Read grotesque humor and excess as satire of learning, appetite, and social forms.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a satiric target behind an exaggerated educational or social scene.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Exaggeration-to-target chart: absurd image, inflated behavior, target, serious question.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Ancient comedy lesson, early modern context, and caution around satire.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Grotesque humor has no serious target; satire endorses whatever it describes; strange style makes interpretation impossible.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student recognizes excess and names the practice or assumption being questioned.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `multiple-choice` for target; `match-pairs` for exaggeration and critique; `passage-question` for supported interpretation.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Satire may use excess to make a question visible. Ask what is exaggerated, what habit or institution is being mocked, and what better possibility the comedy hints at."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use maturity-screened excerpts and avoid unframed adult or sexual satire.

### Lesson 4: Shakespearean Inwardness And Consequence

- **Teaching goal:** Analyze soliloquy, motive, action, and dramatic irony.
- **Student outcome:** The student can connect a dramatic speech to motive, choice, and consequence without treating it as the play's whole thesis.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Scene-turn map: speaker, desire, fear, decision, what audience knows, consequence.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Speaker/author distinction, tragedy and comedy basics, and Unit 3 inward conflict.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A soliloquy is direct author commentary; motive is the same as excuse; one speech explains the whole play.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies a speaker's inner conflict and chooses the consequence that follows from the dramatic situation.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `passage-question` for speech and motive; `order-items` for scene movement; `multiple-choice` for dramatic irony.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Drama reveals thought through speech and action. A soliloquy may show motive or conflict, but the play also tests that speech through choices and consequences."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short speeches or paraphrased scenes; define dramatic irony when used.

## Unit 5: Liberty, Democracy, Economy, And Critique

### Lesson 1: Locke, Rousseau, And The Problem Of Consent

- **Teaching goal:** Compare consent, freedom, political beginning, and social contract language.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify how two modern political arguments define consent or freedom differently.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Term comparison chart: key term, author's definition, problem addressed, consequence.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Claim/reason mapping, ancient and early modern political questions.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Consent and freedom mean the same thing in every text; social contract is a literal signed document in all arguments.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches a definition or consequence to the correct argument and explains the difference.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `match-pairs` for terms and definitions; `passage-question` for author-specific meaning; `multiple-choice` for comparison.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Modern political writers often use the same words differently. When you see consent or freedom, ask what problem the writer is solving and how the term is defined."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Compare only one or two terms at a time; avoid present-day policy debates.

### Lesson 2: Founding Arguments And Constitutional Design

- **Teaching goal:** Track audience, faction, rights, and institutional reasoning in founding-era argument.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify a constitutional problem and the design feature proposed to answer it.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Design argument map: problem, proposed structure, reason, risk reduced, audience.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Consent vocabulary, civic argument mapping, and basic founding context.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A founding document is self-explanatory; institutional design is trivia; slogans replace reasons.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student matches a civic problem to a design response and selects evidence that explains the connection.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `match-pairs` for problems and design features; `passage-question` for claim and audience; `multi-blank-cloze` for argument-map completion.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "A constitutional argument often says: here is a danger, here is a structure, and here is why the structure helps. Track the problem before judging the design."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Define civic terms in prompts; keep institutional details limited to the passage's argument.

### Lesson 3: Tocqueville On Democracy And Equality

- **Teaching goal:** Read democratic analysis as diagnosis rather than simple praise or complaint.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain how equality can produce both strengths and risks in a Tocqueville-style passage.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Democratic diagnosis chart: condition, benefit, danger, habit or institution that moderates it.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Modern political vocabulary and distinction between analysis and evaluation.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Democracy is discussed only to praise or condemn; equality has one simple effect; diagnosis means personal preference.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies a benefit and risk from the same social condition and chooses a balanced explanation.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `passage-question` for balanced interpretation; `multiple-choice` for analysis vs opinion; `match-pairs` for condition and effect.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "A diagnosis can name strengths and dangers at the same time. When reading about democracy and equality, ask what condition is being described and what mixed effects follow."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use controlled excerpts and avoid requiring broad American history beyond the passage.

### Lesson 4: Smith On Commerce, Sympathy, And Explanation

- **Teaching goal:** Distinguish economic and moral explanation from endorsement or command.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify how a Smith-style passage explains coordination, self-interest, sympathy, or commerce.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Explanation frame: observed behavior, mechanism, social result, moral caution.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Argument mapping and awareness that explanation is not automatically approval.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Self-interest always means selfishness; explaining a market process means endorsing every outcome; sympathy is irrelevant to economic thought.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student selects the mechanism a passage uses and avoids confusing explanation with praise.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `passage-question` for mechanism; `multiple-choice` for misconception diagnosis; `fill-blank` for explanation frame.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "An explanation tells how something works. It may also invite moral questions, but do not skip the how. Ask what behavior is observed, what mechanism is named, and what result follows."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep examples concrete; do not require technical economics.

### Lesson 5: Marx And Engels As Critique And Manifesto

- **Teaching goal:** Read critique, class language, and manifesto rhetoric with genre awareness.
- **Student outcome:** The student can distinguish diagnosis, persuasion, prediction, and call to action in a manifesto-style passage.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Manifesto map: grievance, explanation, audience, enemy or obstacle, prediction, demand.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Unit 5 argument skills, satire/polemic caution, and basic industrial context.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Critique is mere complaint; manifesto rhetoric is neutral analysis; agreement or disagreement replaces reading.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the rhetorical job of a sentence and explains how the genre shapes tone and claim.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `match-pairs` for rhetorical moves; `passage-question` for claim and audience; `order-items` for critique-to-call movement.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "A manifesto is written to persuade and mobilize. Track what problem it names, how it explains the problem, who it addresses, and what action it urges."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep political controversy framed as textual analysis; avoid asking students to endorse or reject the ideology.

## Unit 6: Modern Drama, Science, And Capstone Synthesis

### Lesson 1: Ibsen And The Modern Social Stage

- **Teaching goal:** Read realism through public pressure, private motive, and staged consequence.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify how a modern dramatic scene reveals social norms and inner conflict.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Social stage map: public expectation, private desire or fear, speech, action, consequence.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Shakespearean drama, modern social critique, and speaker/character caution.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A play's message is one character's speech; social pressure is just background; private motive excuses public action.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student connects dialogue detail to social pressure and consequence.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `passage-question` for scene evidence; `multiple-choice` for social pressure diagnosis; `order-items` for scene movement.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "Modern drama often shows pressure through ordinary speech. Ask what society expects, what the character wants or fears, and how action changes the situation."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short scenes and name the social norm under study when needed.

### Lesson 2: Schrodinger, Life, And Scientific Reflection

- **Teaching goal:** Interpret scientific reflection as a cross-disciplinary argument about life, models, and limits.
- **Student outcome:** The student can distinguish observation, model, analogy, and philosophical question in a scientific-humanistic passage.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Reflection frame: scientific problem, model or analogy, limit, larger question.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Argument mapping and experience comparing forms.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Scientific reflection is only fact reporting; humanities questions are irrelevant to science; analogy is proof by itself.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student identifies the role of an analogy or model and explains what larger question it opens.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `match-pairs` for argument roles; `passage-question` for model and limit; `multiple-choice` for analogy function.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "A scientific reflection may explain a model and then ask what the model can or cannot answer. Notice the problem, the analogy, the limit, and the larger question."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid technical science beyond the passage; define model, analogy, and reductionism.

### Lesson 3: Cross-Era Comparison Maps

- **Teaching goal:** Build responsible comparisons across form, period, and recurring question.
- **Student outcome:** The student can compare two texts by naming shared question, difference in form, and evidence from each.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Comparison matrix: shared question, Text A claim or image, Text B claim or image, difference in form, evidence from both.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** All previous genre, evidence, and recurring question work.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Synthesis means vague similarity; difference weakens comparison; one text's evidence is enough.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student completes a comparison map and chooses the most defensible comparative claim.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `multi-blank-cloze` for comparison map; `match-pairs` for shared questions; `multiple-choice` for best comparative claim.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "A strong comparison needs both connection and difference. Name the shared question, show evidence from each text, and explain how form or context changes the answer."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Limit comparisons to two texts or forms at a time; provide the recurring question explicitly.

### Lesson 4: Capstone Transfer: A Defensible Reading

- **Teaching goal:** Apply the full reading routine to an unfamiliar excerpt and support a focused claim.
- **Student outcome:** The student can orient, summarize, interpret, choose evidence, and compare a new passage in a compact transfer task.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Full routine: orient, summarize, identify form and speaker, choose evidence, make a narrow claim, compare responsibly.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Completion of Units 1-6.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Transfer means guessing from background knowledge; a capstone claim can be broad and unsupported; comparison can ignore form.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses the best orientation, summary, evidence, and comparative claim for a new passage.
- **Recommended question types and why:** `passage-question` for transfer reading; `order-items` for full routine; `multiple-choice` for defensible claim and evidence.
- **Student-facing intro for `config.intro`:** "For the capstone, use the whole routine. Orient the passage, summarize before judging, notice form and speaker, choose evidence, and make a claim that the passage can support."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one unfamiliar compact excerpt; test precision, not endurance.
