# Unit 2: Ancient Questions Of Justice, Virtue, And The City

Track: Classical Literature
Level: Level 5
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/classical-literature-5/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn to read selected ancient forms as different ways of examining justice, education, virtue, law, citizenship, friendship, and self-command. They practice tracking dialogue, comedy, treatise, biography, and meditation without reducing them to one kind of argument.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

Ancient texts supply the level's first major question set and give later authors something to inherit, revise, reject, or transform. The unit follows the tools unit because students now need those tools in more demanding forms.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: examined life, dialectical questioning, comic inversion, virtue as habit, moral exemplar, city and soul, self-command.
- Vocabulary: dialogue, interlocutor, dialectic, comedy, satire, irony, treatise, virtue, vice, justice, law, city, soul, regime, citizen, rhetoric, education, biography, exemplar, moral contrast, meditation, Stoic, providence.
- Representations and models: dialogue turn chart, comic surface/deeper question chart, virtue-habit map, exemplar evidence table, meditation claim-practice frame.
- Texts and forms: bounded selections from Plato, Aristophanes, Aristotle, Plutarch, Seneca, and Marcus Aurelius as available in the local corpus.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need Unit 1 orientation, summary, speaker, form, and evidence skills. They should know basic ancient Greek and Roman setting terms and be ready for translated prose whose claims unfold indirectly.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Socratic dialogue is just a list of answers.
- Comedy is only entertainment and not intellectual work.
- Ancient virtue means being conventionally nice.
- Biography teaches by perfect heroes instead of moral contrasts.
- Stoic self-command means not caring about anything.
- A character's or interlocutor's speech automatically equals the author's conclusion.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Start with dialogue because it foregrounds questioning and speaker tracking. Move to comedy to complicate literal reading and show how laughter can expose civic and educational problems. Then study Aristotle to give students a more direct conceptual account of virtue and habit. Plutarch follows as a bridge from concept to moral example. Stoic meditation closes the unit by shifting from public city questions to inner discipline.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Every lesson reviews Unit 1 orientation and evidence-fit habits. Dialogue and comedy especially retrieve speaker/author distinction; Aristotle and Stoic meditation retrieve summary before evaluation; Plutarch retrieves the need to choose evidence rather than admire generally.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

The unit prepares Unit 3 questions of soul, city, law, and inward life; Unit 4 questions of education, statecraft, satire, and ambition; Unit 5 debates over justice, law, liberty, and political order; and Unit 6 synthesis tasks around self-command, duty, and the good life.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify how ancient form shapes interpretation.
- Track a dialogue or argument turn.
- Recognize irony and exaggeration in comedy.
- Explain virtue as habit, practice, and judgment.
- Distinguish exemplar evidence from simple hero worship.
- Connect Stoic meditation to self-command without reducing it to indifference.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Socratic Dialogue And The Examined Question**: Track questions, interlocutors, and argument movement.
2. **Comedy, Satire, And The Educated City**: Read comic exaggeration as civic and educational critique.
3. **Aristotle On Virtue, Habit, And Friendship**: Study virtue as trained judgment rather than mere rule following.
4. **Plutarch And The Moral Example**: Analyze biography as comparison, contrast, and moral attention.
5. **Stoic Meditation And Self-Command**: Read aphoristic reflection as a practice for judgment and desire.
