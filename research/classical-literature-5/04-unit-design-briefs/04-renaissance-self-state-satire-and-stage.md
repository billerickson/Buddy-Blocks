# Unit 4: Renaissance Self, State, Satire, And Stage

Track: Classical Literature
Level: Level 5
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/classical-literature-5/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students interpret Renaissance and early modern texts that test inherited ideas about virtue, power, custom, education, selfhood, and dramatic action. They learn to read political counsel, essayistic skepticism, satire, and theater without confusing surface voice with final meaning.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

The unit follows ancient and Christian inheritance because early modern authors often write under pressure from both. It broadens students' form knowledge before modern civic and economic arguments, while drama and satire sharpen the speaker-awareness needed for final synthesis.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: prudence, necessity, fortune, custom, skepticism, satire as critique, dramatic inwardness, appearance and reality, ambition, conscience.
- Vocabulary: prince, republic, fortune, necessity, prudence, counsel, custom, skepticism, essay, self-examination, satire, grotesque, dramatic irony, soliloquy, tragedy, comedy, character foil, motive, ambition, conscience.
- Representations and models: statecraft problem chart, essay trial map, satire surface/deeper target chart, scene-turn chart, motive and consequence map.
- Texts and forms: selected passages from Machiavelli, Montaigne, Rabelais, and Shakespeare as available in the local corpus, with careful maturity screening for satire.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need genre, speaker, and evidence habits; ancient questions of virtue and city; and Christian/Augustinian questions of inwardness, conscience, and ordered loves. They also need introductory early modern context around monarchy, republics, humanism, religious conflict, and theater.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Machiavelli is either simply evil or simply realistic, so close reading is unnecessary.
- Essay means casual opinion.
- Satire should be taken literally.
- Grotesque exaggeration is pointless weirdness.
- A character's most famous speech gives the play's whole meaning.
- Renaissance selfhood is completely modern and detached from older moral questions.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with Machiavelli because statecraft reframes ancient virtue and civic order under political necessity. Move to Montaigne to slow down judgment and show essay as testing. Rabelais follows as an intentionally difficult satire of learning, appetite, and social forms. Shakespeare closes the unit because drama synthesizes speech, motive, action, irony, and consequence.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

The unit reviews ancient city and virtue questions, Christian inwardness, speaker/author caution, and evidence selection. It repeatedly asks students to separate what a voice says from what the work as a form is doing.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

The unit prepares modern political argument by raising questions of power, counsel, law, consent, and public order. It prepares economic and social critique by developing attention to custom and social role. It prepares the final drama lesson through Shakespearean motive, speech, and consequence.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Interpret political counsel as argument in context.
- Identify essayistic testing rather than random opinion.
- Recognize satire, grotesque exaggeration, and ironic distance.
- Track dramatic speech, motive, and consequence.
- Compare early modern selfhood and power with ancient and Christian frameworks.
- Diagnose literal readings of indirect forms.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Machiavelli: Counsel, Fortune, And Necessity**: Read political advice through problem, audience, and consequence.
2. **Montaigne And The Essay As Trial**: Treat the essay as a shaped experiment in judgment.
3. **Rabelais, Satire, And Education**: Read grotesque humor as a way to question learning and appetite.
4. **Shakespearean Inwardness And Consequence**: Analyze soliloquy, motive, action, and dramatic irony.
