# Unit 5: Liberty, Democracy, Economy, And Critique

Track: Classical Literature
Level: Level 5
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/classical-literature-5/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students read modern civic, political, economic, and critical prose as arguments with definitions, assumptions, audiences, evidence, objections, and consequences. They compare modern liberty, democracy, economy, and critique with earlier questions of justice, virtue, city, custom, and human nature.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit follows the early modern state and self because modern political and economic texts intensify those questions in arguments about consent, rights, constitutional design, equality, commerce, labor, and revolution. It also provides major comparison material for the final capstone.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: consent, rights, constitutional order, democratic equality, social conditions, commerce, sympathy, economic explanation, labor, alienation, critique, manifesto rhetoric.
- Vocabulary: natural rights, consent, social contract, constitution, faction, federalism, separation of powers, democracy, equality, mores, commerce, division of labor, self-interest, civil society, ideology, labor, alienation, class, manifesto, revolution, reform, audience.
- Representations and models: argument anatomy chart, audience and occasion card, comparison matrix, claim-reason-objection map, term shift chart, critique vs explanation table.
- Texts and forms: selected passages from Locke, Rousseau, American founding documents or Federalist arguments, Smith, Tocqueville, Marx and Engels, with the local corpus determining exact editions.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need Unit 1 argument and evidence habits, ancient and early modern city/state questions, and introductory context for Enlightenment political thought, founding-era debate, democracy, commercial society, and industrial critique.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Present-day political preference is the same as interpretation.
- Rights, consent, equality, democracy, and liberty mean the same thing in every text.
- A founding document or manifesto should be read without audience or occasion.
- Economic explanation is automatically moral endorsement.
- Critique means mere complaint.
- The conclusion of an argument is enough; reasons and assumptions do not matter.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with Locke and Rousseau because consent and social contract vocabulary frame later civic arguments. Move to founding arguments and constitutional design to study institutions and audience. Tocqueville follows as democratic analysis rather than founding advocacy. Smith then broadens civic reading into commerce and social explanation. Marx and Engels close the unit with critique and manifesto rhetoric, forcing students to distinguish diagnosis, persuasion, and prediction.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

The unit reviews ancient justice, law, regime, and virtue; early modern statecraft; and Unit 1 claim/evidence skills. It also recalls satire and indirect voice when students encounter polemic or manifesto rhetoric.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

The unit prepares final synthesis by giving students modern prose arguments to compare with ancient, Christian, Renaissance, dramatic, and scientific-humanistic texts. It also prepares Ibsen through social pressure, public respectability, and civic norms.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify claims, reasons, assumptions, objections, and audience.
- Distinguish definitions of liberty, equality, consent, democracy, commerce, and labor across authors.
- Compare civic, economic, and critical forms.
- Use evidence before present-day application.
- Diagnose anachronism, slogan reading, and argument flattening.
- Explain how modern authors transform earlier questions.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Locke, Rousseau, And The Problem Of Consent**: Compare consent, freedom, and political beginning.
2. **Founding Arguments And Constitutional Design**: Track audience, faction, rights, and institutional reasoning.
3. **Tocqueville On Democracy And Equality**: Read democratic analysis as diagnosis, not simple celebration or complaint.
4. **Smith On Commerce, Sympathy, And Explanation**: Distinguish economic explanation from moral endorsement.
5. **Marx And Engels As Critique And Manifesto**: Read critique, class language, and manifesto rhetoric with care.
