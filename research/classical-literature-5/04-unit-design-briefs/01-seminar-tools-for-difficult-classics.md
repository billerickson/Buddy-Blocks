# Unit 1: Seminar Tools For Difficult Classics

Track: Classical Literature
Level: Level 5
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/classical-literature-5/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students establish the reading, discussion, and evidence habits they will use throughout the level. By the end of the unit, they can orient a difficult passage, separate summary from interpretation and evaluation, distinguish author from speaker, and choose evidence that supports a focused claim.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

The Level 5 corpus is dense, varied, and historically distant. Beginning with a tools unit reduces cognitive load before students meet dialogue, comedy, confession, civic argument, drama, manifesto, and scientific reflection. It also gives later lessons a shared vocabulary for feedback.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: text orientation, difficulty as normal, summary before judgment, speaker awareness, genre awareness, evidence fit, seminar norms.
- Vocabulary: primary text, translation, edition, excerpt, annotation, close reading, seminar question, summary, interpretation, evaluation, claim, evidence, context, speaker, author, narrator, audience, occasion, genre, form, diction, structure, irony, allusion, objection, turn.
- Representations and models: orientation card, annotation loop, summary-interpretation-evaluation ladder, speaker map, evidence-fit chart, seminar move map.
- Texts and forms: short invented or adapted-style excerpts that let students practice the routine without needing a full author unit yet.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should already be able to read challenging excerpts with support, summarize passages, notice speaker and genre, cite evidence, and discuss ideas respectfully. They need basic historical-period awareness but do not need detailed expertise in every author yet.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- A difficult sentence means the reader is not capable.
- Background context can replace reading the passage closely.
- A passage summary is the same thing as an interpretation.
- The most striking quote is automatically the best evidence.
- The speaker, narrator, character, and author are always the same.
- Discussion is mainly opinion sharing rather than evidence-based inquiry.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with orientation because students need a first move when they meet a hard text. Then separate summary, interpretation, and evaluation so later answers do the right job. After that, teach speaker and form because the corpus includes indirect and dramatic forms. End with evidence because every later unit depends on claims that can be supported from the text.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

This is the opening unit, so review comes from prerequisite literature and humanities work: summarizing, citing, identifying speaker, recognizing genre, and discussing with evidence.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

The unit prepares every later unit. Ancient dialogue needs speaker maps; comedy and satire need genre awareness; confession and drama need author-speaker caution; civic and economic prose need claim/reason tracking; capstone synthesis needs evidence-fit habits.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify orientation details for a passage.
- Distinguish summary, interpretation, evaluation, and unsupported opinion.
- Match forms and speaker roles to interpretive cautions.
- Select the strongest evidence for a claim.
- Choose a useful seminar move such as cite, question, clarify, compare, or revise.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Orient The Text Before You Judge**: Teach the first questions to ask of a difficult passage.
2. **Summary, Interpretation, Evaluation**: Separate the three response jobs students often collapse.
3. **Speaker, Genre, And Form**: Build caution around author, voice, narrator, character, and form.
4. **Evidence That Carries A Claim**: Practice choosing evidence that supports an interpretation.
