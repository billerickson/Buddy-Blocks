# Unit 6: Modern Drama, Science, And Capstone Synthesis

Track: Classical Literature
Level: Level 5
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/classical-literature-5/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students synthesize the level by reading modern drama and scientific-humanistic reflection, then applying the full reading routine to cross-era comparison and transfer. They learn to compare responsibly: naming real continuities while preserving form, period, audience, and evidence.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This is the final unit because it gathers the level's recurring questions after students have met several major forms. Modern drama and scientific reflection test whether classical questions of self, duty, nature, life, freedom, and the good can travel into new genres and disciplines.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: social pressure, public respectability, private motive, responsibility, scientific model, life, disciplinary boundary, analogy, explanation, synthesis, transfer.
- Vocabulary: realism, social pressure, public respectability, private motive, responsibility, duty, selfhood, scientific model, life, reductionism, interdisciplinarity, analogy, evidence, limits of explanation, synthesis, transfer, comparison matrix, capstone claim.
- Representations and models: scene pressure map, scientific reflection frame, cross-era comparison matrix, recurring-question map, capstone evidence plan.
- Texts and forms: selected Ibsen and Schrodinger passages from the local corpus plus short unfamiliar excerpts or adapted passage descriptions for transfer checks.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need all prior units: seminar tools, ancient forms, Christian and Augustinian inwardness, early modern state and stage, and modern civic/economic argument. They should already be able to identify form, speaker, evidence, argument movement, and recurring questions.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Modern texts automatically replace older questions.
- A drama is a message delivered by the most sympathetic character.
- Science and humanities have no shared questions.
- Synthesis means finding a vague similarity.
- A comparison does not need evidence from both texts.
- Transfer means guessing from background knowledge instead of using the passage.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with Ibsen because modern drama extends the dramatic work of Unit 4 into social pressure and public respectability. Move to Schrodinger to test how scientific reflection can still raise humanistic questions about life, explanation, and limits. Then teach comparison maps explicitly before closing with a capstone transfer lesson that asks students to apply the routine to a new excerpt and defend a reading.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

The unit deliberately reviews every prior unit: Unit 1 reading routine, Unit 2 virtue and self-command, Unit 3 inner life and city, Unit 4 drama and appearance/reality, and Unit 5 modern society and critique.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This is the terminal unit for the level. It prepares students for college-level humanities reading and any later companion assignments, seminars, or essays that ask them to sustain a longer argument.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Interpret modern drama through speech, action, social pressure, and consequence.
- Identify scientific-humanistic reflection as argument and analogy, not only fact report.
- Build a comparison that uses evidence from both texts.
- Preserve differences in form, period, audience, and purpose.
- Apply the reading routine to an unfamiliar excerpt.
- Select a defensible capstone claim and evidence plan.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Ibsen And The Modern Social Stage**: Read realism through public pressure, private motive, and staged consequence.
2. **Schrodinger, Life, And Scientific Reflection**: Interpret scientific reflection as a cross-disciplinary argument.
3. **Cross-Era Comparison Maps**: Build responsible comparisons across form, period, and question.
4. **Capstone Transfer: A Defensible Reading**: Apply the full routine to an unfamiliar passage and support a claim.
