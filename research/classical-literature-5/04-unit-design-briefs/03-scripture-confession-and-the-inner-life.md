# Unit 3: Scripture, Confession, And The Inner Life

Track: Classical Literature
Level: Level 5
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/classical-literature-5/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students read selected biblical and Augustinian texts with literary, theological, historical, and philosophical care. They learn to interpret narrative, parable, epistle, confession, memory, will, love, time, and city without turning the unit into devotional assertion or dismissal.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit follows ancient questions because it shows how classical concerns about virtue, law, soul, and city are transformed by scriptural and late antique forms. It also prepares later work on inwardness, conscience, custom, self-examination, and modern identity.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: narrative meaning, parabolic indirection, community argument, conversion, inward conflict, memory, ordered love, earthly and heavenly city.
- Vocabulary: gospel, parable, acts, epistle, confession, conversion, memory, will, love, time, creation, city, pilgrimage, literal sense, figurative language, theological claim, inward conflict, spiritual autobiography.
- Representations and models: parable surface/deeper question chart, community claim/evidence map, memory and will conflict map, ordered-loves comparison chart, two-cities contrast table.
- Texts and forms: selected New Testament passages and selected passages from Augustine's Confessions or City of God as available in the local corpus.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need Unit 1 reading habits and Unit 2 ancient questions. They should know that biblical and Augustinian texts have religious significance while also being literary, historical, and philosophical texts that can be read with disciplined evidence.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Religious texts can only be accepted devotionally or rejected polemically.
- A parable is a simple slogan with no interpretive tension.
- Epistles are private letters with no argument structure.
- Confession is only autobiography and not shaped reflection.
- Augustine's inwardness is disconnected from city and community.
- Figurative language is less serious than literal statement.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with gospel narrative and parable because they make genre-sensitive reading visible. Then move to acts and epistles to show communities reasoning through belief, practice, and conflict. Augustine's confession follows as an intensified account of memory and will. The unit closes with two cities and ordered loves to connect inward life to civic and historical imagination.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

The unit reviews ancient questions of virtue, law, city, soul, and self-command. It also repeats Unit 1 distinctions between summary, interpretation, evaluation, speaker, genre, and evidence, especially where theological content tempts quick opinion.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

The unit prepares Unit 4's essayistic self-examination, dramatic inwardness, and conscience; Unit 5's law, community, and civic order; and Unit 6's synthesis questions about selfhood, responsibility, nature, and the good.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify genre-sensitive reading moves for narrative, parable, epistle, confession, and theological reflection.
- Distinguish textual interpretation from personal belief or disbelief.
- Explain inward conflict with evidence.
- Compare ancient virtue and Christian ordered love.
- Use figurative language and structure as evidence.
- Connect individual selfhood with community or city.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Gospel Narrative, Parable, And Meaning**: Read narrative and parable as forms that require interpretation.
2. **Acts, Epistles, And Communities In Argument**: Track claims, counsel, audience, and community conflict.
3. **Augustine's Confession Of Memory And Will**: Study confession as shaped inquiry into self, memory, and desire.
4. **Two Cities And Ordered Loves**: Connect inner life, loves, community, and historical imagination.
