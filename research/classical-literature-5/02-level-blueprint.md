# Classical Literature 5 Level Blueprint

Track: Classical Literature
Level: Level 5
Source: Accepted research brief at `research/classical-literature-5/01-research-brief.md`

## 1. Level Mission

Classical Literature 5 is a compact Great Books-style capstone for advanced high-school readers. Students learn to enter difficult primary texts with patience, identify the form and situation of each work, track speaker and argument without flattening them into simple opinions, and compare enduring questions across ancient, Christian, early modern, modern civic, literary, and scientific-humanistic texts. The level does not try to cover every available classic. Instead, it builds durable habits for reading selected passages and short works deeply: orienting the text, summarizing before judging, noticing diction and structure, interpreting with evidence, and carrying one central question from text to text.

## 2. Durable Student Outcomes

By the end of Classical Literature 5, students should be able to:

1. Read selected complex primary texts, in translation where applicable, with enough independence to summarize, annotate, question, and discuss them.
2. Identify major forms in the corpus, including dialogue, comedy, tragedy, ethical treatise, biography, confession, meditation, gospel narrative, essay, drama, constitutional argument, manifesto, and scientific reflection.
3. Distinguish author, speaker, narrator, interlocutor, dramatic character, audience, and argument position.
4. Analyze recurring questions of justice, law, virtue, education, soul, city, freedom, equality, custom, faith, reason, economy, technology, mortality, and the good life.
5. Interpret passages through close attention to diction, structure, genre, irony, allusion, evidence, objection, and argumentative movement.
6. Compare texts across periods, naming continuity, disagreement, transformation, critique, and change in historical setting.
7. Use precise textual evidence in seminar discussion, selected explanations, and concise analytic writing.
8. Evaluate philosophical, civic, literary, theological, economic, and scientific-humanistic claims while respecting historical distance and genre.
9. Recognize when satire, polemic, adult themes, religious texts, or political controversy require careful framing rather than quick agreement or dismissal.
10. Build a capstone map of major authors, forms, questions, and interpretive habits that prepares them for college-level humanities reading.

## 3. Concept And Skill Progression

The level begins with seminar habits and difficult-text strategies. Students learn to orient a passage by author, work, form, speaker, situation, audience, and central question. Early lessons emphasize the difference between summary, interpretation, evaluation, and opinion so students do not jump immediately from difficulty to judgment.

Students next study ancient forms and questions. Dialogue, comedy, ethical and political prose, biography, and Stoic meditation introduce the examined life, justice, education, citizenship, virtue, law, friendship, and the relation between soul and city. The skill progression moves from recognizing form and speaker to tracking argument steps, comic inversion, exemplar-based reasoning, and aphoristic moral reflection.

The middle of the level turns to biblical and Augustinian inheritance as literary, theological, historical, and philosophical texts. Students treat narrative, parable, conversion, confession, memory, love, time, and city as interpretive problems. The emphasis is respectful textual understanding, not devotional or anti-devotional instruction.

Renaissance and early modern texts then complicate the map. Machiavelli, Montaigne, Rabelais, and Shakespeare help students read statecraft, custom, satire, skepticism, essayistic self-examination, and dramatic inwardness. Students practice separating literal statement from irony, distinguishing prudential argument from moral endorsement, and noticing how drama lets ideas conflict through action and speech.

Modern civic, democratic, economic, and critical texts extend earlier questions into consent, rights, constitutional design, equality, commerce, labor, revolution, and civil society. Students compare ancient virtue and modern liberty, constitution and democracy, economy and justice, and reform and critique. The final movement adds modern drama and scientific-humanistic reflection, then asks students to synthesize two or more texts around a central question with precise evidence.

## 4. Vocabulary, Representation, Model, Text, And Form Progression

Early vocabulary should establish the reading routine: primary text, translation, edition, excerpt, annotation, seminar question, summary, interpretation, evaluation, claim, evidence, context, speaker, author, narrator, audience, occasion, genre, form, diction, structure, irony, allusion, objection, and turn.

Ancient and classical vocabulary should include dialogue, interlocutor, dialectic, comedy, satire, virtue, vice, justice, law, soul, city, regime, citizen, education, rhetoric, philosophy, biography, exemplar, meditation, Stoic, prudence, and providence. Representations should include speaker maps, argument-step charts, form comparison tables, question webs, and evidence trackers.

Biblical and Augustinian vocabulary should include gospel, parable, act, epistle, confession, conversion, memory, will, love, time, creation, city, pilgrimage, literal sense, figurative language, and theological claim. Models should include narrative movement charts, inner/outer conflict maps, and paired passage comparisons.

Renaissance and early modern vocabulary should include prince, republic, fortune, necessity, counsel, custom, skepticism, essay, self-examination, satire, grotesque, dramatic irony, soliloquy, tragedy, comedy, and character foil. Models should include motive charts, scene-turn maps, irony detectors, and claim/evidence/reasoning frames.

Modern civic, economic, critical, dramatic, and scientific vocabulary should include natural rights, consent, social contract, constitution, faction, federalism, democracy, equality, commerce, division of labor, civil society, ideology, labor, alienation, manifesto, realism, social pressure, scientific model, life, reductionism, and interdisciplinarity. Representations should progress to cross-era comparison matrices and capstone synthesis maps.

## 5. Fluency Expectations

Fluency in this level is not speed through long books. It means students can repeatedly perform the core moves of advanced humanities reading: orient the text, identify form and speaker, summarize the local argument or action, mark key terms, choose evidence, explain how evidence supports an interpretation, and compare one text with another. Students should become fluent in recognizing whether a prompt asks for summary, interpretation, comparison, or evaluation.

Students should also build retrieval fluency for a manageable map of authors, works or work-types, periods, forms, and central questions. This retrieval should be spaced and interleaved so students can connect, for example, Socratic questioning to later conscience, ancient virtue to modern liberty, satire to dramatic irony, or constitutional design to democratic anxiety.

## 6. Conceptual Understanding Expectations

Students should understand that a classic is not valuable because a list says so; a classic remains generative because its form, language, questions, and influence invite repeated interpretation. They should understand that genre shapes meaning: a dialogue tests claims differently from a treatise, a comedy differently from a tragedy, a confession differently from a manifesto.

Students should understand that the author is not automatically identical with the speaker, narrator, dramatic character, or most vivid voice in the text. They should learn to ask what a passage says, who says it, what problem it answers, what assumptions it uses, what objections it anticipates, and what remains unresolved.

Students should also understand historical distance as an interpretive aid rather than a shortcut. Context can clarify audience, occasion, vocabulary, and stakes, but it does not replace close reading. The level should train students to be charitable enough to understand before criticizing and critical enough to test claims with evidence.

## 7. Application And Transfer Expectations

Application appears when students use the reading routine on unfamiliar excerpts from familiar traditions. Given a new short passage, students should orient it, identify form and speaker, summarize the main movement, select useful evidence, and answer a focused interpretive or comparative question.

Transfer appears when students connect questions across genres and periods without erasing differences. They should compare Socratic justice with constitutional justice, Stoic self-command with Christian conversion, Machiavellian necessity with Shakespearean ambition, Enlightenment liberty with democratic equality, economic explanation with social critique, and scientific models of life with older questions about nature and purpose.

Inside Buddy Blocks, transfer should be compact and scorable: passage questions, comparison choices, ordered reasoning, match-pair relationships, constrained text input for key terms, and error diagnosis. Longer seminar discussions and essays can be companion assignments outside the app.

## 8. Review And Spacing Plan

Review should begin in Unit 1 and remain cumulative. Every later unit should revisit author/speaker distinction, genre and form, summary before interpretation, evidence selection, and careful use of context. Short retrieval items should keep the corpus map active without becoming trivia.

Genre review should be interleaved. Dialogue, comedy, treatise, biography, confession, meditation, gospel, essay, drama, constitutional argument, economic prose, manifesto, modern drama, and scientific reflection should reappear in comparison prompts after their first introduction.

Recurring questions should be deliberately spaced: justice, virtue, law, city, education, freedom, equality, economy, faith, reason, custom, selfhood, technology, life, and the good. Later lessons should ask students to recognize old questions in new forms rather than treating each author as isolated.

Feedback should point back to the reading move that would repair the error: identify the speaker, reread the turn in the argument, separate summary from evaluation, notice irony, use the passage instead of background knowledge alone, or test whether the evidence actually supports the claim.

## 9. Mastery Evidence For The End Of The Level

End-of-level mastery should be shown through a balanced body of compact evidence. A mastered student can orient a passage by author, work-type, form, speaker, situation, and question; summarize a difficult passage without collapsing it into opinion; identify important diction, structure, claim, reason, objection, irony, or dramatic turn; and choose evidence that supports a defensible interpretation.

The student can compare at least two texts on a recurring question while preserving their differences in period, form, audience, and purpose. The student can diagnose common misreadings such as author-speaker confusion, literalizing satire, treating context as the whole meaning, using unsupported modern opinion as interpretation, or reducing an argument to only its conclusion.

The strongest evidence is transfer: the student meets a new excerpt from a known tradition, applies the reading routine, makes a plausible claim with precise evidence, and explains how the passage connects to a larger question in the level.

## Source And Product Constraint Note

This blueprint is derived from the accepted Classical Literature 5 research brief and its cited high-quality curriculum references, including advanced ELA expectations, Great Books seminar practice, adolescent literacy guidance, secondary writing guidance, and study guidance on spacing and feedback. The user's physical-library corpus shapes the candidate author set, but this level intentionally selects a coherent subset rather than attempting exhaustive coverage. Buddy Blocks product constraints shape the artifact level: compact lessons, student-facing intros before unsupported recall, diagnostic distractors, app-scorable question types, one fenced `question` YAML block per question during authoring, and no implementation under `src/content/curriculum` at this stage.
