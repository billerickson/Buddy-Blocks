# Spanish 2 Research Brief

Track: Spanish  
Level: Level 2  
Intended student profile: continuing novice learners who can use memorized words, phrases, and simple modeled sentences from Spanish 1. This level should expand Novice Mid communication across familiar topics.

This brief defines outcomes and instructional priorities only. It does not define units, lessons, or questions.

## 1. End-Of-Level Outcomes

By the end of Spanish 2, students should be able to understand and use expanded novice language about school, classes, supplies, people, descriptions, feelings, food requests, places, directions, dates, time, and routines. They should ask and answer predictable questions, interpret short readings/listening for key words and main ideas, produce simple sentence-level descriptions with support, use common present-tense action patterns in practiced contexts, and make simple cultural comparisons.

## 2. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should know Spanish 1 greetings, classroom phrases, numbers, calendar words, family, likes/dislikes, food, places, high-frequency chunks, basic articles/gender, simple adjective agreement, ser/estar/tener/hay/ir/gustar-like chunks, common question words, and basic sound-spelling patterns.

## 3. Major Concept Clusters

- **Novice Mid Communication:** familiar question-answer exchanges, short descriptions, simple preferences, requests, and classroom/school routines.
- **Expanded Vocabulary Domains:** larger numbers, dates/time, classes, supplies, people, feelings, food, places, directions, and everyday activities.
- **Present-Time Patterns:** common -ar actions, high-frequency irregular chunks, negation, questions, and answer frames.
- **Interpretive Skills:** listening/reading for key words, cognates, visuals, main idea, and familiar details.
- **Culture In Context:** school life, meals, schedules, greetings, public places, and regional Spanish-speaking communities.

## 4. Recommended Progression

Start with cumulative Spanish 1 review and classroom survival language. Expand numbers, dates, time, and school contexts because they recur across tasks. Add descriptions of people, feelings, and places, then food requests and directions. Introduce present-tense action patterns through meaning-rich chunks before formal paradigms. Move from recognition to guided sentence production and short exchanges. End with mixed novice tasks using short readings, listening, and simple cultural comparisons.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Students may translate English word order directly into Spanish.
- Students may omit articles because English sometimes does.
- Students may treat adjective agreement as optional decoration.
- Students may overgeneralize one verb ending to all subjects.
- Students may treat `ser`, `estar`, `tener`, and `hay` as interchangeable "is/has/there is" words.
- Students may read cognates without checking context.
- Students may answer a question word they recognize with the wrong kind of information.

## 6. Developmental Constraints And Reading/Load Concerns

Keep input short and highly contextualized. Use audio, visuals, schedules, menus, maps, and classroom scenes. Avoid full verb charts as the main learning object. Introduce typed production after recognition and supported practice. Use accent-tolerant scoring when meaning is the target, while modeling correct accents. Keep culture embedded in language use, not trivia lists.

## 7. Assessment Implications

Assess interpretive listening/reading, predictable interpersonal responses, and supported presentational sentences. Use flash-card ladders, match-pairs, fill-blank, dialogue-builder, listening-question, passage-question, and short text-input after support. Distractors should reveal gender/article errors, wrong question word, false cognate, singular/plural mismatch, wrong verb chunk, or direction/time confusion.

## 8. Spiral Review Priorities

Spanish 1 greetings and classroom language, numbers/calendar/weather, articles and adjective agreement, question words, ser/estar/tener/hay/ir/gustar chunks, food/place vocabulary, cognates, pronunciation, and culture/geography references.

## 9. Concepts That Should Not Be Taught Yet

Avoid full present-tense paradigms for all verb classes, past-tense contrast, object pronouns, subjunctive, conditional, por/para, extended paragraph writing, and unscripted conversation beyond familiar exchanges.
