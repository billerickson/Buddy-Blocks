# Rhetoric 1 Research Brief

Track: Rhetoric  
Level: Level 1  
Intended student profile: beginning rhetoric students, likely upper-elementary learners, who can tell stories, give opinions, and write short paragraphs with support.

This brief defines outcomes and instructional priorities only. It does not define units, lessons, or questions.

## 1. End-Of-Level Outcomes

By the end of Rhetoric 1, students should be able to:

1. Retell a short fable, story, or event with beginning, middle, end, and a clear point.
2. Identify audience, purpose, speaker, and message in simple texts or speeches.
3. State an opinion or claim and give one or two relevant reasons.
4. Use examples, details, and simple evidence to support an idea.
5. Expand a bare sentence or paragraph for clarity, detail, sequence, and emphasis.
6. Compare two people, actions, ideas, or choices using clear criteria.
7. Recognize and use basic rhetorical moves: describe, explain, narrate, praise, blame, compare, support, and conclude.
8. Revise short writing for stronger word choice, clearer order, and more precise reasons.
9. Deliver a short oral response using a clear voice, complete thought, and respectful listener awareness.
10. Notice that language choices can inform, delight, persuade, or move an audience.

## 2. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students entering this level should usually be able to:

- Read short stories, fables, and informational paragraphs.
- Write several related sentences with support.
- Give a personal opinion and one reason orally.
- Identify a story's characters, setting, problem, and lesson at an elementary level.
- Understand basic grammar terms such as sentence, noun, verb, adjective, and paragraph when used in context.
- Listen to a short model and imitate its structure.

## 3. Major Concept Clusters

### Audience And Purpose

Rhetoric begins with the idea that speech and writing are aimed at someone for a reason. Students should ask: Who is this for? What is it trying to do?

### Narration And Fable

Classical preliminary exercises often begin with fable and narrative because story structure is accessible. Students should retell, compress, expand, and identify the point of a short narrative.

### Claim, Reason, Example

Level 1 rhetoric should connect to argument readiness without becoming formal debate. Students need clear opinion/claim statements supported by relevant reasons and examples.

### Description And Amplification

Students should learn to add meaningful detail: who, what, when, where, why, how, sensory detail, precise verbs, and examples.

### Comparison And Evaluation

Comparison helps students move beyond "I like it" toward criteria: stronger/weaker, clearer/less clear, brave/careless, fair/unfair, useful/not useful.

### Revision And Delivery

Rhetoric includes improving language and presenting it. Revision and oral delivery should be short, specific, and practiced.

## 4. Recommended Progression

1. Begin with audience, purpose, and message in familiar examples.
2. Teach retelling and narrative order through short fables and stories.
3. Add the point or lesson of a story: what is it trying to show?
4. Teach description and amplification by expanding sentences and short paragraphs.
5. Teach opinion/claim plus reason in everyday and text-based contexts.
6. Add examples and details as support.
7. Teach comparison using clear criteria and sentence frames.
8. Introduce praise/blame cautiously as evaluation of actions or choices, not personal attack.
9. Teach short revision moves: stronger verb, clearer order, better reason, more specific example.
10. End with mixed mini-rhetoric tasks: retell, support, compare, revise, and present.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- "Rhetoric means fancy words." Rhetoric is purposeful communication.
- "Persuasion means getting your way." Ethical persuasion uses relevant reasons and respects the audience.
- "A reason is good if it is loud or emotional." Reasons should support the claim.
- "More detail is always better." Useful detail serves the purpose.
- "A story lesson is always directly stated." Students may need to infer the point.
- "Praise and blame mean attacking people." Early evaluation should focus on actions, choices, and evidence.
- "Revision means fixing spelling only." Revision improves meaning, order, detail, and effect.
- "Speaking well means memorizing perfectly." Clear, understandable delivery is the Level 1 goal.

## 6. Developmental Constraints And Reading/Load Concerns

- Use short models and short student production.
- Keep rhetorical terminology light and immediately useful.
- Avoid manipulative persuasion, debate tricks, or emotionally loaded issues.
- Separate revision targets; do not ask students to fix purpose, evidence, style, and mechanics all at once.
- Oral tasks should allow rehearsal and optional sentence frames.
- For praise/blame or evaluation tasks, choose safe fictional or historical examples and focus on actions.
- Students may need explicit models before they can imitate rhetorical forms.

## 7. Assessment Implications

- Ask students to identify audience, purpose, message, claim, reason, example, and conclusion.
- Use short fable/story tasks for retelling, ordering, and point/lesson.
- Use sentence expansion and paragraph revision tasks for amplification and clarity.
- Use comparison prompts with clear criteria.
- Use constructed response sparingly: one claim plus one reason, one revised sentence, or one short explanation.
- Distractors should show common errors: irrelevant reason, vague detail, missing audience, unsupported claim, confused sequence.
- Feedback should name the rhetorical move: clearer purpose, stronger reason, more specific example, better order, or audience fit.

## 8. Spiral Review Priorities

- Audience, purpose, speaker, message.
- Beginning/middle/end and point/lesson.
- Claim, reason, example, evidence.
- Description and amplification.
- Compare/contrast language.
- Revision for clarity, detail, order, and word choice.
- Short oral delivery and listener awareness.

## 9. Concepts That Should Not Be Taught Yet

- Formal rhetorical canons as memorized taxonomy.
- Ethos/pathos/logos as abstract labels before students can reason about audience and support.
- Advanced progymnasmata exercises such as refutation, confirmation, thesis, law, or full encomium/invective as mastery targets.
- Formal debate formats, cross-examination, or adversarial rhetoric.
- Logical fallacy catalogs, propaganda analysis, or political persuasion as primary tasks.
- Long essays or speeches as the main assessment.
