# Rhetoric 1 Level Blueprint

Track: Rhetoric
Level: Level 1
Source: Accepted research brief at `research/rhetoric-1/01-research-brief.md`

## 1. Level Mission

Rhetoric 1 introduces students to rhetoric as clear, purposeful communication for a real audience, not as fancy language or winning an argument. Students learn to notice who is speaking, who is listening or reading, what message is being sent, and what the communication is trying to do. They build from familiar stories and fables into short retellings, descriptions, opinions, comparisons, revisions, and brief oral responses. By the end of the level, students should be able to retell a short story with a point, support a simple claim with relevant reasons and examples, compare choices using clear criteria, revise for clarity and effect, and present a short response with audience awareness.

## 2. Durable Student Outcomes

By the end of Rhetoric 1, students should be able to:

1. Identify speaker, audience, purpose, message, and basic effect in short texts, speeches, and scenarios.
2. Retell a short fable, story, or event in clear beginning, middle, and end order.
3. Infer or state the point, lesson, or main message of a short narrative.
4. Expand bare sentences and short paragraphs with useful details, examples, sequence words, and precise word choices.
5. State a simple opinion or claim and support it with one or two relevant reasons.
6. Choose or provide examples and details that actually support a claim.
7. Compare two people, actions, ideas, or choices using a clear criterion such as fairness, usefulness, bravery, clarity, or kindness.
8. Evaluate actions or choices with evidence while avoiding personal attack.
9. Revise short writing for clearer order, stronger reasons, more specific examples, and better word choice.
10. Deliver a brief oral response with a complete thought, clear voice, and listener awareness.

## 3. Concept And Skill Progression

The level begins with the simplest rhetorical habit: asking who the communication is for and what it is trying to do. Students work with familiar messages, classroom scenarios, short speeches, and brief texts so audience, purpose, speaker, and message stay concrete.

Students then move into narration because story structure is accessible and foundational. They identify beginning, middle, and end; place events in order; retell without losing the important point; and recognize that many stories and fables are meant to show a lesson.

Once students can track a whole message, they learn to make messages fuller through description and amplification. They expand bare sentences with who, what, when, where, why, how, sensory detail, precise verbs, and examples. The goal is not "more words" but details that help the audience understand, picture, or care about the idea.

The middle of the level introduces claim, reason, and example in everyday and text-based contexts. Students distinguish an opinion from a supporting reason, reject irrelevant support, and build short responses that say what they think and why.

Students then learn comparison and evaluation. They compare using criteria rather than personal preference alone, and they practice safe forms of praise and blame by evaluating actions, choices, and evidence instead of attacking people.

The final stretch emphasizes revision and delivery. Students revise one target at a time: order, detail, word choice, reason, example, or conclusion. They rehearse short oral responses with a clear voice and awareness of listeners. The level ends with mixed mini-rhetoric tasks that require students to select an appropriate move: retell, describe, support, compare, revise, or present.

## 4. Vocabulary, Representation, Model, Text, And Form Progression

Early vocabulary should remain light and useful: speaker, audience, purpose, message, point, reason, detail, example, story, beginning, middle, end, and lesson. Students should use these words while looking at short, familiar communication examples rather than memorizing definitions.

Narration vocabulary should include character, setting, event, problem, choice, outcome, retell, sequence, first, next, then, finally, point, and moral. Representations should include story chains, beginning-middle-end frames, event-order cards, and short fable passages.

Description and amplification vocabulary should include describe, detail, sensory detail, precise verb, explain, expand, example, order, and emphasis. Models should move from a bare sentence to a fuller sentence, then from a thin paragraph to a clearer paragraph.

Argument-readiness vocabulary should include opinion, claim, reason, support, evidence, relevant, irrelevant, because, example, conclusion, and audience fit. Forms should include sentence frames such as "I think ___ because ___," "One reason is ___," and "For example, ___."

Comparison and evaluation vocabulary should include compare, contrast, same, different, criterion, stronger, weaker, clearer, fair, useful, brave, careful, praise, blame, action, choice, and evidence. Models should include simple comparison charts and two-sentence evaluations.

Revision and delivery vocabulary should include revise, word choice, clearer order, stronger reason, specific example, conclusion, voice, pace, listener, and respectful. Forms should include short revision choices, error-correction items, one-paragraph improvements, speaking prompts, and brief audience-aware presentations.

## 5. Fluency Expectations

Rhetoric 1 fluency is not speed. Students should become fluent in small rhetorical routines: identify audience and purpose, retell events in order, name the point of a story, form a claim-plus-reason sentence, choose a relevant example, use comparison language, revise one target at a time, and speak a complete thought clearly. These routines should become easier through spaced practice so students can use them in new texts and contexts without needing every step retaught.

## 6. Conceptual Understanding Expectations

Students should understand that communication is purposeful: words are chosen for someone and for a reason. They should know that a story can do more than entertain; it can show a lesson or reveal a choice. They should understand that details serve a purpose, reasons must connect to claims, examples make ideas clearer, and comparisons need criteria.

Students should also understand that rhetoric has an ethical dimension. Persuasion is not just getting one's way, and evaluation is not personal attack. Good communication respects the audience, uses relevant support, and can be improved through revision and rehearsal.

## 7. Application And Transfer Expectations

Students should apply these skills to short classroom, family, literature, history, and everyday scenarios. They should be able to recognize when a task asks them to retell, describe, explain, support, compare, revise, or present. Transfer is visible when students meet an unfamiliar short passage or scenario and still identify the audience, purpose, point, reason, example, comparison criterion, or useful revision.

Written production should stay short: a sentence, a few connected sentences, or one compact paragraph. Oral production should also stay short and supported, with optional sentence frames when the cognitive load is high.

## 8. Review And Spacing Plan

Review should be cumulative from the beginning. Audience, purpose, speaker, and message should reappear in narration, claims, comparison, revision, and delivery. Beginning-middle-end order and story point should return when students use examples, compare actions, and present short responses.

Claim, reason, and example should be spaced across later comparison, revision, and capstone tasks. Description and amplification should reappear as students improve reasons, examples, and oral responses. Revision should begin as tiny choices early in the level and become a named habit by the final units.

Question sets should use delayed retrieval and diagnostic distractors: wrong answers should reveal common confusions such as vague detail, irrelevant reason, missing audience, confused sequence, unsupported claim, or personal attack instead of evidence-based evaluation.

## 9. Mastery Evidence For The End Of The Level

A student who has mastered Rhetoric 1 can read or listen to a short communication and identify its speaker, audience, purpose, message, and likely effect. The student can retell a short fable or event in order, state its point or lesson, and add useful details without burying the main idea.

The student can write or choose a simple claim, support it with a relevant reason and example, compare two choices using a criterion, and revise short writing for clarity, order, detail, or stronger support. The student can deliver a brief oral response that is understandable, complete, and aware of listeners. Strong mastery includes transfer: the student can decide which rhetorical move fits a new task and explain that choice in age-appropriate language.
