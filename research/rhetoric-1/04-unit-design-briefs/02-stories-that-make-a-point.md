# Unit 2: Stories That Make A Point

Track: Rhetoric
Level: Level 1
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/rhetoric-1/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn to retell short stories and fables in order while preserving the point. By the end of the unit, they can identify key events, leave out distracting extras, infer a simple lesson, and connect a character's choice to the message of the story.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

Narration is the most accessible early rhetorical form. After students understand that communication has purpose and message, short stories give them concrete practice seeing how order, choices, and outcomes create a point for an audience.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: stories have ordered events; some events are key to the point; fables often show a lesson through character choices and outcomes.
- Vocabulary: story, fable, narrator, character, setting, event, beginning, middle, end, sequence, retell, key event, extra detail, point, lesson, moral, choice, outcome.
- Representations and models: beginning-middle-end frames, story chains, event cards, key-detail sort, point-and-proof chart.
- Texts and forms: short fables, short personal-event retellings, brief story summaries, oral retellings with sentence frames.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need basic story vocabulary such as character, setting, problem, and solution; ability to read or listen to short fables; and comfort with sequence words such as first, next, then, and finally.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Retelling means repeating every detail.
- Events can be out of order as long as they are all mentioned.
- The most exciting event is automatically the point.
- A story's lesson must be stated directly.
- A character's choice does not matter when explaining the lesson.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Start with beginning-middle-end order so students can hold the whole story. Then distinguish key events from extra details before asking students to retell. Once retelling is stable, add the point or lesson of a fable. End by connecting choices and outcomes to the lesson, which prepares students for later examples, reasons, comparison, and evaluation.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Every story task should review Unit 1 by asking who the story is for and what message it might send. Students should repeatedly ask, "What is this story trying to show?"

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

Narrative order prepares students to organize descriptions and paragraphs. Story point prepares claim and conclusion work. Character choices and outcomes prepare comparison and fair evaluation in Unit 5.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Order events from a short story.
- Identify key events and remove distracting details.
- Retell a short fable in a few clear sentences.
- Identify or infer the point, lesson, or moral.
- Explain how a character's choice supports the lesson.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Story Parts In Order**: Use beginning, middle, and end to track a short story.
2. **Key Events And Extra Details**: Decide which events are necessary for a clear retelling.
3. **Retell A Fable Clearly**: Retell a short fable with key events and a clear ending.
4. **Find The Point Or Lesson**: Identify or infer what a fable is trying to show.
5. **Connect Choices To The Lesson**: Explain how a character's action and outcome support the point.
