# Unit 1: Purposeful Communication

Track: Rhetoric
Level: Level 1
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/rhetoric-1/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students understand rhetoric as purposeful communication for an audience. By the end of the unit, they can identify speaker, audience, purpose, message, and likely effect in short, familiar examples and choose language that better fits a simple audience-purpose situation.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit comes first because all later rhetoric work depends on audience and purpose. Retellings, descriptions, claims, comparisons, revisions, and speeches are stronger when students ask who the communication is for and what it is trying to do.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: communication has a speaker or writer, an audience, a purpose, a message, and an effect.
- Vocabulary: rhetoric, communicate, speaker, writer, audience, listener, reader, purpose, message, inform, explain, entertain, persuade, effect, respectful.
- Representations and models: audience-purpose-message triangle, simple scenario cards, speech bubble examples, short notes and announcements.
- Texts and forms: classroom directions, birthday invitations, short announcements, polite requests, short explanatory notes.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should be able to read short sentences and paragraphs, listen to short models, understand everyday communication situations, give an opinion orally, and recognize when words are polite or unkind.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Rhetoric means fancy words.
- The audience is always "everyone."
- Purpose and message are the same thing.
- Persuasion means forcing someone to agree.
- A strong message ignores how the listener might receive it.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin by redefining rhetoric as purposeful communication, then separate the people in communication: speaker or writer and audience. Next, distinguish purpose from message so students can say both what a communication says and what it tries to do. End with audience-fit choices because students need to apply the lens, not only name parts.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

This is the first unit, so review draws from students' everyday knowledge of speaking, listening, reading, and writing. Use familiar school, home, and story scenarios instead of abstract rhetorical terms.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares every later unit. Story lessons need message and audience. Description lessons need purpose-driven details. Claim lessons need audience-fit reasons. Revision and delivery lessons require students to notice how words affect listeners.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify speaker or writer, audience, purpose, and message.
- Distinguish purpose from message.
- Choose a language choice that fits a given audience and purpose.
- Explain why a communication is respectful and effective for its audience.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **What Is Rhetoric For?**: Introduce rhetoric as purposeful communication, not fancy language.
2. **Speaker And Audience**: Identify who is communicating and who the communication is for.
3. **Purpose, Message, And Effect**: Distinguish what a communication says from what it tries to do.
4. **Audience-Fit Choices**: Choose words, details, and tone that fit a simple audience and purpose.
