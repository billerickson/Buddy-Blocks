# Unit 5: Comparison And Fair Evaluation

Track: Rhetoric
Level: Level 1
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/rhetoric-1/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students compare and evaluate using criteria and evidence. By the end of the unit, they can compare two choices by one clear criterion, use comparison language accurately, and evaluate actions respectfully by focusing on evidence rather than personal attack.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

Comparison is a natural next step after claim and support. Students already know how to give a reason and example; this unit teaches them to use those tools when judging how two actions, choices, or ideas are alike, different, stronger, weaker, fairer, or more useful.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: comparison needs a criterion; evaluation should focus on actions, choices, and evidence; praise and blame should be fair and specific.
- Vocabulary: compare, contrast, same, different, criterion, fair, useful, clear, brave, careful, stronger, weaker, praise, blame, action, choice, evidence, respectful.
- Representations and models: comparison chart, criterion label, action-evidence-evaluation frame, fair/unfair comment sort.
- Texts and forms: short story choices, classroom scenarios, two-option decisions, two-sentence evaluations.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need claim-reason-example structure, basic comparison language, story choice and outcome knowledge, and audience awareness.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Comparing means only saying which one you like more.
- The criterion can change halfway through a comparison.
- Praise means exaggerating.
- Blame means attacking the person.
- Evidence is optional if the opinion is strong.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with criteria because students need a stable basis for comparison. Then teach comparison language such as same, different, stronger, and weaker. Next, move into evaluating actions with evidence. End with praise and blame in a safe, action-focused form so students learn respectful evaluation before later rhetoric levels become more formal.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Use audience-purpose checks from Unit 1, story choices from Unit 2, useful details from Unit 3, and claim-reason-example language from Unit 4. Every comparison should include a reason or evidence.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares revision lessons that ask students to remove personal attack, strengthen evidence, and choose respectful wording. It also prepares later rhetoric levels that include fuller praise, blame, confirmation, and refutation.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Name or choose a clear criterion for comparison.
- Use comparison and contrast words accurately.
- Select evidence for an evaluation.
- Identify unfair or personal criticism.
- Rewrite a harsh evaluation into action-focused language.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Compare With A Criterion**: Choose one clear basis for comparing two things.
2. **Same, Different, Stronger, Weaker**: Use comparison language with evidence.
3. **Evaluate Actions With Evidence**: Judge an action or choice using a reason and example.
4. **Praise And Blame Without Attack**: Keep evaluation respectful and focused on actions.
