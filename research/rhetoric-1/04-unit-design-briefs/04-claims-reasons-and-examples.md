# Unit 4: Claims, Reasons, And Examples

Track: Rhetoric
Level: Level 1
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/rhetoric-1/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students develop argument-readiness by connecting claims, reasons, and examples. By the end of the unit, they can identify a claim, tell whether a reason supports it, reject irrelevant support, and build a short claim-reason-example response.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit follows narration and amplification because students now have two key tools: they can state a point and add useful detail. Claim work becomes stronger when students already know that a message is for an audience and that examples should clarify.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: a claim says what someone thinks or wants the audience to accept; a reason tells why; an example or detail can make the reason clearer.
- Vocabulary: opinion, claim, reason, support, because, evidence, example, relevant, irrelevant, specific, vague, conclusion, audience fit.
- Representations and models: claim-reason-example chain, relevant/irrelevant reason sort, because-test, short response frame.
- Texts and forms: everyday opinion prompts, text-based claims from short passages, one- to three-sentence responses, simple conclusion sentences.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should be able to give a personal opinion and one oral reason, identify audience and purpose, retell a story's point, and choose useful details or examples from Unit 3.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- A claim proves itself.
- Any sentence after "because" counts as a reason.
- A reason is strong because it is loud or emotional.
- An example can be unrelated if it is interesting.
- A personal preference is enough support for every audience.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

First separate claims from non-claims so students know what is being supported. Then distinguish reasons from claims. Next, focus on relevance because unsupported or irrelevant reasons are a major misconception. After that, teach examples as clarification. End with a compact production lesson that combines claim, reason, and example in a short response.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Audience and purpose should guide which reasons fit. Story points from Unit 2 can become claims, and details from Unit 3 can become examples. Students should also review that useful details serve a purpose.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares comparison and evaluation by giving students support language. It prepares revision by creating clear targets: strengthen a reason, replace an irrelevant reason, add a specific example, or write a clearer conclusion.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify claims and opinions.
- Distinguish a claim from a reason.
- Select relevant support for a claim.
- Identify irrelevant or vague support.
- Add a specific example.
- Write or complete a claim-reason-example response.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Claim Or Not?**: Identify statements that can be supported as opinions or claims.
2. **Reasons That Support**: Distinguish a reason from a claim and connect it with because.
3. **Relevant And Irrelevant Support**: Choose support that actually fits the claim.
4. **Examples Make Reasons Clear**: Add specific examples or details that clarify a reason.
5. **Build A Claim-Reason-Example Response**: Produce a compact supported response with a simple conclusion.
