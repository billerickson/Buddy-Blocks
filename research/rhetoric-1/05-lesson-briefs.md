# Rhetoric 1 Lesson Briefs

Sources: accepted level blueprint, course map, and unit design briefs in `research/rhetoric-1/`.

## Unit 1: Purposeful Communication

### Lesson 1: What Is Rhetoric For?

- **Teaching goal:** Introduce rhetoric as purposeful communication that helps a speaker or writer do something for an audience.
- **Student outcome:** The student can recognize rhetoric as clear communication for a purpose, not fancy language or tricking people.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A simple communication model: someone says or writes something, to someone, for a reason.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Everyday speaking, listening, reading, and writing experiences.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Rhetoric means fancy words; persuasion means getting your way; communication quality is about sounding impressive.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given short examples, the student can choose which one shows purposeful communication and explain the purpose in simple words.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for recognizing purposeful communication; `match-pairs` for purpose words to examples; `constructed-response` for a brief explanation of why rhetoric is useful.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Start with familiar examples: a sign, an invitation, a story, and a polite request. Ask what each one is trying to do.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep examples one sentence long; avoid abstract theory and formal rhetorical labels beyond rhetoric, purpose, audience, and message.

### Lesson 2: Speaker And Audience

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to identify who is communicating and who the communication is for.
- **Student outcome:** The student can name the speaker or writer and the intended audience in a short scenario.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Speaker-audience pairs show that communication has a source and a receiver.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Understanding of basic roles such as teacher, student, parent, friend, reader, and listener.
- **Likely misconceptions:** The audience is always everyone; the speaker is always the main character; the reader of a prompt is automatically the intended audience.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a short note, sign, announcement, or speech bubble, the student identifies the speaker and audience.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for speaker or audience identification; `match-pairs` for matching messages to audiences; `passage-question` for a short scenario with audience clues.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show the same message changing audience: "Please walk quietly" from a teacher to a class versus from a librarian to visitors.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar school and family scenarios; keep names and roles clear.

### Lesson 3: Purpose, Message, And Effect

- **Teaching goal:** Help students distinguish what a communication says from what it tries to do and how it may affect an audience.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify the message, purpose, and likely effect of a short communication.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Message is the main idea sent; purpose is why it is sent; effect is what it may make the audience think, feel, or do.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Speaker and audience from the prior lesson; basic main idea recognition.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Purpose and message are the same; effect is always exactly what the speaker wanted; every communication has only one possible effect.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a short announcement or note, the student can choose its message, purpose, and likely audience effect.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for purpose words to examples; `multiple-choice` for message and effect; `multi-blank-cloze` for completing "The message is... The purpose is..."
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a simple sign such as "Wet floor. Please walk around." Discuss what it says, why it says it, and what it asks people to do.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use concrete purposes such as inform, explain, entertain, persuade, and warn; avoid subtle irony or mixed motives.

### Lesson 4: Audience-Fit Choices

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students that word choice, detail, and tone should fit the audience and purpose.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose the best message or revision for a simple audience-purpose situation.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A good communication choice respects the audience and helps the purpose succeed.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Speaker, audience, purpose, message, and basic respectful language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** The same words fit every audience; being loud or bossy makes a message stronger; polite language is only about manners, not effect.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given an audience and purpose, the student chooses a fitting message and rejects choices that are too vague, too harsh, or off-purpose.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for best audience-fit sentence; `dialogue-builder` for choosing a respectful next line; `error-correction` for revising a poor audience-fit sentence.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare two requests for the same thing, one to a friend and one to a teacher, and ask what changes and what stays respectful.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep choices short and emotionally safe; avoid manipulative or adversarial examples.

## Unit 2: Stories That Make A Point

### Lesson 1: Story Parts In Order

- **Teaching goal:** Use beginning, middle, and end to organize a short story or fable.
- **Student outcome:** The student can place story events in a clear order and name whether each event belongs at the beginning, middle, or end.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A beginning sets up characters or situation, a middle shows the main action or problem, and an end shows the outcome.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic story experience and sequence words.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Events can be retold in any order; the end is just the last sentence, even if it does not show an outcome.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given three to five events from a simple fable, the student orders them and labels beginning, middle, or end.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for event sequencing; `multiple-choice` for identifying story part; `passage-question` for a short fable context.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a three-event story and physically move cards into beginning, middle, and end.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use short events with clear cause-and-effect; avoid stories with flashbacks or parallel plots.

### Lesson 2: Key Events And Extra Details

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to choose events needed for a clear retelling and leave out extra details that do not affect the point.
- **Student outcome:** The student can distinguish key events from extra details in a short story.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A key event helps the listener understand what happened or why the story matters.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Beginning-middle-end order and basic story parts.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Every detail belongs in a retelling; favorite or funny details are always key; short retellings are weak.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a story and a list of details, the student selects the key events needed for a brief retelling.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for key event selection; `match-pairs` for key versus extra categories; `constructed-response` for a one-sentence reason why an event matters.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show a retelling that includes a distracting detail and ask whether it helps the listener understand the story.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one short passage at a time; limit detail lists to four or five items.

### Lesson 3: Retell A Fable Clearly

- **Teaching goal:** Combine order and key events into a concise fable retelling.
- **Student outcome:** The student can retell a short fable with key events and a clear outcome.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A clear retelling preserves the story's order and outcome without copying every word.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Story order, key events, and basic fable familiarity.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Retelling means copying the whole story; a retelling can skip the outcome; extra adjectives matter more than key actions.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student chooses or writes a short retelling that includes key events in order and the outcome.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for retelling sentence order; `passage-question` for choosing the best retelling; `constructed-response` for a two- or three-sentence retell.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Model a "too long," "too short," and "just right" retelling of the same fable.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use fables under 120 words; production should be short and scaffolded.

### Lesson 4: Find The Point Or Lesson

- **Teaching goal:** Help students identify or infer the point, lesson, or moral of a short fable.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose a lesson that fits the events and outcome of a fable.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A fable's lesson is what the story tries to show about choices, actions, or life.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Retelling, character choices, and outcome.
- **Likely misconceptions:** The lesson is always directly stated; any nice-sounding sentence can be the lesson; the lesson is the same as the setting or character name.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a fable, the student selects a fitting lesson and rejects lessons that do not match the events.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for fable lesson; `multiple-choice` for best moral; `constructed-response` for a short "I know because..." explanation.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Ask, "What does this story want the listener to learn?" after retelling the key events.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep moral choices concrete; avoid abstract proverbs that require adult background knowledge.

### Lesson 5: Connect Choices To The Lesson

- **Teaching goal:** Show how a character's choice and outcome support the point of a story.
- **Student outcome:** The student can explain how an action in a story helps show the lesson.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** The lesson is supported by what characters choose and what happens because of those choices.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Story lesson, key events, character choice, and outcome.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A lesson floats apart from the story; only the ending matters; a character's feelings are enough evidence without actions.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a fable lesson, the student chooses the event or action that best supports it and explains why.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for choices to outcomes; `passage-question` for evidence of lesson; `constructed-response` for a one-sentence choice-to-lesson link.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a two-column chart: "choice" and "what it shows."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep explanations to one sentence; use familiar action verbs and clear outcomes.

## Unit 3: Description And Amplification

### Lesson 1: Useful Details, Not Just More Details

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to judge whether a detail helps the audience understand the point.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose useful details and reject distracting details in a short sentence or paragraph.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A useful detail serves the purpose; a distracting detail pulls attention away from it.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Audience, purpose, message, and main idea.
- **Likely misconceptions:** More detail is always better; favorite details are always useful; a detail is useful if it is true.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a main idea and detail choices, the student selects details that clarify the main idea for the audience.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for best detail; `match-pairs` for useful versus distracting details; `passage-question` for detail choice in a paragraph.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare two details for the same sentence and ask which one helps the reader know more about the main idea.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use one main idea at a time; avoid requiring background knowledge to judge relevance.

### Lesson 2: Expand With Who, What, When, And Where

- **Teaching goal:** Use simple question prompts to expand bare sentences with basic clarifying information.
- **Student outcome:** The student can add who, what, when, or where information to make a sentence clearer.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Asking who, what, when, and where helps a writer add useful basic details.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Sentence awareness and useful detail judgment.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Expansion means adding random words; every sentence must answer every question; added details can contradict the original sentence.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student completes or chooses a sentence expansion that adds clear, relevant basic information.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for adding a detail; `multi-blank-cloze` for completing an expanded sentence; `constructed-response` for writing one expanded sentence.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Start with "The child ran." Add who, where, and when without changing the core meaning.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep sentences short; avoid long multi-clause expansions.

### Lesson 3: Precise Words And Sensory Clues

- **Teaching goal:** Help students choose specific verbs and sensory details that fit the meaning and purpose.
- **Student outcome:** The student can replace vague words with more precise words and add a fitting sensory clue.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Precise words help readers picture the action or object without adding clutter.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic verbs and adjectives, useful detail, and audience-purpose fit.
- **Likely misconceptions:** The fanciest word is the best word; any sensory word improves a sentence; precise words can ignore tone or audience.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a sentence, the student selects a more precise word or sensory detail that matches the scene.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for precise word choice; `fill-blank` for sensory detail; `error-correction` for replacing vague wording.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare "went" with "tiptoed," "hurried," and "stomped," and ask what each verb shows.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use common precise verbs; avoid vocabulary above the level unless context makes meaning clear.

### Lesson 4: Examples That Clarify The Main Idea

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to add examples that explain or support a main idea.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose or write an example that makes a main idea clearer.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** An example is a specific case that helps the audience understand a general idea.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Useful details, main idea, and basic audience-purpose awareness.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Examples are extra decorations; an example can be unrelated if it is interesting; a general restatement is the same as an example.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a main idea, the student chooses a specific example that fits and explains why it helps.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for best example; `match-pairs` for main ideas to examples; `constructed-response` for adding one example.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show "Our class helped the community" and compare vague support with a specific example.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep examples concrete and familiar; production can be one sentence.

## Unit 4: Claims, Reasons, And Examples

### Lesson 1: Claim Or Not?

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to recognize a claim or opinion as a statement that can be supported.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify which statements are claims and which are facts, questions, or details.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A claim says what someone thinks, believes, or wants the audience to accept.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Message and purpose; basic opinion language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Every sentence is a claim; questions are claims; facts and claims are the same.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given short statements, the student identifies claims and explains what could be supported.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for claim recognition; `match-pairs` for statement type; `text-input` for completing a simple "I think..." claim after support.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare "The library opens at nine" with "The library is the best quiet place to read."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar topics and avoid controversial claims.

### Lesson 2: Reasons That Support

- **Teaching goal:** Distinguish a reason from a claim and connect it with because.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose a reason that tells why a claim may be true or worth accepting.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A reason answers "Why do you think that?" and should connect directly to the claim.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Claim recognition and basic because sentences.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Repeating the claim is a reason; any sentence after because is support; emotional volume makes a reason stronger.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a claim, the student chooses a reason that supports it and rejects a repeated claim or unrelated sentence.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for best reason; `fill-blank` for because frame; `constructed-response` for one claim-plus-reason sentence.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Ask "Why?" after a claim and test whether the answer actually gives support.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep reasons one sentence long; avoid requiring outside facts.

### Lesson 3: Relevant And Irrelevant Support

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to decide whether support actually fits the claim and audience.
- **Student outcome:** The student can classify support as relevant or irrelevant and explain the mismatch in simple language.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Relevant support connects to the claim; irrelevant support may be true or interesting but does not help prove the claim.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Claim and reason structure, useful detail from Unit 3.
- **Likely misconceptions:** True details are always relevant; funny or personal details are strong support; all reasons are equally useful.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a claim and several support statements, the student chooses the relevant support and identifies the off-topic one.
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for relevant versus irrelevant support; `multiple-choice` for best support; `passage-question` for support in context.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a simple claim and show one reason that fits and one that changes the topic.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep claims concrete; use obvious but plausible off-topic distractors.

### Lesson 4: Examples Make Reasons Clear

- **Teaching goal:** Show how a specific example or detail can strengthen a reason.
- **Student outcome:** The student can add or choose an example that clarifies a reason for a claim.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Claim-reason-example chain: claim says what, reason says why, example shows how or when.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Claim, reason, relevance, and examples that clarify.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A second reason is always an example; an example can be vague; examples do not need to connect to the reason.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a claim and reason, the student selects a specific example that makes the reason clearer.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for strongest example; `multi-blank-cloze` for completing claim-reason-example; `constructed-response` for one added example.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Put the claim, reason, and example on three linked cards and ask what job each one does.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep examples familiar and specific; do not require long paragraphs.

### Lesson 5: Build A Claim-Reason-Example Response

- **Teaching goal:** Combine claim, reason, and example into a short supported response with a simple conclusion.
- **Student outcome:** The student can write or complete a compact response that includes a claim, one reason, one example, and a closing sentence.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A complete Level 1 supported response follows a simple chain: claim, because reason, for example, conclusion.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Claims, reasons, relevant support, examples, and audience-purpose awareness.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Longer is automatically stronger; the conclusion should introduce a new claim; examples can replace reasons.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student orders or writes a four-part supported response that stays on one claim.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for response order; `multi-blank-cloze` for response frame; `constructed-response` for a short produced response.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Model a four-sentence response and color-code each part by job.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use sentence frames; limit production to one compact paragraph.

## Unit 5: Comparison And Fair Evaluation

### Lesson 1: Compare With A Criterion

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students that a comparison needs one clear basis or criterion.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose a criterion that fits two things being compared.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A criterion tells what feature or quality is being compared.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Claims, reasons, useful details, and comparison words such as same and different.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Comparing means naming a favorite; the criterion can change during the comparison; any difference is equally important.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given two choices, the student identifies a fitting comparison criterion such as fairness, usefulness, clarity, or bravery.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for criterion choice; `match-pairs` for criteria to examples; `constructed-response` for naming one comparison basis.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare two classroom jobs by one criterion at a time, such as helpfulness or difficulty.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use two items or actions only; avoid multi-criterion scoring.

### Lesson 2: Same, Different, Stronger, Weaker

- **Teaching goal:** Build accurate comparison and contrast language connected to evidence.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use same, different, stronger, and weaker to compare two actions or ideas with support.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Comparison language should name the relationship and point to evidence.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Clear criterion and reason-example support.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Stronger means louder or more exciting; different means one is bad; comparison words do not need evidence.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given two short examples and a criterion, the student chooses the accurate comparison statement.
- **Recommended question types:** `fill-blank` for comparison words; `multiple-choice` for supported comparison; `constructed-response` for one comparison sentence.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a comparison chart with one criterion and evidence under each option.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep comparison sets short; use familiar adjectives and clear evidence.

### Lesson 3: Evaluate Actions With Evidence

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to evaluate actions or choices using evidence rather than personal feeling alone.
- **Student outcome:** The student can judge an action or choice and support the judgment with a reason and example.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Evaluation says how well an action fits a criterion and uses evidence from what happened.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Claim-reason-example structure, story choices, outcomes, and comparison criteria.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Feelings alone are enough; evidence can be invented; evaluating an action means judging a person's worth.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a short scenario, the student selects or writes an evidence-based evaluation of an action.
- **Recommended question types:** `passage-question` for scenario evidence; `multiple-choice` for best evaluation; `constructed-response` for a short action-evidence judgment.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a character choice from a fable and ask, "Was the action careful, fair, or useful? What shows that?"
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use safe fictional or school scenarios; avoid morally complex or emotionally loaded topics.

### Lesson 4: Praise And Blame Without Attack

- **Teaching goal:** Teach respectful praise and blame as evaluation of actions, choices, and evidence.
- **Student outcome:** The student can revise a personal attack or exaggeration into a fair, action-focused evaluation.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Fair evaluation names the action, gives evidence, and avoids attacking the person.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Evaluation with evidence, audience awareness, and respectful wording.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Praise means exaggeration; blame means insult; respectful wording makes evaluation weak.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given an unfair comment, the student identifies the problem and chooses or writes a fairer version.
- **Recommended question types:** `error-correction` for revising unfair comments; `multiple-choice` for respectful evaluation; `dialogue-builder` for choosing a fair response.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare "He is terrible" with "His choice was unfair because he changed the rule after the game started."
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Focus on fictional or low-stakes scenarios; avoid labels that target identity or personal traits.

## Unit 6: Revision And Short Delivery

### Lesson 1: Revision Is More Than Editing

- **Teaching goal:** Distinguish meaning-focused revision from surface editing.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify whether a change improves meaning, order, support, word choice, or mechanics.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Editing fixes surface correctness; revision improves communication.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic writing, audience-purpose awareness, and earlier response types.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Revision means spelling only; any change is revision; a draft is finished once mechanics are correct.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a change to a short response, the student names whether it is revision or editing and what it improves.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for revision versus editing; `match-pairs` for changes to improvement targets; `constructed-response` for explaining a useful revision.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show two changes to the same sentence: one fixes a capital letter and one adds a needed reason.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep drafts very short; focus on one change at a time.

### Lesson 2: Revise For Clear Order

- **Teaching goal:** Teach students to reorder events, reasons, or sentences so a response is easier to follow.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify and fix confusing order in a short response.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Clear order helps the audience follow what happened or why an idea makes sense.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Story order, sequence words, claim-reason-example order.
- **Likely misconceptions:** All included ideas matter more than their order; sequence words can be added randomly; conclusions can come before support.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given scrambled sentences, the student orders them logically and explains which sentence should come first or last.
- **Recommended question types:** `order-items` for sentence order; `multiple-choice` for best first sentence; `error-correction` for fixing an out-of-order response.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare two responses with the same sentences but different order and ask which one is easier to follow.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use three or four sentence responses; avoid long paragraphs.

### Lesson 3: Revise For Stronger Reasons And Examples

- **Teaching goal:** Improve support by replacing vague, weak, or irrelevant reasons and examples.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose a revision that strengthens support for a claim or evaluation.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Strong support is relevant and specific enough to help the audience see why the claim makes sense.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Claim, reason, example, relevant support, and evaluation with evidence.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Vague support is fine if the claim is clear; adding any example improves support; repeating the claim strengthens it.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a weak response, the student selects or writes a stronger reason or example.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for strongest revision; `error-correction` for replacing weak support; `constructed-response` for adding one specific example.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Use a response with "because it is good" and revise it into a reason plus example.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep revision choices close in length so students judge quality, not size.

### Lesson 4: Word Choice For Audience And Purpose

- **Teaching goal:** Revise word choice so a message fits its audience and purpose.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose words that are clear, respectful, and effective for a simple audience-purpose situation.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Word choice affects how the audience understands and receives the message.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Audience-fit choices, precise words, respectful evaluation, and basic revision.
- **Likely misconceptions:** The strongest word is the fanciest word; harsh words make a message more persuasive; word choice is only about correctness.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a sentence and audience, the student chooses a better word or phrase and explains the fit.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for best word choice; `dialogue-builder` for audience-fit response; `error-correction` for revising harsh or vague wording.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Compare "Give me that now" and "Could I use that after you are done?" for audience and purpose.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use low-stakes requests and feedback; avoid sarcasm or subtle register shifts.

### Lesson 5: Prepare And Deliver A Short Response

- **Teaching goal:** Prepare and deliver a brief oral response with clear voice, complete thought, and listener awareness.
- **Student outcome:** The student can use a simple note frame to produce a short spoken response that fits the audience and purpose.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** Good delivery helps listeners understand the message; it does not require perfect memorization.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Audience-purpose fit, claim-reason-example, retelling, revision, and respectful language.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Speaking well means memorizing perfectly; faster is better; listeners do not affect delivery choices.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student completes a speaking prompt with a clear message, one support point, and a listener-aware delivery checklist.
- **Recommended question types:** `speaking-prompt` for delivery practice; `constructed-response` for planning notes; `multiple-choice` for delivery-choice recognition.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Model a short response with a note card: point, one reason or event, final sentence.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep oral responses under 30 seconds; allow sentence frames and rehearsal.

## Unit 7: Cumulative Rhetoric Studio

### Lesson 1: Choose The Rhetorical Move

- **Teaching goal:** Help students decide which Rhetoric 1 move fits a task: retell, describe, support, compare, revise, or present.
- **Student outcome:** The student can choose a fitting rhetorical move for a short scenario and explain the clue.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** The task purpose tells which move will help most.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** All prior Level 1 moves and vocabulary.
- **Likely misconceptions:** The newest skill fits every task; a task can be answered well without noticing its purpose; describing and supporting are the same move.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given mixed task prompts, the student chooses the move and names a clue such as "tell what happened," "give a reason," or "make it clearer."
- **Recommended question types:** `match-pairs` for task to move; `multiple-choice` for best move; `constructed-response` for explaining a move choice.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Present a move menu and sort five short prompts before answering any of them.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Use familiar tasks; do not combine more than two moves in early items.

### Lesson 2: Fix The Weak Response

- **Teaching goal:** Diagnose what is weak or missing in a short response and choose a focused fix.
- **Student outcome:** The student can identify whether a response needs audience fit, order, detail, support, comparison, or revision.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A weak response often misses one rhetorical job; naming the missing job helps choose a useful fix.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Audience fit, order, useful details, claim support, comparison criteria, and revision targets.
- **Likely misconceptions:** Weak responses only have spelling problems; the fix is always to add more; all weak responses have the same problem.
- **Evidence of mastery:** Given a short weak response, the student chooses the most useful diagnosis and revision.
- **Recommended question types:** `multiple-choice` for diagnosis; `error-correction` for focused revision; `match-pairs` for weakness to fix.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Show three weak responses with different problems and name the missing rhetorical move in each.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Keep weak responses short; make only one main weakness prominent per item.

### Lesson 3: Level 1 Mini Performance

- **Teaching goal:** Bring Rhetoric 1 together in a short planned, revised, and delivered response.
- **Student outcome:** The student can plan a compact response, include a fitting rhetorical move, revise one target, and present or describe the response.
- **Key idea, model, text, representation, form, or procedure:** A strong Level 1 response fits audience and purpose, uses the right move, includes support or detail, and is revised before delivery.
- **Prerequisite knowledge:** Full Rhetoric 1 sequence.
- **Likely misconceptions:** A performance must be long; revision happens after communication instead of during it; oral and written responses use unrelated skills.
- **Evidence of mastery:** The student completes a short written or spoken response with a checklist showing audience, purpose, move, support/detail, and one revision.
- **Recommended question types:** `constructed-response` for planning and written response; `speaking-prompt` for oral delivery; `multi-blank-cloze` for completing a planning frame.
- **Suggested intro/teaching note:** Keep the final task small: one short passage or scenario, one chosen move, one revision, and one brief presentation.
- **Reading-level and cognitive-load constraints:** Avoid long essay or speech expectations; use checklist support and allow sentence frames.
