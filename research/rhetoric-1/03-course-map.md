# Rhetoric 1 Course Map

Track: Rhetoric
Level: Level 1
Sources: Accepted research brief at `research/rhetoric-1/01-research-brief.md` and level blueprint at `research/rhetoric-1/02-level-blueprint.md`

## Course Design Notes

Rhetoric 1 moves from noticing purpose in communication to producing short, clear, supported responses. The sequence begins with audience, speaker, purpose, and message; builds narrative order and story point through fables; adds description and amplification; introduces claim, reason, and example; develops comparison and evidence-based evaluation; then consolidates revision and oral delivery. The final unit asks students to choose a rhetorical move instead of being told exactly which one to use. Lesson counts are intentionally uneven: narration and support need more practice, while the capstone unit is shorter and cumulative.

## Unit 1: Purposeful Communication

### Unit Purpose

Introduce rhetoric as communication aimed at someone for a reason. Students learn to identify speaker, audience, purpose, message, and likely effect in familiar texts, classroom situations, and short speeches.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify the speaker or writer in a short communication.
- Identify the intended audience in a simple scenario or text.
- Distinguish purpose from message.
- Match language choices to audience and purpose.
- Explain, in a short sentence, why a communication fits or misses its audience.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Rhetoric, communication, speaker, writer, audience, listener, reader, purpose, message, inform, explain, entertain, persuade, effect, respectful.

### Prerequisites

Students should be able to read short paragraphs, listen to short models, give a personal opinion orally, and understand basic classroom communication situations.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This opening unit supplies the lens for the entire level. Audience and purpose return in narration, description, claims, comparison, revision, delivery, and cumulative tasks.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Rhetoric means fancy words.
- The audience is always "everyone."
- Purpose and message mean the same thing.
- Strong communication is only about sounding impressive.
- Persuasion means getting your way.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

3-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use short audience-purpose retrieval at the start of later units. Revisit this unit whenever students choose details, reasons, examples, or speaking choices.

## Unit 2: Stories That Make A Point

### Unit Purpose

Build narrative control through fables, short stories, and personal-event retellings. Students identify beginning, middle, end, key events, and the point or lesson a story is meant to show.

### Student Outcomes

- Put story events in beginning, middle, and end order.
- Retell a short story without losing key events.
- Distinguish key events from extra details.
- Identify or infer a story's point, lesson, or message.
- Explain how an event or choice supports the story's point.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Story, fable, narrator, character, setting, event, beginning, middle, end, sequence, retell, key event, extra detail, point, lesson, moral, choice, outcome.

### Prerequisites

Students need basic story knowledge such as character, setting, problem, and solution; ability to listen to or read a short fable; and familiarity with sequence words.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit uses Unit 1 audience and message ideas in story form. It prepares students to use examples, evaluate actions, compare choices, and present brief narrative responses later.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Retelling means repeating every detail.
- A story's lesson is always stated directly.
- Sequence does not matter if all events are mentioned.
- The funniest or most exciting event must be the main point.
- A character's choice and the story's lesson are unrelated.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Later claim, comparison, revision, and delivery tasks should reuse short fable and story contexts. Event ordering and story point should be delayed-review priorities.

## Unit 3: Description And Amplification

### Unit Purpose

Teach students how to make ideas clearer and more vivid by adding useful details. Students expand sentences and short paragraphs with precise words, sensory details, examples, and logical order while avoiding detail overload.

### Student Outcomes

- Distinguish useful details from distracting or unrelated details.
- Expand a bare sentence with who, what, when, where, why, or how.
- Choose precise verbs and descriptive words that fit the purpose.
- Add an example that helps the audience understand.
- Improve a thin paragraph without burying the main idea.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Describe, detail, useful detail, distracting detail, sensory detail, precise verb, expand, amplify, example, clarify, order, emphasis, main idea.

### Prerequisites

Students need basic sentence awareness, familiar adjective and verb language, short paragraph reading, and the Unit 1 idea that details should serve an audience and purpose.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit strengthens retellings from Unit 2 and prepares students to provide examples and evidence in Unit 4. It also supplies revision targets for Unit 6.

### Misconceptions To Address

- More detail is always better.
- Any adjective makes writing stronger.
- A precise word is the longest or fanciest word.
- Examples are decoration rather than support.
- Adding details can ignore order or purpose.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

3-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use detail-choice prompts in later claim and revision lessons. Review "useful for the audience and purpose" as the test for amplification.

## Unit 4: Claims, Reasons, And Examples

### Unit Purpose

Introduce age-appropriate argument readiness. Students learn to state a simple claim or opinion, support it with relevant reasons, and strengthen it with examples or details.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify a claim or opinion in a short response.
- Distinguish a reason from a claim.
- Choose a relevant reason that supports a claim.
- Add a specific example or detail to make support clearer.
- Write or complete a short claim-reason-example response.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Opinion, claim, reason, support, because, evidence, example, relevant, irrelevant, specific, vague, conclusion, audience fit.

### Prerequisites

Students should be able to give a personal opinion and one oral reason, identify main messages, read short scenarios, and add useful details from Unit 3.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit depends on audience-purpose awareness and useful detail. It prepares students for comparison, evaluation, revision of reasons, and cumulative persuasive or explanatory mini-tasks.

### Misconceptions To Address

- A claim proves itself.
- A reason is good if it is loud or emotional.
- Any sentence after "because" is a reason.
- A personal preference is enough support for every audience.
- Examples do not need to connect to the claim.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Claim, reason, example, and relevance should be spaced through all remaining units. Later comparison and revision tasks should ask students to diagnose irrelevant reasons and vague examples.

## Unit 5: Comparison And Fair Evaluation

### Unit Purpose

Help students compare and evaluate actions, choices, people, or ideas using clear criteria and evidence. Praise and blame are introduced cautiously as evaluation of actions or choices, not personal attack.

### Student Outcomes

- Compare two things by naming a clear criterion.
- Use same, different, compare, and contrast language accurately.
- Distinguish preference from evidence-based comparison.
- Evaluate an action or choice using a reason and example.
- Revise unfair or personal criticism into evidence-based evaluation.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Compare, contrast, same, different, criterion, fair, useful, clear, brave, careful, stronger, weaker, praise, blame, action, choice, evidence, respectful.

### Prerequisites

Students need claim-reason-example structure, basic comparison words, story choice and outcome ideas from Unit 2, and audience awareness from Unit 1.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit combines narration, claims, reasons, and detail. It prepares students for revision and delivery tasks that require respectful, audience-aware wording.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Comparing means saying which one you like better.
- Praise means exaggerating.
- Blame means attacking a person.
- A criterion can change halfway through a comparison.
- Evidence is optional if the opinion is strong.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

3-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Review relevance, examples, and audience fit while comparing. Later revision tasks should revisit personal attack versus action-focused evaluation.

## Unit 6: Revision And Short Delivery

### Unit Purpose

Teach revision as improving meaning and effect, not only fixing spelling. Students practice focused revision targets and short oral delivery with clear voice, complete thought, and listener awareness.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify one useful revision target in short writing.
- Improve word choice, order, detail, reason, example, or conclusion.
- Choose a revision that better fits an audience and purpose.
- Prepare a brief oral response from a simple note or sentence frame.
- Use clear voice, complete thought, and respectful listener awareness in a speaking task.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Revise, edit, clarity, word choice, order, stronger reason, specific example, conclusion, audience fit, oral response, voice, pace, listener, respectful.

### Prerequisites

Students need the earlier level routines: audience-purpose analysis, retelling, useful detail, claim-reason-example support, and fair comparison.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit turns all previous skills into improvement habits. It directly prepares the final unit, where students choose and polish short rhetorical responses.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Revision means spelling fixes only.
- The first draft is finished if it has no obvious mistakes.
- Longer revision is always better revision.
- Speaking well means memorizing perfectly.
- Audience awareness is only for writing, not speaking.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Interleave revision targets from previous units rather than isolating them. Use short prompts so the focus stays on one improvement at a time.

## Unit 7: Cumulative Rhetoric Studio

### Unit Purpose

Bring the level together through mixed mini-rhetoric tasks. Students choose whether a task calls for retelling, describing, supporting, comparing, revising, or presenting, and they complete short responses with audience and purpose in mind.

### Student Outcomes

- Choose an appropriate rhetorical move for a short task.
- Combine two or more Level 1 skills, such as retell plus point or claim plus example.
- Diagnose a weak response and name the missing rhetorical move.
- Produce a compact written or oral response for a familiar audience.
- Explain how the response fits its purpose.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Rhetorical move, retell, describe, explain, claim, reason, example, compare, revise, present, audience, purpose, message, support, conclusion.

### Prerequisites

Students need all prior Rhetoric 1 concepts and routines, with especially strong control of audience-purpose, story point, useful detail, relevant support, fair comparison, and focused revision.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit integrates the full level and prepares Rhetoric 2, where students will do more explicit progymnasmata work with fable, narrative, chreia, proverb, imitation, amplification, reduction, and oral narration.

### Misconceptions To Address

- The newest skill is the right move for every task.
- A response is strong if it is long.
- Audience and purpose can be ignored once the content is correct.
- Revision is separate from communication.
- Oral and written rhetoric use completely different skills.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

2-4 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Use deliberately mixed review with delayed retrieval of all major routines. Keep tasks compact but varied so mastery reflects transfer rather than same-day pattern matching.
