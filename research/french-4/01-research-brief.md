# French 4 Research Brief

Track: French  
Level: Level 4  
Intended student profile: learners at the Novice High to Intermediate Low transition who can produce connected sentences and are ready for scaffolded narration, paragraph-length communication, and more flexible familiar-topic interaction.

This brief defines outcomes and instructional priorities only. It does not define units, lessons, or questions.

## 1. End-Of-Level Outcomes

By the end of French 4, students should be able to communicate at an Intermediate Low level in familiar contexts: narrate simple past events with scaffolded passé composé and imparfait recognition/use, discuss near future and simple future plans, use object pronouns and commands in controlled contexts, make comparisons, describe travel, shopping, health, chores, celebrations, and Francophone regions, explain preferences with reasons, interpret short connected resources, and write short paragraphs with sentence strings.

## 2. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should control connected present-tense sentences, être/avoir/aller/faire, regular present patterns, questions, negation, articles/partitives in common contexts, adjective agreement, daily routines, hobbies, community vocabulary, and short text/audio interpretation.

## 3. Major Concept Clusters

- **Intermediate Low Communication:** sentence strings, familiar-topic conversations, short presentations, and paragraph writing.
- **Narration Across Time:** passé composé and imparfait in scaffolded contexts; sequencing past events and describing background.
- **Plans And Preferences:** near future, simple future exposure, reasons, comparisons, and polite functional phrases.
- **Expanded Domains:** travel, shopping, health, chores, celebrations, Francophone regions, and community life.
- **Cohesion Tools:** object pronouns, connectors, sequence words, commands, and paragraph organization.

## 4. Recommended Progression

Review present-tense communication and connected sentences, then introduce past-time narration through photos, timelines, and short stories. Teach passé composé/imparfait contrast as meaning in context before full accuracy. Add near future and plans, then travel/shopping/health/celebration domains. Introduce object pronouns and commands through controlled communicative contexts. End with short paragraph and conversation tasks.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Past tense is one French form matching English past.
- Passé composé and imparfait can be chosen by a single keyword rule.
- Auxiliary choice in passé composé is random.
- Object pronouns go where English puts objects.
- Commands are just present-tense forms with louder tone.
- Future meaning requires only one future tense.
- Francophone culture means only France.

## 6. Developmental Constraints And Reading/Load Concerns

Keep past narration scaffolded with visuals and timelines. Do not expect full spontaneous control of passé composé/imparfait or all auxiliary patterns. Limit pronoun types and placements. Maintain sound-spelling/listening practice. Keep authentic resources short and glossed. Paragraph writing should be modeled and concise.

## 7. Assessment Implications

Assess meaning and communication before full form accuracy. Use listening/passage questions for past-time meaning, multi-blank cloze for controlled forms, dialogue-builder for travel/shopping/health, constructed response for short paragraphs, and match-pairs for pronoun/reference relationships. Distractors should reveal auxiliary confusion, tense-as-time-only errors, pronoun placement confusion, and culture overgeneralization.

## 8. Spiral Review Priorities

Present tense, être/avoir/aller/faire, regular verbs, negation, articles/partitives, adjective agreement, questions, connectors, time expressions, routines, places, food, weather, directions, passé composé/imparfait meaning, near future, object pronouns, comparisons, commands, and Francophone geography.

## 9. Concepts That Should Not Be Taught Yet

Avoid full subjunctive system, all object-pronoun combinations, complex relative clauses, literary analysis in French, advanced debate, and spontaneous abstract discussion as required mastery.
