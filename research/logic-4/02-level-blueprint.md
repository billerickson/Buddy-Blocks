# Logic 4 Level Blueprint

Track: Logic
Level: Level 4
Source: Accepted research brief at `research/logic-4/01-research-brief.md`

## 1. Level Mission

Logic 4 helps students move from informal argument awareness into controlled formal and applied reasoning. Students learn to translate simple claims into symbolic form, use truth conditions and truth tables to test compound statements, distinguish deductive validity from inductive strength and abductive explanation, and evaluate claims that use data, probability, science, sources, objections, and rebuttals. By the end of the level, students should be able to slow down a short real-world argument, name its structure, check whether the support actually fits the conclusion, and choose a fair revision or response without relying on slogans, surface certainty, or background knowledge alone.

## 2. Durable Student Outcomes

By the end of Logic 4, students should be able to:

1. Translate familiar statements into simple symbolic notation using statement variables, negation, conjunction, disjunction, and conditionals.
2. Interpret the truth conditions for `not`, `and`, `or`, and `if...then` without confusing ordinary conversational shortcuts with logical structure.
3. Complete and read basic truth tables for one- and two-variable compound statements.
4. Use truth-table evidence to identify when an argument form is valid or when a conclusion can be false while premises are true.
5. Distinguish deductive, inductive, and abductive reasoning by the kind of support each gives a conclusion.
6. Evaluate introductory probability, trend, graph, sample, and correlation claims without overclaiming from limited data.
7. Analyze scientific explanations by separating hypothesis, evidence, alternative explanation, replication, and conclusion.
8. Build and interpret small argument maps with main claim, linked reasons, objections, rebuttals, and missing assumptions.
9. Recognize the structure of a simple debate case and choose fair rebuttals that address reasons and evidence.
10. Apply logical, data, and argument-map tools to short academic, civic, media, and everyday arguments.

## 3. Concept And Skill Progression

The level begins with propositions as statements that can be true or false. Students translate ordinary sentences into statement letters and then add connectives one at a time: negation, conjunction, inclusive disjunction, and conditional statements. Early work should keep symbols minimal and consistent so notation supports thinking rather than becoming the main challenge.

Students then learn truth conditions and truth tables. They begin with single connectives, then combine two statement variables, and finally use small tables to test whether a conclusion follows in every relevant row. Conditional reasoning receives special attention because students commonly assume the converse or treat a conditional as proof that the antecedent happened.

After formal tools are stable, the level shifts to reasoning types. Students compare deductive certainty, inductive strength, and abductive best explanation. They learn that inductive reasoning can be strong without being guaranteed and that abductive reasoning selects the best available explanation while remaining open to revision.

The middle of the level applies those distinctions to data, probability, and scientific reasoning. Students interpret samples, trends, graphs, correlation claims, probability language, hypotheses, evidence, alternative explanations, and replication exposure. They practice identifying what a dataset or study can support and what it cannot support yet.

The final portion makes argument structure visible. Students build compact argument maps, label linked reasons, find missing assumptions, add objections, and choose rebuttals that actually meet the objection. They then analyze debate cases and short real-world arguments using the full toolkit: symbols when structure matters, data cautions when evidence is quantitative, and maps when reasons and objections need to be organized.

## 4. Vocabulary, Representation, Model, Text, And Form Progression

Early vocabulary should stabilize statement, proposition, variable, symbol, negation, not, conjunction, and, disjunction, or, inclusive or, conditional, antecedent, consequent, converse, premise, conclusion, valid, invalid, true, false, and truth table. Representations should progress from natural-language sentences to statement letters, simple symbolic forms, two-column truth tables, and small argument forms.

Reasoning vocabulary should include deductive, inductive, abductive, certainty, strength, pattern, evidence, explanation, best explanation, counterexample, and alternative. Texts should move from one-sentence examples to short scenarios where students classify the kind of support and choose a fair conclusion.

Data and science vocabulary should include sample, representative, trend, graph, scale, probability, likely, unlikely, certain, impossible, correlation, causation, hypothesis, evidence, observation, experiment, replication, alternative explanation, claim, and conclusion. Representations should include small tables, simple graphs, brief study summaries, cause/effect diagrams, and explanation charts.

Argument vocabulary should include main claim, reason, linked reasons, support, assumption, objection, rebuttal, debate case, source, evidence, warrant, and overclaim. Representations should progress from underlining claims and reasons to compact argument maps, objection/rebuttal pairs, debate-case outlines, and short applied argument passages.

## 5. Fluency Expectations

Logic 4 fluency is not speed-first. Students should become fluent enough to translate common forms such as "not P," "P and Q," "P or Q," and "if P then Q"; complete truth rows for basic connectives; recognize common conditional errors; classify reasoning types; and label argument-map parts without restarting from definitions each time. Retrieval practice should be brief, spaced, and mixed with application so students retain vocabulary and structures while learning to use them in context.

Students should also develop practical fluency in careful reading: finding the exact claim, identifying what evidence is offered, asking whether the evidence supports the conclusion, and noticing when a graph, sample, probability statement, or rebuttal is being overused.

## 6. Conceptual Understanding Expectations

Students should understand that symbolic logic models the structure of statements, not every shade of ordinary conversation. A truth table shows what follows from a form under possible truth values; it does not prove whether a real-world claim is factually true. Students should understand inclusive `or`, the difference between a conditional and its converse, and the idea that validity depends on form rather than on whether the premises happen to be true.

Students should understand that deductive, inductive, and abductive reasoning answer different support questions. Deduction asks whether the conclusion must follow if the premises are true. Induction asks how strongly a pattern or evidence supports a likely conclusion. Abduction asks which explanation best fits the available evidence while staying open to better evidence.

Students should understand that data and scientific evidence have limits. A graph can show a trend without explaining its cause; a sample can be too small or unrepresentative; correlation alone does not prove causation; and a hypothesis becomes stronger when evidence rules out alternatives and can be checked again.

Students should understand argument maps as reasoning checks rather than decorative outlines. Linked reasons may work together, objections target specific parts of the support, rebuttals should answer objections, and missing assumptions often carry the weight of an argument.

## 7. Application And Transfer Expectations

Students should transfer logic tools to short academic, scientific, civic, media, debate, and everyday examples. They should symbolize simple claims when structure matters, choose a truth-table row that exposes an invalid form, classify the type of reasoning a passage uses, and explain why a conclusion is certain, likely, possible, or not yet supported.

Students should apply data and science cautions to new scenarios by asking about sample, scale, trend, probability, correlation, alternative explanations, and replication. They should be able to choose a more careful claim from the same evidence rather than only reject weak claims.

Students should use argument maps and debate structures to respond fairly. Transfer is visible when students identify the main claim, separate reasons from evidence, notice missing assumptions, choose a targeted objection, and select a rebuttal that addresses the objection instead of merely repeating the original claim.

## 8. Review And Spacing Plan

Review should be cumulative from the first unit. Statement, premise, conclusion, valid, sound, conditional, necessary/sufficient exposure, negation, conjunction, disjunction, and truth table should recur in short mixed checks after formal-symbol lessons have ended. Conditional and converse errors should be revisited during deduction, data, science, and applied argument work.

Reasoning-type practice should be spaced across data, scientific explanation, argument maps, debate, and final transfer tasks. Students should repeatedly decide whether a claim is deductive, inductive, or abductive and then answer the more important follow-up: what kind of support would make it stronger?

Data and science concepts should recur in later argument analysis. Probability language, sample quality, correlation/causation, alternative explanation, and overclaiming from a graph should appear in small, varied contexts so students learn to identify the same reasoning issue across different topics.

Argument-map vocabulary should be reviewed by using maps as a tool for later debate and real-world passages. Objections and rebuttals should revisit earlier misconceptions: converse errors, weak induction, unsupported causal claims, and conclusions that outrun evidence.

## 9. Mastery Evidence For The End Of The Level

End-of-level mastery should be evidenced by a balanced body of compact tasks. A student who has mastered Logic 4 can translate simple natural-language claims into symbolic forms, complete truth tables for basic compound statements, and use truth-table evidence to explain why a simple argument form is valid or invalid.

The student can classify deductive, inductive, and abductive reasoning; identify when a conclusion is guaranteed, probable, or only the best current explanation; and improve a weak claim by naming the evidence or assumption it still needs. The student can interpret simple data and science scenarios without confusing correlation with causation, probability with certainty, or a graph with proof beyond its data.

The strongest mastery evidence is transfer. Given a short unfamiliar argument, the student can find the main claim, map the reasons, identify a missing assumption or objection, choose a fair rebuttal, and state a more careful conclusion that fits the evidence.
