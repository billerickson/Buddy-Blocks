# Logic 4 Course Map

Track: Logic
Level: Level 4
Sources: Accepted research brief at `research/logic-4/01-research-brief.md` and level blueprint at `research/logic-4/02-level-blueprint.md`

## Course Design Notes

This course map moves from formal structure to applied analysis. Students first learn to symbolize statements and read truth conditions, then use truth tables to test simple forms. The middle units broaden support types: deductive, inductive, abductive, data, probability, and scientific explanation. The final units make reasoning visible through argument maps, debate cases, objections, rebuttals, and short real-world arguments. The unit sequence is intentionally uneven: symbolic and mapping work need more lessons because later transfer depends on those tools, while scientific reasoning can be compact if data cautions have already been developed.

## Unit 1: Symbolic Statements And Connectives

### Unit Purpose

Introduce students to propositions, statement variables, and the basic connectives that let ordinary claims be represented symbolically. The unit emphasizes meaning before manipulation: students translate familiar sentences, read symbols back into words, and avoid common errors with `not`, `and`, and inclusive `or`.

### Student Outcomes

- Decide whether a sentence is a proposition that can be true or false.
- Use statement letters to represent simple claims.
- Translate negation, conjunction, and disjunction between words and symbols.
- Distinguish inclusive `or` from "exactly one" language.
- Explain what must be true for a simple compound statement to be true.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Statement, proposition, true, false, variable, symbol, translate, negation, not, conjunction, and, disjunction, or, inclusive or, compound statement, truth condition.

### Prerequisites

Students should already know premises, conclusions, categorical statements, validity/truth/soundness exposure, informal fallacies, and basic conditional language from earlier logic levels.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit formalizes prior work with claims and prepares every later truth-table and argument-form lesson. It also supports applied argument analysis by giving students a compact way to separate claim structure from topic details.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Treating questions, commands, or opinions without a truth claim as propositions.
- Believing a statement letter is a secret abbreviation for a word rather than a placeholder for a whole claim.
- Reading `not P` as the opposite of one word instead of the denial of the whole statement.
- Thinking `P or Q` always means exactly one option.
- Assuming symbols make a statement more true or more important.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Keep premise, conclusion, valid, true, false, and counterexample active in quick review. Begin spaced retrieval of the three basic connectives immediately, then revisit inclusive `or` during truth tables, data claims, and debate analysis.

## Unit 2: Conditionals, Truth Tables, And Validity

### Unit Purpose

Teach students to interpret conditionals and use truth tables as systematic checks for compound statements and simple argument forms. The unit treats truth tables as reasoning tools, not as isolated symbol puzzles.

### Student Outcomes

- Translate simple `if...then` statements into `P -> Q` form.
- Identify antecedent, consequent, and converse.
- Complete truth rows for negation, conjunction, disjunction, and conditionals.
- Build and read basic two-variable truth tables.
- Use a truth-table row to test whether a simple argument form is valid.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Conditional, if, then, antecedent, consequent, converse, truth table, row, column, compound statement, possible case, valid form, invalid form, counterexample row, premises, conclusion.

### Prerequisites

Students need Unit 1 symbols and connectives, earlier exposure to validity and conditionals, and comfort reading small tables.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit extends Unit 1 into systematic testing. It prepares Unit 3's distinction between deductive certainty and weaker support, and it gives later applied units a way to expose conditional and converse errors in science, data, and debate claims.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Assuming `if P then Q` proves `if Q then P`.
- Thinking a conditional is false whenever the antecedent is false.
- Treating a truth table as a real-world fact checker.
- Deciding validity by whether the conclusion sounds believable.
- Looking at only one row instead of checking the relevant possible cases.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Review Unit 1 connectives inside truth rows rather than as detached definitions. Revisit premise/conclusion and valid/invalid vocabulary in every argument-form question. Later units should include short conditional checks so converse errors do not remain isolated.

## Unit 3: Deductive, Inductive, And Abductive Reasoning

### Unit Purpose

Broaden students' understanding of support. Students learn that good reasoning does not always have the same kind of force: deduction can guarantee, induction can make a conclusion likely, and abduction can choose the best explanation while remaining revisable.

### Student Outcomes

- Classify deductive, inductive, and abductive reasoning in short passages.
- Explain whether a conclusion is guaranteed, strongly supported, weakly supported, or the best available explanation.
- Identify counterexamples for deductive claims and missing evidence for inductive claims.
- Compare alternative explanations and choose which one currently fits best.
- Revise overconfident conclusions into claims that match the support.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Deductive, inductive, abductive, guarantee, certainty, strong, weak, likely, pattern, sample, evidence, explanation, best explanation, alternative, counterexample, revise.

### Prerequisites

Students need premise/conclusion vocabulary, validity exposure, truth-table validity checks, and familiarity with examples, patterns, and explanations in ordinary reading.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit clarifies what truth tables can and cannot do, then prepares students for data and scientific reasoning. It also supplies language for final argument analysis: "valid," "strong," and "best explanation" should not be used interchangeably.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Thinking inductive reasoning is just guessing.
- Treating abductive reasoning as certain proof.
- Calling an argument deductive only because it sounds formal.
- Rejecting all non-deductive reasoning as useless.
- Confusing a counterexample with an objection that only weakens probability.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

3-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Interleave short deductive-validity checks with reasoning-type classification. Later data, science, and debate units should keep asking what kind of support a claim offers before judging whether the support is enough.

## Unit 4: Data, Probability, And Cause Claims

### Unit Purpose

Help students evaluate claims that use numbers, graphs, samples, probability language, and correlations. Students practice making the careful claim that the evidence supports instead of treating every graph or percentage as decisive proof.

### Student Outcomes

- Interpret simple samples, trends, graph scales, and percentage or probability language.
- Distinguish possible, likely, unlikely, certain, and impossible claims.
- Identify when a sample may be too small, biased, or unrepresentative.
- Distinguish correlation from causation in introductory scenarios.
- Choose a more careful conclusion from limited data.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Data, sample, representative, biased, trend, graph, scale, percent, probability, possible, likely, unlikely, certain, impossible, correlation, causation, cause, evidence, overclaim.

### Prerequisites

Students need inductive reasoning language from Unit 3, basic graph and percent exposure, and the habit of separating a claim from its evidence.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit applies induction and prepares scientific reasoning. It also gives students tools for argument maps and debate cases when evidence comes from surveys, graphs, studies, or probability statements.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Believing bigger-looking bars always show a bigger difference regardless of scale.
- Treating a small or biased sample as proof about everyone.
- Reading "likely" as guaranteed.
- Thinking correlation proves causation.
- Accepting a caption's claim without checking the graph or data.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

4-5 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Continue spaced practice with deductive/inductive/abductive classification. Review inclusive `or` and conditional language in probability and cause claims when useful. Revisit sample, trend, and correlation concepts in the science and applied argument units.

## Unit 5: Scientific Reasoning And Competing Explanations

### Unit Purpose

Apply logic and data cautions to scientific explanations. Students learn to separate hypothesis, evidence, observation, alternative explanation, replication, and conclusion so they can judge whether an explanation is well supported.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify hypothesis, evidence, observation, and conclusion in a short scientific scenario.
- Explain why alternative explanations matter.
- Recognize how replication or repeated trials can strengthen a claim.
- Distinguish evidence for a hypothesis from proof that no other explanation is possible.
- Choose a careful scientific conclusion that fits the available evidence.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Hypothesis, observation, evidence, experiment, variable exposure, alternative explanation, replication, repeated trial, conclusion, supports, rules out, revisable.

### Prerequisites

Students need reasoning-type distinctions, sample and correlation cautions, and basic reading of short study or experiment summaries.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This unit applies Units 3-4 to science contexts and prepares students to map arguments where evidence and alternatives must be organized. It gives final transfer tasks a disciplined way to evaluate explanatory claims.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Thinking one observation proves a hypothesis.
- Ignoring alternative explanations that fit the same evidence.
- Treating replication as needless repetition.
- Confusing correlation with a tested cause.
- Assuming scientific conclusions cannot be revised.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

3-4 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Review abductive reasoning through best-explanation tasks. Revisit data cautions, especially sample size, correlation, and overclaiming. Later argument-map lessons should include science-flavored passages so this reasoning remains active.

## Unit 6: Argument Maps, Debate Cases, And Applied Analysis

### Unit Purpose

Make complex arguments visible and teach students to respond fairly. Students map claims and support, identify linked reasons and missing assumptions, handle objections and rebuttals, recognize debate case structure, and apply the full Logic 4 toolkit to short real-world arguments.

### Student Outcomes

- Identify a main claim, reasons, evidence, objections, rebuttals, and missing assumptions.
- Build and interpret compact argument maps with linked reasons.
- Choose objections that target a specific reason, assumption, or evidence link.
- Choose rebuttals that answer the objection instead of repeating the claim.
- Recognize the structure of a simple debate case.
- Evaluate short academic, civic, media, and everyday arguments using formal, data, science, and mapping tools.

### Key Concepts And Vocabulary

Main claim, reason, evidence, linked reasons, support, assumption, missing assumption, objection, rebuttal, debate case, source, warrant exposure, overclaim, fair response, revise.

### Prerequisites

Students need premise/conclusion vocabulary, basic formal-symbol and truth-table awareness, reasoning-type distinctions, data cautions, and scientific explanation vocabulary.

### Connections To Prior And Later Units

This final unit consolidates the level. It turns earlier tools into practical argument analysis: symbolic form for structure, truth tables for validity, reasoning-type language for support, data cautions for evidence, and science reasoning for explanations.

### Misconceptions To Address

- Treating an argument map as a decorative outline instead of a reasoning check.
- Listing many reasons without seeing whether they work together.
- Calling any disagreement an objection, even if it misses the argument.
- Answering an objection by repeating the original claim.
- Treating a confident source, graph, or debate speech as enough proof by itself.

### Suggested Lesson Count Range

5-6 lessons.

### Review, Spacing, Or Interleaving Notes

Interleave all prior vocabulary and tools. Final lessons should require students to choose which tool fits the problem rather than being told "use a truth table" or "find a correlation error." Review should emphasize fair, careful revision of claims.
