# Unit 4: Data, Probability, And Cause Claims

Track: Logic
Level: Level 4
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/logic-4/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn to evaluate claims that use samples, graphs, probability language, and causal interpretations. By the end of the unit, students should be able to identify what the evidence actually supports, what it leaves open, and how to state a more careful conclusion.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

The unit follows reasoning-type work because most data claims are inductive and many cause claims rely on abductive explanations. Students now have the language to say "this supports the claim somewhat" without pretending that every chart or percentage gives proof.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: sample-to-population scope, graph scale, trend reading, probability as likelihood, correlation versus causation, evidence limits.
- Vocabulary: data, sample, representative, biased, trend, graph, scale, percent, probability, possible, likely, unlikely, certain, impossible, correlation, causation, overclaim.
- Representations and models: small survey summaries, simple bar and line graphs described in text, probability word ladders, correlation/cause diagrams, careful-claim revisions.
- Texts and forms: brief study summaries, graph captions, survey claims, everyday probability predictions, and cause claims from observed patterns.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need inductive and abductive reasoning language, basic graph and percentage familiarity, and the ability to distinguish evidence from conclusion.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- A sample from one group automatically represents everyone.
- A graph's caption is always the best interpretation of the data.
- "Likely" means guaranteed.
- Correlation proves causation.
- A dramatic number is strong evidence even when the source or scope is weak.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Start with samples because scope controls what a data claim can mean. Then read graphs and scales so students check the evidence itself. Next, teach probability words as degrees of likelihood rather than promises. End with correlation and causation because cause claims require students to combine data caution with alternative-explanation thinking.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Review induction in every sample and trend lesson. Review abduction when evaluating cause claims. Use short connective and conditional review when statements combine data claims with "or" or "if...then" wording.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares scientific reasoning by making students cautious with samples, trends, probability, and cause. It prepares argument maps and debate cases by giving students diagnostic language for evidence quality.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Match a sample to the population it can reasonably support.
- Identify when a graph scale or trend has been overread.
- Interpret likely, unlikely, certain, possible, and impossible correctly.
- Distinguish correlation from causation.
- Choose a careful conclusion that fits limited data.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Samples And Scope**: Decide who a sample can and cannot represent.
2. **Graphs, Trends, And Scale**: Read simple graph evidence before accepting the caption.
3. **Probability Words And Careful Predictions**: Interpret likelihood language without turning it into certainty.
4. **Correlation, Cause, And Overclaim**: Distinguish related changes from proven causes and revise overstrong claims.
