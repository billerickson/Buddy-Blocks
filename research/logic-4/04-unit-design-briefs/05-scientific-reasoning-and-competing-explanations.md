# Unit 5: Scientific Reasoning And Competing Explanations

Track: Logic
Level: Level 4
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/logic-4/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students apply logic and data cautions to scientific explanations. By the end of the unit, they should separate hypothesis, evidence, observation, alternative explanation, replication, and conclusion in short science scenarios and choose claims that fit the available evidence.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit comes after data and cause claims because scientific reasoning often uses data while asking a stronger explanatory question. Students need to see that a hypothesis can be supported without every alternative being ruled out, and that repeated checks matter.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: hypothesis as testable explanation, evidence versus conclusion, alternative explanations, replication, revisable scientific claims.
- Vocabulary: hypothesis, observation, evidence, experiment, variable exposure, alternative explanation, replication, repeated trial, conclusion, supports, rules out, revisable.
- Representations and models: hypothesis/evidence/conclusion charts, alternative-explanation tables, repeated-trial summaries, careful-conclusion revisions.
- Texts and forms: short nonpartisan science scenarios, classroom experiment summaries, observation sets, and selected best-conclusion prompts.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need deductive/inductive/abductive distinctions, data and correlation cautions, and the ability to read short scenarios with evidence and conclusions.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- One observation proves a hypothesis.
- Evidence that supports a hypothesis automatically rules out every alternative.
- Replication is just doing extra work after the answer is known.
- A correlation is the same as a tested cause.
- Scientific conclusions should never be revised.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin by identifying the parts of a scientific explanation. Then focus on alternative explanations because that is the central link between abduction and science. End with replication and careful conclusions so students practice judging how much confidence the evidence deserves.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Review abduction through best-explanation questions. Review data scope, correlation, and probability when scientific scenarios use studies or measurements. Bring back conditional language when hypotheses are stated as if/then predictions.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares final argument-map and applied-analysis lessons by giving students a disciplined way to handle explanatory evidence. Scientific passages in Unit 6 can now be mapped without reducing them to "the graph says so."

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify hypothesis, evidence, observation, and conclusion.
- Choose an alternative explanation that still fits the evidence.
- Explain how repeated checks or replication strengthen a claim.
- Distinguish support from proof that no alternative is possible.
- Select a careful scientific conclusion.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Hypotheses, Evidence, And Conclusions**: Sort the parts of a scientific explanation.
2. **Alternative Explanations In Science**: Identify another explanation that could fit the same evidence.
3. **Replication And Careful Conclusions**: Judge how repeated checks affect confidence and choose a claim that fits the evidence.
