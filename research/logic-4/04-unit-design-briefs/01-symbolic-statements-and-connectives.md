# Unit 1: Symbolic Statements And Connectives

Track: Logic
Level: Level 4
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/logic-4/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn to treat simple claims as propositions and represent their structure with statement letters and basic connectives. By the end of the unit, students should translate among words, whole-claim letters, negation, conjunction, disjunction, and truth-condition language without treating symbols as decoration or changing the meaning of ordinary claims.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit belongs first because the rest of Logic 4 depends on separating structure from topic. Truth tables, validity checks, reasoning-type comparisons, data claims, and argument maps all require students to notice exactly what claim is being made before judging whether it is supported.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: proposition, truth value, whole-claim representation, negation, conjunction, disjunction, inclusive `or`, truth conditions.
- Vocabulary: statement, proposition, true, false, variable, symbol, translate, not, and, or, inclusive, compound statement.
- Representations and models: natural-language sentences, statement-letter keys, symbolic forms such as `P`, `not P`, `P and Q`, `P or Q`, and simple truth-condition charts.
- Texts and forms: short familiar statements, examples and non-examples of propositions, translation pairs, and brief "what would make this true?" prompts.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should know that arguments have premises and conclusions, that statements can be evaluated for truth, and that earlier informal logic work distinguishes truth from validity. They should be able to read short academic sentences and follow basic if/then, all/some, and cause/effect language from prior levels.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- A question, command, or exclamation is automatically a proposition.
- A statement letter stands for one word instead of a whole claim.
- Negation simply chooses an opposite word rather than denying the whole proposition.
- `Or` always means exactly one option.
- A symbolic form is more trustworthy than the statement it represents.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Start by sorting propositions from non-propositions so students know what can be symbolized. Then introduce statement letters as placeholders for whole claims. After that, add negation because it forces attention to the whole claim. End with `and`, `or`, and inclusive `or` so students can combine two statements and describe the truth conditions before formal truth tables appear in Unit 2.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

This is the opening unit, so review draws from earlier logic levels: premise, conclusion, true, false, valid, sound, condition, and counterexample. Students should retrieve those words briefly but not re-study earlier categorical or syllogistic content in depth.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares Unit 2 truth tables and validity checks by making statement variables and connectives meaningful. It prepares every applied unit by giving students a way to slow down claims and represent structure before evaluating support.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify propositions and non-propositions.
- Match statement letters to whole claims.
- Translate simple negations, conjunctions, and disjunctions.
- Identify when an inclusive `or` statement is true.
- Explain the truth condition for a basic compound statement.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Propositions And Truth Claims**: Decide which sentences make claims that can be true or false.
2. **Statement Letters For Whole Claims**: Use letters such as `P` and `Q` to stand for complete statements.
3. **Negation Changes The Claim**: Translate `not P` and identify the whole claim being denied.
4. **And, Or, And Inclusive Or**: Combine statements with `and` and inclusive `or` and describe when each compound is true.
