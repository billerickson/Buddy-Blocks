# Unit 6: Argument Maps, Debate Cases, And Applied Analysis

Track: Logic
Level: Level 4
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/logic-4/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students make arguments visible and respond to them fairly. By the end of the unit, they should map a compact argument, identify linked reasons and missing assumptions, choose targeted objections and rebuttals, recognize simple debate-case structure, and apply the full Logic 4 toolkit to short real-world arguments.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

This unit belongs last because it consolidates the level. Argument maps and debate cases require students to identify claims, evaluate support, notice data limits, handle scientific explanations, and distinguish objections from mere disagreement.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: main claim, support structure, linked reasons, missing assumptions, objections, rebuttals, debate case structure, applied transfer.
- Vocabulary: main claim, reason, evidence, linked reasons, assumption, missing assumption, objection, rebuttal, debate case, source, overclaim, fair response, revise.
- Representations and models: compact argument maps, reason/evidence tables, assumption bridges, objection/rebuttal pairs, debate case outlines, transfer checklists.
- Texts and forms: short academic, civic, media, science, and everyday arguments with controlled reading load and nonpartisan contexts.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need the full Logic 4 sequence: proposition and connective vocabulary, conditionals and validity, reasoning-type distinctions, data/probability/cause cautions, and scientific explanation language.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- An argument map is only an outline.
- Several reasons automatically make an argument strong.
- An objection is any statement that disagrees with the conclusion.
- A rebuttal can simply repeat the original claim.
- A debate case wins if it sounds confident or has a graph.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with claim, reason, and evidence because students need the basic map pieces. Then add linked reasons and missing assumptions to show how support works together. Next, teach objections and rebuttals as targeted moves. After that, introduce debate case structure. End with applied analysis that asks students to choose the right tool for the argument instead of being told which one to use.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Every lesson should retrieve earlier vocabulary in context: conditionals and converse errors, deductive/inductive/abductive support, sample and cause cautions, and scientific alternative explanations. Review is embedded in argument passages rather than isolated drills.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This is the final unit for Logic 4. It prepares future logic or rhetoric work by establishing careful argument analysis habits: map before judging, answer the actual support, and revise claims to match evidence.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify main claim, reasons, evidence, and assumptions.
- Complete or interpret compact argument maps.
- Choose an objection that targets a specific support link.
- Choose a rebuttal that answers the objection.
- Identify debate case parts.
- Evaluate a short argument by selecting the relevant Logic 4 tool and a careful conclusion.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Claims, Reasons, And Evidence In Maps**: Identify the basic pieces of an argument map.
2. **Linked Reasons And Missing Assumptions**: Show how reasons work together and find the assumption connecting them.
3. **Objections And Rebuttals**: Choose objections that target support and rebuttals that answer those objections.
4. **Debate Case Structure**: Recognize claim, reasons, evidence, impact, objection, and rebuttal in a compact case.
5. **Applied Argument Analysis**: Use the right Logic 4 tool to evaluate a short unfamiliar argument.
