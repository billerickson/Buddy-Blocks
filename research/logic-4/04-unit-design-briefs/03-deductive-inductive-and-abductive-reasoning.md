# Unit 3: Deductive, Inductive, And Abductive Reasoning

Track: Logic
Level: Level 4
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/logic-4/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students distinguish three major kinds of reasoning support: deductive arguments that aim to guarantee the conclusion, inductive arguments that support likely conclusions from patterns or evidence, and abductive arguments that choose the best current explanation. By the end of the unit, students should classify reasoning and judge whether the claimed strength matches the evidence.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

The unit follows truth tables because students have just seen a formal example of deductive validity. They are ready to learn that much good reasoning outside formal logic is not guaranteed but can still be strong, useful, and revisable.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: must-follow support, likely support, best-explanation support, counterexample, evidence strength, revision.
- Vocabulary: deductive, inductive, abductive, guarantee, certainty, strong, weak, likely, pattern, sample, explanation, alternative, counterexample.
- Representations and models: support-strength scale, example/evidence/conclusion charts, explanation comparison tables, and claim-revision pairs.
- Texts and forms: short deductive forms, pattern-based claims, observation-to-explanation scenarios, and selected revisions of overconfident conclusions.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need premise/conclusion vocabulary, validity and truth-table experience, basic examples of evidence, and enough reading stamina to compare short passages.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- Inductive reasoning is just a guess.
- Abductive reasoning proves the true cause.
- Deductive means "smart" or "formal-looking" rather than must-follow.
- Non-deductive reasoning is automatically weak.
- A single counterexample weakens every kind of reasoning in the same way.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with deduction as must-follow support so students can connect to Unit 2. Then introduce induction as pattern and evidence strength, including limits. Next, teach abduction as best explanation among alternatives. End with mixed support tests where students choose the question that fits the reasoning type and revise claims that overstate their support.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Review conditional validity and counterexample rows when teaching deduction. Reuse symbolic forms sparingly so students see continuity without overloading the broader reasoning comparison.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares data and science work by giving students language for likely conclusions, limited evidence, and best explanations. It prepares argument-map lessons by helping students evaluate the kind of support a reason is trying to provide.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Classify deductive, inductive, and abductive reasoning.
- Identify whether a conclusion is guaranteed, likely, or a best explanation.
- Choose evidence that would strengthen an inductive claim.
- Compare alternative explanations in an abductive scenario.
- Revise a conclusion so its strength matches the support.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **Deduction And Must-Follow Support**: Recognize arguments where the conclusion must follow if the premises are true.
2. **Induction And Strong Patterns**: Judge pattern-based evidence as stronger or weaker support.
3. **Abduction And Best Explanations**: Choose the explanation that best fits the available evidence while staying revisable.
4. **Choosing The Right Support Test**: Match a claim to the right evaluation question and revise overstrong conclusions.
