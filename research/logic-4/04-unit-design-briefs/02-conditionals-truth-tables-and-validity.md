# Unit 2: Conditionals, Truth Tables, And Validity

Track: Logic
Level: Level 4
Stage: Unit Design Brief
Source: `research/logic-4/03-course-map.md`

## 1. Unit Goal

Students learn to interpret conditional statements and use truth tables as systematic reasoning checks. By the end of the unit, students should identify antecedents and consequents, avoid the converse trap, complete basic truth rows, and use a counterexample row to decide whether a simple argument form is valid.

## 2. Why This Unit Belongs Here

Conditionals and truth tables extend Unit 1's symbolic language into formal testing. This unit comes before reasoning-type and data units because students need a clear example of deductive support before they compare it with inductive and abductive support.

## 3. Key Concepts, Vocabulary, Representations, Texts, Forms, Or Models

- Concepts: conditional truth conditions, antecedent/consequent structure, converse error, truth-table rows, validity by form.
- Vocabulary: conditional, if, then, antecedent, consequent, converse, truth table, row, column, valid, invalid, counterexample row, premises, conclusion.
- Representations and models: `P -> Q`, `Q -> P`, two-variable truth tables, premise/conclusion forms, marked rows where premises are true and conclusion false.
- Texts and forms: short if/then rules, school and everyday examples, symbolic forms, and small tables with true/false values.

## 4. Prerequisite Knowledge

Students need Unit 1 propositions, statement letters, negation, conjunction, disjunction, and truth-condition language. They should also remember earlier vocabulary for premise, conclusion, validity, truth, and counterexample.

## 5. Common Misconceptions

- If `P -> Q` is true, then `Q -> P` must also be true.
- A conditional is false just because `P` is false.
- A truth table tells whether real-world facts happened.
- An argument is valid if its conclusion sounds reasonable.
- One favorable row is enough to prove validity.

## 6. Lesson Sequence Rationale

Begin with if/then parts because students need to read conditional structure before testing it. Then isolate the converse trap so the most common conditional error is named early. Next, build truth rows for the connectives students already know. End with simple validity tests that ask whether any row has true premises and a false conclusion.

## 7. Review Connections To Earlier Units

Review Unit 1 translations inside every truth-table lesson. Include short checks on `and`, `or`, and `not` so truth tables do not become a separate skill detached from connective meaning.

## 8. Preparation For Later Units

This unit prepares Unit 3 by defining deductive must-follow support. It prepares Units 4-6 by making converse errors and overstrong conditional claims easier to spot in data, science, debate, and media arguments.

## 9. Assessment Targets

- Identify antecedent and consequent in an if/then statement.
- Distinguish a conditional from its converse.
- Complete truth rows for basic connectives and conditionals.
- Interpret a two-variable truth table.
- Use a truth-table row as evidence that an argument form is valid or invalid.

## 10. Recommended Lesson Titles

1. **If Then And Its Parts**: Identify antecedents and consequents and translate simple conditionals.
2. **The Converse Trap**: Compare a conditional with its converse and avoid reversing support.
3. **Truth Tables For Connectives**: Complete truth rows for `not`, `and`, `or`, and `if...then`.
4. **Valid Or Invalid By Truth Table**: Use possible rows to test whether a conclusion must follow.
